Defining the Scope of Work for Your Property Manager

Many property management disputes trace back to a single root cause: unclear expectations about who is responsible for what. When the scope of work is not explicitly defined—in writing, before the relationship begins—both parties operate from different assumptions. The landlord assumes the manager handles everything. The manager assumes that "everything" means only what is listed in the contract. The gap between those assumptions is where problems develop.

Defining the scope of work for your property manager is one of the most important steps you can take before signing a management agreement. It protects you from surprises, gives your manager clear direction, and creates an enforceable standard of performance.

This guide covers what should be included in a property manager's scope of work, how to document it, and how to handle situations where the scope needs to be adjusted over time. For context on the broader management agreement, read our guide on the property management agreement explained.

What Is a Property Manager Scope of Work?

A scope of work is a written document—typically incorporated into or attached to the management agreement—that defines the specific services the property manager will provide, the standards to which those services will be performed, the boundaries of their authority, and the tasks that remain the owner's responsibility.

It is not a vague description of services. It is a specific, operational list of what the manager will and will not do, with enough detail to be used as a basis for performance review.

A well-constructed scope of work answers three questions:

  1. What will the manager do?
  2. To what standard will they do it?
  3. What will the owner handle directly?

Core Components of a Property Manager Scope of Work

1. Leasing and Marketing

This section defines the manager's responsibility for keeping units occupied. It should include:

  • Where and how the manager will advertise vacancies (listing platforms, yard signs, social media, MLS)
  • The standard advertising copy and photography process
  • How quickly marketing will be launched after a vacancy is confirmed
  • Who conducts showings and how showing requests are handled
  • The tenant screening criteria to be applied consistently to all applicants in compliance with fair housing requirements
  • Who prepares and signs the lease
  • Whether lease renewals are handled proactively and at what timeline before expiration

Leaving these details vague leads to situations where a manager waits two weeks to list a vacancy, uses low-quality photos, and does not follow up on inquiries promptly—all technically within a contract that says "will market the property."

2. Rent Collection and Financial Management

This section governs the manager's financial responsibilities. It should specify:

  • The rent due date and grace period
  • When and how late notices are issued
  • When and how late fees are assessed
  • The process for handling partial payments
  • The owner disbursement date and method
  • The format and content of monthly owner statements
  • The manager's authority to approve maintenance spending without owner approval (the authorization threshold)
  • How major expenses requiring owner approval are escalated and documented

For guidance on what constitutes a strong rent collection process, see our rent collection best practices guide.

3. Maintenance and Repairs

This is often the section with the most room for ambiguity. Define clearly:

  • The maintenance authorization threshold (the dollar amount below which the manager can approve work without owner approval)
  • The process for obtaining and comparing vendor bids on larger projects
  • The standard for vendor qualification (licensed, insured, bonded)
  • Response time expectations for emergency versus routine maintenance requests
  • Whether the manager coordinates preventive maintenance and at what frequency
  • How maintenance invoices are documented and shared with owners
  • Whether the manager charges a markup on vendor invoices and at what percentage

The maintenance authorization threshold is particularly important. A threshold set too low (say, $100) will result in constant calls for routine repairs. A threshold set too high (say, $1,500) exposes you to significant unexpected charges. Most experienced managers set this between $200 and $500.

4. Tenant Relations

Define the manager's role in the day-to-day tenant relationship:

  • Who tenants contact for routine requests, complaints, and emergencies
  • How the manager handles neighbor disputes, lease violations, or noise complaints
  • The process for issuing lease violation notices
  • Under what circumstances the manager will initiate eviction proceedings, and what owner approval is required
  • How move-in and move-out inspections are conducted and documented

5. Property Inspections

Specify the inspection schedule and documentation standard:

  • Move-in inspection process (checklist, photos, tenant signature)
  • Move-out inspection process (comparison to move-in, documentation of damage)
  • Periodic interior inspections (quarterly, semi-annually, or annually)
  • Drive-by exterior inspections (frequency and documentation)
  • Who reviews inspection reports and how issues are communicated to the owner

6. Owner Reporting and Communication

Define the reporting cadence and format:

  • Monthly owner statement format and delivery date
  • Quarterly or annual portfolio performance reviews
  • Escalation protocol for urgent issues (maintenance emergencies, tenant disputes, legal matters)
  • Owner response time expectations (when the manager needs a decision from you, how quickly should you respond?)
  • Preferred communication channel (email, owner portal, phone)

7. Compliance and Legal Responsibilities

Clarify who is responsible for legal and regulatory compliance:

  • Who is responsible for ensuring leases comply with current landlord-tenant law
  • Who files for eviction if required, and who pays the legal fees
  • Who handles notices required by law (habitability, right of entry, etc.)
  • Who maintains records required for compliance (lease files, inspection reports, maintenance history)

Defining What the Owner Is Responsible For

An effective scope of work is bilateral. It defines not only what the manager does, but what the owner remains responsible for. Common owner responsibilities include:

  • Providing adequate capital for maintenance and repairs (funding the property operating account)
  • Responding to manager escalations within a defined timeframe
  • Approving major capital expenditures
  • Maintaining adequate landlord insurance
  • Ensuring the property meets habitability standards at the start of the management relationship
  • Paying invoices or funding the operating account when maintenance needs exceed available funds

This section protects the manager as well as the owner—it makes clear that the manager's ability to perform depends on the owner meeting their obligations.

How to Incorporate Scope of Work Into the Management Agreement

The scope of work can be incorporated into the management agreement in several ways:

  1. As a dedicated section within the agreement: Many professional management agreements include a detailed services addendum that specifies each service category
  2. As a separate exhibit or attachment: Some managers prefer to attach a detailed scope of work as an exhibit, referenced in the main agreement
  3. As a separately executed statement of work: For complex portfolios or custom arrangements, a standalone scope document signed by both parties is cleanest

Regardless of format, the scope of work should be a legally binding part of the management agreement—not a verbal understanding or an informal email. For a template of what a complete management agreement should cover, see our property management contract template.

Adjusting the Scope Over Time

Property portfolios evolve, and the scope of work should evolve with them. Situations that commonly require a scope adjustment include:

  • Adding new properties to the management portfolio
  • Transitioning from conventional leasing to short-term rental management
  • Adding capital improvement projects to the manager's responsibilities
  • Expanding the maintenance authorization threshold as trust builds
  • Removing a service the owner decides to handle directly

Any material change to the scope of work should be documented in writing—either through a formal agreement amendment or a signed addendum. Verbal agreements about scope changes are unenforceable and create confusion.

Common Scope of Work Mistakes to Avoid

Relying on the standard management agreement alone: Most standard management agreements are written by property management associations to protect the manager's interests. They are often vague on service standards and performance expectations. A custom scope of work fills those gaps.

Setting the maintenance threshold too high or too low: Both extremes create problems. Calibrate the threshold based on your comfort with unexpected expenses and the manager's track record.

Omitting communication standards: Without defined response time expectations for both parties, the relationship defaults to each party's unstated preferences—which rarely match.

Not revisiting the scope annually: A scope of work written at the start of a relationship may not reflect the realities of a portfolio that has grown, changed property types, or shifted market conditions.

Summary

Defining the scope of work for your property manager is not a bureaucratic exercise—it is a practical tool for protecting your investment, setting clear expectations, and creating an accountable management relationship. Cover leasing, rent collection, maintenance, tenant relations, inspections, reporting, and compliance. Define what the owner is responsible for. Document it in writing and revisit it annually.

A clear scope of work is the foundation of a management relationship that works for both parties. Combined with a well-structured management agreement, it gives you the leverage to hold your manager accountable and the framework to resolve disputes before they escalate.
