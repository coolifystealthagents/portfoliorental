2e:T2401,<p>When a tenant breaks their lease and moves out early, most landlords assume the tenant owes rent for the remaining lease term. In many states, that is only partially correct. A growing majority of states require landlords to make reasonable efforts to re-rent the unit — you cannot simply let it sit empty and bill the former tenant for every month of remaining rent.</p>
<p>This guide explains the duty to mitigate, what reasonable efforts look like, and how to document your actions.</p>
<h2 id="what-is-the-duty-to-mitigate">What Is the Duty to Mitigate?</h2>
<p>The duty to mitigate damages is a legal obligation requiring landlords to take reasonable steps to minimize the financial loss caused by a tenant's lease breach. In practical terms, it means you must try to find a new tenant rather than leaving the unit vacant and charging the departed tenant for the full remaining lease term.</p>
<h3 id="the-legal-principle">The Legal Principle</h3>
<p>Contract law generally requires the injured party to take reasonable steps to reduce their losses. This principle is well-established in <a href="https://www.law.cornell.edu/wex/landlord-tenant_law">landlord-tenant law</a>. Applied to rental housing, this means:</p>
<ul>
<li>When a tenant vacates early, you must make reasonable efforts to re-rent</li>
<li>You can still recover damages from the breaching tenant for lost rent during the reasonable re-rental period</li>
<li>You cannot recover rent for periods where you failed to make reasonable re-renting efforts</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="state-by-state-requirements">State-by-State Requirements</h2>
<p>States fall into three categories:</p>
<h3 id="states-requiring-mitigation">States Requiring Mitigation</h3>
<p>The majority of states now require landlords to mitigate. For state-specific legal details, <a href="https://www.nolo.com/legal-encyclopedia/landlord-tenant-law">Nolo's landlord-tenant guide</a> provides a comprehensive reference. This includes California, Colorado, Connecticut, Delaware, Florida, Illinois, Indiana, Iowa, Kentucky, Maine, Maryland, Massachusetts, Michigan, Minnesota, Montana, Nebraska, New Hampshire, New Jersey, New York, Ohio, Oregon, Pennsylvania, Tennessee, Texas, Virginia, Washington, Wisconsin, and others.</p>
<h3 id="states-not-requiring-mitigation">States Not Requiring Mitigation</h3>
<p>A shrinking number of states do not impose a statutory duty to mitigate. In these states, the landlord may be able to hold the tenant liable for the full remaining lease term without re-renting efforts. However, even in these states, courts may impose a common law duty to mitigate.</p>
<h3 id="states-with-limited-or-unclear-requirements">States With Limited or Unclear Requirements</h3>
<p>Some states have mixed case law or limited statutory guidance. In these states, best practice is to mitigate regardless, as it strengthens your legal position in any dispute.</p>
<p>Check your <a href="/blog/landlord-tenant-law-by-state">state's landlord-tenant laws</a> for specific requirements.</p>
<h2 id="what-constitutes-reasonable-mitigation-efforts">What Constitutes Reasonable Mitigation Efforts?</h2>
<h3 id="listing-the-unit">Listing the Unit</h3>
<p>You must advertise the vacant unit through the same channels you use for other vacancies:</p>
<ul>
<li>Online listing platforms (Zillow, Apartments.com, etc.)</li>
<li>Your website and social media</li>
<li>Yard signs if appropriate</li>
<li>Local publications or community boards</li>
</ul>
<p>List the unit at a reasonable market rate. You do not need to reduce the rent below market value to mitigate, but you cannot price it above market to discourage interest.</p>
<h3 id="showing-the-unit">Showing the Unit</h3>
<p>Respond to inquiries and show the unit to interested prospects on a normal schedule. You do not need to drop everything for emergency showings, but you should treat the unit the same as any other vacancy.</p>
<h3 id="screening-applicants">Screening Applicants</h3>
<p>Apply your standard screening criteria to replacement tenants. You do not need to accept a less-qualified applicant to fill the vacancy faster. Rejecting applicants who do not meet your normal standards is reasonable.</p>
<h3 id="preparing-the-unit">Preparing the Unit</h3>
<p>Make the unit rent-ready through normal turnover procedures. If the departing tenant caused damage, document it and complete repairs on a normal timeline.</p>
<h3 id="what-you-do-not-need-to-do">What You Do Not Need to Do</h3>
<ul>
<li>Accept a below-market rent to fill the unit faster</li>
<li>Lower your screening standards</li>
<li>Prioritize this unit over other available units</li>
<li>Rent to someone you would normally reject</li>
<li>Spend more on marketing than you normally would</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="recovering-damages-from-the-breaching-tenant">Recovering Damages From the Breaching Tenant</h2>
<p>Even with mitigation, the breaching tenant may owe:</p>
<h3 id="lost-rent">Lost Rent</h3>
<p>The difference between the rent the breaching tenant owed and the rent you eventually receive from a replacement tenant, for the period until the new tenant moves in.</p>
<p><strong>Example:</strong> Tenant breaks lease with 6 months remaining at $1,500/month. You re-rent the unit after 2 months at $1,500/month. The breaching tenant owes 2 months × $1,500 = $3,000 in lost rent.</p>
<h3 id="re-renting-costs">Re-Renting Costs</h3>
<p>Reasonable costs incurred to re-rent the unit:</p>
<ul>
<li>Advertising costs beyond normal vacancy marketing</li>
<li>Additional turnover costs caused by early departure</li>
<li>Leasing agent commissions if applicable</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="rent-differential">Rent Differential</h3>
<p>If the replacement tenant pays less than the breaching tenant, the difference for the remaining lease term may be recoverable.</p>
<p><strong>Example:</strong> Original lease at $1,500/month with 6 months remaining. New tenant signs at $1,400/month. Breaching tenant may owe the $100/month difference for the 4 remaining months = $400.</p>
<h2 id="documenting-your-mitigation-efforts">Documenting Your Mitigation Efforts</h2>
<p>Documentation is critical if you pursue the breaching tenant for damages. Courts will evaluate whether your efforts were reasonable.</p>
<h3 id="create-a-mitigation-log">Create a Mitigation Log</h3>
<p>Record every step with dates:</p>
<ul>
<li>Date tenant vacated (or gave notice of intent to vacate)</li>
<li>Date unit was inspected and any needed repairs identified</li>
<li>Date repairs were completed</li>
<li>Date unit was listed for rent</li>
<li>Listing platforms and asking price</li>
<li>Number of inquiries received</li>
<li>Dates and outcomes of showings</li>
<li>Applications received and screening results</li>
<li>Date new tenant signed lease</li>
<li>Date new tenant moved in</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="retain-evidence">Retain Evidence</h3>
<p>Save copies of:</p>
<ul>
<li>Rental listings with dates</li>
<li>Marketing expenses and receipts</li>
<li>Showing logs</li>
<li>Application denials with documented reasons</li>
<li>The new lease agreement</li>
<li>Any communications with the breaching tenant</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="calculate-damages-clearly">Calculate Damages Clearly</h3>
<p>Present a clear accounting to the breaching tenant:</p>
<ul>
<li>Remaining lease obligation</li>
<li>Minus rent received from replacement tenant</li>
<li>Plus reasonable re-renting costs</li>
<li>Minus security deposit applied to damages</li>
<li>Equals net amount owed</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="common-mistakes">Common Mistakes</h2>
<h3 id="failing-to-list-the-unit-promptly">Failing to List the Unit Promptly</h3>
<p>Waiting weeks to list a vacant unit undermines your mitigation efforts. Begin marketing as soon as you know the tenant is leaving, ideally before they vacate.</p>
<h3 id="over-pricing-the-unit">Over-Pricing the Unit</h3>
<p>Listing the unit significantly above market rate appears like you are not genuinely trying to mitigate. Price it at the same rate as comparable units in your portfolio or market.</p>
<h3 id="not-documenting-efforts">Not Documenting Efforts</h3>
<p>If you cannot prove your mitigation efforts, courts may reduce or eliminate your damage claim. Document everything from day one.</p>
<h3 id="applying-security-deposit-incorrectly">Applying Security Deposit Incorrectly</h3>
<p>Follow your <a href="/blog/security-deposit-laws-by-state">state's security deposit laws</a> when applying the deposit to unpaid rent or damages. Improper security deposit handling is a separate violation that can undermine your entire damage claim.</p>
<h2 id="key-takeaways">Key Takeaways</h2>
<p>In most states, you must make reasonable efforts to re-rent a unit when a tenant breaks their lease. This does not mean you lose your right to recover damages — it means you cannot collect rent for periods where you failed to try. List the unit promptly at market rate, screen applicants using your normal criteria, and document every step. Your mitigation log is the foundation for any damage claim against the breaching tenant.</p>
