27:T3a38,<p>A pay-or-quit notice is the document that formally starts the eviction clock. Before you can file an eviction lawsuit for nonpayment of rent, most states require you to serve this notice and wait for the specified period to expire. Getting the notice wrong — wrong language, wrong timeline, wrong service method — can get your entire eviction case dismissed and force you to start over.</p>
<p>This guide covers what a pay-or-quit notice is, the elements it must contain, state-specific timelines, a free template, and the service methods that will hold up in court.</p>
<h2 id="what-is-a-pay-or-quit-notice">What Is a Pay-or-Quit Notice?</h2>
<p>A pay-or-quit notice (also called a notice to pay rent or quit, pay rent or vacate notice, or 3-day notice depending on your state) is a formal written demand from a landlord to a tenant who has failed to pay rent. It gives the tenant two options:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Pay</strong> the full amount of overdue rent within the specified number of days, or</li>
<li><strong>Quit</strong> (vacate) the property by the end of that period</li>
</ol>
<p>If the tenant does neither — neither pays the full amount owed nor vacates — the landlord may proceed to file an eviction lawsuit (unlawful detainer action). The notice is a mandatory prerequisite to filing in virtually every state.</p>
<p>The notice does not file itself. It does not automatically remove the tenant. It is step one in the <a href="/blog/eviction-process-step-by-step">eviction process step by step</a>, not the final step.</p>
<h2 id="why-the-pay-or-quit-notice-must-be-perfect">Why the Pay-or-Quit Notice Must Be Perfect</h2>
<p>Courts scrutinize these notices closely because a defective notice voids the entire eviction. Common defects that get evictions dismissed:</p>
<ul>
<li>Wrong amount claimed (must be exactly what is owed — not including future rent, court costs, or late fees unless the lease and state law specifically permit)</li>
<li>Incorrect notice period (e.g., serving a 3-day notice in a state that requires 5 days)</li>
<li>Missing required information (wrong address, missing tenant name, failure to state the lease date)</li>
<li>Improper service method (handing it to a child instead of an adult, or skipping certified mail when required)</li>
<li>Serving the notice on a weekend or holiday when the notice period begins counting from a business day in your state</li>
</ul>
<p>Take this document seriously. Many tenants — and their attorneys — will challenge the notice on technical grounds even when they clearly owe rent.</p>
<h2 id="state-specific-pay-or-quit-notice-timelines">State-Specific Pay-or-Quit Notice Timelines</h2>
<p>This is the single biggest variable landlords get wrong. States range from 3 days to 14 days for the required notice period:</p>
<h3 id="3-day-notice-states">3-Day Notice States</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>California</strong> — 3 days (business days; excludes weekends and judicial holidays)</li>
<li><strong>Florida</strong> — 3 days (calendar days; excludes weekends and legal holidays)</li>
<li><strong>Arizona</strong> — 5-day notice is standard, but local rules vary</li>
<li><strong>Nevada</strong> — 7 days for month-to-month, with a 3-day notice option in specific circumstances</li>
<li><strong>Georgia</strong> — demand letter required; court filing can occur within days</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="5-day-notice-states">5-Day Notice States</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Illinois</strong> — 5 days (calendar days)</li>
<li><strong>Wisconsin</strong> — 5 days (calendar days)</li>
<li><strong>Indiana</strong> — 10-day notice, but some localities permit shorter periods</li>
<li><strong>Ohio</strong> — 3-day notice (check local court rules)</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="7-day-notice-states">7-Day Notice States</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Texas</strong> — 3-day notice is required, but local practice varies; check your lease</li>
<li><strong>Washington</strong> — 14-day notice for month-to-month tenancies</li>
<li><strong>Oregon</strong> — 72 hours (3 days) for nonpayment in most circumstances</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="14-day-notice-states">14-Day Notice States</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>New York</strong> — 14 days for nonpayment cases (post-2019 Housing Stability and Tenant Protection Act)</li>
<li><strong>Massachusetts</strong> — 14 days</li>
<li><strong>Michigan</strong> — 7 days</li>
</ul>
<p>Always verify your specific state's current requirements. Legislatures change these timelines, and a guide written before a legislative change will give you the wrong answer. The <a href="https://www.hud.gov/states">HUD state housing resources page</a> and your state court's self-help website are reliable sources.</p>
<p>For a comprehensive state-by-state breakdown of landlord-tenant laws, see our guide to <a href="/blog/landlord-tenant-law-by-state">landlord-tenant law by state</a>.</p>
<h2 id="required-elements-of-a-valid-pay-or-quit-notice">Required Elements of a Valid Pay-or-Quit Notice</h2>
<p>Regardless of your state, a valid pay-or-quit notice must typically contain:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Date of the notice</strong></li>
<li><strong>Full names of all tenants</strong> named on the lease (including all adults; omitting a named tenant is a defect)</li>
<li><strong>Address of the rental property</strong>, including unit number</li>
<li><strong>Amount of rent owed</strong> — itemized by month or billing period</li>
<li><strong>The exact period the rent covers</strong> (e.g., "for the rental period of April 1 through April 30, 2026")</li>
<li><strong>Payment instructions</strong> — where and to whom payment must be made</li>
<li><strong>The deadline</strong> for payment or vacating</li>
<li><strong>A clear statement</strong> of the consequence (eviction proceedings will be filed if neither payment nor vacancy occurs)</li>
<li><strong>Landlord's signature</strong> and contact information</li>
<li><strong>Proof of service section</strong> (how and when the notice was served)</li>
</ol>
<p>Some states require additional elements:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>California</strong> requires including the daily rent rate and the precise amount owed through a specific date</li>
<li><strong>New York</strong> requires specific statutory language</li>
<li><strong>Washington</strong> requires the notice to include information about tenant rights and available assistance programs</li>
</ul>
<p>Check your state's statutory requirements carefully. Many state court websites provide approved forms.</p>

<h2 id="how-to-fill-out-the-template-correctly">How to Fill Out the Template Correctly</h2>
<p><strong>Calculating the amount owed:</strong> Include only rent that is currently due and unpaid. Do not include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Future rent not yet due</li>
<li>Court costs or attorney fees (unless your state statute specifically permits this)</li>
<li>Late fees (unless your lease and state law expressly allow them to be included in a pay-or-quit notice)</li>
</ul>
<p>In many states, overstating the amount owed — even by a dollar — is grounds for dismissal.</p>
<p><strong>Setting the deadline:</strong> Count the days carefully. In most states, the day of service is Day Zero, and counting begins the following day. So if you serve a 3-day notice on Monday, Day 1 is Tuesday, Day 2 is Wednesday, and the deadline expires at the end of Day 3 (Thursday). If Day 3 falls on a weekend or holiday, the deadline typically extends to the next business day — but verify this for your state.</p>
<p><strong>Identifying all tenants:</strong> Name every adult listed on the lease. If you miss a co-tenant, the notice is defective as to that person.</p>
<h2 id="how-to-serve-a-pay-or-quit-notice">How to Serve a Pay-or-Quit Notice</h2>
<p>Service methods that are legally recognized vary by state, but most accept one or more of the following:</p>
<h3 id="personal-service">Personal Service</h3>
<p>Hand-deliver the notice directly to the tenant. This is the cleanest method — there is no question of whether the tenant received it. Have a witness present if possible, and document the date, time, and circumstances.</p>
<h3 id="substituted-service">Substituted Service</h3>
<p>If the tenant is not home, most states allow you to leave the notice with a competent adult at the residence (not a child) and mail a copy by first-class mail. Document who you left it with.</p>
<h3 id="post-and-mail-service">Post-and-Mail Service</h3>
<p>If no adult is available after reasonable attempts, most states allow posting the notice on the main entry door and mailing a copy by first-class mail. This method typically adds days to the notice period and some courts scrutinize it closely — use it only when necessary.</p>
<h3 id="certified-mail">Certified Mail</h3>
<p>Some states accept or require certified mail with return receipt. Certified mail creates a delivery record but can be refused or go unclaimed. In states where this is accepted, send both certified and first-class mail simultaneously to maximize the chance of confirmed delivery.</p>
<p>Never serve notice by text message, social media, or email unless your state explicitly authorizes these methods and your lease contains an electronic notice agreement.</p>
<h3 id="documenting-service">Documenting Service</h3>
<p>Whatever method you use, complete a proof of service immediately after serving the notice. Include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Date and time of service</li>
<li>Method of service</li>
<li>Name of person served (or description of how notice was posted)</li>
<li>Server's signature</li>
</ul>
<p>Your rent collection practices also affect how quickly these situations arise. Our guide to <a href="/blog/rent-collection-best-practices">rent collection best practices</a> covers how to structure payment systems and late notice procedures to minimize unpaid rent situations.</p>
<h2 id="what-happens-after-the-notice-period-expires">What Happens After the Notice Period Expires</h2>
<p>If the tenant pays in full before the deadline, the eviction is resolved — no further action is needed. However, accepting partial payment can, in some states, waive your right to proceed with the eviction. Check your state's rules before accepting anything less than the full amount stated in the notice.</p>
<p>If the tenant neither pays nor vacates, you may file an eviction complaint with your local court. Bring:</p>
<ul>
<li>The original signed notice</li>
<li>Proof of service</li>
<li>Your lease agreement</li>
<li>Documentation of all communications with the tenant</li>
<li>Your rent ledger showing what is owed</li>
</ul>
<p>The clerk will schedule a hearing date, and you will be required to serve the tenant with the court summons. From there, the formal eviction process proceeds through hearing, judgment, and writ of possession.</p>
<p>For a complete guide to the process that follows, see our <a href="/blog/eviction-process-step-by-step">eviction process step by step article</a>. For managing late rent before it reaches the eviction stage, see our <a href="/blog/late-rent-payment-policy">late rent payment policy guide</a>.</p>
<h2 id="summary">Summary</h2>
<p>The pay-or-quit notice is the foundation of any nonpayment eviction. Getting it right matters — defective notices cause case dismissals, delays, and additional costs.</p>
<p>Key reminders:</p>
<ul>
<li>Use the correct notice period for your state (3, 5, 7, or 14 days)</li>
<li>Include only rent actually owed — overstating the amount is fatal to many cases</li>
<li>Name all tenants on the lease</li>
<li>Use a legally recognized service method and document proof of service</li>
<li>Do not accept partial payment without understanding whether it waives your eviction rights</li>
<li>Use your state's official form if one is available</li>
</ul>
<p>The few extra minutes spent preparing a correct notice are far less costly than a dismissal that resets your eviction timeline by weeks or months.</p>
