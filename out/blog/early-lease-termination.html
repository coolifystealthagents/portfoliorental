Early Lease Termination: Rights, Fees & Procedures

A tenant who wants out of their lease before the end of the term is one of the most common disruptions in property management. Whether the reason is a job relocation, a relationship change, a financial hardship, or something else entirely, early lease termination creates a gap in your income and triggers a series of decisions that need to be handled carefully.

Your rights as a landlord in this situation, and your tenant's rights, are shaped primarily by three factors: what your lease says, what your state's law requires, and whether any special exemptions apply (such as military deployment). Getting this right protects your income and keeps you on the right side of the law.

What Is Early Lease Termination?

Early lease termination occurs when a tenant vacates the rental unit before the agreed-upon lease end date, or when a landlord ends the tenancy before the lease term is complete for reasons permitted by law.

For tenants, "breaking the lease" typically means they want to leave and are asking to be released from their remaining financial obligations. For landlords, early termination may arise when grounds such as non-payment, lease violations, or the need to reclaim the property exist.

This article focuses primarily on tenant-initiated early termination, which is the more common scenario and the one that most directly affects landlord cash flow.

Legal Grounds That Entitle a Tenant to Break a Lease

Not all early departures are created equal. Some tenants have a legal right to end their lease early without penalty under specific circumstances.

Knowing these situations prevents you from pursuing remedies that don't exist.

Uninhabitable Conditions

If a rental unit becomes genuinely uninhabitable, due to a serious maintenance failure, mold, pest infestation, structural safety issues, or failure to provide heat, many states permit tenants to end the lease without penalty. The legal doctrine is sometimes called the "implied warranty of habitability."

To avoid this outcome, respond to all maintenance requests promptly and document your repair efforts. A landlord who ignores habitability complaints has little recourse when a tenant leaves.

Landlord Harassment or Privacy Violations

In most states, tenants can break a lease without penalty if the landlord materially breaches the lease, including repeated unauthorized entry, harassment, or failure to provide essential services. Document your property visits carefully and follow notice requirements for entry.

Domestic Violence, Sexual Assault, or Stalking

Most states have enacted statutes allowing survivors of domestic violence, sexual assault, or stalking to end a lease early without penalty upon providing documentation (such as a police report or protective order). These laws vary by state; consult your landlord-tenant law by state reference to understand the specific requirements in your jurisdiction.

Military Service (SCRA)

This is one of the most important exemptions and is covered in depth below.

Job Loss or Financial Hardship

In most states, financial hardship alone is not a legal basis for breaking a lease without penalty. A tenant who loses their job is still obligated under the lease, though you may choose to negotiate a mutual termination for practical reasons.

The Servicemembers Civil Relief Act (SCRA)

The Servicemembers Civil Relief Act (SCRA) is a federal law that gives active-duty military members specific rights related to lease termination, regardless of what the lease says. As a landlord, you are legally required to comply with SCRA provisions regardless of whether your lease includes a military clause.

Who Is Protected

SCRA protection applies to:

  • Active-duty members of the Army, Navy, Marine Corps, Air Force, Space Force, and Coast Guard
  • Reservists and National Guard members called to active duty
  • Commissioned officers of the Public Health Service and National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration on active duty

Grounds for SCRA Termination

A servicemember can end a lease early without penalty if:

  1. They receive Permanent Change of Station (PCS) orders requiring relocation 35 or more miles away
  2. They are deployed for 90 days or more
  3. They are released from active duty

Notice and Effective Date

To invoke SCRA protection, the servicemember must provide written notice of termination along with a copy of their military orders. The termination becomes effective:

  • For monthly leases: 30 days after the first date the next rent is due following delivery of the notice
  • For fixed-term leases: The last day of the month that is at least 30 days after notice is delivered

Example: A servicemember delivers their SCRA termination notice on April 10. Their rent is due on the 1st of each month. The notice period starts May 1, so the termination is effective May 31.

Penalties for Non-Compliance

Landlords who refuse to honor a valid SCRA termination request face significant liability. Courts have awarded damages, attorneys' fees, and in some cases punitive damages against landlords who violate SCRA.

Do not attempt to hold a military tenant to their lease obligations when they have provided proper SCRA notice with valid orders.

Early Termination Clauses in Your Lease

While some early termination rights are imposed by law, others can be negotiated and included in the lease itself. An early termination clause defines the conditions and financial consequences if a tenant chooses to break the lease outside of legally protected circumstances.

A well-drafted early termination clause typically includes:

  • The written notice period required (commonly 60 days)
  • The early termination fee amount or formula
  • Whether the security deposit is forfeited separately or applied toward the fee
  • Confirmation that the tenant remains liable for rent until a replacement tenant is secured or the lease term ends (whichever comes first)

Calculating Early Termination Fees

Early termination fees compensate you for the actual costs of a mid-lease vacancy: lost rent during the gap, marketing costs, screening costs, and turnover expenses.

Common fee structures:

Flat fee: A fixed amount equal to one to three months' rent. Simple and predictable. The risk is that it may not accurately reflect your actual costs, it could be too much in some cases, making it hard to enforce, or too little in others.

Remaining rent liability: The tenant remains liable for all rent due through the end of the lease term. However, most states impose a duty to reduce, meaning you must make reasonable efforts to re-rent the unit, and once you do, the tenant's liability ends (minus any remaining gap).

Hybrid formula: The most legally defensible approach. The tenant pays an administrative fee (e.g., one month's rent) plus actual re-letting costs, and remains liable for rent until a replacement tenant is found or the lease ends, whichever comes first. Your mitigation efforts reduce their exposure.

The Duty to Reduce

Most states require landlords to make reasonable efforts to find a new tenant after a vacancy, even if the tenant who left was at fault. If you simply let the unit sit empty for three months without any advertising, a court may limit the departing tenant's liability to the period when you were actively marketing the unit.

This duty to reduce doesn't mean you have to accept any applicant, you can still use your normal screening standards. But you cannot sit back and collect damages while making no effort to re-rent.

Reletting vs. Buyout: Two Strategic Options

When a tenant wants to exit early, you have two primary strategic paths:

Reletting (Subletting or Assignment)

Some leases allow tenants to find a replacement tenant (with landlord approval) to assume the remaining lease obligations. This approach:

  • Eliminates the vacancy gap entirely if the replacement tenant is qualified
  • Transfers financial obligation to the new tenant
  • Requires you to screen the replacement tenant using your normal criteria

If you permit this option, make sure your lease specifies that you retain the right to approve or deny the proposed replacement, and that approval is based on the same standards as any other applicant.

Buyout or Mutual Termination Agreement

A lease buyout is a negotiated mutual agreement to end the tenancy in exchange for a payment. The amount is typically somewhere between the early termination fee and the full remaining rent liability.

Benefits of a negotiated buyout:

  • Provides certainty, you receive a defined payment and can begin marketing immediately
  • Avoids the administrative and emotional burden of chasing a departing tenant for ongoing rent
  • Allows you to move quickly on a replacement tenant without the complexity of a partial lease assignment

A mutual termination agreement should be in writing, signed by both parties, and specify the payment amount, the vacate date, the condition in which the unit must be returned, and a mutual release of further obligations.

What to Do When a Tenant Breaks a Lease Without Notice

The most disruptive scenario is when a tenant simply disappears, stops paying rent and abandons the unit without formal notice or communication. This is called abandonment.

Steps to take:

  1. Attempt to contact the tenant in writing (certified mail and email)
  2. Visit the property to document its condition and assess whether it appears abandoned
  3. Check your state's abandonment procedures, most states require a waiting period before you can treat the unit as officially abandoned and re-enter without notice
  4. Document everything: photographs, your contact attempts, dates, and observations
  5. Follow your state's process for reclaiming the unit and begin re-letting immediately to reduce damages

For guidance on turnover procedures once the unit is vacant, see our rental property turnover process guide.

Protecting Your Security Deposit Rights

When a tenant exits early, the security deposit often becomes a point of contention. Your rights regarding the deposit after an early termination depend on:

  • Your state's security deposit laws
  • What your lease specifies
  • Whether the tenant's departure caused documented damage

The deposit can typically be applied to:

  • Unpaid rent through the vacate date
  • Physical damage beyond normal wear and tear
  • Cleaning costs not covered by normal wear
  • Any early termination fee if explicitly specified in the lease

You cannot double-collect, if the early termination fee is set at two months' rent and the deposit covers one month, you can claim the remaining one month through other means, but the deposit cannot be used to pay both the fee and unpaid rent independently in excess of your actual documented costs.

See our full security deposit laws guide for state-specific deposit handling requirements.

Summary

Early lease termination is one of the more complex situations in property management, but a clear legal framework and well-drafted lease provisions make it manageable.

Key takeaways:

  • Know which situations legally entitle tenants to exit without penalty (habitability failures, DV exemptions, SCRA military orders)
  • Include a clear early termination clause in every lease with defined fees and notice requirements
  • Understand your state's duty to reduce, you must actively re-let the unit
  • Consider offering a negotiated buyout to create certainty and move on quickly
  • Handle SCRA requests promptly and correctly, non-compliance carries serious liability
  • Document everything: the departure notice, the unit's condition, your re-letting efforts, and any damages claimed
