24:T3841,<p>Most people think of IRAs as accounts for stocks, bonds, and mutual funds. But the tax code actually allows self-directed IRAs (SDIRAs) to invest in a much broader range of assets — including real estate. A self-directed IRA for real estate investing allows you to purchase rental properties, earn rental income, and accumulate appreciation all within a tax-advantaged retirement account structure.</p>
<p>For real estate investors who already understand property analysis and management, SDIRAs offer a compelling way to deploy retirement capital into their area of expertise rather than relying on the public markets. But the rules governing SDIRAs are complex, the penalties for violations are severe, and the mechanics are fundamentally different from conventional investing. This guide breaks it all down clearly.</p>
<h2 id="what-is-a-self-directed-ira">What Is a Self-Directed IRA?</h2>
<p>A self-directed IRA is an individual retirement account that allows you to invest in assets beyond the standard menu (stocks, bonds, mutual funds, CDs) offered by most financial institutions. SDIRAs are governed by the same IRS rules as regular IRAs — including annual contribution limits, required minimum distributions, and tax treatment — but they allow for "alternative investments" including:</p>
<ul>
<li>Rental properties (single-family, multifamily, commercial)</li>
<li>Raw land</li>
<li>Real estate notes and mortgages</li>
<li>Tax liens</li>
<li>Private equity and LLCs</li>
<li>Precious metals</li>
</ul>
<p>The "self-directed" part means you, as the account holder, make the investment decisions rather than relying on a financial advisor or fund manager. However, the IRA itself — not you personally — is the legal owner of all assets and party to all transactions.</p>
<h2 id="traditional-ira-vs-roth-ira-which-is-better-for-real-estate">Traditional IRA vs. Roth IRA: Which Is Better for Real Estate?</h2>
<p>Both traditional and Roth IRA structures can be self-directed for real estate investing, but they have different tax profiles:</p>
<table>
<thead>
<tr>
<th>Feature</th>
<th>Traditional SDIRA</th>
<th>Roth SDIRA</th>
</tr>
</thead>
<tbody>
<tr>
<td>Contributions</td>
<td>Pre-tax (deductible)</td>
<td>After-tax (no deduction)</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Growth</td>
<td>Tax-deferred</td>
<td>Tax-free</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Withdrawals</td>
<td>Taxed as ordinary income</td>
<td>Tax-free (after 59½ + 5 years)</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>RMDs required</td>
<td>Yes (after 73)</td>
<td>No (during owner's lifetime)</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Best for</td>
<td>Lower income now, higher later</td>
<td>Higher income now, lower later</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>
<p><strong>The Roth SDIRA is generally superior for real estate investing</strong> if you can qualify to contribute. All rental income, appreciation, and eventual sale proceeds grow and can be distributed entirely tax-free. A Roth IRA that owns a rental property appreciating from $200,000 to $400,000 over 15 years — with $1,500/month in rental income the entire time — generates hundreds of thousands of dollars in tax-free wealth.</p>
<p>Income limits apply to direct Roth IRA contributions, but higher-income investors can use the "backdoor Roth" strategy to convert traditional IRA funds to Roth. Consult a CPA for guidance.</p>
<h2 id="setting-up-a-self-directed-ira-for-real-estate">Setting Up a Self-Directed IRA for Real Estate</h2>
<h3 id="step-1-choose-a-custodian">Step 1: Choose a Custodian</h3>
<p>Unlike regular IRAs at Fidelity or Vanguard, SDIRAs require a custodian that specializes in alternative investments. These custodians handle IRS reporting, hold assets, and facilitate transactions — but they don't provide investment advice or validate your investment decisions.</p>
<p>Well-known SDIRA custodians include Equity Trust Company, Entrust Group, Pensco (now part of Inspira Financial), and IRA Financial. Fees vary significantly — compare setup fees, annual fees, transaction fees, and asset-based fees before choosing.</p>
<h3 id="step-2-fund-the-sdira">Step 2: Fund the SDIRA</h3>
<p>You can fund a self-directed IRA through:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Annual contributions</strong>: Up to $7,000/year in 2026 ($8,000 if 50 or older)</li>
<li><strong>Rollover from a 401(k)</strong>: If you've left an employer, you can roll an old 401(k) into a SDIRA without tax or penalty</li>
<li><strong>IRA-to-IRA transfer</strong>: Move funds from an existing IRA at another custodian</li>
</ul>
<p>Most real estate purchases require substantially more than $7,000, so rollovers from old 401(k)s or existing IRAs are the most common funding mechanism. For an overview of how SDIRAs fit alongside other approaches, see our guide to <a href="/blog/rental-property-financing-options">rental property financing options</a>.</p>
<h3 id="step-3-the-ira-makes-the-investment-not-you">Step 3: The IRA Makes the Investment — Not You</h3>
<p>This is the critical operational difference. When your SDIRA buys a rental property:</p>
<ul>
<li>The IRA (not you personally) is listed on the purchase contract, deed, and all closing documents</li>
<li>All purchase funds come from the IRA account</li>
<li>All rental income goes directly to the IRA — not to you</li>
<li>All expenses (property taxes, insurance, maintenance, repairs) are paid by the IRA</li>
<li>You cannot use personal funds to cover IRA property expenses, nor can IRA funds flow to you personally</li>
</ul>
<p>This separation must be maintained strictly at all times. Any commingling of IRA and personal funds is a prohibited transaction that can disqualify the entire IRA — triggering immediate taxes and potentially a 10% early withdrawal penalty on the full account value.</p>
<h2 id="prohibited-transaction-rules">Prohibited Transaction Rules</h2>
<p>The IRS's prohibited transaction rules (Section 4975) are the most important compliance requirement for SDIRA real estate investors. These rules prohibit transactions between the IRA and "disqualified persons," which includes:</p>
<ul>
<li>You (the IRA account holder)</li>
<li>Your spouse</li>
<li>Lineal descendants (children, grandchildren) and their spouses</li>
<li>Your parents and grandparents</li>
<li>Any entity (business, trust, estate) in which disqualified persons own 50% or more</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="what-this-means-practically">What This Means Practically</h3>
<p><strong>You cannot</strong>:</p>
<ul>
<li>Live in the IRA-owned property (even temporarily)</li>
<li>Vacation in the property</li>
<li>Let your children or parents stay in the property for free or reduced rent</li>
<li>Do repair work on the property yourself (no "sweat equity")</li>
<li>Buy property from yourself or a disqualified person</li>
<li>Sell property to yourself or a disqualified person</li>
<li>Personally guarantee a loan on the IRA property</li>
<li>Receive property management fees from the IRA for managing the property</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>You can</strong>:</p>
<ul>
<li>Choose which properties the IRA purchases</li>
<li>Direct a third-party property manager to manage the property</li>
<li>Direct repair contractors to perform work (using IRA funds)</li>
<li>Make investment decisions for the IRA</li>
</ul>
<p>The penalties for prohibited transactions are severe. <a href="https://www.irs.gov/retirement-plans/prohibited-transactions" rel="noopener" target="_blank">IRS guidance on prohibited transactions</a> outlines the specific rules — the entire IRA can be deemed distributed (fully taxable) in the year the prohibited transaction occurs.</p>
<h2 id="financing-real-estate-in-an-sdira-non-recourse-loans">Financing Real Estate in an SDIRA: Non-Recourse Loans</h2>
<p>SDIRAs can use debt financing to purchase real estate, but with one major restriction: any mortgage must be a <strong>non-recourse loan</strong>. This means the lender can only pursue the property itself as collateral — they cannot come after you personally or other IRA assets if the loan defaults.</p>
<p>Non-recourse loans for SDIRA real estate are a specialized lending product. They typically require:</p>
<ul>
<li>30–40% down payment (from the IRA)</li>
<li>Higher interest rates than conventional loans (1–2% premium)</li>
<li>The IRA (not you) to be the borrower on record</li>
</ul>
<p>If your SDIRA uses debt financing to purchase property, Unrelated Business Income Tax (UBIT) — described below — will apply to the debt-financed portion of income. This is an important cost factor.</p>
<p>Major lenders offering non-recourse loans for SDIRAs include North American Savings Bank, First Western Federal Savings Bank, and Sunwest Trust. Rates and terms vary significantly.</p>
<h2 id="ubit-the-tax-hidden-in-sdira-real-estate">UBIT: The Tax Hidden in SDIRA Real Estate</h2>
<p>Unrelated Business Income Tax (UBIT) is a tax that applies to IRAs — even tax-exempt entities — when they generate "unrelated business taxable income" (UBTI). For real estate SDIRAs, UBIT is triggered in two scenarios:</p>
<p><strong>1. Debt-financed income</strong>: If the SDIRA uses a mortgage to purchase property, the proportion of income attributable to the debt is subject to UBIT. For example, if the IRA borrows 60% of the purchase price, 60% of the net rental income is subject to UBIT. UBIT rates can reach 37% on higher income amounts.</p>
<p><strong>2. Active business income</strong>: If the IRA engages in property flipping or other activities deemed a "trade or business" rather than passive investment, income may be subject to UBIT.</p>
<p>Passive rental income from unfinanced (all-cash) SDIRA properties is generally <strong>not</strong> subject to UBIT, making all-cash purchases the most tax-efficient structure.</p>
<p>The UBIT calculation and filing requirements are handled through IRS Form 990-T, which the IRA files separately. Your SDIRA custodian should guide you through this process.</p>
<h2 id="practical-strategies-for-sdira-real-estate-investing">Practical Strategies for SDIRA Real Estate Investing</h2>
<h3 id="strategy-1-all-cash-rental-properties">Strategy 1: All-Cash Rental Properties</h3>
<p>Purchase rental properties with 100% IRA cash — no debt. All rental income flows into the IRA tax-deferred (traditional) or tax-free (Roth) with no UBIT complications. This is the cleanest, most tax-efficient structure. The limitation is that you need sufficient IRA balance to cover the full purchase price.</p>
<h3 id="strategy-2-partner-with-personal-funds-or-a-partner-s-ira">Strategy 2: Partner with Personal Funds or a Partner's IRA</h3>
<p>Your IRA can co-invest with personal funds or another investor's IRA, holding a proportional ownership interest. If the IRA owns 60% of a property and you personally own 40%, the IRA receives 60% of income, expenses, and appreciation. This allows smaller SDIRAs to access larger deals.</p>
<p><strong>Critical rule</strong>: The transaction between the IRA and the co-investing partner must be at arm's length. Co-investing between your IRA and yourself personally would be a prohibited transaction.</p>
<h3 id="strategy-3-lending-your-ira-funds-private-note-investing">Strategy 3: Lending Your IRA Funds (Private Note Investing)</h3>
<p>Instead of direct property ownership, your SDIRA can act as a private lender — originating mortgages secured by real estate. The borrower makes monthly interest payments to your IRA. This avoids property management, UBIT, and prohibited transaction complexity. Returns are typically 8–12% annually.</p>
<p>This is essentially <a href="/blog/private-money-lending-real-estate">private money lending for real estate</a> — but with the tax-advantaged wrapper of the SDIRA.</p>
<h2 id="the-checkbook-control-llc-structure">The Checkbook Control LLC Structure</h2>
<p>To avoid delays and paperwork when making investment decisions (each transaction typically requires custodian approval, which can take days), many SDIRA investors create a <strong>checkbook control IRA LLC</strong>:</p>
<ol>
<li>The SDIRA is established with a custodian</li>
<li>The IRA establishes an LLC (IRA-owned)</li>
<li>The LLC receives IRA funds and opens a business checking account</li>
<li>You, as the LLC manager, have checkbook control — you can make purchases, pay expenses, and execute transactions directly</li>
</ol>
<p>This structure dramatically speeds up operations. But it also places greater compliance responsibility on you, since prohibited transactions become easier to accidentally trigger. Work with an attorney experienced in SDIRA LLC structures.</p>
<h2 id="who-should-use-a-self-directed-ira-for-real-estate">Who Should Use a Self-Directed IRA for Real Estate?</h2>
<p>SDIRAs for real estate work best for:</p>
<ul>
<li>Investors with significant retirement savings ($100,000+) to deploy — particularly those already focused on <a href="/blog/build-rental-property-portfolio">building a rental portfolio</a> who want to add a tax-advantaged layer</li>
<li>Those with old 401(k)s from previous employers available to roll over</li>
<li>Real estate investors who are confident in their property selection and management capabilities</li>
<li>Long-horizon investors who don't need the rental income personally before retirement</li>
<li>Roth IRA holders with large account balances who want tax-free compounding</li>
</ul>
<p>SDIRAs are less appropriate for investors who:</p>
<ul>
<li>Need rental income to fund current living expenses (income is trapped in the IRA)</li>
<li>Want to be hands-on in property maintenance (prohibited for SDIRAs)</li>
<li>Have limited retirement savings that would be fully tied up in illiquid real estate</li>
</ul>
<p>As part of a comprehensive <a href="/blog/rental-property-financing-options">rental portfolio financing strategy</a>, SDIRAs complement direct ownership by sheltering a portion of your real estate activity within a tax-advantaged retirement structure. Combined with direct ownership (for income and cash flow) and a 1031 exchange strategy (for tax-deferred growth), SDIRAs represent an advanced planning tool for serious long-term investors building toward retirement on <a href="/blog/how-many-properties-to-retire">rental income</a>.</p>
<p>Always consult a CPA and real estate attorney before establishing and funding a self-directed IRA for real estate. The compliance requirements are non-negotiable, and the cost of errors is extremely high.</p>
