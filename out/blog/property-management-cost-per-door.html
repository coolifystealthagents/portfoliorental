When you manage multiple rental units, the most useful way to measure management cost is per door. Not as a percentage of rent. Not as a lump sum. Per door.

Property management cost per door tells you exactly how much you spend to manage each individual unit in your portfolio. This number lets you compare companies, measure efficiency, and find the pricing sweet spot for your investment strategy.

Whether you own 5 units or 500, understanding your cost per door helps you make smarter financial decisions. This guide breaks down the averages, the variables, and the strategies for getting the best value from your property management spend.

For a broader view of all management fees, see our property management fees breakdown. For help deciding if professional management is right for you, start with our complete guide to property management services.

What Is Cost Per Door?

Cost per door is the total annual property management expense divided by the number of units in your portfolio. It includes every fee you pay, not just the monthly management charge.

Formula:

Total Annual Management Cost / Number of Units = Cost Per Door

For example, if you pay $12,000 per year to manage 5 rental units, your cost per door is $2,400 per year or $200 per month.

This number is more useful than a percentage because it accounts for all fees: management, leasing, maintenance markups, inspection charges, and everything else. A company quoting 8% monthly management may actually cost more per door than a company quoting 10% once you add in all the extra fees.

Average Property Management Cost Per Door

Based on industry data and market surveys, including cost standards published by NARPM, here are the typical cost-per-door ranges for residential property management in the U.S.

By Portfolio Size

Portfolio Size Average Monthly Cost Per Door Average Annual Cost Per Door
1-4 units $175 - $275 $2,100 - $3,300
5-19 units $140 - $220 $1,680 - $2,640
20-49 units $110 - $180 $1,320 - $2,160
50-99 units $85 - $150 $1,020 - $1,800
100+ units $65 - $120 $780 - $1,440

The pattern is clear: as your portfolio grows, the cost per door drops. This is the economy of scale at work. A property manager visiting 10 units in one apartment building is far more efficient than visiting 10 scattered single-family homes.

By Property Type

Property Type Average Monthly Cost Per Door
Single-family home $175 - $250
Small multifamily (2-4 units) $140 - $200
Midsize multifamily (5-20 units) $100 - $160
Large multifamily (20+ units) $70 - $130
Condo/townhome $150 - $225

Single-family homes cost the most per door because each property requires individual attention: separate showings, separate inspections, separate vendor dispatches. Multifamily properties benefit from consolidation.

By Region

Region Average Monthly Cost Per Door
Northeast (NYC, Boston, Philly) $175 - $300
Southeast (Atlanta, Charlotte, Nashville) $120 - $200
Midwest (Chicago, Indianapolis, Columbus) $100 - $175
Southwest (Dallas, Phoenix, Las Vegas) $125 - $200
West Coast (LA, San Francisco, Seattle) $175 - $325

High-cost-of-living areas have higher management costs. However, rents are also higher in these markets, so the cost as a percentage of revenue may actually be lower.

Factors That Affect Your Cost Per Door

The averages above are useful benchmarks, but your actual cost depends on several specific factors.

1. Property Age and Condition

Older properties require more maintenance, which drives up the cost per door. A 1960s rental home with original plumbing and electrical will generate more repair requests than a 2020 build. If your properties need frequent attention, expect to pay more in maintenance markups and coordination fees.

2. Tenant Quality

Higher-quality tenants generate fewer issues. They pay rent on time, take care of the property, and communicate reasonably. If your properties attract stable, long-term tenants, your management costs will be lower because there is less leasing, fewer disputes, and less damage.

Strong tenant screening is one of the most valuable services a property manager provides. It directly affects your cost per door over time. Learn more about this in our guide on what a property manager does.

3. Turnover Rate

Every tenant turnover triggers expenses: leasing fees, cleaning costs, repair costs, and vacancy loss. If your portfolio has high turnover, your cost per door will spike. A property with 50% annual turnover costs far more to manage than one with 20% turnover.

The national average turnover rate for rental properties is about 54% annually. Properties with rents below $1,000 tend to turn over faster. Higher-rent units and well-maintained properties retain tenants longer.

4. Geographic Concentration

If your properties are spread across a wide area, management costs increase. The property manager spends more time and fuel traveling between properties. Concentrated portfolios, especially multifamily buildings, drive costs down.

If you own 10 single-family homes in 10 different zip codes, expect to pay 20-40% more per door than an investor with 10 units in a single building.

5. Level of Service

Full-service management costs more per door than partial service. If you only need tenant placement and occasional maintenance coordination, your cost per door will be significantly lower than a fully managed setup. Our property management services guide explains the different service levels available.

6. Local Market Competition

In markets with many property management companies, competition drives prices down. In smaller markets with fewer options, companies can charge more. Before negotiating, research how many companies operate in your area and what they charge.

How to Calculate Your True Cost Per Door

Many landlords underestimate their cost per door because they only count the monthly management fee. Here is how to calculate the real number.

Step 1: List All Annual Fees

Add up every fee you pay to your property management company over one year:

  • Monthly management fees (x12)
  • Leasing fees (number of turnovers x leasing fee)
  • Lease renewal fees
  • Maintenance markups
  • Inspection fees
  • Setup or onboarding fees
  • Any other charges

Step 2: Divide by Units

Take the total from Step 1 and divide by the number of units in your portfolio.

Step 3: Compare

Compare your cost per door to the benchmarks in this article. If you are significantly above the average for your portfolio size and property type, it is time to negotiate or shop for a new manager. If you are below average, make sure you are not sacrificing quality for price.

Example Calculation

Let us say you own 8 single-family homes that each rent for $1,600 per month. Your property management company charges:

  • 10% monthly management fee: $1,600 x 10% x 12 months x 8 units = $15,360
  • Leasing fee (75% of first month's rent, 3 turnovers per year): $1,600 x 75% x 3 = $3,600
  • Lease renewal fee ($200 per renewal, 5 renewals): $1,000
  • Maintenance markup (15% on $8,000 total repairs): $1,200
  • Inspection fees ($100 x 8 units x 2 per year): $1,600

Total annual cost: $22,760

Cost per door: $22,760 / 8 = $2,845 per year or $237 per month

At $237 per month per door, this falls within the expected range for single-family homes. But is there room to improve? Almost certainly.

Strategies to Reduce Your Cost Per Door

Here are practical ways to bring down your per-unit management cost without sacrificing quality.

1. Consolidate Your Portfolio

If you are buying more properties, focus on geographic concentration. Purchase in the same neighborhoods or even the same buildings. This reduces travel time for your property manager and opens the door to volume discounts.

2. Negotiate Volume Discounts

As your portfolio grows, renegotiate your management fees. Most companies offer discounted rates at 5, 10, 20, and 50-unit thresholds. If you are close to a threshold, ask the company to apply the lower rate in exchange for your growth commitment.

3. Reduce Turnover

Tenant retention is one of the most powerful ways to reduce your cost per door. Each avoided turnover saves you a leasing fee, turnover maintenance costs, and vacancy loss. Work with your property manager to implement retention strategies:

  • Respond to maintenance requests quickly
  • Consider modest rent increases rather than large jumps
  • Offer lease renewal incentives
  • Maintain the property well

For more revenue-boosting tactics, explore our strategies to maximize rental income.

4. Invest in Property Condition

Properties in good condition generate fewer maintenance requests and attract better tenants. Spending money upfront on capital improvements, like new HVAC systems, modern appliances, and updated flooring, reduces ongoing maintenance costs and can lower your cost per door over time.

5. Switch to Flat-Fee Management

If your rents are above $1,500 per month, a flat-fee management company may save you money compared to a percentage-based model. At $2,000 per month rent, a 10% fee costs $200 per door. A flat fee of $125-$150 saves $50-$75 per unit per month. Over a year across 10 units, that is $6,000-$9,000 in savings.

For a detailed comparison of fee structures, review our property management fees breakdown.

6. Self-Manage Where It Makes Sense

Some landlords take a hybrid approach. They self-manage nearby properties and hire a property manager for distant ones. This keeps costs low on local units while maintaining professional oversight where you cannot be present.

7. Audit Your Current Costs

Review your management company's invoices line by line at least once a year. Look for:

  • Maintenance charges that seem high
  • Fees that were not in your original agreement
  • Services you are paying for but not receiving
  • Opportunities to consolidate or eliminate charges

A simple annual audit can uncover hundreds or thousands of dollars in unnecessary costs.

When a Higher Cost Per Door Is Justified

Lower is not always better. There are situations where paying a premium per door makes good business sense.

High-value properties. If your units rent for $3,000+ per month, spending $250 per door for excellent management is a tiny fraction of revenue. The stakes are higher, and you want the best service available.

Difficult properties. Older properties, student housing, or units in challenging neighborhoods require more management attention. A company willing to handle these properties well deserves higher compensation.

Growth phase. If you are actively acquiring properties and need your manager to handle onboarding, renovations, and initial lease-up, the additional work justifies a higher cost per door during that period.

Premium services. Some managers offer extras like detailed market analysis, acquisition advisory, or renovation management. If these services help you grow your portfolio and increase returns, the higher cost per door pays for itself.

Tracking Cost Per Door Over Time

Once you know your cost per door, track it quarterly. Create a simple spreadsheet that records:

  • Total management fees paid
  • Number of units managed
  • Cost per door
  • Vacancy rate
  • Turnover rate
  • Maintenance costs per unit

Over time, you will see trends. Rising costs per door with stable performance may signal fee creep. Declining costs with stable performance may indicate efficiency gains. Use this data in annual reviews with your property manager.

Understanding your cost per door also helps you evaluate new acquisitions. Before buying another property, estimate the management cost per door and factor it into your rental property ROI calculator projections.

The Bottom Line

Property management cost per door is the single best metric for evaluating your management spend. It cuts through confusing fee structures and gives you a clear, comparable number.

For most residential landlords, a cost per door between $150 and $250 per month is reasonable for single-family homes, with lower costs for multifamily units and larger portfolios. If you are paying significantly more, look for ways to negotiate, consolidate, or switch providers.

The goal is not to minimize cost per door at all costs. The goal is to find the right balance between cost and quality, a principle echoed by the Institute of Real Estate Management (IREM) in their best practices for property management operations. A property manager who charges $200 per door and delivers excellent service, low vacancy, and strong tenant retention is worth far more than one who charges $120 and lets your properties deteriorate.
