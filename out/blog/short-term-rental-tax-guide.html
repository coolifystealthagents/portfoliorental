Taxes are one of the most confusing parts of running a short-term rental. The rules are different from long-term rentals. The deductions can be substantial. And the consequences of getting it wrong range from overpaying to IRS penalties.

This short-term rental tax guide walks you through the key rules, forms, deductions, and strategies that every STR host should understand. While this guide provides general information, always consult a tax professional for advice specific to your situation.

The 14-Day Rule

The IRS has a special rule that can make your short-term rental income completely tax-free. It is called the 14-day rule, and it works like this:

If you rent your property for 14 days or fewer during the year, you do not have to report any of the rental income on your tax return. This applies regardless of how much you earn during those 14 days.

How the 14-Day Rule Works in Practice

Say you own a home near a major stadium. You rent it out for six football weekends at $500 per night. That is 12 nights and $6,000 in income. Under the 14-day rule, you owe zero federal income tax on that money.

Important Limits

The 14-day rule has strict boundaries:

  • You must rent for 14 days or fewer in the entire calendar year
  • If you rent for even one day more than 14, all rental income becomes taxable
  • You cannot deduct rental expenses if you use this rule
  • You must still use the property as your personal residence for the greater of 14 days or 10% of the rental days

This rule is most useful for people who own homes near event venues, popular vacation spots, or cities that host major annual events. If you rent for more than 14 days, you need to understand how the IRS classifies your rental activity.

Schedule E vs Schedule C: Where to Report Your Income

How you report your STR income on your tax return depends on whether the IRS considers your activity a rental or a business. This distinction has major tax implications.

Schedule E: Rental Activity

Most short-term rental hosts report their income on Schedule E (Supplemental Income and Loss). This applies when you:

  • Provide the property for guest use without significant personal services
  • Do not materially participate in the rental activity in a way that qualifies it as a business

Schedule E income is considered passive income. The main advantage is that it is not subject to self-employment tax, which saves you 15.3% on your net income. The main disadvantage is that passive losses can only offset passive income, which may limit your ability to deduct losses against your regular income.

Schedule C: Business Activity

If the IRS considers your STR a business, you report income on Schedule C. This typically applies when you provide substantial services to guests beyond just the rental space. Examples of substantial services include:

  • Daily maid service or housekeeping during the stay
  • Concierge services
  • Guided tours or excursions
  • Meal preparation
  • Regular transportation services

The advantage of Schedule C is that you can deduct business losses against your other income. The disadvantage is that your net income is subject to self-employment tax at 15.3%.

How to Determine Which Schedule to Use

For most Airbnb and VRBO hosts who provide a furnished space with standard amenities, Schedule E is the correct form. You are providing a rental, not running a hotel with daily services.

However, if you provide significant personal services to guests during their stay, the IRS may classify your activity as a business. Talk to a tax professional if you are unsure.

Self-Employment Tax

Self-employment tax is the 15.3% tax that covers Social Security (12.4%) and Medicare (2.9%). This tax applies to business income reported on Schedule C but not to rental income reported on Schedule E.

When STR Hosts Owe Self-Employment Tax

You will owe self-employment tax if:

  • You provide substantial services to guests (making it a Schedule C business)
  • You are a real estate professional who actively manages STR properties as a business
  • The IRS determines your rental activity is a trade or business

How to Minimize Self-Employment Tax

If you want to avoid self-employment tax, limit the personal services you provide to guests. Standard STR amenities like furnished spaces, Wi-Fi, kitchen access, and basic supplies generally do not trigger Schedule C classification. But adding daily housekeeping, breakfast service, or guided activities could push you into business territory.

Occupancy Tax Collection

Beyond federal income taxes, most short-term rental hosts must collect and remit occupancy taxes. These go by many names:

  • Transient occupancy tax (TOT)
  • Hotel tax or bed tax
  • Lodging tax
  • Tourism tax
  • Short-term rental tax

Who Collects Occupancy Taxes

In many cities and states, Airbnb and VRBO automatically collect and remit occupancy taxes on your behalf. However, this is not universal. Make sure you understand all applicable short-term rental regulations in your area, as tax collection requirements often go hand in hand with permitting and zoning rules. In some locations, the host is responsible for registering with the local tax authority, collecting the tax from guests, and remitting it on a monthly or quarterly basis.

How to Stay Compliant

  1. Check your local requirements. Contact your city or county tax office to learn which occupancy taxes apply in your area.
  2. Verify platform collection. Log into your Airbnb or VRBO account and check whether the platform collects taxes for your area.
  3. Register if required. Some jurisdictions require you to register for a tax ID or business license even if the platform collects the tax.
  4. Keep records. Track all occupancy taxes collected and remitted. You will need this for your annual tax return.

Failing to collect and remit occupancy taxes can result in penalties, interest, and even the loss of your short-term rental permit.

Deductible Expenses

One of the biggest tax advantages of short-term rentals is the wide range of expenses you can deduct. These deductions reduce your taxable income and lower your tax bill.

Common STR Tax Deductions

Mortgage interest. You can deduct the interest portion of your mortgage payment for the rental property. This is often the largest single deduction for STR hosts.

Property taxes. Real estate taxes paid on your rental property are deductible against your rental income.

Insurance premiums. Your short-term rental insurance, liability insurance, and umbrella policy premiums are all deductible.

Cleaning and turnover costs. Every dollar you pay for cleaning between guests is deductible. This includes cleaning supplies, laundry services, and cleaning crew payments.

Supplies and consumables. Toilet paper, soap, coffee, linens, towels, and other items you provide to guests are deductible.

Platform fees. The service fees charged by Airbnb, VRBO, and other platforms are deductible.

Professional services. Fees paid to accountants, lawyers, property managers, and tax preparers related to your STR business are deductible.

Repairs and maintenance. Fixing a leaky faucet, replacing a broken window, or repainting a room are deductible as expenses in the year they occur.

Utilities. Electric, gas, water, sewer, internet, cable, and phone costs for the rental property are deductible.

Furnishings and equipment. Furniture, appliances, electronics, and decor purchased for the rental can be deducted. Items under $2,500 can often be deducted in full the year you buy them under the de minimis safe harbor election. Larger purchases are depreciated over time.

Travel expenses. If you travel to your rental property for management purposes, you can deduct mileage, airfare, and other travel costs. Keep detailed records of every trip.

Software and tools. Subscriptions to pricing tools, channel managers, accounting software, and other STR management tools are deductible.

Advertising costs. If you pay for listing promotions, professional photography, or a website for your rental, those costs are deductible.

Depreciation for Short-Term Rentals

Depreciation is one of the most powerful tax benefits for rental property owners. It allows you to deduct a portion of your property's value each year, even though your property may actually be increasing in value.

How Depreciation Works

The IRS allows you to depreciate residential rental property over 27.5 years. This means you divide the property's depreciable basis (purchase price minus land value, plus improvements) by 27.5 and deduct that amount each year.

For example, if your property's depreciable basis is $275,000, your annual depreciation deduction is $10,000. This $10,000 reduces your taxable rental income, potentially saving you thousands in taxes.

What You Can Depreciate

  • The building itself (not the land)
  • Major improvements and renovations
  • Appliances and fixtures (depreciated over 5 to 7 years)
  • Furniture and furnishings (depreciated over 5 to 7 years)
  • Landscaping and exterior improvements (depreciated over 15 years)

Depreciation Recapture

When you sell the property, the IRS requires you to pay tax on the depreciation you claimed. This is called depreciation recapture and is taxed at a rate of up to 25%. Even though you will owe recapture tax later, the time value of money makes depreciation a net positive strategy for most hosts.

Material Participation and the STR Tax Loophole

There is a powerful tax strategy available to short-term rental hosts that is not available to long-term landlords. It involves material participation and how the IRS classifies STR losses.

The STR Material Participation Strategy

Long-term rental income is always classified as passive, regardless of how involved you are. But short-term rentals with an average stay of seven days or fewer are not automatically classified as passive activities.

If you materially participate in your STR, the losses can be classified as non-passive. This means they can offset your W-2 income, business income, and other active income. For high-income earners, this can save tens of thousands in taxes.

What Counts as Material Participation

The IRS has seven tests for material participation. The most common ones for STR hosts are:

  1. 500 hours test. You participate in the activity for more than 500 hours during the year.
  2. Substantially all test. Your participation constitutes substantially all of the participation in the activity.
  3. 100 hours and more than anyone else test. You participate for more than 100 hours and no other person participates more than you.

Activities that count toward material participation include guest communication, pricing management, marketing, property maintenance, cleaning coordination, financial management, and strategic planning.

Documenting Your Hours

If you plan to use this strategy, keep a detailed time log. Record every activity you perform related to your STR, the date, and the time spent. The IRS can ask for this documentation in an audit.

Cost Segregation Studies

A cost segregation study is an advanced tax strategy that accelerates depreciation deductions. Instead of depreciating your entire property over 27.5 years, a cost segregation study identifies components that can be depreciated over 5, 7, or 15 years.

How Cost Segregation Works

An engineer or tax professional examines your property and reclassifies components into shorter depreciation categories:

  • 5-year property: Appliances, carpeting, decorative fixtures, and certain electrical components
  • 7-year property: Furniture, office equipment, and certain fixtures
  • 15-year property: Landscaping, sidewalks, driveways, and fencing

By accelerating depreciation on these components, you take larger deductions in the early years of ownership. This can create significant paper losses that offset your rental income and potentially your other income.

When Cost Segregation Makes Sense

Cost segregation studies typically make sense for properties worth $250,000 or more. The study itself costs $3,000 to $10,000 depending on the property, so the tax savings need to outweigh this cost.

If you are using the material participation strategy to generate non-passive losses, cost segregation can dramatically increase the size of those losses and your tax savings.

Quarterly Estimated Taxes

If you expect to owe $1,000 or more in taxes from your STR income, the IRS requires you to make quarterly estimated tax payments. Failing to do so can result in underpayment penalties.

When Estimated Taxes Are Due

  • Q1: April 15
  • Q2: June 15
  • Q3: September 15
  • Q4: January 15 of the following year

How to Calculate Estimated Payments

There are two safe harbor methods to avoid underpayment penalties:

  1. Pay 100% of last year's tax liability divided into four equal payments (110% if your AGI exceeds $150,000)
  2. Pay 90% of the current year's expected tax liability divided into four payments

Most STR hosts use method one because it is simpler and does not require you to project your current year income accurately.

Setting Aside Money for Taxes

A good practice is to set aside 25% to 35% of your net rental income in a separate savings account for taxes. Transfer this money each month so it is ready when quarterly payments are due.

Record Keeping Best Practices

Good records are your best defense in an audit and your best tool for maximizing deductions. Here is what to track and how to keep it organized.

What Records to Keep

  • All rental income from every platform and source
  • Every expense receipt related to your STR
  • Mileage logs for trips to the property
  • Time logs for material participation
  • Photos of property condition before and after guest stays
  • Copies of all tax returns and supporting documents
  • Bank and credit card statements
  • Platform 1099 forms and payout reports
  • Property improvement receipts and invoices
  • Insurance policies and premium payments

How Long to Keep Records

The IRS can audit returns filed within the last three years in most cases. However, if they suspect a substantial understatement of income, they can go back six years. Keep your records for at least seven years to be safe.

Tools for Record Keeping

Use accounting software like QuickBooks or Stessa to track income and expenses automatically. Take photos of receipts with your phone and save them digitally. Use a cloud storage service to back up all your tax documents.

Common Tax Mistakes to Avoid

Many STR hosts make costly tax mistakes. Here are the most common ones:

  1. Not reporting all income. The IRS receives copies of your 1099 forms from Airbnb and VRBO. They know what you earned. Report everything.
  2. Missing deductions. Many hosts forget to deduct supplies, mileage, software subscriptions, and other smaller expenses that add up.
  3. Confusing repairs and improvements. Repairs are deducted in the current year. Improvements must be depreciated over time. A new coat of paint is a repair. A kitchen remodel is an improvement.
  4. Ignoring occupancy taxes. Failing to collect and remit local occupancy taxes can lead to fines and legal trouble.
  5. Not making estimated payments. The underpayment penalty is avoidable if you plan ahead.
  6. Poor record keeping. Without records, you cannot prove your deductions in an audit.

Final Thoughts

Short-term rental taxes are more complex than long-term rental taxes, but the opportunities are also greater. Strategies like material participation, cost segregation, and accelerated depreciation can save you thousands of dollars every year.

The key is to understand the rules, keep excellent records, and work with a tax professional who understands short-term rentals. Do not try to navigate the most advanced strategies on your own. The cost of professional tax advice is itself a deductible expense, and the savings it generates will far exceed the fee.

Start with the basics. Report your income accurately, claim all your legitimate deductions, make your estimated payments on time, and build from there. Your STR tax strategy should grow alongside your portfolio.
