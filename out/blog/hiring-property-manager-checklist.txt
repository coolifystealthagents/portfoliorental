26:T477b,<p>Hiring a property manager is one of the most important decisions you will make as a rental property owner. The right manager protects your investment, keeps tenants happy, and helps you grow your portfolio. The wrong one can cost you thousands in lost rent, unnecessary repairs, and legal headaches.</p>
<p>This hiring a property manager checklist walks you through every step, from initial research to signing the contract. Follow it closely, and you will find a manager who earns their fee many times over.</p>
<h2 id="why-a-checklist-matters">Why a Checklist Matters</h2>
<p>Most landlords hire a property manager based on a Google search and a single phone call. That is how you end up with a manager who overcharges, underperforms, or both. A structured vetting process protects your investment and sets the relationship up for success.</p>
<p>The property management industry has low barriers to entry in many states. That means quality varies enormously. Your job is to separate the professionals from the amateurs before you hand over the keys to your rental property.</p>
<h2 id="step-1-define-what-you-need">Step 1: Define What You Need</h2>
<p>Before you start searching, get clear on what you need from a property manager. Are you looking for <a href="/blog/full-service-property-management">full service property management</a> that handles everything? Or do you only need help with specific tasks like tenant placement or maintenance coordination?</p>
<p>Write down your requirements:</p>
<ul>
<li>Number of properties and unit types</li>
<li>Services you need (tenant placement, rent collection, maintenance, accounting, compliance)</li>
<li>Your budget for management fees</li>
<li>Your communication preferences (weekly updates, monthly reports, real-time portal access)</li>
<li>Any special requirements (Section 8, short-term rentals, commercial properties)</li>
</ul>
<p>This clarity helps you ask the right questions and compare candidates on what actually matters to you.</p>
<h2 id="step-2-research-candidates-in-your-market">Step 2: Research Candidates in Your Market</h2>
<p>Start with at least five candidates. Use these sources:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Referrals from other investors.</strong> This is the most reliable source. Ask landlords in your local real estate investment group who they use and why.</li>
<li><strong>Online directories.</strong> <a href="https://www.narpm.org/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">NARPM (National Association of Residential Property Managers)</a>, <a href="https://www.irem.org/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">IREM (Institute of Real Estate Management)</a>, and local real estate boards maintain member directories.</li>
<li><strong>Online reviews.</strong> Check Google, Yelp, and the Better Business Bureau. Look for patterns in reviews, not just star ratings.</li>
<li><strong>Real estate agents.</strong> Local agents often know which property managers have good reputations.</li>
</ul>
<p>Avoid hiring the first company you talk to. Comparison is essential. For guidance on what separates good managers from great ones, read our guide on <a href="/blog/how-to-choose-property-manager">how to choose a property manager</a>.</p>
<h2 id="step-3-verify-licensing-and-credentials">Step 3: Verify Licensing and Credentials</h2>
<p>Every state has different licensing requirements for property managers. In most states, property managers need a real estate broker's license or must work under a licensed broker. Some states have specific property management licenses.</p>
<p><strong>Verify the following:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>State real estate license (active and in good standing)</li>
<li>Property management certifications (CPM, RMP, MPM are industry-recognized)</li>
<li>Business license in your city or county</li>
<li>Any disciplinary actions or complaints filed with the state licensing board</li>
</ul>
<p>Do not skip this step. An unlicensed property manager exposes you to legal liability and may not carry the insurance needed to protect your assets.</p>
<h2 id="step-4-confirm-insurance-coverage">Step 4: Confirm Insurance Coverage</h2>
<p>Your property manager should carry adequate insurance. Request certificates of insurance for:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>General liability insurance</strong> ($1 million minimum recommended)</li>
<li><strong>Errors and omissions insurance</strong> (also called professional liability) to cover mistakes in management</li>
<li><strong>Workers' compensation</strong> if they have employees</li>
<li><strong>Fidelity bond or crime insurance</strong> to protect against employee theft of rent funds</li>
</ul>
<p>Make sure you are listed as an additional insured on their general liability policy. This gives you direct protection if a claim arises from the manager's actions at your property.</p>
<h2 id="step-5-evaluate-their-portfolio-size-and-focus">Step 5: Evaluate Their Portfolio Size and Focus</h2>
<p>Ask how many units they manage and what types of properties dominate their portfolio. A company managing 50 single-family homes operates very differently from one managing 2,000 apartment units.</p>
<p><strong>Key questions:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>How many total units do you manage?</li>
<li>What percentage are similar to my property type?</li>
<li>What is your manager-to-unit ratio? (A good benchmark is 1 manager per 100 to 150 units.)</li>
<li>Are you actively growing, and can you handle more properties without sacrificing service?</li>
</ul>
<p>A manager with too few units may lack the systems and vendor relationships you need. One with too many may not give your property enough attention. Look for a sweet spot where they have proven systems but still treat each owner as a priority.</p>
<h2 id="step-6-understand-their-fee-structure">Step 6: Understand Their Fee Structure</h2>
<p>Property management fees are not standardized. They vary by market, property type, and service level. Ask for a complete fee schedule in writing. For context on what is normal, review our <a href="/blog/property-management-fees-breakdown">property management fees breakdown</a>.</p>
<p><strong>Fees to ask about:</strong></p>
<table>
<thead>
<tr>
<th>Fee Type</th>
<th>Typical Range</th>
<th>What to Watch For</th>
</tr>
</thead>
<tbody>
<tr>
<td>Monthly management fee</td>
<td>8% to 12% of collected rent</td>
<td>Is it based on collected or scheduled rent?</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Leasing/placement fee</td>
<td>50% to 100% of first month's rent</td>
<td>What is included (marketing, screening, lease prep)?</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Lease renewal fee</td>
<td>$150 to $300</td>
<td>Is this negotiable or waivable?</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Maintenance markup</td>
<td>0% to 20% on vendor invoices</td>
<td>Is there a markup, and is it disclosed?</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Vacancy fee</td>
<td>$0 to flat monthly fee</td>
<td>Do they charge when the unit is empty?</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Setup/onboarding fee</td>
<td>$0 to $500</td>
<td>One-time fee to set up your account</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Early termination fee</td>
<td>$0 to $1,000+</td>
<td>What if you want to cancel the contract?</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>
<p><strong>Red flag:</strong> Any manager who cannot or will not provide a clear, written fee schedule before you sign a contract. Transparency about <a href="/blog/property-management-cost-per-door">property management cost per door</a> is a sign of professionalism.</p>
<h2 id="step-7-review-their-tenant-screening-process">Step 7: Review Their Tenant Screening Process</h2>
<p>Your tenants determine your rental income, property condition, and legal risk. A strong screening process is non-negotiable.</p>
<p><strong>Ask about their screening criteria:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Minimum credit score requirement</li>
<li>Income-to-rent ratio (3:1 is standard)</li>
<li>Criminal background check scope</li>
<li>Eviction history check</li>
<li>Employment and income verification method</li>
<li>Landlord reference checks (how many previous landlords?)</li>
<li>Fair housing compliance procedures</li>
</ul>
<p>Request a copy of their rental application and screening criteria document. Make sure it complies with federal Fair Housing Act requirements and any state or local laws in your market.</p>
<h2 id="step-8-assess-their-maintenance-management">Step 8: Assess Their Maintenance Management</h2>
<p>Maintenance is where property managers earn or lose their fee. Poor maintenance management leads to expensive repairs, unhappy tenants, and property damage.</p>
<p><strong>Ask these questions:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>How do tenants submit maintenance requests? (Online portal, phone, email?)</li>
<li>What is your average response time for routine requests? For emergencies?</li>
<li>Do you have in-house maintenance staff or use outside vendors?</li>
<li>How do you select and vet vendors?</li>
<li>What is your spending authorization limit before contacting the owner?</li>
<li>Do you conduct routine property inspections? How often?</li>
<li>Can I see a sample maintenance report?</li>
</ul>
<p>A good manager has a system. They use property management software for request tracking, have pre-vetted vendor lists, and conduct regular inspections to catch problems early. This proactive approach helps <a href="/blog/maximize-rental-income-strategies">maximize your rental income</a> by preventing costly emergency repairs.</p>
<h2 id="step-9-evaluate-their-technology-and-reporting">Step 9: Evaluate Their Technology and Reporting</h2>
<p>Modern property management runs on technology. Your manager should use professional software that gives you visibility into your property's performance.</p>
<p><strong>Must-have technology features:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Owner portal</strong> with real-time access to financial statements, maintenance updates, and documents</li>
<li><strong>Tenant portal</strong> for online rent payments and maintenance requests</li>
<li><strong>Automated accounting</strong> with monthly and annual financial reports</li>
<li><strong>Digital lease management</strong> for electronic signatures and document storage</li>
<li><strong>Marketing syndication</strong> that lists vacancies across multiple platforms automatically</li>
</ul>
<p>Ask for a demo of their owner portal. If they cannot show you real-time reporting, they are behind the industry standard.</p>
<h2 id="step-10-check-their-communication-style">Step 10: Check Their Communication Style</h2>
<p>Poor communication is the number one complaint landlords have about property managers. Set expectations before you sign.</p>
<p><strong>Clarify the following:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Who is your primary point of contact?</li>
<li>How quickly do you respond to owner inquiries? (24-hour maximum is reasonable.)</li>
<li>What is your standard reporting schedule? (Monthly statements at minimum.)</li>
<li>How do you communicate important updates? (Email, phone, portal notification?)</li>
<li>Will you notify me before making decisions above a certain dollar threshold?</li>
</ul>
<p>A good manager is proactive. They contact you before small problems become big ones. They send reports on time. They answer your calls and emails within a business day.</p>
<h2 id="step-11-request-and-check-references">Step 11: Request and Check References</h2>
<p>Ask for references from at least three current property owners. Not past owners. Current ones. A company should be proud to connect you with clients who are happy right now.</p>
<p><strong>Questions to ask references:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>How long have you worked with this manager?</li>
<li>How do they handle maintenance requests and emergencies?</li>
<li>Are their financial reports accurate and timely?</li>
<li>Have you ever had a dispute with them? How was it resolved?</li>
<li>Have they helped reduce vacancies or increase rent?</li>
<li>Would you hire them again?</li>
</ul>
<p>Also check online reviews beyond what they provide. Look for patterns. One bad review can be a fluke. Five complaints about the same issue is a pattern.</p>
<h2 id="step-12-review-the-management-agreement-carefully">Step 12: Review the Management Agreement Carefully</h2>
<p>The <a href="/blog/property-management-agreement-explained">property management agreement</a> is the foundation of your relationship. Read every word before you sign.</p>
<p><strong>Key sections to review:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Term and termination.</strong> How long is the contract? What are the cancellation terms and fees?</li>
<li><strong>Services included.</strong> What exactly is covered in the base management fee?</li>
<li><strong>Fee schedule.</strong> Every fee should be listed and explained.</li>
<li><strong>Maintenance authority.</strong> What is the dollar limit for repairs without your approval?</li>
<li><strong>Owner responsibilities.</strong> What are you required to provide or fund?</li>
<li><strong>Liability and indemnification.</strong> Who is responsible when things go wrong?</li>
<li><strong>Trust account handling.</strong> How are rent deposits and security deposits held?</li>
</ul>
<p>Have a real estate attorney review the agreement before you sign. The cost of a legal review ($200 to $500) is tiny compared to the risk of a bad contract. For template language and negotiation tips, see our <a href="/blog/property-management-contract-template">property management contract template</a> guide.</p>
<h2 id="step-13-interview-the-actual-manager-not-just-the-sales-team">Step 13: Interview the Actual Manager (Not Just the Sales Team)</h2>
<p>Many property management companies have a polished sales process. But the person who sells you is usually not the person who manages your property. Ask to meet the property manager who will handle your account.</p>
<p><strong>Evaluate them on:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Knowledge of your local rental market</li>
<li>Experience with your property type</li>
<li>Communication style and responsiveness</li>
<li>Problem-solving approach (give them a hypothetical scenario)</li>
<li>Enthusiasm and professionalism</li>
</ul>
<p>You are entering a long-term business relationship. Make sure you trust and respect the actual person managing your investment.</p>
<h2 id="step-14-start-with-a-trial-period">Step 14: Start with a Trial Period</h2>
<p>If possible, negotiate a shorter initial contract term, such as six months instead of a full year. This gives you time to evaluate their performance with lower risk.</p>
<p>During the trial period, assess:</p>
<ul>
<li>Vacancy fill time compared to their promise</li>
<li>Quality of tenant placement</li>
<li>Maintenance response times</li>
<li>Financial reporting accuracy and timeliness</li>
<li>Communication responsiveness</li>
<li>Overall satisfaction</li>
</ul>
<p>Some managers will push back on a short trial. That is okay. But if they refuse any form of performance evaluation period, consider it a warning sign.</p>
<h2 id="step-15-set-up-success-metrics-and-review-schedule">Step 15: Set Up Success Metrics and Review Schedule</h2>
<p>Once you hire a manager, define what success looks like. Agree on key performance indicators and review them quarterly.</p>
<p><strong>Recommended KPIs:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Vacancy rate</strong> (target: under 5%)</li>
<li><strong>Rent collection rate</strong> (target: 97% or higher)</li>
<li><strong>Average days to fill a vacancy</strong> (target: under 30 days)</li>
<li><strong>Maintenance cost as a percentage of rent</strong> (benchmark: 10% to 15%)</li>
<li><strong>Tenant retention rate</strong> (target: 60% or higher)</li>
<li><strong>Owner satisfaction</strong> (your own assessment)</li>
</ul>
<p>Track these numbers using your <a href="/blog/rental-property-cash-flow-analysis">rental property cash flow analysis</a> alongside the manager's reports. If performance slips, address it quickly. A good manager welcomes accountability.</p>
<h2 id="quick-reference-hiring-checklist">Quick-Reference Hiring Checklist</h2>
<p>Use this summary as a printable checklist:</p>
<ul class="contains-task-list">
<li class="task-list-item"><input type="checkbox" disabled> Define your management needs and budget</li>
<li class="task-list-item"><input type="checkbox" disabled> Research at least 5 candidates</li>
<li class="task-list-item"><input type="checkbox" disabled> Verify state licensing and credentials</li>
<li class="task-list-item"><input type="checkbox" disabled> Confirm insurance coverage (liability, E&#x26;O, fidelity bond)</li>
<li class="task-list-item"><input type="checkbox" disabled> Evaluate portfolio size and property type focus</li>
<li class="task-list-item"><input type="checkbox" disabled> Get a complete written fee schedule</li>
<li class="task-list-item"><input type="checkbox" disabled> Review tenant screening process and criteria</li>
<li class="task-list-item"><input type="checkbox" disabled> Assess maintenance management systems</li>
<li class="task-list-item"><input type="checkbox" disabled> Evaluate technology platform and owner portal</li>
<li class="task-list-item"><input type="checkbox" disabled> Clarify communication expectations</li>
<li class="task-list-item"><input type="checkbox" disabled> Check at least 3 current owner references</li>
<li class="task-list-item"><input type="checkbox" disabled> Review the management agreement with an attorney</li>
<li class="task-list-item"><input type="checkbox" disabled> Interview the actual property manager assigned to you</li>
<li class="task-list-item"><input type="checkbox" disabled> Negotiate a trial period</li>
<li class="task-list-item"><input type="checkbox" disabled> Define KPIs and a quarterly review schedule</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="final-thoughts">Final Thoughts</h2>
<p>Hiring a property manager is an investment in your rental business. Done right, it pays for itself through lower vacancies, better tenants, fewer legal problems, and more free time for you to grow your portfolio.</p>
<p>Do not rush this decision. Use this hiring a property manager checklist to vet every candidate thoroughly. The time you spend now will save you months of frustration later.</p>
