How to Evaluate Property Management Company Reviews

Online reviews have become one of the first places landlords look when evaluating a property management company. And for good reason—they offer a window into real experiences from real clients. But reviews are also easy to misinterpret, cherry-pick, or game. A company with a 4.8-star average might have one hundred reviews left by tenants, not owners. A company with a 3.9 average might have earned that from a single difficult period three years ago that has since been corrected.

This guide shows you how to evaluate property management company reviews with the same rigor you would apply to any major business decision. You will learn where to find the most useful reviews, how to separate signal from noise, and how to use reviews as one part of a broader due diligence process.

Where to Find Property Management Company Reviews

Not all review platforms are equally useful. Here is where to look and what each source tells you.

Google Business Profile

Google reviews are the most broadly representative. Most customers—both property owners and tenants—default to Google when leaving feedback. The volume of Google reviews typically exceeds other platforms, which makes the average rating more statistically meaningful.

What to look for: Look at the total number of reviews (more is better), the distribution of ratings (not just the average), and how recently reviews were left. A company with 200 reviews over five years tells a different story than one with 200 reviews left in the past six months.

Yelp

Yelp has a highly active landlord and tenant community in many markets, particularly on the coasts. It is also known for more detailed, narrative-style reviews than Google.

What to look for: Yelp's filtering algorithm sometimes hides reviews from infrequent contributors. Click "other reviews that are not currently recommended" to see filtered reviews alongside the main ones. This gives a more complete picture.

Better Business Bureau (BBB)

The BBB is less about star ratings and more about formal complaints. A company with an A+ rating has not necessarily earned great reviews—it has simply responded to and resolved formal complaints appropriately.

What to look for: The number of complaints filed in the past 12 months, the nature of those complaints, and how they were resolved. Patterns involving financial disputes, unresponsiveness, or habitability issues should raise concern.

Apartment Association and NARPM Directories

Industry associations like the National Association of Residential Property Managers (NARPM) maintain member directories. While membership does not guarantee quality, it signals professional commitment and access to continuing education. Some local apartment associations also publish member reviews and ratings.

Local Real Estate Investor Forums

Platforms like BiggerPockets, local Facebook groups for landlords, and regional real estate investor association (REIA) forums often contain candid, detailed reviews of local property management companies. These are peer-to-peer assessments from people with the same goals and concerns as you.

How to Read Reviews Critically

Separate Owner Reviews From Tenant Reviews

This is the most important filter you can apply. Property management companies serve two very different stakeholders—property owners and tenants—and reviews from each group reflect entirely different experiences.

A company may have excellent owner reviews and poor tenant reviews, or vice versa. Your goal is to find a manager who serves both well, because unhappy tenants cause turnover, vacancy, and legal risk. But your primary lens should be owner reviews.

When reading reviews, look for language that signals the reviewer's role:

  • Tenant reviews often mention maintenance response times, security deposit returns, and communication from the on-site staff
  • Owner reviews focus on financial reporting, vacancy rates, communication with the management team, and fee transparency

Some platforms allow users to tag their reviewer role. Where that is not available, read the content carefully.

Look for Patterns, Not Outliers

One negative review in 50 is an outlier. The same complaint appearing in 10 of 50 reviews is a pattern. Common patterns worth investigating:

  • Multiple mentions of slow communication or unreturned calls
  • Several references to surprise fees or billing discrepancies
  • Recurring complaints about maintenance delays or unresolved issues
  • Multiple owners mentioning turnover or tenant quality problems

Conversely, consistent praise for specific behaviors—"they always call me before spending anything over $300," "our vacancy rate dropped from 15% to 4% in the first year"—is a strong positive signal.

Check the Recency of Reviews

A property management company with strong reviews from three years ago may look different today if there has been significant staff turnover, ownership changes, or rapid portfolio growth that strained their systems.

Prioritize reviews from the past 12 to 18 months. If recent reviews are notably worse than historical ones, ask the company directly about what changed.

Read the Responses to Negative Reviews

How a company responds to criticism is as revealing as the criticism itself. Look for:

  • Ownership and accountability: Does the company acknowledge the issue and explain what they did to resolve it?
  • Specificity: Generic "we're sorry you feel that way" responses suggest the complaint was not taken seriously.
  • Professionalism: Defensive, combative, or dismissive responses are a red flag in themselves.
  • Follow-through: Some reviews note that a company initially responded poorly but then reached out privately to resolve the situation. That kind of resolution-oriented behavior is a positive sign.

Red Flags in Reviews to Take Seriously

Certain complaint types are worth taking more seriously than others because they signal systemic problems rather than isolated incidents.

Financial irregularities: Any review alleging missing disbursements, unexplained charges, or security deposit mishandling should prompt thorough investigation. These are not operational inconveniences—they are potential legal and financial risks.

Habitability complaints: Tenant reviews describing habitability issues that were reported to management and ignored create landlord liability, even if the manager is the one who failed to act.

Communication failures: A high volume of complaints about unreturned calls, missed emails, or information withheld from owners signals an organizational problem that is unlikely to resolve itself.

Staff turnover: Reviews mentioning frequent changes in who manages a property indicate internal instability that will affect service continuity for your properties.

How to Use Reviews in Context

Reviews are one input in a multi-part evaluation process. They should never be the only factor in a hiring decision, but they should never be ignored either.

Here is how reviews fit into a complete due diligence framework:

  1. Initial research: Use Google and Yelp reviews to create an initial shortlist. Eliminate companies with consistent patterns of serious complaints before spending time on interviews.
  2. Interview preparation: Use specific complaints you read about to formulate targeted interview questions. "I noticed a few reviews mentioning slow maintenance responses—how do you handle that?"
  3. Reference validation: Cross-reference what references say with what reviewers wrote. Consistency across both sources is a positive signal.
  4. Contract review: If reviews mention specific fee disputes or contract surprises, read the agreement extra carefully for those elements.

The Limits of Online Reviews

No review system is perfect. Be aware of these limitations:

Review solicitation: Some companies actively prompt satisfied clients to leave reviews, creating a positive skew. Others do not ask at all, meaning their review count may underrepresent actual satisfaction.

Fake reviews: The problem of fake reviews is well-documented across all platforms. Google has faced ongoing criticism for its inability to fully eliminate fake content. Clusters of reviews from accounts with no other review history, all posted within a short window, may indicate manufactured activity.

Selection bias: People who have very good or very bad experiences are more likely to leave reviews than those with average experiences. This means extreme ratings in both directions may be slightly overrepresented relative to the true distribution of client satisfaction.

Recency and context: A single bad year due to an unusual circumstance—a pandemic eviction moratorium, a natural disaster, a key staff member's unexpected departure—can skew the historical record in ways that no longer reflect the company's current capabilities.

Use reviews as evidence, not as verdict. Combine them with direct interviews, reference calls, and contract review to make a complete assessment.

Summary

Property management company reviews are a valuable but imperfect tool. To use them effectively, you need to know where to look, how to distinguish owner reviews from tenant reviews, how to identify patterns rather than outliers, and how to interpret company responses to criticism.

Most importantly, reviews are one data point in a larger hiring process—not a substitute for interviews, reference checks, and careful contract review. Use them to filter candidates early, prepare better interview questions, and validate what references tell you.
