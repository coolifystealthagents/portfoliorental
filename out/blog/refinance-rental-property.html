When and How to Refinance a Rental Property

Refinancing a rental property is one of the most powerful moves in a real estate investor's playbook. Done at the right time and with the right loan, a refinance can lower your monthly payment, pull out equity to fund new acquisitions, shorten your payoff timeline, or eliminate costly short-term debt. Done carelessly, it can increase your risk and erode your cash flow.

This guide covers every aspect of how to refinance a rental property: when it makes financial sense, which refinance products are available, how to qualify, and how to use the equity in your existing properties to accelerate portfolio growth.

Why Investors Refinance Rental Properties

There are four primary reasons landlords and real estate investors refinance:

1. Lower the Interest Rate

If rates have dropped since you originated your loan — or if you initially used high-rate short-term financing like a hard money loan — refinancing to a lower rate reduces your monthly payment and improves cash flow. Even a 1% rate reduction on a $200,000 loan saves roughly $125 per month, or $1,500 per year.

2. Access Equity Through a Cash-Out Refinance

This is the most strategic reason investors refinance. A cash-out refinance allows you to borrow against the equity you've built in a property and receive the difference as cash. That cash can be deployed to purchase additional rental properties, fund renovations, or pay down higher-interest debt.

This approach is central to the BRRRR strategy, where investors systematically pull equity from stabilized properties to fund the next acquisition.

3. Switch Loan Types

An investor might start with an adjustable-rate mortgage (ARM) or short-term hard money financing and want to lock in long-term fixed-rate debt. Refinancing into a 30-year fixed loan provides payment certainty, which is essential for accurate rental property cash flow analysis.

4. Remove a Co-Borrower or Restructure Ownership

If a partnership dissolves, an investor dies, or ownership is transferred to an LLC, a refinance may be necessary to restructure the loan and update the title.

Types of Refinance Loans for Rental Properties

Not all refinance products work the same way. Here are the main options available to rental property investors.

Rate-and-Term Refinance

This refinance replaces your existing loan with a new one at a different rate, term, or both — but you don't take cash out. The primary goal is to lower your payment or change your loan structure. Most lenders require at least 6–12 months of ownership before allowing a rate-and-term refinance.

Cash-Out Refinance

A cash-out refinance pays off your existing loan and issues a new, larger loan, with the difference paid to you in cash. Conventional guidelines for investment properties typically allow you to borrow up to 75% of the appraised value (75% LTV). DSCR lenders may allow up to 80% in some cases.

DSCR Refinance

DSCR loans (Debt Service Coverage Ratio loans) are refinance products underwritten on the rental income of the property rather than the borrower's personal income. This makes them ideal for:

  • Self-employed investors with complex tax returns
  • Investors who've maxed out conventional loan limits
  • LLCs and other entity structures that conventional lenders won't finance

DSCR refinances typically require the property to generate rent that covers at least 1.0–1.25x the proposed monthly payment.

Conventional Investment Property Refinance

Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac guidelines allow refinancing of investment properties with competitive rates, but requirements are stricter than for primary residences:

  • Credit score of 680+ (720+ preferred for best rates)
  • Maximum 75% LTV for single-family rentals
  • 6+ months of reserves (mortgage payments) in liquid accounts
  • Debt-to-income ratio under 45%

Investors are generally limited to 10 conventionally financed properties.

Portfolio Loan Refinance

Portfolio lenders keep loans in-house rather than selling them to the secondary market, giving them more underwriting flexibility. If you own multiple properties or have non-standard income, a portfolio lender may offer refinance options that conventional lenders won't.

When Does It Make Sense to Refinance a Rental Property?

The decision to refinance should be driven by math, not emotion. Here are the key metrics to evaluate.

The Break-Even Period

Refinancing costs money: origination fees, appraisal, title insurance, and closing costs typically run 2–4% of the loan amount. To determine if a rate-reduction refinance makes sense, calculate your break-even period:

Break-Even = Closing Costs ÷ Monthly Savings

If closing costs are $5,000 and you save $200/month, your break-even is 25 months. If you plan to hold the property for at least that long, the refinance makes financial sense.

The Cash-Out Math

For a cash-out refinance, the question isn't just what you're spending — it's what you can earn with the extracted equity. If you pull out $50,000 at a 7% mortgage rate and deploy it into a property earning a 12% return on investment, the arbitrage is significant. But if you're taking cash out just to spend it, you're eroding the equity that represents your financial security.

Rate Environment

In a falling rate environment, there may be value in waiting. In a rising rate environment, locking in now before rates increase further may be smart. Compare your current rate to market rates and factor in how long you plan to hold the property.

Seasoning Requirements

Most lenders require the loan to be "seasoned" — in existence for a certain period — before refinancing:

  • Conventional lenders: typically 6–12 months
  • DSCR lenders: often as little as 3–6 months after the property is leased
  • Hard money to permanent: most require 90 days of rental history

If you're coming off a hard money loan for rental property, plan your refinance timeline early and start the process 60–90 days before the loan matures.

How to Qualify for a Rental Property Refinance

Qualifying for a rental property refinance is more demanding than refinancing a primary residence. Here's what lenders evaluate.

Credit Score

For conventional refinances, 720+ opens up the best rate tiers. Below 680, your options narrow significantly. DSCR lenders often have more flexibility, accepting scores as low as 640 in some programs.

Loan-to-Value (LTV)

The lower your LTV, the better your rate and the more programs you qualify for. Most investment property refinances cap at 75–80% LTV. To take cash out, you need sufficient equity built up through appreciation, principal paydown, or property improvements.

Rental Income Documentation

For conventional refinances, lenders use 75% of your rental income to offset the mortgage payment in their debt-to-income calculation. You'll need to provide:

  • Current lease agreements
  • 12–24 months of bank statements showing rental deposits
  • Schedule E from your most recent tax returns

For DSCR loans, the rent from the property being refinanced is the primary qualifying factor — personal income documentation is minimized or eliminated.

Reserves

Most investment property lenders require 6 months of mortgage payments in liquid reserves per investment property. If you own multiple properties, this reserve requirement can stack quickly. Fannie Mae's selling guide outlines specific reserve requirements for investment property borrowers.

Step-by-Step: How to Refinance a Rental Property

Follow this process to refinance efficiently and minimize costs.

Step 1: Define your goal. Are you lowering your rate, pulling cash out, or both? Your goal determines which loan product is right.

Step 2: Check your equity position. Get a rough estimate of your property's current value using online tools, then subtract your current loan balance to estimate available equity.

Step 3: Pull your credit report. Know your score before applying. Dispute any errors that could hurt your rate.

Step 4: Gather documentation. Current lease, 2 years of tax returns, 2 months of bank statements, and current mortgage statement.

Step 5: Shop multiple lenders. Compare rates and fees from at least 3–5 lenders. Even a 0.25% rate difference can mean thousands of dollars over the life of the loan.

Step 6: Lock your rate. Once you choose a lender, lock your rate to protect against market movement during underwriting.

Step 7: Property appraisal. The lender will order an appraisal. Be ready to provide access and any documentation of recent improvements.

Step 8: Underwriting and closing. Answer lender requests promptly. Sign closing documents and receive your cash (for cash-out refinances, funds are typically available 3 business days after closing).

Using a Cash-Out Refinance to Build Your Portfolio

The cash-out refinance is a core portfolio-building tool when used strategically. Here's how it connects to a larger growth strategy:

  1. Acquire a property using conventional financing, private money, or hard money
  2. Improve and stabilize the property, adding value through renovation and reliable tenants
  3. Cash-out refinance at the higher appraised value, pulling out 70–75% of the new value
  4. Redeploy the equity into the next acquisition

This cycle — when executed with disciplined underwriting and deal selection — can allow a single invested dollar to be recycled through multiple properties over time. Combined with a strong buy-and-hold strategy, this approach builds both cash flow and net worth simultaneously.

Tax Implications of Refinancing Rental Property

Refinancing generally does not create a taxable event. The cash you receive in a cash-out refinance is not income — it's debt. However, there are important tax considerations:

  • Interest deductibility: Mortgage interest on rental properties is generally tax-deductible as a business expense. Consult your CPA to confirm based on your specific situation.
  • Closing costs: Some refinance costs are deductible in the year paid; others must be amortized over the life of the loan.
  • Cash-out proceeds: Not taxable, but the increased loan balance means higher interest expense — which is deductible.

Review rental property tax deductions to understand how refinancing fits into your overall tax strategy. IRS Publication 527 covers residential rental property tax rules in detail.

Key Takeaways

Refinancing a rental property is one of the highest-leverage financial moves available to real estate investors. Whether you're lowering your rate to improve cash flow, pulling equity to fund the next deal, or transitioning from short-term financing to long-term stability, the refinance is a tool you'll use repeatedly as your portfolio grows.

The most important things to get right: understand your goal before you apply, shop multiple lenders, know your seasoning requirements, and always run the math before pulling the trigger. Done right, strategic refinancing is the engine that allows a single property to fund many more.
