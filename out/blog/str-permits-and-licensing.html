One of the most common mistakes new short-term rental hosts make is launching a listing before confirming they have the right permits and licenses. In many cities, operating without the proper documentation can result in fines of thousands of dollars per day, forced removal of your listing, and even criminal citations in some jurisdictions.

The landscape of STR permits and licensing is fragmented and constantly evolving. What's required in Nashville is entirely different from what's needed in San Francisco or Austin. This guide gives you a framework for understanding permit requirements, walks through several major markets, and shows you exactly how to research what's needed in your city.

For the full compliance picture, start with our short-term rental management guide.

Why STR Permits and Licensing Exist

Cities regulate short-term rentals for several legitimate reasons:

  • Housing supply concerns: Municipalities argue that STRs reduce long-term housing availability, driving up rents for residents.
  • Safety standards: Hotels must meet fire, health, and building codes. STR regulations attempt to apply similar standards to private homes.
  • Tax collection: Permits enable cities to track and collect lodging taxes, also called occupancy taxes or transient occupancy taxes (TOT).
  • Neighborhood quality: Permit requirements often include noise, parking, and guest conduct standards.
  • Data collection: Some cities use permit registries to monitor and cap the number of active STRs in certain zones.

Understanding the rationale helps you anticipate what requirements you'll face and why they matter to local authorities.

The Three Layers of STR Permitting

STR compliance typically involves three distinct government levels, each with separate requirements:

1. State-Level Registration or Licensing

Some states require STR hosts to register at the state level, obtain a state sales tax permit, or comply with state lodging laws. Florida, for example, requires vacation rental operators to obtain a license from the Department of Business and Professional Regulation (DBPR) — one of the most stringent state-level requirements in the country.

2. City or County Permits

This is where the bulk of STR regulation lives. Cities and counties issue STR-specific permits, business licenses, or certificates of operation. Requirements vary enormously — from simple online registration to full inspections.

3. HOA or Building Rules

Private associations can impose additional restrictions beyond government permits. See our guide on airbnb hoa rules restrictions for details on navigating that layer.

Overview of STR Permit Requirements by Major Market

Nashville, Tennessee

Nashville has one of the more structured STR permit systems in the country. The city distinguishes between:

  • Owner-occupied STRs (Non-Owner Occupied Residential): Hosts who live in the property as their primary residence face fewer restrictions.
  • Non-owner-occupied STRs: Subject to a permit cap, zoning restrictions, and are generally not permitted in residential zones.

Key requirements include a permit from the Metro Codes Department, proof of owner occupancy (if applicable), a fire inspection, and payment of the applicable permit fee. Permits must be renewed annually. Nashville has implemented neighborhood caps limiting the number of non-owner-occupied permits per census block.

Austin, Texas

Austin requires all STR operators to obtain a Short-Term Rental License from the Development Services Department. The city classifies STRs into:

  • Type 1: Owner-occupied properties where the host lives on-site
  • Type 2: Non-owner-occupied properties in residential zones
  • Type 3: STRs in certain commercial zones

Type 2 licenses face the greatest restrictions, including caps and zoning limitations. Hosts must also collect and remit hotel occupancy tax to both the state of Texas and the City of Austin. Licenses require renewal every two years and non-compliance can result in significant fines.

New York City, New York

New York City has some of the strictest STR laws in the nation. Local Law 18, which took effect in 2023, requires all STR hosts to register with the Mayor's Office of Special Enforcement (OSE) and be physically present during the guest's stay. The law effectively bans whole-apartment rentals for stays under 30 days.

Registration requires a valid government-issued ID, a self-certification, and a fee. The law has substantially reduced the number of active listings in the city.

San Francisco, California

San Francisco requires hosts to register as a "short-term residential rental" with the Office of Short-Term Rentals. The host must:

  • Be a permanent resident of the unit (primary residence only)
  • Register with the Planning Department
  • Collect and remit transient occupancy tax
  • Limit rentals to 90 days per year when not present (unlimited when the host is on-site)

The city actively enforces these rules and cross-references rental platforms against its permit registry.

Miami-Dade County, Florida

Florida's statewide preemption law limits the ability of local governments to ban STRs outright, but Miami-Dade County and the City of Miami have implemented registration requirements, zoning rules, and safety inspections. Hosts must also comply with Florida's state vacation rental licensing through DBPR, making Florida one of the more complex states for STR compliance.

Denver, Colorado

Denver requires a Short-Term Rental License issued by the Excise and Licenses department. The property must be the host's primary residence. Hosts must:

  • Submit a license application with proof of primary residency
  • Pass a brief inspection or self-certification
  • Collect Denver's Lodger's Tax and file monthly returns
  • Renew annually

Denver actively enforces its primary residence requirement and has revoked licenses found to be operating from non-primary residences.

Chicago, Illinois

Chicago's Shared Housing Ordinance requires all STR hosts to register as either a "Shared Housing Unit" (for rentals of individual rooms or partial units) or a "Vacation Rental" (for whole-unit rentals). Key requirements include:

  • A city registration certificate
  • Compliance with zoning rules (STRs are not permitted in all zones)
  • Payment of the city hotel accommodation tax
  • Passing building inspections in some cases

Phoenix, Arizona

Arizona's state preemption law prevents cities from banning STRs but allows limited regulation. Phoenix requires STR hosts to:

  • Obtain a Transaction Privilege Tax (TPT) license from the Arizona Department of Revenue
  • Register the rental with the city
  • Comply with noise, parking, and guest conduct rules

Arizona is generally considered more STR-friendly than states like New York or California.

How to Research STR Permit Requirements in Your City

Because regulations change frequently, always verify current requirements directly from official sources. Here's the research process:

Step 1: Search your city's official website Search "[Your City] short-term rental permit" on your city or county government website. Look for departments like Planning, Zoning, Licenses, or Development Services.

Step 2: Check your state's requirements Search your state's department of revenue and department of business/professional regulation for any statewide STR registration or tax requirements.

Step 3: Contact the licensing department directly Many city websites are outdated. A phone call to the licensing office can confirm current requirements and processing times.

Step 4: Consult a local attorney or permit expediter In complex markets like NYC or San Francisco, professional help is often worth the cost.

Step 5: Check platform resources Airbnb and Vrbo maintain regulatory information pages for major markets. These are helpful starting points, but always verify with official government sources as platform information can lag behind regulatory changes.

Common STR Permit Application Requirements

Regardless of market, most permit applications require some combination of:

  • Proof of property ownership or tenancy (deed, lease, or mortgage statement)
  • Proof of primary residency (driver's license, utility bills, voter registration)
  • Building or fire inspection (or self-certification)
  • Proof of adequate insurance — many cities require a minimum liability coverage level
  • Emergency contact information for a local contact reachable 24/7
  • Application fee (typically $50–$500 depending on the market)
  • Business license (some cities require this separately — see our business license short term rental guide)

The SBA licensing guide covers general business permit requirements.

Permit Caps and Lotteries

In some high-demand markets, the number of available STR permits is capped. When caps are reached:

  • New applicants may be placed on a waitlist
  • Some cities conduct annual lotteries for a limited number of permits
  • Permits may become transferable assets, sometimes trading at significant premiums

If you're entering a capped market, understand the waitlist process before making a purchase decision. A property without a permit in a capped market may be unable to operate as an STR for years.

Consequences of Operating Without a Permit

The enforcement landscape has hardened significantly. Consequences of operating without required permits include:

  • Daily fines: Commonly $500–$5,000 per day of unlicensed operation
  • Listing removal: Platforms like Airbnb comply with city enforcement requests and will remove non-compliant listings
  • Back taxes: Regulators can assess back lodging taxes with interest and penalties
  • Criminal citations: Some jurisdictions treat repeat violations as misdemeanors
  • Permit denial: Getting caught operating without a permit can disqualify you from obtaining one later

Protecting yourself also means having proper coverage. Read our short-term rental insurance guide to ensure your policy aligns with permit requirements.

Staying Current as Regulations Change

STR regulations are one of the most rapidly changing areas of local law. To stay current:

  • Subscribe to your city's planning or licensing department newsletters
  • Join local host associations or online communities (Facebook groups, local Airbnb host forums)
  • Set a calendar reminder to review your permit status 90 days before renewal
  • Follow STR advocacy organizations like Hosts for Housing or your state's vacation rental association
  • Review Airbnb's Help Center regulatory pages for your market annually

Summary and Key Takeaways

STR permits and licensing requirements are non-negotiable and vary dramatically by city. Here's what every host needs to know:

  • Three layers of compliance exist: state, city/county, and private (HOA/building)
  • Major markets like NYC, San Francisco, and Nashville have strict, actively-enforced requirements
  • Primary residency requirements eliminate many investment property STR opportunities in certain cities
  • Always research requirements directly from official government sources
  • Operating without a permit risks fines, listing removal, and back tax assessments
  • Regulations change frequently — build a system for staying current
