1d:T2dce,<p>Bonus depreciation for rental property is one of the most powerful — and time-sensitive — tax tools available to real estate investors. When properly applied, it allows landlords to deduct a large percentage of the cost of qualifying property in the first year, rather than spreading it across many years of standard depreciation. The result is a significant reduction in taxable income precisely when you need it most: at acquisition or renovation.</p>
<p>But bonus depreciation rules have been changing. Phase-outs are in effect, percentages are declining, and strategic timing matters more than ever. This guide explains how bonus depreciation works for rental properties, what qualifies, how to combine it with cost segregation for maximum impact, and what investors need to know as the rules continue to evolve.</p>
<h2 id="what-is-bonus-depreciation">What Is Bonus Depreciation?</h2>
<p>Bonus depreciation — formally known as the additional first-year depreciation deduction under IRC Section 168(k) — allows businesses and investors to immediately deduct a percentage of the cost of qualifying property placed in service during the tax year, rather than depreciating it over the standard recovery period. The IRS provides detailed guidance on <a href="https://www.irs.gov/publications/p946">depreciation of rental property</a> in Publication 946.</p>
<p>For rental property investors, bonus depreciation applies primarily to:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Personal property</strong> identified through a cost segregation study (5-year and 7-year assets such as appliances, carpet, fixtures, and specialty lighting)</li>
<li><strong>Land improvements</strong> that qualify as 15-year property (parking lots, landscaping, fencing, driveways)</li>
<li>Certain <strong>qualified improvement property (QIP)</strong> — interior improvements to nonresidential buildings</li>
</ul>
<p>Standard residential rental property (the building structure itself) does not qualify for bonus depreciation — it is depreciated over 27.5 years using straight-line depreciation, as outlined in <a href="https://www.irs.gov/publications/p527">IRS Publication 527</a>. This is why cost segregation is so critical: it unlocks the bonus depreciation potential that is hidden within the building.</p>
<h2 id="the-bonus-depreciation-phase-out-schedule">The Bonus Depreciation Phase-Out Schedule</h2>
<p>The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 (TCJA) temporarily expanded bonus depreciation to 100% for property placed in service between September 27, 2017, and December 31, 2022. That period has passed, and bonus depreciation is now phasing down:</p>

<p>This declining schedule creates urgency. Property placed in service in 2025 captures 40% bonus depreciation; in 2026, only 20%. After that — unless Congress acts — the provision sunsets entirely.</p>
<p>Legislation has been introduced in multiple sessions to extend or restore 100% bonus depreciation, but as of the date of this article, the phase-out remains in effect. Investors should monitor legislative developments and plan accordingly.</p>
<h2 id="how-bonus-depreciation-works-in-practice">How Bonus Depreciation Works in Practice</h2>
<p>Without cost segregation, a residential rental property purchased for $900,000 (excluding land) is depreciated at $32,727/year over 27.5 years — no bonus depreciation applies to the building itself.</p>
<p>With a cost segregation study that identifies $180,000 in 5-year and 15-year assets, those assets qualify for bonus depreciation:</p>
<p><strong>In 2025 (40% rate):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>$180,000 × 40% = <strong>$72,000 bonus depreciation in year one</strong></li>
<li>Remaining $108,000 depreciated over standard 5- or 15-year schedules</li>
<li>$720,000 (structural) depreciated at $26,182/year</li>
</ul>
<p>Total year-one depreciation without cost seg: <del>$32,727
Total year-one depreciation with cost seg and bonus: **</del>$109,000+**</p>
<p>At a 32% marginal tax rate, the difference is roughly $24,700 in additional first-year tax savings — real money that improves your cash-on-cash return and can fund the next investment.</p>
<p>To understand how cost segregation studies identify these qualifying assets, see our detailed guide on <a href="/blog/cost-segregation-study-rental">cost segregation for rental properties</a>.</p>
<h2 id="bonus-depreciation-vs-section-179">Bonus Depreciation vs. Section 179</h2>
<p>Section 179 is a different first-year expensing provision that is often confused with bonus depreciation. Key differences:</p>

<p>For rental property investors, <strong>bonus depreciation is generally more useful</strong> because it can create or deepen a tax loss, while Section 179 is limited to the income from the rental activity (making it less useful for landlords in a loss position).</p>
<p>Section 179 also does not apply to residential rental property directly, though it can apply to certain personal property items within rentals when other conditions are met. For a broader overview, Investopedia covers the <a href="https://www.investopedia.com/terms/r/rentalreal-estate-loss-allowance.asp">rental real estate loss allowance</a> and how it interacts with depreciation strategies. Your CPA can help evaluate which provision is optimal for each asset.</p>
<h2 id="the-passive-activity-loss-interaction">The Passive Activity Loss Interaction</h2>
<p>Here is the critical limitation that investors must understand: bonus depreciation on rental property creates a larger depreciation deduction, which often creates or deepens a rental loss. Whether that loss is currently usable depends on the <a href="/blog/passive-activity-loss-rules-rental">passive activity loss rules</a>.</p>
<p>If your rental is treated as passive income (the default):</p>
<ul>
<li>The bonus depreciation-generated loss may be suspended if you exceed the $150,000 AGI threshold</li>
<li>Suspended losses carry forward and are released upon property sale or when passive income arises</li>
</ul>
<p>If you qualify as a <a href="/blog/real-estate-professional-tax-status">real estate professional</a>:</p>
<ul>
<li>The loss is non-passive and can offset any income — including wages and business profits</li>
<li>This makes bonus depreciation through cost segregation an extraordinarily powerful tool</li>
</ul>
<p>The combination of real estate professional status + cost segregation + bonus depreciation is one of the highest-leverage tax strategies in real estate. For the right investor profile, it can effectively eliminate federal income tax for years.</p>
<h2 id="qualified-improvement-property-qip-and-bonus-depreciation">Qualified Improvement Property (QIP) and Bonus Depreciation</h2>
<p>One of the most significant changes in the TCJA — corrected by the CARES Act in 2020 — was the reclassification of qualified improvement property (QIP) as 15-year property, making it eligible for bonus depreciation.</p>
<p>QIP includes interior improvements to nonresidential buildings (not residential rental property) placed in service after the building was first placed in service. Think: tenant improvements, interior renovations in office buildings, and retail fit-outs.</p>
<p>For landlords with commercial rental properties — retail centers, office buildings, mixed-use properties — QIP can be a significant source of bonus depreciation on renovation costs.</p>
<h2 id="bonus-depreciation-on-used-property">Bonus Depreciation on Used Property</h2>
<p>A major expansion in the TCJA was extending bonus depreciation to <strong>used property</strong>. Prior to 2018, bonus depreciation only applied to new property. Now it applies to used property as long as you or your predecessor has not previously used or depreciated the specific property.</p>
<p>This means:</p>
<ul>
<li>Purchasing a used rental building and conducting a cost segregation study can generate bonus depreciation on all identified personal property and land improvements — even if the property is decades old</li>
<li>Look-back cost segregation studies on properties purchased in prior years can also trigger bonus depreciation (through Form 3115) for the identified assets, subject to the applicable year's rate</li>
</ul>
<p>This is a substantial opportunity for investors who acquired properties before knowing about bonus depreciation.</p>
<h2 id="recapture-considerations-at-sale">Recapture Considerations at Sale</h2>
<p>Bonus depreciation, like all depreciation, is subject to recapture when you sell. Section 1245 recapture applies to personal property (5-year and 7-year assets) at ordinary income rates. Section 1250 recapture applies to real property improvements at a maximum 25% rate.</p>
<p>Effective planning for recapture includes:</p>
<ul>
<li>Using a <a href="/blog/1031-exchange-rental-property-guide">1031 exchange</a> to defer recapture indefinitely by rolling into a new property</li>
<li>Holding until death, where the step-up in basis eliminates recapture for heirs</li>
<li>Modeling the after-tax economics at the time of purchase so recapture is factored into your return projections from day one</li>
</ul>
<p>Your <a href="/blog/rental-property-cpa-tips">rental property CPA</a> should help you model the recapture impact alongside current deduction benefits so you have a complete picture.</p>
<h2 id="strategic-planning-when-to-maximize-bonus-depreciation">Strategic Planning: When to Maximize Bonus Depreciation</h2>
<p>Given the phase-out schedule, here is practical guidance for investors:</p>
<p><strong>Act sooner rather than later</strong>: Each year of delay reduces the bonus depreciation rate. A purchase in 2025 captures 40%; in 2026, only 20%.</p>
<p><strong>Prioritize cost segregation on recent acquisitions</strong>: If you purchased rental properties in the last few years and have not commissioned a cost segregation study, the window for capturing meaningful bonus depreciation is narrowing.</p>
<p><strong>Consider renovation timing</strong>: If you're planning a major renovation, completing it (placing improvements in service) before December 31 of the current year captures that year's applicable rate.</p>
<p><strong>Coordinate with your tax projection</strong>: Bonus depreciation is only valuable if you have tax liability to offset (or can use the losses under real estate professional status). Work with your CPA to ensure the deductions are actually being used.</p>
<p>Bonus depreciation is one of the key levers in any strategy to <a href="/blog/maximize-rental-income-strategies">maximize rental income and returns</a>. Combined with accurate <a href="/blog/rental-property-depreciation-guide">rental property depreciation</a>, disciplined <a href="/blog/rental-property-bookkeeping">bookkeeping</a>, and a good tax advisor, it can dramatically improve your after-tax returns on every property you acquire or renovate.</p>
