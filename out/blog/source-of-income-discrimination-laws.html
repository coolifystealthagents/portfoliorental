Source-of-Income Discrimination Laws Explained

Refusing a tenant because they pay rent with a housing voucher used to be standard practice in many rental markets. That is changing fast. A growing number of states and cities now prohibit source-of-income discrimination, meaning landlords cannot reject applicants solely because they use Section 8 vouchers, veterans assistance, or other government subsidies.

This guide explains the current legal landscape, which jurisdictions have these laws, and how to stay compliant as a landlord.

What Is Source-of-Income Discrimination?

Source-of-income (SOI) discrimination occurs when a landlord refuses to rent to a tenant or treats them differently because of how they pay rent. The most common scenario involves landlords rejecting tenants who use Housing Choice Vouchers, commonly known as Section 8.

SOI discrimination can also apply to tenants who rely on:

  • Social Security or disability payments
  • Veterans Affairs Supportive Housing (VASH) vouchers
  • Child support or alimony
  • Emergency rental assistance
  • Unemployment benefits
  • Any lawful source of income

The key principle is that if the tenant can demonstrate ability to pay rent through any legal means, rejecting them based on how they earn or receive that money may violate SOI protections where they exist.

Federal Law vs State and Local Law

There is no federal law that prohibits source-of-income discrimination. The Fair Housing Act protects against discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, national origin, familial status, and disability, but it does not include income source as a protected class.

However, many states, counties, and cities have passed their own SOI protections. As of 2026, the trend is accelerating. Landlords must know their local requirements.

States With SOI Protections

The following states have enacted statewide source-of-income discrimination laws:

  • California
  • Colorado
  • Connecticut
  • Delaware
  • Illinois (Cook County and Chicago)
  • Iowa (limited)
  • Maine
  • Maryland
  • Massachusetts
  • Minnesota
  • Montana
  • New Jersey
  • New York
  • North Dakota
  • Oklahoma
  • Oregon
  • Utah
  • Vermont
  • Virginia
  • Washington
  • Washington D.C.

Cities With SOI Protections

Even in states without statewide laws, many cities have local ordinances. Major cities with SOI protections include Austin, Denver, Kansas City, Philadelphia, Pittsburgh, Seattle, and dozens more. Always check your specific municipality.

What SOI Laws Require From Landlords

In jurisdictions with SOI protections, landlords generally must:

Accept Voucher Holders as Applicants

You cannot refuse to show a unit, provide an application, or process a prospective tenant because they indicate they will use a housing voucher. The application process must be the same as for any other applicant.

Apply the Same Screening Criteria

You may still screen voucher holders using the same criteria you apply to all applicants, including credit history, rental history, and background checks. SOI laws do not require you to lower your screening standards.

Cooperate With Housing Authority Inspections

Section 8 requires a Housing Quality Standards (HQS) inspection before a tenant moves in. In SOI jurisdictions, refusing to allow this inspection or deliberately failing it to avoid voucher tenants can be treated as discrimination.

Not Advertise Against Vouchers

Listings that say "No Section 8" or "No vouchers" violate SOI laws where they exist. Even if you would consider a voucher holder, discriminatory advertising language is a separate violation.

How Section 8 Works for Landlords

Understanding the program reduces the friction of compliance. Here is how Section 8 operates from the landlord's perspective.

Rent Calculation

The housing authority determines a payment standard for your area based on fair market rents. HUD's rental assistance programs explain how payment standards and voucher amounts are calculated. The tenant pays roughly 30% of their adjusted income toward rent, and the housing authority pays the remainder directly to you.

Payment Reliability

Housing authority payments arrive on a predictable monthly schedule. Many landlords find voucher payments more reliable than payments from market-rate tenants because the government portion is guaranteed.

Lease Requirements

You sign a standard lease with the tenant plus a Housing Assistance Payments (HAP) contract with the housing authority. The HAP contract specifies payment amounts and obligations for both parties.

Inspections

The unit must pass an initial HQS inspection and periodic reinspections. These inspections check for basic health and safety standards that your property should already meet.

Common Compliance Mistakes

Blanket No-Voucher Policies

A policy that automatically rejects all voucher holders is illegal in SOI jurisdictions. You must evaluate each applicant individually.

Rent Inflation for Voucher Holders

Charging voucher holders a higher rent than market-rate tenants for the same unit is discriminatory. Your rent must be consistent regardless of how the tenant pays.

Processing Delays

Deliberately dragging out the application process for voucher holders to discourage them from renting is a form of discrimination. Process all applications on the same timeline.

Requiring Voucher Disclosure Before Showing

You cannot require prospective tenants to disclose their voucher status before viewing a unit or receiving an application.

Managing Voucher Tenants Effectively

SOI compliance does not mean you cannot manage your properties effectively. Best practices include:

  • Set clear screening criteria that apply to every applicant equally
  • Document your screening process so you can demonstrate consistent treatment
  • Build relationships with housing authorities — they can be helpful partners in resolving issues
  • Understand your local payment standards so you price units competitively
  • Maintain your property to HQS standards as a baseline, which benefits all tenants

For tips on building effective screening processes, see our tenant screening best practices guide.

Penalties for SOI Violations

Violations of source-of-income discrimination laws can result in:

  • Administrative complaints with state or local human rights agencies
  • Civil lawsuits with compensatory and punitive damages
  • Attorney fees and costs paid by the landlord
  • Injunctive relief requiring policy changes
  • Fines ranging from $10,000 to $100,000+ depending on jurisdiction and severity

The HUD Office of Fair Housing investigates complaints and enforces fair housing laws. Testing organizations actively send paired testers to rental properties to check for SOI discrimination, similar to how fair housing testing works for other protected classes.

Key Takeaways

Source-of-income discrimination laws are expanding across the country. Even if your state does not currently have SOI protections, your city or county might. Stay informed about your local requirements, screen all applicants consistently, and treat voucher holders the same way you treat market-rate applicants. The legal and financial risks of noncompliance far outweigh any perceived inconvenience of working with housing assistance programs.
