Essential Qualifications to Look For in a Property Manager

Hiring a property manager without checking their qualifications is like hiring a contractor without checking their license. The consequences may not be immediately visible, but they tend to emerge at the worst possible times—a missed eviction deadline, an improperly handled security deposit, or a maintenance issue that spirals into a habitability claim.

The challenge is that property management has low barriers to entry in many markets. In some states, a basic real estate license is all that is legally required. That means the market includes a wide spectrum, from highly credentialed professionals with decades of experience to individuals managing a handful of properties as a side business.

This guide defines what meaningful property manager qualifications look like—legal requirements, professional credentials, technical skills, and the intangibles that separate adequate managers from excellent ones.

For a broader overview of the hiring process, read our guides on how to choose a property manager and what a property manager does.

Legal Qualifications: What Is Required by Law

The legal requirements for property managers vary significantly by state. Understanding what is required in your market is the first step in evaluating any candidate.

Real Estate License

The majority of U.S. states require property managers to hold an active real estate license—typically either a salesperson's license under a supervising broker or a broker's license in their own name. The specific requirement depends on the state and the scope of activity.

States that require a real estate license for property management include California, Texas, Florida, Georgia, North Carolina, and most others. A small number of states—including Idaho and Maine—have exemptions for certain property management activities, but these are the exception.

What to do: Verify a manager's license through your state's real estate commission website. License status, any disciplinary actions, and the supervising broker's information are all typically public record. This check takes five minutes and should be non-negotiable.

Property Management License

A handful of states, including Oregon and Montana, issue a dedicated property management license separate from a general real estate license. In these states, holding a real estate license alone is not sufficient to manage properties for hire.

Broker's License

Operating a property management company—as opposed to working as an agent within one—typically requires a broker's license. This represents a higher standard of education and experience than a salesperson's license and is an indicator of serious professional commitment.

Professional Credentials and Certifications

Beyond legal licensing, a range of voluntary professional credentials signal deeper expertise and commitment to the field. These are not required by law but are meaningful differentiators when evaluating candidates.

Residential Management Professional (RMP)

The RMP designation is issued by the National Association of Residential Property Managers (NARPM) and requires a minimum of two years of full-time property management experience, completion of educational coursework, and demonstrated adherence to NARPM's professional standards.

This is one of the most relevant credentials for landlords hiring managers for single-family or small multifamily properties. An RMP designation indicates that a manager has invested in formal education beyond the minimum required by law and has had their credentials reviewed by a professional association.

Master Property Manager (MPM)

The MPM is NARPM's highest residential designation, requiring significantly more experience (at least five years in full-time management), a larger managed portfolio, and ongoing continuing education. An MPM-credentialed manager represents the upper tier of residential property management professionals.

Certified Property Manager (CPM)

The CPM designation is issued by the Institute of Real Estate Management (IREM) and is widely recognized across both residential and commercial property management. It requires formal coursework, portfolio requirements, and a comprehensive exam.

Certified Apartment Manager (CAM)

Issued by the National Apartment Association (NAA), the CAM credential is relevant for managers who specialize in larger multifamily properties. If your portfolio includes apartment buildings, a CAM-credentialed manager brings specialized knowledge of multifamily operations.

Accredited Residential Manager (ARM)

Another IREM designation, the ARM focuses specifically on residential property management and is a foundational credential for managers building their professional profile. It is less senior than the CPM but indicates formal education and professional commitment.

Experience-Based Qualifications

Credentials matter, but experience in the specific context of your portfolio type is equally important. Here is what to look for.

Years in Full-Time Property Management

Three to five years of full-time, professional property management experience is a reasonable minimum for a manager you intend to trust with a significant asset. Part-time or side-hustle property management rarely involves the volume of situations needed to develop sound judgment.

Ask specifically about full-time experience—not total years in real estate, which can include time as a sales agent or leasing specialist with limited management responsibility.

Portfolio Size and Type

A manager who has successfully managed 100 single-family homes has different and more relevant experience than one who managed a single 200-unit apartment building—even if the unit counts are similar. Ensure the manager's historical experience aligns with what you need. For a complete picture of what professional management entails, read what a property manager does.

Local Market Knowledge

Property management is intensely local. A manager who knows your market's rental rates, typical vacancy periods, common maintenance issues, and the local landlord-tenant legal landscape will outperform an otherwise qualified manager who is new to the area.

Ask how long they have been operating in the specific market where your properties are located and how many properties they currently manage in that market.

Technical and Operational Skills

Beyond credentials and experience, certain technical competencies are essential for professional property management.

Financial Reporting and Accounting

A qualified property manager should be able to produce accurate, timely financial reports—monthly income and expense statements, owner disbursements, and year-end summaries for tax purposes. They should use professional property management software (AppFolio, Buildium, Rentec Direct, or equivalent) rather than manual spreadsheets.

Legal and Compliance Knowledge

Qualified managers understand the federal Fair Housing Act, ADA requirements, state landlord-tenant law, and local ordinances. They should be able to explain how these frameworks affect screening criteria, lease terms, eviction procedures, and security deposit handling.

Tenant Screening Competency

A consistent, documented tenant screening process that is legally compliant and effectively identifies qualified applicants is a core competency for any property manager. The ability to screen effectively reduces vacancy losses, eviction risk, and turnover costs significantly.

Vendor Management

Managing a network of licensed, insured contractors—plumbers, electricians, HVAC technicians, landscapers—requires organization, negotiation skill, and quality oversight. A qualified manager has established vendor relationships that provide faster response times and competitive pricing.

Technology Proficiency

Property management technology has advanced significantly. A qualified manager should be fluent in their chosen property management platform and able to offer you real-time access to your property's financial data, maintenance history, and lease documentation through an owner portal. For guidance on evaluating full-service offerings, read our hiring property manager checklist.

Soft Skills That Define Excellent Property Managers

Technical qualifications are table stakes. The managers who consistently deliver results combine technical competence with a specific set of interpersonal and organizational skills.

Clear, proactive communication: The best managers do not wait for owners to ask—they provide regular updates, flag issues early, and communicate clearly when problems arise. This skill is difficult to assess from credentials alone but shows up clearly in reference checks and the interview process itself.

Judgment under pressure: Property management involves constant judgment calls: how to handle a tenant who is three weeks late on rent, whether a maintenance issue constitutes a habitability emergency, how to respond to a fair housing complaint. Sound judgment, developed through experience and professional training, is what separates great managers from mediocre ones.

Accountability: A qualified manager takes responsibility when things go wrong, documents what happened, explains what they are doing to fix it, and puts systems in place to prevent recurrence. This quality is best assessed through references and the manager's response to difficult questions in the interview.

Summary

Property manager qualifications span legal requirements, professional credentials, experience, technical skills, and interpersonal qualities. No single credential tells the whole story, but a manager who combines an active license, a professional designation from NARPM or IREM, substantial relevant experience, and strong references has demonstrated a meaningful commitment to professional practice.
