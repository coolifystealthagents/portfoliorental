30:T3105,<p>Security cameras at rental properties protect against theft, vandalism, and liability disputes — but they also raise serious privacy concerns that landlords must navigate carefully. Installing a camera in the wrong location, or without proper disclosure, can violate tenant privacy rights and create significant legal liability. Understanding the rules before you install is essential.</p>
<p>This guide covers where landlords can and cannot install security cameras, disclosure requirements, tenant rights, and how to build a legally compliant camera system that protects your property without violating privacy.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="the-core-legal-principle-privacy-expectation">The Core Legal Principle: Privacy Expectation</h2>
<p>Security camera law is built on the concept of "reasonable expectation of privacy." People have a reasonable expectation of privacy:</p>
<ul>
<li>Inside their homes and apartments</li>
<li>In private areas like bedrooms and bathrooms</li>
<li>In private conversations</li>
</ul>
<p>People generally do NOT have a reasonable expectation of privacy:</p>
<ul>
<li>On public sidewalks and streets</li>
<li>In common areas of buildings (lobbies, hallways, parking lots)</li>
<li>In the exterior of their own home when viewed from a public area</li>
</ul>
<p>For landlords, this translates into clear rules about where cameras are permissible.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="where-landlords-can-install-security-cameras">Where Landlords Can Install Security Cameras</h2>
<p>The following locations are generally permissible for landlord-installed security cameras:</p>
<h3 id="exterior-of-the-building">Exterior of the Building</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Exterior entry points</strong> (front door, back door, side doors) — Permitted; these face areas without privacy expectation</li>
<li><strong>Driveways and parking areas</strong> — Permitted; these are common or semi-public areas</li>
<li><strong>Exterior walls and corners</strong> — Permitted when aimed at the exterior, parking, or street</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="common-areas-in-multi-family-properties">Common Areas in Multi-Family Properties</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Lobby and main entrance</strong> — Widely permitted; common in apartment buildings</li>
<li><strong>Mail area and package room</strong> — Permitted; package theft is a primary use case</li>
<li><strong>Laundry rooms</strong> — Generally permitted (but aim cameras at entry/equipment, not changing areas)</li>
<li><strong>Pool and recreation areas</strong> — Permitted as long as cameras do not capture private spaces</li>
<li><strong>Parking garages and lots</strong> — Permitted</li>
<li><strong>Stairwells and corridors</strong> — Permitted; primarily for security documentation</li>
<li><strong>Elevator interiors</strong> — Widely permitted in larger buildings</li>
</ul>
<hr>
<h2 id="where-landlords-cannot-install-security-cameras">Where Landlords Cannot Install Security Cameras</h2>
<p>The following locations are generally prohibited for landlord cameras — these are areas where tenants have a reasonable expectation of privacy:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Inside rental units</strong> — Placing cameras inside a tenant's unit (living room, bedroom, kitchen, bathroom) without explicit consent is a serious privacy violation and is illegal in virtually every jurisdiction</li>
<li><strong>Bathrooms or changing areas</strong> — Any camera in or aimed at a bathroom, shower, or changing area is illegal, full stop</li>
<li><strong>Bedroom windows</strong> — Cameras should not be positioned to look through the windows of a tenant's bedroom or other private areas</li>
<li><strong>Common laundry room changing areas</strong> — Where people change, cameras violate privacy</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="a-specific-rule-cameras-must-not-peer-into-private-spaces">A Specific Rule: Cameras Must Not Peer Into Private Spaces</h3>
<p>Even exterior cameras that are technically positioned on common or landlord property can create legal issues if they are positioned or angled to view into private spaces — for example, a camera aimed such that it captures the interior of a ground-floor tenant's bedroom through the window. The camera's position does not override the privacy expectation of the space being viewed.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="state-specific-laws">State-Specific Laws</h2>
<p>Laws governing surveillance cameras vary by state. Key considerations:</p>
<h3 id="california">California</h3>
<p>California's Penal Code Section 647(j)(1) prohibits any installation of a device to observe, record, or amplify a person in "private area of another person" without consent. California courts take a broad view of privacy expectations. Landlords in California should be conservative about camera placement and always disclose camera locations to tenants.</p>
<h3 id="new-york">New York</h3>
<p>New York prohibits unauthorized installation of observation devices in rooms where privacy is expected. Camera placement in common areas is generally permissible.</p>
<h3 id="illinois">Illinois</h3>
<p>Illinois has audio recording restrictions under the Illinois Eavesdropping Act. If cameras capture audio, two-party consent may be required for any audio recording. Consider disabling audio on cameras in common areas.</p>
<h3 id="washington">Washington</h3>
<p>Washington requires all parties to a private conversation to consent to recording. Cameras capturing audio in any setting may require consent.</p>
<h3 id="federal-laws">Federal Laws</h3>
<p>The federal <a href="https://www.congress.gov/bill/108th-congress/senate-bill/1301">Video Voyeurism Prevention Act</a> prohibits capturing video of intimate areas. While primarily aimed at individual voyeurism, the principle applies to any surveillance.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="audio-recording-an-additional-complication">Audio Recording: An Additional Complication</h2>
<p>Many modern security cameras capture audio as well as video. Audio recording adds a layer of legal complexity because most states have wiretapping or eavesdropping statutes that govern audio recording.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Two-party/all-party consent states</strong> — California, Illinois, Washington, Massachusetts, and others require all parties to a conversation to consent before audio is recorded. In these states, audio-recording cameras in common areas (where conversations may be captured) can violate state eavesdropping law.</li>
<li><strong>One-party states</strong> — Many states only require one party to consent. Even here, recording private conversations has limitations.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Best practice:</strong> Disable audio recording on exterior and common area cameras. You gain little operational benefit from audio at these locations, and you eliminate the audio recording legal complexity entirely.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="disclosure-requirements">Disclosure Requirements</h2>
<p>Even where cameras are legally permissible, disclosure to tenants is both a legal requirement and best practice. Review <a href="/blog/tenant-privacy-laws">tenant privacy laws</a> for guidance.</p>
<h3 id="lease-disclosure">Lease Disclosure</h3>
<p>Include a provision in your lease or a camera addendum that:</p>
<ul>
<li>Lists all camera locations on the property</li>
<li>States that cameras are for security purposes only</li>
<li>Confirms that cameras are not placed in any private areas</li>
<li>States your data retention and access policy</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="posting-notice">Posting Notice</h3>
<p>In common areas of multi-family buildings, post visible notices indicating that security cameras are in use. This is required by law in some states and is best practice regardless.</p>
<h3 id="no-hidden-cameras">No Hidden Cameras</h3>
<p>Never install hidden cameras anywhere on a rental property. Even in legally permissible areas, hidden cameras create significant legal and ethical liability. Always install cameras visibly and disclose them.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="camera-data-storage-retention-and-access">Camera Data: Storage, Retention, and Access</h2>
<h3 id="retention-period">Retention Period</h3>
<p>Most landlords retain camera footage for 7-30 days on a rolling basis. Longer retention requires more storage. Best practices:</p>
<ul>
<li>7-14 days is standard for exterior and common area cameras</li>
<li>Retain specific footage related to incidents as long as it may be needed for legal purposes</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="who-has-access">Who Has Access?</h3>
<p>Limit access to camera footage to property management personnel with a legitimate need. Understand <a href="/blog/landlord-liability-protection">landlord liability protection</a> before sharing footage.</p>
<h3 id="responding-to-subpoenas-and-law-enforcement-requests">Responding to Subpoenas and Law Enforcement Requests</h3>
<p>The <a href="https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/privacy-security">FTC's privacy guidelines</a> apply to surveillance data. If law enforcement requests camera footage, work with counsel before providing it. In general, police with a valid warrant are entitled to footage; informal requests should be handled carefully.</p>
<h3 id="tenant-requests-for-footage">Tenant Requests for Footage</h3>
<p>Tenants may request camera footage related to incidents involving them (package theft from the lobby, for example). You are generally not legally required to provide footage unless compelled by a court, but cooperating with reasonable requests demonstrates good faith.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="what-you-can-do-with-camera-systems">What You Can Do With Camera Systems</h2>
<p>Landlords with compliant camera installations can:</p>
<ul>
<li>Monitor common areas for security incidents</li>
<li>Document access to the property for evidence in disputes</li>
<li>Deter and document property vandalism, theft, and trespassing</li>
<li>Verify vendor and contractor access (particularly useful with <a href="/blog/keyless-entry-rental-property">keyless entry</a> integration)</li>
<li>Provide footage to law enforcement following documented incidents</li>
</ul>
<hr>
<h2 id="ring-and-doorbell-cameras">Ring and Doorbell Cameras</h2>
<p>Video doorbell cameras (Ring, Nest Hello, Eufy Video Doorbell) are popular and increasingly common at rental property entry points. Rules:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Landlord-installed doorbells</strong> — You own and control the device; rules above apply</li>
<li><strong>Tenant-installed doorbells</strong> — Tenants may install their own doorbell cameras but the same legal rules apply (cannot record audio without consent in two-party states, etc.)</li>
<li><strong>Field of view</strong> — Doorbell cameras with a wide field of view may capture footage of neighboring properties, public sidewalks, or adjacent tenant areas; be aware of what they capture</li>
</ul>
<p>Some landlords include a provision in the lease addressing tenant-installed cameras (requiring disclosure, prohibiting placement aimed at other units, etc.).</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="smart-camera-systems-for-rental-properties">Smart Camera Systems for Rental Properties</h2>
<h3 id="self-managed-systems">Self-Managed Systems</h3>
<p>For small portfolios, self-managed systems work well:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Arlo Pro 4</strong> — Wireless, weather-resistant, clear 2K video, good app</li>
<li><strong>Eufy Security 2K</strong> — Local storage, no monthly fee</li>
<li><strong>Ring Stick Up Cam</strong> — Widely compatible with Ring ecosystem, subscription required for cloud storage</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="professionally-monitored-systems">Professionally Monitored Systems</h3>
<p>For larger properties or landlords who want professional monitoring:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>SimpliSafe</strong> and <strong>ADT</strong> offer camera-integrated security systems</li>
<li>Commercial systems (Avigilon, Hikvision) are appropriate for larger multi-family buildings</li>
</ul>
<hr>
<h2 id="conclusion">Conclusion</h2>
<p>Security cameras are a valuable protection tool for rental properties when installed and used in compliance with privacy law. The key rules are simple: cameras in private spaces (inside units, bathrooms) are prohibited; cameras in exterior and common areas with proper disclosure are generally permissible; audio recording requires extra care in consent-required states. Always disclose camera locations in your lease and visibly post notice in common areas.</p>
