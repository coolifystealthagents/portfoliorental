How to Check Property Manager References Effectively

Most landlords treat reference checks as a formality—a box to tick after they have already made up their mind. That is a mistake. A well-conducted property manager reference check is one of the most revealing steps in the entire hiring process. Former and current clients will tell you things that no interview, website, or sales pitch ever will.

This guide shows you exactly how to conduct a property manager references check: who to call, what questions to ask, how to interpret what you hear, and what it means when a manager struggles to produce references at all. Used alongside a structured interview and contract review, a thorough reference check dramatically reduces your risk of a bad hire.

For the full hiring framework, start with our guide on how to choose a property manager and the complete hiring property manager checklist.

Why Reference Checks Matter More Than You Think

A property manager interview tells you what someone says they do. A reference check tells you what they actually do. The gap between those two things is where bad hires happen.

Consider what references can reveal that an interview cannot:

  • Whether the manager communicates proactively or only when pushed
  • How they handle difficult tenants, maintenance crises, or disputes
  • Whether financial reporting is accurate and delivered on time
  • How responsive they are to owner concerns after the contract is signed
  • Whether their actual performance matches their sales pitch

According to the Society for Human Resource Management, reference checks remain one of the highest-validity predictors of future job performance. The same principle applies to vendor due diligence.

Who to Ask For as a Reference

Not all references are equally useful. Be specific about what you need.

Ask for:

  • Current clients who have been with the manager for at least 12 months
  • Clients who own property types similar to yours (single-family, small multifamily, etc.)
  • Clients who own properties in the same geographic market as yours
  • Clients who manage a similar number of units to your portfolio

Avoid relying on:

  • Short-term clients who have only worked with the manager for a few months
  • Clients who are also personal friends of the manager
  • Testimonials on the manager's website, which are curated and unverifiable

Ask for three to five references and make clear you intend to contact all of them. A manager who only offers one reference or who seems reluctant to provide contacts is worth scrutinizing further.

15 Questions to Ask During a Reference Call

Prepare these questions in advance and take notes on every call. Do not just ask whether the reference is "satisfied"—ask for specifics.

About Communication

  1. How quickly does the manager or their team typically respond to your calls and emails?
  2. Do they contact you proactively when something comes up, or do you usually have to follow up to get information?
  3. How would you describe the quality of their monthly owner reports? Are they clear, accurate, and delivered on time?

About Tenant Management

  1. How does the manager handle tenant screening? Have you had any problem tenants placed in your properties?
  2. What has been your experience with lease renewals? Do they handle them proactively or let leases lapse?
  3. Have you ever had to go through an eviction with them? If so, how was it handled?

About Maintenance

  1. How are maintenance requests handled? Is there a clear system, or does it feel disorganized?
  2. Have you ever been surprised by a maintenance charge? Were invoices and vendor bills transparent?
  3. Are repairs completed in a timely way, or do issues drag on?

About Financial Performance

  1. Has your vacancy rate improved or worsened since hiring this manager?
  2. Are rent collections handled consistently and professionally?
  3. Have there been any billing errors, missing disbursements, or unexplained charges?

About the Overall Relationship

  1. Has there been anything the manager said they would do that they have not followed through on?
  2. Knowing what you know now, would you hire this manager again?
  3. Is there anything you wish you had known before hiring them?

How to Interpret What You Hear

Reference checks require active listening and some interpretation. Here is how to read between the lines.

Enthusiastic endorsements with specifics are the best signal. When a reference says "Their response time is excellent—I tested it last month during a maintenance emergency and they had a contractor on-site within four hours," that is meaningful. Specific, recent examples indicate genuine experience and recall.

Vague, generic praise should prompt follow-up. "They're great, very professional" tells you nothing. Probe with: "Can you give me a specific example of a time they handled something particularly well?" If the reference cannot come up with one, the relationship may be more superficial than it appears.

Hesitation before answering is worth noting. If a reference pauses for a long time before describing communication quality or says "well, they're getting better," that is a soft no. Most people avoid directly criticizing a referral, so hesitation or qualified praise often signals real dissatisfaction.

Negative answers delivered matter-of-factly should be taken seriously. If a reference says "we had two maintenance invoices last year that were for work we couldn't verify was done," that is a specific allegation you should investigate.

What to Do If a Manager Cannot Provide References

If a property manager cannot produce two or three references within 24 to 48 hours, ask yourself why. Possible explanations include:

  • They are newer to the business and lack an established client base
  • Their client relationships are poor and few would speak positively
  • They have not maintained a professional network

A newer company may be able to compensate for limited references with strong credentials, detailed process documentation, and transparent financials. But no references at all is a meaningful data point that should inform your decision.

Checking Independent Sources Beyond Provided References

Provided references are curated. To get a more balanced view, supplement them with independent research.

Google and Yelp reviews: Search the company name and read reviews from both owners and tenants. Owner reviews and tenant reviews often reveal different failure modes. A manager with happy owners but furious tenants may be cutting corners on habitability that will eventually create legal exposure for you.

Better Business Bureau: Check for formal complaints and how the company responded.

State licensing board: Verify the manager's license is current and check for any disciplinary actions. In most states, this information is publicly available through the state real estate commission website.

Local landlord associations: Many metropolitan areas have local landlord associations or real estate investor networks where you can ask whether anyone has experience with a particular management company.

LinkedIn: Review the professional history of the principal broker and key staff. Look for tenure and industry credentials. For context on what credentials matter most, see our property management services complete guide.

Practical Tips for the Reference Call Itself

  • Call, do not email. Phone calls yield more candid, nuanced responses than written communications where people are more guarded.
  • Introduce yourself and explain the purpose. Be upfront: "I'm considering hiring [Company Name] to manage my rental properties and I'd appreciate 10 to 15 minutes of your time."
  • Use open-ended questions. "Tell me about your experience with their maintenance process" yields more than "Is their maintenance process good?"
  • Leave space for silence. After a hesitant or partial answer, pause rather than moving on immediately. People often add important qualifications when given a moment.
  • Ask the final question every time. "Would you hire them again?" is the most direct signal of overall satisfaction.

Building a Reference Check Scorecard

After completing all of your reference calls, score each manager across five dimensions on a simple 1 to 5 scale:

Dimension Score (1–5)
Communication responsiveness
Tenant screening quality
Maintenance handling
Financial reporting accuracy
Overall satisfaction / rehire intent

Total the scores and compare across the managers you are evaluating. Combined with your interview notes and contract review, this gives you a structured basis for the hiring decision.

Summary

A property manager reference check is not a formality—it is one of the highest-value steps in the entire hiring process. By asking the right questions, interpreting answers carefully, and supplementing provided references with independent research, you can uncover the real performance history of any manager you are considering.

Be specific in what you ask for, take notes on every call, and pay close attention to hesitation and qualified praise. Combined with a thorough interview process and a careful contract review, a rigorous reference check is your best protection against a costly property management mistake.
