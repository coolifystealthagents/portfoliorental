Appliances are among the most tenant-visible elements of a rental unit and among the most frequent sources of maintenance requests. A refrigerator that stops cooling, a dryer that takes two cycles to dry a load, or a dishwasher that leaks onto the floor — these are the maintenance calls that disrupt tenants and drain your maintenance budget. Yet most appliance failures are preventable with a systematic maintenance schedule.

This guide covers maintenance tasks for every major appliance found in rental properties, organized by frequency, along with guidance on repair versus replacement decisions and how to extend appliance lifespan in a rental environment. Pair it with a preventive maintenance schedule to cover your full property.

Why Appliance Maintenance Matters for Landlords

The financial argument for appliance maintenance is straightforward:

  • Extended lifespan — Properly maintained appliances last significantly longer. A refrigerator with clean condenser coils and proper door seals can easily reach 15+ years; one that is neglected may fail at 8-10 years.
  • Lower repair costs — Maintenance prevents small issues from becoming major failures. A dryer duct cleaned annually costs $100-$150; a dryer fire or duct replacement costs $500-$2,000. The Energy.gov appliance guide offers tips on improving energy efficiency.
  • Tenant satisfaction — Tenants who have well-functioning appliances require fewer urgent maintenance calls and are more likely to renew their leases.
  • Legal compliance — In many states, appliances specified in the lease (refrigerator, stove, etc.) must be maintained in working order as part of the landlord's habitability obligation. The IRS rental property guide covers deducting appliance costs.

Appliance Lifespan Reference

Use these averages to plan capital reserves and replacement timing:

Appliance Average Lifespan
Refrigerator 12-15 years
Electric range/oven 13-15 years
Gas range/oven 15-17 years
Dishwasher 9-12 years
Microwave (built-in/OTR) 9-10 years
Washer (top-load) 10-12 years
Washer (front-load) 11-14 years
Dryer 13-14 years
Garbage disposal 10-12 years
Water heater (tank) 8-12 years
Water heater (tankless) 18-25 years

These are averages for well-maintained appliances. Heavy use by rental tenants, combined with neglect, can shorten these lifespans significantly.

Refrigerator Maintenance

Every 6 Months

  • Clean condenser coils — Dust and pet hair on condenser coils (located at the bottom front or back of the refrigerator) reduce efficiency and cause overheating. Clean with a coil brush or vacuum. This is the single most important refrigerator maintenance task.
  • Check door gaskets — Inspect the rubber door seals for cracks, tears, or gaps. A loose seal allows cold air to escape and the compressor to overwork. Test by closing the door on a piece of paper — it should grip firmly.
  • Clean door gaskets — Wipe gaskets with warm soapy water to remove food residue that causes them to deteriorate

Annually

  • Check drip pan and drain tube — The defrost drip pan collects condensate. Clean it and confirm the defrost drain tube is clear to prevent overflow and odor.
  • Verify temperature settings — Refrigerator should be set to 35-38°F; freezer to 0-5°F. Improper settings cause food spoilage and inefficiency.
  • Inspect ice maker and water lines — If present, check water supply lines for kinking, calcium buildup, and connection integrity.

Stove and Oven Maintenance

After Each Tenant Turnover

  • Deep clean oven interior, burners, grates, and drip pans
  • Test all burners and oven heating elements for proper operation
  • Test oven thermostat accuracy with an oven thermometer
  • Inspect oven door gasket (ceramic fiber seal around door opening)

Annually

  • Gas stoves — Check burner ports for clogging; clean with a fine wire or toothpick. Clogged ports cause uneven, yellow flames (should be blue).
  • Electric stoves — Check heating coils for hot spots or burning; replace damaged coils
  • Range hood and filter — Clean or replace range hood grease filter; check fan operation and vent duct for grease buildup

Safety Check

  • Test the smoke detector near the kitchen annually
  • Ensure the gas shut-off valve behind a gas stove is accessible and operational

Dishwasher Maintenance

Monthly

  • Clean the filter — Most modern dishwashers have a removable filter at the bottom of the tub. Remove and rinse monthly to prevent odor and drainage issues.

Every 6 Months

  • Inspect door gasket — Check the rubber door seal for cracks or food debris causing improper sealing
  • Run a cleaning cycle — Use a commercial dishwasher cleaner (like Affresh) or place a cup of white vinegar in the upper rack and run a hot cycle to remove mineral deposits and odor
  • Check spray arms — Confirm spray arm holes are not clogged; clear any blockages with a toothpick

Annually

  • Inspect water supply line and drain hose — Check for kinking, cracks, and secure connections
  • Check float switch — The float switch prevents overfilling; ensure it moves freely
  • Check door latch — Verify the door latch mechanism engages securely

Washer and Dryer Maintenance

Dryer (Critical: Fire Safety)

The dryer duct is a significant fire hazard. Lint accumulation in dryer ducts is responsible for approximately 15,000 dryer fires annually.

Every 6 Months:

  • Professional duct cleaning — Have a professional clean the dryer vent duct from the dryer to the exterior termination. This is non-negotiable for tenant-occupied units.
  • Inspect the duct at the dryer connection — Check for kinking or compression of flexible duct; replace with rigid metal duct where possible
  • Verify exterior termination — Confirm the exterior vent flap opens and closes freely; clear bird nests or debris

Annually:

  • Clean the dryer interior — Remove the lint trap housing and vacuum interior lint from the area around the drum
  • Test heating element — Verify the dryer heats properly; a dryer that takes multiple cycles indicates a failing heating element or blocked duct

For Stacked or Combination Units: These are especially prone to duct issues. Inspect and clean duct connections semi-annually.

Washer Maintenance

Monthly:

  • Leave door open (front-load washers) — After each use, leave the door slightly ajar to allow drying and prevent mold growth in the drum seal

Every 6 Months:

  • Run a cleaning cycle — Use a washer cleaner tablet (Affresh, for example) in an empty hot cycle to remove soap scum and mildew
  • Inspect door seal (front-load) — Check the rubber gasket for mold, debris buildup, and tears; clean with a bleach solution
  • Check water supply hoses — Inspect braided stainless steel supply hoses for kinking, bulging, or corrosion. Replace standard rubber hoses with braided stainless steel. Replace hoses every 5-7 years as preventive maintenance — a burst washing machine hose is one of the most common sources of catastrophic water damage in rental units.

Annually:

  • Inspect drain hose — Confirm the drain hose is properly secured in the standpipe and not kinked
  • Test water level settings — Verify proper water fill for different load sizes

Garbage Disposal Maintenance

Monthly

  • Run cold water with ice cubes — Running the disposal with ice sharpens the grinding components and clears light buildup
  • Freshen with citrus — Grinding lemon or orange peels reduces odor

Annually

  • Test reset button — Locate the reset button (usually on the bottom of the unit) and confirm it is accessible
  • Inspect under-sink connections — Check the disposal mounting, drain connections, and power cord for secure installation

Tenant Instructions

Educate tenants on what should NOT go in the disposal:

  • Grease and cooking oils
  • Fibrous vegetables (celery, artichokes)
  • Eggshells (in large quantities)
  • Coffee grounds
  • Pasta, rice, and bread (swell and clog drains)
  • Bones and hard items

Microwave Maintenance

After Each Tenant Turnover

  • Clean interior thoroughly, including the turntable and support ring
  • Inspect door seal and hinges
  • Test all functions including the light and fan

Annually

  • Clean range hood filter if it is an over-the-range unit
  • Inspect the power cord and mounting brackets

Building an Appliance Maintenance Program

For landlords with multiple units, an organized approach is essential:

Appliance Inventory

Maintain a spreadsheet or property management software record for each unit listing:

  • Each appliance's make, model, and serial number
  • Purchase or installation date
  • Warranty status
  • Service history

Maintenance Trigger Points

Schedule appliance inspections at:

  • Each unit turnover (thorough inspection of all appliances)
  • Semi-annual maintenance visits (even for occupied units)

Budget Allocation

Budget approximately $200-$400 per unit per year for appliance maintenance and minor repairs, plus a capital reserve of $200-$300 per unit per year for eventual replacement. See our guide on maintenance cost budgeting for a full framework.

Repair vs Replace Decisions

Use these rules of thumb:

  • Age threshold — If the appliance is within 3-4 years of its expected lifespan, replacement is often more cost-effective than a major repair
  • Repair cost rule — If the repair cost exceeds 50% of the replacement cost, replace
  • Reliability history — A second major repair in two years is a strong signal to replace
  • Energy efficiency — Older appliances are significantly less efficient; a new refrigerator uses 40-60% less electricity than one from 15 years ago

Conclusion

Appliance maintenance is a high-value, low-cost investment in your rental property portfolio. A systematic schedule — combining tenant turnover inspections, semi-annual service tasks, and annual checks — prevents the expensive emergency maintenance calls and premature replacements that erode rental income. The most important individual tasks are dryer duct cleaning (fire safety), refrigerator coil cleaning (efficiency and lifespan), and washer supply hose inspection (flood prevention).
