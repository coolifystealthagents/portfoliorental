2a:T31c6,<p>Mobile home parks — now more commonly called manufactured housing communities — have developed a devoted following among real estate investors over the past decade. Warren Buffett has called manufactured housing "compelling" from an affordability perspective. Sam Zell built a real estate empire that included significant manufactured housing exposure. Frank Rolfe and Dave Reynolds popularized MHP investing through their Mobile Home University platform.</p>
<p>The appeal is straightforward: consistent cash flow, low tenant turnover (tenants own their homes and rent only the land), and a supply-constrained asset class with virtually no new competition being built. But mobile home park investing is also a specialized niche with unique due diligence requirements, management challenges, and community dynamics that beginners need to understand before deploying capital.</p>
<h2 id="how-mobile-home-parks-work">How Mobile Home Parks Work</h2>
<p>The most important distinction in MHP investing is the land-lease model. In most mobile home parks:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Residents own their manufactured homes</strong> — they've purchased the physical structure</li>
<li><strong>The park owner (investor) owns the land</strong> — residents pay lot rent for the space their home occupies</li>
</ul>
<p>This inverts the typical landlord relationship. The park owner isn't responsible for maintaining the homes themselves (residents handle that), only for the common infrastructure: roads, utilities infrastructure, common area landscaping, and any shared amenities.</p>
<p>Lot rents — the monthly payment residents make to occupy a space — are typically $250-600/month depending on location, though rates in high-cost markets can be significantly higher. Because residents own their homes, moving is extremely expensive and disruptive — they'd have to either pay to relocate the home (often $5,000-15,000, if the home can even be moved) or abandon it. This creates extraordinary tenant stickiness.</p>
<h3 id="park-owned-homes">Park-Owned Homes</h3>
<p>Some mobile home parks include a mix of tenant-owned and park-owned homes (POH). When the park owns the home and rents it out as a complete unit, the economics are more like traditional rental property — higher potential income per space but also higher management and maintenance responsibility.</p>
<p>Many sophisticated MHP investors specifically target parks with high tenant-owned percentages and work to transition park-owned homes to tenant ownership over time — reducing management burden while increasing the stickiness of the tenant base.</p>
<h2 id="why-investors-are-attracted-to-mobile-home-parks">Why Investors Are Attracted to Mobile Home Parks</h2>
<h3 id="high-cash-flow-yields">High Cash Flow Yields</h3>
<p>Mobile home parks often produce higher cap rates than most other commercial real estate: 6-10% is common, with some deals in tertiary markets or underperforming parks reaching 10-12%+. Understanding how to interpret these figures is essential — our <a href="/blog/cap-rate-real-estate">cap rate guide</a> explains how to use this metric accurately. These returns compare favorably to multifamily (typically 5-8%) and far exceed retail or office in today's market.</p>
<p>The combination of lot rents with minimal maintenance responsibility (for tenant-owned homes) and low labor costs creates attractive net operating income relative to acquisition price.</p>
<h3 id="low-turnover">Low Turnover</h3>
<p>A resident who owns a manufactured home on your lot and can't easily move it is going to stay for a long time. Annual resident turnover in well-managed mobile home parks is often 5-15% — compared to 30-50% in apartment complexes. Low turnover means fewer vacant lots, lower leasing costs, and more predictable income.</p>
<h3 id="affordable-housing-demand">Affordable Housing Demand</h3>
<p>Manufactured housing is the largest source of unsubsidized affordable housing in the United States. As housing costs have risen across the country, demand for affordable alternatives has grown. The residents in mobile home parks are often working-class families who simply cannot afford market-rate apartments or homes — and there's nothing else at a comparable price point.</p>
<p>This socioeconomic reality creates persistent demand, but it also creates ethical considerations around rent increases and community management that serious investors should grapple with thoughtfully.</p>
<h3 id="supply-constraints">Supply Constraints</h3>
<p>New mobile home parks are almost never approved by local governments today. Zoning regulations in most jurisdictions effectively prohibit new MHP development — too much NIMBYism around manufactured housing. This means the existing supply of parks is essentially fixed while demand for affordable housing continues to grow. Supply-constrained markets with growing demand are generally excellent for investors.</p>
<h3 id="value-add-potential">Value-Add Potential</h3>
<p>Many mobile home parks were developed decades ago and are poorly managed. Common value-add opportunities include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Raising below-market lot rents to market levels</li>
<li>Filling vacant lots</li>
<li>Converting park-owned homes to tenant ownership</li>
<li>Improving common area infrastructure</li>
<li>Implementing professional management and billing systems</li>
</ul>
<p>The <a href="/blog/value-add-real-estate-strategy">value-add strategy</a> applied to mobile home parks can produce substantial returns because many parks are significantly below market on rents and operation.</p>
<h2 id="key-metrics-for-evaluating-mobile-home-parks">Key Metrics for Evaluating Mobile Home Parks</h2>
<h3 id="lot-count-and-occupancy">Lot Count and Occupancy</h3>
<p>Total lots and occupied lots determine your income base. Look for parks where current occupancy leaves room for improvement. A 100-lot park that's 65% occupied has significant income upside if vacancies can be filled — but also requires investigation of why 35 lots are empty.</p>
<h3 id="lot-rent-vs-market-lot-rent">Lot Rent vs. Market Lot Rent</h3>
<p>Like all income-producing real estate, the spread between current rents and market rents determines value-add potential. Research comparable parks in the area for current lot rent levels.</p>
<h3 id="infrastructure-public-vs-private-utilities">Infrastructure: Public vs. Private Utilities</h3>
<p>This is one of the most critical due diligence areas for mobile home parks:</p>
<p><strong>Public utilities</strong> (municipal water and sewer): Ideal. Less management burden, clear liability, no major system replacement risk on the investor.</p>
<p><strong>Private utilities</strong> (well, septic, or park-owned systems): Significant additional risk. Well and septic systems require maintenance, can fail catastrophically, and can make the park difficult to finance. Private utility parks require experienced operators and should be underwritten conservatively.</p>
<h3 id="road-condition">Road Condition</h3>
<p>The roads within the park are the landlord's responsibility. Assess current condition and estimate replacement timeline and cost. Paved roads in good condition require minimal near-term capital; deteriorated roads or unpaved surfaces represent significant pending capital expenditure.</p>
<h3 id="home-conditions-and-poh-percentage">Home Conditions and POH Percentage</h3>
<p>Walk through the park and assess the overall condition of homes. Parks with many dilapidated homes create community appearance problems, potential liability issues, and indicate a management culture of low standards. Note the percentage of park-owned vs. tenant-owned homes.</p>
<h3 id="local-market-and-regulations">Local Market and Regulations</h3>
<ul>
<li>Is there demand for affordable housing in this market?</li>
<li>Are there competing parks with lower lot rents that could poach residents?</li>
<li>Does local zoning protect the park from redevelopment pressure?</li>
<li>Are there any pending legal or regulatory issues affecting the park?</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="due-diligence-for-mobile-home-parks">Due Diligence for Mobile Home Parks</h2>
<p>MHP due diligence requires specialized expertise beyond standard real estate inspection:</p>
<p><strong>Infrastructure inspection</strong>: Hire a professional who specifically evaluates utility systems, roads, electrical systems, and storm drainage — ideally someone with MHP-specific experience.</p>
<p><strong>Rent roll verification</strong>: Verify every lot's occupancy status, rent amount, and payment history. Sellers sometimes count vacant lots as occupied based on residents who stopped paying months ago.</p>
<p><strong>Utility audit</strong>: Obtain 24 months of utility bills and compare water/sewer usage to what the park is billing residents. Significant discrepancy may indicate leaks, billing gaps, or sub-metering issues.</p>
<p><strong>Title and zoning</strong>: Confirm zoning permits continued use as a mobile home park (some older parks have nonconforming status that creates uncertainty). Review title for any easements, access issues, or liens.</p>
<p><strong>Environmental</strong>: Investigate for any environmental contamination, particularly if the park has older homes with potentially hazardous materials or if industrial properties are nearby.</p>
<h2 id="financing-mobile-home-parks">Financing Mobile Home Parks</h2>
<p>MHP financing is more complex than residential:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Commercial bank loans</strong>: Available for parks with strong financials and public utilities. Typically 20-30% down, 20-25 year amortization.</li>
<li><strong>Agency financing</strong>: Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac both have manufactured housing community loan programs for qualifying parks (minimum 50 lots, no more than 20% POH).</li>
<li><strong>SBA loans</strong>: For owner-operators, SBA programs can provide favorable terms.</li>
<li><strong>Seller financing</strong>: Common in smaller park transactions where sellers carry back part of the purchase price. This can bridge gaps in conventional financing.</li>
</ul>
<p>Parks with private utilities, high vacancy, or poor records often struggle with conventional financing and may require <a href="/blog/creative-financing-rental-property">creative financing options</a> or bridge financing.</p>
<h2 id="getting-started-in-mobile-home-park-investing">Getting Started in Mobile Home Park Investing</h2>
<h3 id="education-first">Education First</h3>
<p>MHP investing has a steeper learning curve than residential real estate. Invest in education before investing capital:</p>
<ul>
<li><a href="https://www.mhuniversity.com" rel="noopener" target="_blank">Mobile Home University</a> (Frank Rolfe and Dave Reynolds) offers structured training programs specifically for MHP investing</li>
<li>Attend MHP-specific conferences and networking events</li>
<li>Join online communities of MHP investors to build knowledge and connections</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="start-small-or-partner">Start Small or Partner</h3>
<p>Parks with 20-50 lots represent an entry point that's more accessible financially and operationally than 200-lot communities. Some investors start as limited partners in MHP syndications before pursuing direct ownership.</p>
<h3 id="find-quality-deal-flow">Find Quality Deal Flow</h3>
<p>MHP deals often trade off-market. Network with brokers who specialize in manufactured housing, look for long-time owners approaching retirement (prime sellers), and consider direct mail to park owners in target markets.</p>
<h2 id="mobile-home-park-investing-and-your-broader-strategy">Mobile Home Park Investing and Your Broader Strategy</h2>
<p>Mobile home parks fit into a broader portfolio as a high-yield commercial real estate asset that provides income diversification from traditional multifamily. Many sophisticated investors hold both conventional multifamily and one or more manufactured housing communities for the complementary risk-return profiles.</p>
<p>For context on how MHPs compare to other commercial and residential rental options, our guide on <a href="/blog/commercial-vs-residential-rental">commercial vs residential rental investing</a> provides a useful framework. And for investors evaluating whether this fits their overall plan, our <a href="/blog/rental-property-business-plan">rental property business plan guide</a> provides the framework for assessing any new asset class against your goals and resources.</p>
<p>Mobile home park investing rewards expertise and patience. Investors who take the time to understand the niche deeply, conduct rigorous due diligence, and manage communities with genuine professionalism consistently find the returns compelling.</p>
