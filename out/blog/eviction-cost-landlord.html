How Much Does Eviction Cost a Landlord?

The number landlords most often cite when they ask about eviction cost is the court filing fee — which in most states runs $100 to $400. That number is almost meaningless. The real cost of eviction, when you account for lost rent, attorney fees, property turnover, and the time you will spend managing the process, can easily reach $5,000 to $15,000 per incident, and in costly urban markets with slow court systems, the figure can climb significantly higher.

Understanding the full scope of eviction cost matters for two reasons: it informs how aggressively you should pursue alternatives like cash for keys, and it reinforces why tenant screening is the highest-ROI investment a landlord can make.

Breaking Down the Full Cost of Eviction

1. Court Filing Fees

Every eviction begins with filing an unlawful detainer (eviction) complaint in your local court. Filing fees vary by state and county:

  • Low end: $50 to $100 (some small claims or justice court filings in states like Texas and Arizona)
  • Typical range: $100 to $400 for most states
  • High end: $400 to $800 in California, New York, and some urban courts where additional fees apply

Additional court costs — process server fees, sheriff fees for executing a writ of possession, and any fees for postponed hearings — can add $100 to $500 on top of the filing fee.

2. Attorney Fees

This is typically the largest discretionary cost in an eviction. Some landlords handle simple, uncontested evictions themselves; others retain an attorney for every case. The choice depends on complexity, your comfort with legal procedures, and local court practices.

  • DIY eviction (no attorney): You pay only court costs. Suitable for straightforward, uncontested cases with clearly documented violations.
  • Basic attorney-handled eviction: $500 to $1,500 in most markets for a standard non-payment case that goes uncontested
  • Contested eviction with hearing: $1,500 to $5,000+ when the tenant files an answer, raises habitability defenses, requests continuances, or appeals
  • Complex or lengthy evictions: In states like New York, California, or New Jersey where tenant protections are strong and procedures are detailed, attorney fees for a contested eviction can exceed $5,000 to $10,000

The eviction process step by step covers the procedural sequence that drives these costs.

3. Lost Rent During the Eviction Process

This is often the single largest cost component, yet landlords frequently underestimate it because they think of it as rent they are owed rather than money they are actively losing.

The eviction timeline from first missed payment to physical removal of the tenant varies dramatically by state:

  • Fast states (30 to 45 days): Texas, Georgia, Indiana — minimal lost rent
  • Average states (60 to 90 days): Most of the Midwest and Southeast — two to three months of lost rent
  • Slow states (4 to 12+ months): New York, New Jersey, California, Illinois — especially in post-pandemic backlogs and cases with strong tenant defenses

If your monthly rent is $1,500 and the eviction takes three months from the last payment to physical possession, that is $4,500 in lost rent — and you will likely not recover it.

Add to this the reality that tenants being evicted frequently stop paying rent as soon as the process begins, meaning lost rent starts accruing from the first missed payment, not from the filing date.

4. Property Damage and Repair Costs

The data on this is not reassuring: a meaningful percentage of evictions involve property damage that goes beyond the security deposit. Common damage in eviction situations includes:

  • Holes in walls
  • Destroyed flooring or carpet
  • Broken doors, windows, or fixtures
  • Appliance damage
  • Unauthorized alterations or additions
  • Trash and debris removal (junk removal alone can cost $200 to $800)
  • Deep cleaning after a neglected unit (biohazard cleaning if the situation involves health hazards: $500 to $3,000+)

After applying the security deposit, out-of-pocket repair costs of $500 to $3,000 are common. In severe cases — especially situations involving hoarding, extensive damage, or long-term neglect — repairs can exceed $10,000.

5. Turnover Costs After Eviction

Even assuming the property is in reasonable condition, getting it ready for the next tenant costs money:

  • Professional cleaning: $150 to $400
  • Paint (partial or full): $300 to $1,500
  • Carpet cleaning or replacement: $200 to $2,000
  • Minor repairs and touch-ups: $100 to $500
  • Landscaping or exterior cleanup: $100 to $500
  • Lock rekeying: $50 to $150

Typical post-eviction turnover costs, assuming no major damage, run $700 to $3,000.

6. Vacancy Between Tenants

Even after a fast, efficient eviction, you will likely have at least two to four weeks of vacancy while the unit is cleaned, repaired, and re-marketed. In a slower market or if significant repairs are needed, vacancy can stretch to one to three months.

At $1,500/month, one month of vacancy is $1,500. Three months is $4,500. This cost is often overlooked entirely because landlords focus on the eviction process rather than the full vacancy timeline.

7. Re-Leasing Costs

Finding a new tenant after eviction involves:

  • Listing fees or property management commissions (typically one month's rent for a management company to place a tenant)
  • Background and credit check fees ($25 to $75 per applicant)
  • Time spent showing the unit and reviewing applications

Budget $200 to $1,500 for re-leasing costs depending on whether you self-manage or use a professional.

Total Eviction Cost Estimates by Situation

The table below provides realistic total cost ranges based on eviction complexity and market:

Scenario Low Estimate High Estimate
Fast state, uncontested, minimal damage $2,000 $4,000
Average state, uncontested, moderate damage $4,000 $8,000
Slow state (CA, NY, NJ), uncontested $6,000 $12,000
Any state, contested with hearing $5,000 $15,000
Complex case (health hazards, major damage) $10,000 $25,000+

These figures assume the landlord recovers nothing from the security deposit and nothing from any judgment against the tenant. In practice, some cost recovery is possible but typically partial.

Cost of Eviction by State: Key Examples

California: The backlog in California courts has improved post-pandemic but remains significant. Combined with strict procedural requirements, attorney-dependent filings, and mandatory waiting periods, total eviction costs in California range from $6,000 to $20,000 or more. Many California landlords find that cash for keys at $3,000 to $8,000 is substantially cheaper.

New York: New York has some of the strongest tenant protections and slowest court timelines in the country. Evictions in New York City commonly take 6 to 12 months. Total eviction costs of $10,000 to $30,000+ are not unusual, which explains the prevalence of buyout agreements.

Texas: Texas has one of the most landlord-friendly and efficient eviction processes in the country. A straightforward non-payment eviction can proceed from filing to possession in 3 to 4 weeks. Total costs often range from $1,500 to $4,000.

Florida: Florida courts are moderately efficient. Total eviction costs typically range from $3,000 to $8,000, with contested cases going higher.

The HUD website and state court websites can provide current filing fee schedules for your jurisdiction.

Ways to Minimize Eviction Costs

Invest in Better Tenant Screening

The most cost-effective eviction prevention strategy is rigorous upfront screening. Every dollar spent on thorough tenant screening — credit checks, criminal background, eviction history, income verification, reference calls — is insurance against a future eviction.

A single avoided eviction more than pays for years of screening costs. Set clear, written criteria and apply them consistently to every applicant.

Set and Enforce a Clear Late Rent Policy

Consistent, early action on late rent prevents small delinquencies from becoming large ones. A tenant who is one month behind is far more likely to self-correct or accept a cash for keys offer than a tenant who is four months behind and dug in. Your late rent payment policy should specify late fees, grace periods, and the point at which formal notices begin.

Use Cash for Keys as a First Option

If a tenant is not paying and shows no sign of recovery, a well-structured cash for keys offer can resolve the situation in two to three weeks for a fraction of eviction cost. Landlords who reflexively pursue formal eviction without first attempting cash for keys often spend far more than necessary.

File Promptly When Necessary

Once you have decided formal eviction is needed, move quickly. Every week of delay is additional lost rent. Serve the required notice immediately, file as soon as the notice period expires, and do not miss court dates.

Use an Experienced Eviction Attorney

In contested cases, trying to represent yourself against a tenant who has legal aid counsel is a recipe for delays and potential dismissal. An experienced landlord-tenant attorney more than pays for themselves in a complex case by keeping the process on track and avoiding procedural errors that add months to the timeline.

Maintain a Cash Reserve for Eviction Costs

Every landlord should have a cash reserve specifically for handling vacancy, eviction, and turnover costs. A reserve of three to six months' rent per unit is a reasonable target. Without this reserve, a single eviction can create real financial stress.

The Hidden Cost: Your Time

Beyond out-of-pocket expenses, eviction consumes substantial time. Managing notices, attending court hearings, coordinating with attorneys, supervising repairs and turnover — the time investment often runs 20 to 50 hours for a simple case and much more for a contested one. For landlords who bill their time at any meaningful rate, this is a real cost even if it does not appear in your expense ledger.

Summary

The full cost of eviction — court fees, attorney fees, lost rent, property damage, turnover costs, and vacancy — typically ranges from $3,000 on the low end to $15,000 or more in challenging markets or contested cases. Understanding this reality should directly inform how you approach tenant screening, lease enforcement, and the decision whether to pursue alternatives.

Key cost reduction strategies:

  • Invest in rigorous tenant screening to avoid evictions before they start
  • Implement and consistently enforce a clear late payment policy
  • Evaluate cash for keys before defaulting to formal eviction
  • File promptly once formal eviction is necessary — delays are expensive
  • Keep a cash reserve of three to six months' rent per unit for emergencies
  • Use an experienced attorney for contested cases
