Solar Panels for Rental Properties: Cost and ROI

Solar panels are one of the most discussed energy efficiency upgrades in the rental market. The hesitation is understandable: the "split incentive" problem — landlords pay for solar installation but tenants benefit from lower utility bills — makes the ROI calculus complicated. But when structured correctly, solar can increase property value, reduce your operating costs, qualify for significant rental property tax deductions, and make your rentals more attractive in an increasingly energy-conscious rental market.

This guide breaks down the real costs, returns, tax advantages, and practical structures for solar on rental properties.

The Split Incentive Problem (And How to Solve It)

The fundamental challenge with solar on rental properties is alignment:

  • If tenants pay utilities, they benefit from lower utility bills but the landlord pays for the installation
  • If landlords pay utilities (master metered), landlords benefit directly from solar savings

This "split incentive" problem has historically slowed solar adoption in rental properties. However, there are several ways to align the economics:

Solution 1: Landlord Pays Utilities

For properties where the landlord covers utility costs (common in some multi-family structures, student housing, or furnished rentals), solar directly reduces your operating costs. This is the cleanest ROI structure.

Solution 2: Green Rent Premium

Install solar, then advertise the property as solar-powered with lower estimated utility costs. Charge a rent premium of $50-$150/month reflecting the value to tenants. If the solar system generates $150/month in electricity savings for tenants, a $75/month rent increase captures half the benefit for the landlord.

Solution 3: Utility Billing Passthrough

In some states, landlords can bill tenants for solar electricity through a RUBS (Ratio Utility Billing System) or direct sub-metering. The landlord generates solar power, sells it to tenants at a rate below the utility retail rate, and the tenant saves money while the landlord recoupes installation costs.

Solution 4: Single-Family Rental with Tenant Pays Utilities

For single-family rentals where tenants pay utilities directly, include a lease provision that the tenant benefits from the solar installation (lower utility bills) but the system remains the landlord's property. The landlord's return comes from property value increase and tax incentives, not monthly utility savings.

Solar Installation Costs in 2026

Solar panel costs have declined significantly over the past decade and continue to fall as technology improves and supply chains mature.

Residential Solar (Single-Family)

  • Typical system size: 6-10 kW for most single-family homes
  • Installed cost (pre-incentive): $2.50-$3.50 per watt; a 8 kW system costs $20,000-$28,000 installed
  • Federal Investment Tax Credit (ITC): 30% of total installed cost — on a $24,000 system, that is a $7,200 tax credit
  • Net cost after federal ITC: $16,800-$19,600

Multi-Family Solar

For multi-family properties, solar options include:

Rooftop Solar (Common Area Electricity)

  • System sized to common area loads (lighting, laundry, elevators, HVAC in common areas)
  • Directly offsets landlord electricity costs
  • Simple ROI calculation
  • Typical system: 10-30 kW; installed cost $25,000-$75,000 (pre-incentive)

Community Solar / Virtual Net Metering

  • Some states allow virtual net metering where solar production is allocated to individual units' utility bills
  • Available in California, New York, Massachusetts, and other states with supportive policy
  • Allows multi-unit residents to benefit from building solar without individual system installations

Tax Incentives for Solar on Rental Properties

Federal Investment Tax Credit (ITC)

The federal solar ITC provides a credit equal to 30% of the qualified system cost:

  • Available for both residential and commercial properties
  • For residential rental property (single-family or small multi-family where you materially participate): claimed on Form 5695
  • For commercial rental property (multi-family where it is primarily investment property): claimed as a business energy credit on Form 3468
  • The ITC cannot exceed your tax liability in a given year, but unused credits can be carried forward

Depreciation

For rental properties, solar systems qualify for:

  • MACRS 5-year depreciation — Accelerated depreciation over 5 years for business property
  • Bonus depreciation — Subject to phase-down schedules, bonus depreciation allows a large first-year deduction
  • Combined, the tax benefits from the ITC and accelerated depreciation can significantly improve the system's financial return for landlords in higher tax brackets

State Incentives

Many states have additional solar incentives:

  • Net metering — Allows you to sell excess solar production back to the grid at retail rates; available in most states
  • State tax credits — California, Massachusetts, New York, and others offer state-level credits
  • Property tax exemptions — Many states exempt solar systems from property tax assessment increases
  • Sales tax exemptions — Many states exempt solar equipment from sales tax

Check the DSIRE database (dsireusa.org) for current incentives in your state.

ROI Analysis: A Realistic Example

Property: Single-family rental in a moderate-sunshine state (e.g., Texas, North Carolina)

System: 8 kW rooftop solar

Installed cost: $24,000

Federal ITC (30%): -$7,200

Net cost: $16,800

Annual electricity savings (at $0.12/kWh average): ~$1,200-$1,800/year (depending on location and system production)

Simple payback period (without incentives): 24,000 ÷ 1,500 = 16 years

Simple payback period (after ITC): 16,800 ÷ 1,500 = 11 years

With state incentives and depreciation: Could reduce to 7-9 years

System lifespan: 25-30 years; significant income-generating years after payback

Property value increase: Zillow and NREL research suggests solar homes sell for 3-4% premium; for a $350,000 property that is $10,500-$14,000 in added value — this alone nearly covers the post-ITC cost.

The ROI improves significantly with:

  • Higher electricity rates (California, Hawaii, New England)
  • Higher solar production areas (Southwest)
  • Better state incentives
  • Landlord-paid utility structures

Solar in High-Electricity-Cost Markets

Solar's financial case is strongest in markets with high retail electricity rates:

  • California: Average rate $0.25-$0.35/kWh; payback periods of 5-8 years after incentives in many cases
  • Hawaii: $0.35-$0.45/kWh; among the strongest ROI markets nationally
  • Massachusetts, Connecticut, New York: $0.18-$0.25/kWh; strong incentives improve ROI
  • Texas, Arizona, Nevada: High solar production (sunny climates) compensates for moderate electricity rates

In markets with low electricity rates ($0.08-$0.10/kWh, common in parts of the Southeast and Midwest), solar ROI is extended and may not make financial sense without significant incentives.

Battery Storage: Should You Add It?

Solar battery systems (like the Tesla Powerwall, Enphase IQ Battery, or Franklin Electric iFlex) store excess solar production for use during peak rates or power outages.

For rental properties:

  • Battery storage adds $8,000-$15,000 to system cost
  • ROI is most favorable in markets with time-of-use electricity rates where evening electricity is expensive
  • Backup power capability is a premium feature that some tenants value highly
  • In markets with strong net metering (credit for excess production), batteries add less incremental value

Battery storage remains more niche for rental property applications in 2026 but is worth evaluating in high-rate markets or where grid reliability is a concern.

Lease and Tenant Considerations

When installing solar on a rented property:

  • Lease disclosures: Note in the lease that the property has solar panels and that the system remains the landlord's property
  • Access for maintenance: Include a lease provision allowing the landlord reasonable access to maintain solar equipment (inverter checks, panel cleaning)
  • Tenant usage: Clarify whether any solar-related apps or monitoring systems will be tenant-accessible
  • Do not represent utility bill savings: Energy production varies; avoid making specific utility savings representations in lease marketing materials

Choosing a Solar Installer

For rental properties, work with established, certified installers:

  • Verify NABCEP certification (North American Board of Certified Energy Practitioners — nabcep.org)
  • Get at least three competitive bids
  • Review what warranty covers workmanship vs equipment
  • Confirm the installer will handle permit applications and utility interconnection
  • Verify the installer carries general liability insurance. See contractor insurance verification for what to check

Avoid door-to-door solar sales and pressure tactics that are common in the residential solar market.

Conclusion

Solar panels can be a financially compelling investment for rental properties — particularly in high-electricity-cost markets, for landlords who pay utilities, or where the economics of rent premiums and property value increases align. The key is structuring the arrangement so the landlord captures a reasonable return on the investment, leveraging federal and state tax incentives, and choosing an experienced installer with proper credentials.
