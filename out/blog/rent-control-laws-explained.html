Rent Control Laws Explained: What Every Landlord Needs to Know

Rent control is one of the most debated topics in real estate. Some say it protects tenants from skyrocketing rents. Others say it discourages landlords from investing in housing. Whatever your opinion, one thing is clear: if you own rental property in a rent-controlled area, you must understand the rules.

This guide explains rent control laws in plain language. We cover what rent control is, where it exists, how it works, and what landlords need to do to stay compliant.

What Is Rent Control?

Rent control is a set of laws that limit how much a landlord can raise rent. These laws put a cap on rent increases, usually tied to a percentage or an inflation index like the Consumer Price Index (CPI).

There are two main types of rent regulation:

Rent Control (Strict)

Traditional rent control sets a hard limit on rent. The rent is frozen or can only go up by a very small amount. This type is rare today but still exists in a few older programs.

Rent Stabilization (Modern)

Rent stabilization is the more common form. It allows annual rent increases but caps them at a set percentage. For example, a city might allow landlords to raise rent by 3% per year or by the rate of inflation, whichever is lower.

Most people use the term "rent control" to describe both types. In this article, we will use it the same way.

Where Does Rent Control Exist?

Rent control is not a federal law. It exists at the state and local level. Only a handful of states allow rent control, and the rules vary widely.

States With Rent Control Laws

As of 2026, the following states have some form of rent control or allow local governments to enact it:

  • California: Statewide rent cap of 5% plus inflation (max 10%) under the Tenant Protection Act. Many cities have additional local rent control ordinances.
  • New York: Rent stabilization applies to many apartments in New York City and some surrounding counties. Rent control applies to a shrinking number of older units.
  • Oregon: Statewide rent cap of 7% plus inflation. The first state to pass statewide rent control in 2019.
  • New Jersey: No statewide law, but many cities and towns have local rent control ordinances.
  • Maryland: No statewide law, but some municipalities have rent stabilization programs.
  • Washington, D.C.: Strong rent control laws covering most rental units built before 1976.
  • Minnesota: Minneapolis passed a rent stabilization ordinance. St. Paul voters approved a 3% cap.

States That Ban Rent Control

Many states have laws that specifically prohibit rent control. These "preemption" laws prevent cities and towns from passing their own rent control ordinances. States with rent control bans include:

  • Texas
  • Florida
  • Georgia
  • North Carolina
  • Tennessee
  • Arizona
  • Colorado (repealed its ban, but no rent control has been enacted yet)
  • Indiana
  • Wisconsin
  • Alabama
  • And many others

If your property is in one of these states, rent control does not apply to you. But laws can change, so stay informed. You can check the latest rules in our guide on landlord-tenant law by state.

How Rent Control Works

Rent control laws vary by location, but most share some common features.

Rent Increase Caps

The core of rent control is the cap on rent increases. This cap limits how much you can raise rent each year. Common approaches include:

  • Fixed percentage: The rent can go up by a set amount, such as 3% or 5% per year
  • CPI-based: The increase is tied to the Consumer Price Index, which measures inflation
  • CPI plus a fixed amount: For example, CPI plus 3%, with a maximum cap of 10%
  • Rent board determination: A local rent board sets the allowable increase each year

In California, for example, the statewide law caps annual increases at 5% plus the local CPI change, with a maximum of 10%. But cities like San Francisco and Los Angeles have their own stricter rules, often allowing increases of only 2% to 4%.

When Can You Raise Rent?

Most rent control laws allow rent increases once per year. You usually need to give advance notice, typically 30 to 90 days before the increase takes effect.

Some laws require you to file the increase with a local rent board before notifying the tenant. Others only require that you follow standard notice procedures.

Just Cause Eviction

Many rent control laws come with just cause eviction requirements. This means you cannot evict a tenant simply because their lease has ended. You need a specific legal reason, such as:

  • Nonpayment of rent
  • Lease violations
  • Nuisance behavior
  • Owner move-in (you or a family member want to live there)
  • Major renovation that requires the unit to be vacant
  • Withdrawal of the unit from the rental market (Ellis Act in California)

Just cause eviction rules make it harder to remove tenants, even if you want to sell the property or raise the rent to market rate. Make sure your lease agreement template accounts for any rent control provisions that apply to your property.

Exemptions From Rent Control

Not every property is subject to rent control. Most laws include exemptions.

Common Exemptions

  • Newer construction: Many laws exempt buildings built after a certain date. In California, the statewide law exempts buildings less than 15 years old.
  • Single-family homes: Some laws exempt single-family homes, especially if they are not owned by a corporation or REIT.
  • Owner-occupied buildings: Small buildings where the owner lives in one of the units are often exempt.
  • Subsidized housing: Government-subsidized units may be exempt because they already have rent limits.
  • Luxury units: A few programs exempt high-rent units above a certain threshold.

Vacancy Decontrol

Vacancy decontrol is a key concept for landlords. It means that when a tenant moves out voluntarily, the landlord can raise the rent to market rate for the next tenant. Once the new tenant moves in, rent control kicks in again for future increases.

This is how most modern rent stabilization programs work. You are locked into small annual increases while a tenant stays, but you can reset the rent when the unit turns over.

However, not all programs have vacancy decontrol. In some strict rent control cities, the rent stays controlled even when a tenant leaves. Check your local laws carefully.

How Rent Control Impacts Landlords

Rent control has real financial consequences for landlords. Here is what you need to understand.

Reduced Income Growth

The most obvious impact is slower rent growth. If the market would support a 10% increase but you are capped at 3%, you lose potential income every year. Over time, this gap compounds. A property that could generate $2,000 per month at market rate might only bring in $1,500 under rent control.

Longer Tenant Stays

Tenants in rent-controlled units tend to stay longer because they are getting below-market rent. This can be good because it reduces turnover costs. But it can also be bad because you cannot raise the rent to market rate while they stay.

Maintenance Challenges

When rent increases are limited, landlords sometimes struggle to keep up with maintenance costs. Property taxes, insurance, and repair costs go up every year. If your rent cannot keep pace, your margins shrink.

This does not mean you can skip maintenance. You are still legally required to keep the property in good condition. Failing to maintain the property can result in fines, lawsuits, and even criminal charges in extreme cases.

Property Value Effects

Rent control can affect property values. A property generating below-market rent is worth less than a comparable property at market rent. Buyers factor in the restricted income when making offers.

However, properties with vacancy decontrol may hold value better because the buyer knows they can reset rents when units turn over.

Compliance Strategies for Landlords

If your property is subject to rent control, you need a plan to stay compliant and still earn a fair return.

Know Your Local Laws

Rent control rules vary from city to city. Read your local ordinance carefully. You can look up the statutory basis for rent regulation at Cornell Law for background on how these laws are structured. Attend rent board meetings. Join your local landlord association. Ignorance of the law is not a defense.

Track Allowable Increases

Keep a calendar of when you can raise rent and by how much. Calculate the maximum increase allowed each year and apply it consistently. If you skip a year, you may lose that increase permanently in some jurisdictions.

File Required Paperwork

Many rent-controlled cities require landlords to register their rental units and file annual rent increase notices with the rent board. Missing these filings can result in fines or void your rent increase.

Document Everything

Keep records of every rent increase, notice, and communication with tenants. If a tenant challenges a rent increase, your documentation is your defense.

Make Capital Improvements

Some rent control laws allow landlords to pass through the cost of major improvements to tenants. For example, if you replace the roof or upgrade the plumbing, you may be able to add a surcharge to the rent to recover those costs.

Check your local rules for capital improvement pass-through provisions. They can help you offset the cost of maintaining an older building.

Use Legal Rent Increase Mechanisms

Beyond annual CPI increases, there may be other legal ways to raise rent:

  • Banking: Some programs let you "bank" unused increases from prior years
  • Utility pass-throughs: If you pay utilities, you may be able to pass increased costs to tenants
  • Capital improvement surcharges: As mentioned above
  • Hardship petitions: If your expenses significantly exceed your income, you may petition for a larger increase

Optimize Your Pricing Strategy

In rent-controlled markets, your initial rent is critical because future increases are limited. Set the starting rent as close to market rate as possible when a unit turns over.

For help with pricing, read our guide on rental pricing strategy.

Rent Control and Your Tenants

Rent control changes the landlord-tenant dynamic. Understanding this helps you manage your properties more effectively.

Tenants Know Their Rights

Tenants in rent-controlled areas tend to be well-informed about their rights. They know how much you can raise rent, when you can evict, and what you must maintain. Treat them fairly and follow the rules. Tenants who feel mistreated are more likely to file complaints.

Communication Is Key

Be transparent with your tenants about rent increases. Give proper notice and explain the calculation. Tenants are more likely to accept an increase when they understand how it was determined.

For more on managing tenant relationships, check out our guide on tenant rights and responsibilities.

Tenant Buyouts

In some rent-controlled markets, landlords offer tenants cash to vacate voluntarily. This is legal in most places, but many cities have strict rules about tenant buyouts. You may need to:

  • Make the offer in writing
  • Inform the tenant of their right to refuse
  • File the buyout agreement with the city
  • Provide a cooling-off period

Never pressure a tenant into accepting a buyout. Coercive buyout tactics can result in significant fines and legal trouble.

Future Trends in Rent Control

Rent control is expanding. As housing costs rise across the country, more states and cities are considering rent regulation. Here are some trends to watch:

More States Considering Statewide Laws

Several states have introduced rent control bills in recent years. While most have not passed yet, the trend is clear. States like Illinois, Massachusetts, and Washington have seen active campaigns for rent regulation.

Stronger Tenant Protections

Even in states without rent control, legislatures are passing stronger tenant protections. Longer notice periods for rent increases, limits on late fees, and restrictions on security deposits are becoming more common.

Repeal of Preemption Laws

Some states that currently ban rent control are seeing efforts to repeal those bans. Colorado already repealed its preemption law. Others may follow.

Technology and Enforcement

Cities are using technology to enforce rent control. Online portals for rent registration, automated compliance checks, and data analytics are making enforcement easier and more consistent.

What to Do If You Are Not Sure

Rent control laws are complex and they change often. If you own rental property and are not sure whether rent control applies to you, take these steps:

  1. Check your state law for statewide rent caps or preemption laws
  2. Check your city or county ordinances for local rent control rules
  3. Contact your local rent board if one exists
  4. Consult a real estate attorney who specializes in landlord-tenant law
  5. Join a landlord association for updates and resources

Final Thoughts

Rent control is a reality for many landlords. Whether you agree with it or not, you must comply if your property is covered. Understanding the rules, tracking your increases, and documenting everything are the keys to success.

The good news is that rent control does not have to kill your returns. With smart pricing, proper use of exemptions, and strategic capital improvements, you can still build a profitable rental portfolio in a rent-controlled market.

Stay informed, stay compliant, and stay focused on providing good housing. For additional legal resources, Nolo's rent control guides provide state-by-state overviews. That is the best strategy for any landlord, rent control or not.
