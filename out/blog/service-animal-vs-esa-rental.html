Service Animals vs ESAs in Rentals: Key Differences

Landlords regularly receive requests to keep animals in no-pet properties. Knowing the difference between a service animal and an emotional support animal is critical because the legal rules, verification rights, and your obligations differ significantly between the two. Confusing them can lead to either wrongful denials or unnecessary compliance exposure.

This guide breaks down the distinctions and gives you a clear framework for handling each type of request.

Definitions and Legal Basis

Service Animals

A service animal is a dog (or in some cases, a miniature horse) that is individually trained to perform specific tasks for a person with a disability. Examples of tasks include:

  • Guiding a person who is blind
  • Alerting a person who is deaf to sounds
  • Pulling a wheelchair
  • Detecting and responding to seizures
  • Retrieving dropped items
  • Providing stability and balance support
  • Interrupting destructive behaviors related to psychiatric disability

Service animals are protected under both the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) and the Fair Housing Act.

Emotional Support Animals (ESAs)

An emotional support animal provides therapeutic emotional benefit to a person with a mental or emotional disability through companionship. ESAs do not require specialized training. Any animal can be an ESA — dogs, cats, birds, rabbits, and other species.

ESAs are protected under the Fair Housing Act but not under the ADA. This distinction matters because it affects where the animal is allowed and what documentation you can request.

Therapy Animals

Therapy animals are trained to provide comfort to people in hospitals, schools, and nursing homes. They have no legal protections in rental housing and are treated as pets.

Landlord Obligations for Service Animals

What You Can Ask

For service animals, you are limited to two questions when the disability is not obvious:

  1. Is this animal required because of a disability?
  2. What task or work has the animal been trained to perform?

That is it. You cannot:

  • Request documentation or certification
  • Ask about the nature of the disability
  • Require a demonstration of the task
  • Ask for proof of training

What You Must Allow

  • The animal in any unit regardless of pet policies
  • No pet deposits, pet fees, or pet rent
  • No breed, size, or weight restrictions
  • The animal in all common areas of the property

When You Can Deny

You may exclude a specific service animal if it:

  • Poses a direct threat to the health or safety of others that cannot be mitigated
  • Would cause substantial physical damage to the property that cannot be mitigated
  • Is out of control and the handler cannot or does not take effective action

You cannot deny based on the breed alone, fear of dogs in general, or allergies of other tenants (you must attempt to accommodate both parties).

Landlord Obligations for Emotional Support Animals

What You Can Ask

For ESAs, you have more verification rights than for service animals:

  • Request a letter from a licensed healthcare provider confirming:
    • The tenant has a disability under the Fair Housing Act
    • The ESA provides disability-related therapeutic benefit
    • There is a nexus between the disability and the animal

Evaluating ESA Documentation

HUD's 2020 guidance allows landlords to scrutinize ESA documentation. You may question letters that appear unreliable, such as those from:

  • Online-only providers with no established patient relationship
  • Providers not licensed in the tenant's state
  • Providers who issued letters for a fee without a clinical evaluation

See our ESA landlord guidelines for the complete verification process.

What You Must Allow

Once an ESA is verified:

  • The animal in any unit regardless of pet policies
  • No pet deposits, pet fees, or pet rent
  • No breed, size, or weight restrictions (unless a specific animal poses a documented direct threat)

When You Can Deny

The grounds for denying an ESA are similar to service animals. Review our reasonable accommodation guide for the full legal standard landlords must meet:

  • Direct threat to safety that cannot be mitigated
  • Substantial property damage risk from a specific animal
  • Unreliable or fraudulent documentation
  • The animal would impose an undue burden (rare — courts set a high bar)

Handling Requests: Step-by-Step

Step 1: Identify the Type of Animal

Ask whether the animal is a service animal trained to perform tasks, or an emotional support animal. This determines which verification path to follow.

Step 2: Follow the Appropriate Verification Process

  • Service animal: Ask the two permitted questions if the disability is not obvious
  • ESA: Request healthcare provider documentation if the disability is not obvious

Step 3: Evaluate and Respond

Review the information provided and respond in writing within 10 to 14 days. Approve, deny with specific reasoning, or request additional information if the initial submission was insufficient.

Step 4: Document Everything

Keep a record of the request, documentation received, your evaluation, and your written response. Store disability-related information confidentially.

Common Scenarios

Tenant Claims Pet Is a Service Animal After Move-In

A tenant may request reclassification of an existing pet as an assistance animal. Evaluate the request on its merits using the framework in our disability accommodation guide. If they provide valid documentation of a disability and need, the request must be accommodated regardless of timing.

Multiple Assistance Animals

A tenant may request more than one service animal or ESA. Evaluate each individually. Multiple animals may be reasonable if the documentation supports distinct disability-related needs for each.

Neighbor Complaints About the Animal

Address behavior issues (noise, aggression, waste) the same way you would for any tenant issue. Document complaints, communicate with the handler, and address the specific behavior. You cannot revoke the accommodation unless the animal poses an unmitigable direct threat.

Tenant Has Allergies to Another Tenant's Assistance Animal

You must attempt to accommodate both parties. Solutions may include:

  • Assigning units in different areas of the property
  • Improving HVAC filtration in common areas
  • Establishing separate common area usage schedules

You cannot remove a verified assistance animal to accommodate another tenant's allergies.

Fraud Prevention

ESA fraud is a legitimate concern. Protect yourself while staying compliant:

  • Use HUD's guidance to evaluate documentation reliability
  • Check that healthcare providers are licensed in the tenant's state
  • Be skeptical of online-only ESA certification services
  • Know your state's ESA fraud laws — several states have enacted penalties for misrepresenting a pet as an ESA

Never use fraud concerns as a blanket reason to deny all ESA requests. Evaluate each individually.

Key Takeaways

Service animals and emotional support animals both have legal protections in rental housing, but the rules for each differ. Service animals require almost no verification. ESAs allow more scrutiny of documentation. Neither can be charged pet fees. Build separate procedures for each type, train your staff, and document every interaction. When in doubt, consult a fair housing attorney before denying any assistance animal request.
