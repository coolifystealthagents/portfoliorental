Landscaping Tips for Rental Properties on a Budget

Landscaping is often treated as an afterthought by rental property owners, yet it has a direct and measurable impact on rental income. Properties with well-maintained landscaping rent faster, attract better tenants, and often command higher rents — studies consistently show that curb appeal improvements can increase rental property value by 5-15%. At the same time, overly complex landscaping is a maintenance burden that can drain your operating budget.

The goal for a rental property landscape is simple: maximum curb appeal with minimum maintenance cost. This guide covers practical landscaping strategies that achieve that balance, from plant selection to tenant responsibility structures.

The Business Case for Good Landscaping

Before diving into tactics, understand why landscaping matters financially:

  • Faster leasing — A well-maintained exterior is the first impression prospective tenants see. Poor curb appeal increases vacancy time.
  • Higher rents — Attractive outdoor spaces justify premium pricing, especially in competitive markets.
  • Better tenants — Tenants who choose a well-maintained property tend to take better care of it.
  • Reduced liability — Overgrown shrubs, dead trees, and poor drainage create hazards and legal exposure.
  • Property value — Well-maintained landscaping contributes to appraised property value.

Designing for Low Maintenance

The best time to invest in landscaping is when a unit turns over. Use the vacancy period to redesign toward lower maintenance requirements.

Choose the Right Plants

Select plants that are:

  • Native or drought-tolerant — Native plants are adapted to local rainfall and soil conditions and require minimal watering and care once established. The EPA WaterSense program offers guidance on water-efficient landscaping
  • Slow-growing — Fast-growing plants require more frequent pruning and create more debris
  • Disease and pest resistant — Choose varieties known to thrive in your region with minimal treatment
  • Proportionate to the space — Plant at mature size, not planting size — a shrub planted 2 feet from a foundation will become a problem as it grows

Replace High-Maintenance Features

High-maintenance landscaping elements to remove or avoid:

  • Annual flower beds — Require replanting every year; replace with perennials or low-growing shrubs
  • Formal hedges requiring precise shearing — Replace with informal naturalistic plantings
  • Large mulched beds with sparse plants — Weeds fill the gaps quickly; use dense ground cover plantings instead
  • Ornamental features requiring seasonal storage — Fountains, decorative pots, and seasonal decorations add labor costs

Ground Cover Alternatives to Lawn

Lawn is the most maintenance-intensive landscaping element. Where possible, reduce lawn area by:

  • Installing mulched planting beds with dense shrubs or ground cover plants
  • Using pea gravel or decomposed granite in low-traffic areas
  • Installing hardscape (patios, walkways) in high-traffic areas
  • Replacing narrow strips of grass (between sidewalk and street) with low-growing perennials or native ground covers

Tenant vs Landlord: Landscaping Responsibilities

Clarifying who is responsible for landscaping maintenance is one of the most important decisions a landlord makes. The answer depends on property type and your management model.

For Single-Family Homes and Duplexes

Most landlords assign lawn and basic landscaping maintenance to tenants in single-family homes. This arrangement works well when:

  • The lease clearly specifies what tenants are responsible for (lawn mowing, leaf raking, light weeding, snow removal)
  • Expectations are set at move-in with a walkthrough of outdoor areas
  • The yard is simple enough that a typical tenant can maintain it
  • Failure to maintain is documented and addressed promptly

Even when tenants are responsible for basic maintenance, the landlord typically retains responsibility for:

  • Tree trimming and removal
  • Sprinkler system maintenance and repair
  • Major landscaping improvements
  • Any features requiring professional equipment

For Multi-Family Properties

For apartment buildings, landscaping is almost always the landlord's responsibility. Contract with a professional landscaping service for:

  • Weekly or bi-weekly lawn care (mowing, edging, blowing)
  • Monthly or seasonal pruning
  • Seasonal color (annual plantings at entry areas for curb appeal)
  • Snow removal (where applicable)

Get multiple bids and negotiate annual contracts for better pricing than per-visit rates. Reliable, contracted landscape crews tend to provide more consistent service than ad-hoc mow-and-go operations.

Lawn Care Basics for Rental Properties

If you maintain the lawn yourself or oversee tenant maintenance, these fundamentals apply:

Mowing

  • Mow at the correct height for the grass species (3-4 inches for most cool-season grasses, 2-3 inches for warm-season grasses)
  • Never remove more than one-third of the grass blade in a single mowing
  • Keep blades sharp — dull blades tear grass rather than cut cleanly, increasing disease susceptibility

Fertilization

  • Apply fertilizer 2-4 times per year depending on grass type and region
  • A basic lawn fertilizer program (spring, summer, fall) maintains density and green color
  • Consider organic slow-release fertilizers — they are lower-risk for water contamination and require less frequent application

Weed Control

  • Pre-emergent herbicide applied in early spring prevents annual weed germination
  • Spot-treat weeds that break through rather than broadcasting herbicide indiscriminately
  • In problem areas, dense planting and mulch are more sustainable than ongoing herbicide application

Irrigation

  • If the property has an irrigation system, test all heads at the start of the season and repair broken or misaligned heads
  • Program controllers for efficiency — deep infrequent watering (1 inch per week) produces deeper root growth than shallow daily watering
  • Winterize irrigation systems before freezing temperatures

Tree Maintenance

Trees are valuable assets on a rental property but require active management:

  • Annual inspection — Look for dead or dying branches, signs of disease or pest damage, and proximity to structures or utility lines
  • Pruning — Hire a certified arborist for any pruning beyond basic dead-wooding. Improper pruning damages trees and can create liability.
  • Dead tree removal — Dead or dying trees adjacent to buildings, walkways, or parking areas should be removed promptly — a fallen tree or limb creates serious premises liability exposure
  • Root considerations — Tree roots can damage foundations, sewer lines, and hardscape over time. Consider this when planting new trees or when purchasing properties with large established trees.

Seasonal Landscaping Schedule

Spring (March–May)

  • Clean up winter debris — remove dead plant material, rake leaves from beds
  • Apply pre-emergent weed control to lawn and beds
  • Divide and transplant overgrown perennials
  • Fertilize lawn
  • Inspect and start irrigation system
  • Mulch planting beds (2-3 inches of fresh mulch suppresses weeds and retains moisture)
  • Plant warm-season annuals at entry areas for seasonal color

Summer (June–August)

  • Maintain mowing schedule (weekly during active growth)
  • Water deeply and infrequently during drought periods
  • Monitor for pest and disease problems
  • Deadhead flowering plants to extend bloom period
  • Trim shrubs that are encroaching on walkways or buildings

Fall (September–November)

  • Core aerate and overseed thin lawn areas
  • Apply fall fertilizer
  • Plant cool-season annuals (pansies, ornamental kale) for fall color
  • Rake and remove fallen leaves (matted leaves smother grass)
  • Cut back perennial plants after frost
  • Winterize irrigation system

Winter (December–February)

  • Apply de-icing materials to walkways and entry areas as needed
  • Remove snow from walkways promptly — ice on tenant walkways creates significant liability
  • Inspect trees for storm damage after major wind or ice events
  • Plan spring improvements during the off-season

Budget Landscaping Improvements With High ROI

If your rental property needs a landscaping refresh, these improvements deliver the most curb appeal per dollar. Factor them into your maintenance cost budget to avoid surprises:

  1. Fresh mulch — Two to three inches of dark bark mulch in planting beds dramatically improves appearance for $3-$5 per cubic yard. A typical front yard improvement costs $150-$400.

  2. Seasonal color at the entry — A few flats of seasonal flowers at the front entry can transform the first impression for less than $100.

  3. Defined bed edges — Clean, defined edges between lawn and planting beds create a well-maintained look. A steel or plastic edging product installs easily and lasts for years.

  4. Shrub replacement — Removing overgrown or dying foundation shrubs and replacing with appropriate-sized, healthy plants is one of the highest-impact landscaping investments.

  5. Walkway repair — Cracked or heaved walkways are both an eyesore and a liability. Repairing or replacing a front walkway ($500-$2,000) significantly improves curb appeal and reduces trip-and-fall risk.

Landscaping Lease Provisions

Your lease should address landscaping clearly:

  • Specify which maintenance tasks are the tenant's responsibility (mowing, watering, weeding, leaf removal, snow removal)
  • State the standard to which the lawn must be maintained (mowed to no more than 4 inches, for example)
  • Specify that tenant cannot remove existing plants, trees, or hardscape without written permission
  • Address approval requirements for tenant-added landscaping
  • Include consequences for failure to maintain assigned landscaping tasks

Conclusion

Effective rental property landscaping is about designing for durability, assigning responsibilities clearly, and maintaining consistently. A low-maintenance landscape designed with native plants, minimal lawn, and defined beds will cost less to maintain and look better than high-maintenance alternatives. Curb appeal drives faster leasing and higher rents — making landscaping one of the most financially impactful maintenance categories for rental property owners.
