Loss-of-Rent Insurance: Coverage and Claims

When a covered event — fire, storm, burst pipe — makes your rental unit uninhabitable, you lose rental income for every month the unit is vacant during repairs. Loss-of-rent insurance (also called fair rental value coverage or rental income insurance) replaces that lost income, keeping your cash flow intact while the property is restored.

This guide explains how the coverage works, how to file claims, and how to choose the right coverage level.

What Is Loss-of-Rent Insurance?

Loss-of-rent insurance is a component of most landlord insurance policies that reimburses you for rental income lost when a covered peril makes a unit uninhabitable. It bridges the income gap between the date the unit becomes uninhabitable and the date it is restored and re-rented.

How It Works

  1. A covered event damages the property (fire, storm, vandalism, etc.)
  2. The unit becomes uninhabitable and the tenant must vacate
  3. You file a claim with your insurance company
  4. The insurer pays your lost rental income during the repair period
  5. Coverage continues until the property is repaired and reasonably ready to rent

Coverage Triggers

Loss-of-rent coverage typically activates when:

  • A covered peril under your landlord policy causes the damage
  • The damage makes the unit legally or practically uninhabitable
  • The tenant vacates or cannot occupy the unit
  • Repairs are underway within a reasonable timeframe

Coverage does not activate for:

  • Voluntary vacancy (you chose not to rent)
  • Damage from excluded perils (flooding, earthquake — see flood insurance for rentals for separate coverage options)
  • Tenant-caused damage that your landlord policy excludes
  • Normal wear and maintenance closures
  • Market vacancy (no tenants despite availability)

What It Covers

Fair Rental Value

Most policies pay the fair rental value of the unit — the rent you would have received minus any expenses you no longer incur because the unit is vacant (such as tenant-related utilities if the landlord pays them).

Duration

Coverage lasts for the period reasonably necessary to repair the property, up to the policy limit. Common limits are:

  • 12 months of lost rent (most standard policies)
  • 24 months for some premium policies
  • A dollar cap instead of or in addition to a time limit

Additional Expenses

Some policies also cover reasonable additional expenses you incur because of the loss, such as:

  • Advertising costs to find new tenants after repairs
  • Relocation assistance provided to displaced tenants
  • Temporary housing costs if required by your lease or local law (see HUD rental assistance programs for tenant resources)

How to File a Loss-of-Rent Claim

Step 1: Document the Damage

Immediately after the event:

  • Photograph and video the damage extensively
  • Secure the property to prevent further damage
  • Contact your insurance company to report the claim

Step 2: Establish the Rental Income

Provide your insurer with documentation of the rental income you are losing:

  • Current lease agreement showing rent amount
  • Rent payment history (bank statements, ledger records)
  • If between tenants, evidence of the unit's fair rental value (market comparables)

Step 3: Cooperate With Adjusters

Your insurer will send an adjuster to assess:

  • The extent of damage
  • The estimated repair timeline
  • Whether the unit is truly uninhabitable
  • The fair rental value to be reimbursed

Step 4: Begin Repairs Promptly

Your duty to mitigate applies to insurance claims. You must take reasonable steps to repair the property promptly. Unnecessary delays in beginning or completing repairs can reduce your claim:

  • Get repair estimates quickly
  • Authorize emergency repairs to prevent further damage
  • Select contractors and begin work within a reasonable timeframe
  • Document the repair timeline and any unavoidable delays

Step 5: Submit Monthly Claims

Most insurers pay loss-of-rent claims on a monthly basis during the repair period. Submit:

  • Monthly invoices for the fair rental value amount
  • Progress updates on repairs
  • Contractor timelines and any delay explanations
  • Documentation of any additional expenses

Choosing the Right Coverage Level

Calculate Your Exposure

Determine how much income you could lose from your highest-risk property:

  • Monthly rent × 12 months = maximum annual exposure per unit
  • Factor in the typical repair timeline for major damage (fire: 6-12 months, water damage: 2-6 months, storm damage: 3-6 months)

Coverage Recommendations

Portfolio Size Minimum Loss-of-Rent Coverage
Single unit 12 months of rent
2-5 units 12 months of total rent across all units
5+ units Consider a portfolio policy with adequate aggregate limits

Policy Limit Types

  • Stated amount — A fixed dollar amount (e.g., $30,000)
  • Percentage of dwelling value — Often 10-20% of the insured dwelling value
  • Time-based — 12 or 24 months of fair rental value

Ensure your limit is sufficient for a worst-case scenario, not just a minor repair. Pairing this coverage with a rental property reserve fund provides an additional buffer during extended vacancies.

Common Claim Pitfalls

  • Insufficient documentation — Without a lease and payment history, proving your rental income is difficult
  • Delayed repairs — Insurers can reduce claims if you did not begin repairs promptly
  • Underinsurance — If your loss-of-rent limit is too low, you absorb the difference
  • Excluded perils — Damage from flood, earthquake, or other excluded events does not trigger loss-of-rent coverage unless you have separate coverage. Consider umbrella insurance to extend your overall protection
  • Failure to mitigate — You must take reasonable steps to restore the property and find new tenants

Loss of Rent vs Rent Guarantee Insurance

Loss-of-rent insurance and rent guarantee insurance cover different risks:

  • Loss-of-rent insurance covers income lost when the property is physically uninhabitable due to a covered peril
  • Rent guarantee insurance covers income lost when a tenant stops paying rent (default/eviction)

Both can be valuable, but they address different scenarios. Most landlord policies include loss-of-rent but not rent guarantee coverage.

Key Takeaways

Loss-of-rent insurance is a critical component of your landlord insurance that replaces income during covered repair periods. The IRS treats reimbursed rental income as taxable; see IRS Publication 527 for reporting requirements. Verify your policy includes adequate coverage, document your rental income thoroughly, begin repairs promptly after a loss, and file claims on a regular schedule during the repair period. The cost of this coverage is minimal compared to the financial impact of months without rental income.
