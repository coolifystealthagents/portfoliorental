Knowing how much you can legally raise rent — and when and how you must notify tenants — is a fundamental part of running a rental business. As more states and cities enact rent stabilization laws, the landscape has grown significantly more complex. Failing to follow the correct procedures for a rent increase can result in the increase being void, penalties, or eviction cases being dismissed.

This guide provides a comprehensive overview of rent increase limits by state in 2026, the notice requirements that apply, and best practices for implementing rent increases effectively. The CFPB housing portal is also a useful resource for tenants and landlords navigating these regulations.

States With Statewide Rent Increase Limits

Most states in the U.S. do not have statewide rent control. Landlords in unregulated states may raise rent by any amount — but only at the end of a lease term (for fixed-term leases) or with proper notice (for month-to-month tenancies). However, a growing number of states have enacted statewide limits:

California

California's AB 1482 (Tenant Protection Act of 2019) caps annual rent increases for covered units at 5% plus local CPI, with a maximum of 10% per year. The 2026 allowable increase varies by region based on each area's CPI. Exemptions include:

  • Single-family homes and condos not owned by corporations or REITs (with proper notice to tenant)
  • Units built within the last 15 years (rolling exemption)
  • Units already subject to stricter local rent control

Many California cities (Los Angeles, San Francisco, Oakland, Santa Monica, Berkeley, etc.) have local ordinances with lower caps — the more restrictive rule governs.

Oregon

Oregon's statewide rent stabilization law caps annual increases at 7% plus the annual change in the Consumer Price Index (CPI). For 2025, the Oregon Rental Housing Association reported allowable increases in the range of 9-10%, subject to annual recalculation. Exemptions include units with a certificate of occupancy issued within the last 15 years.

Washington

Washington's statewide just cause protections are paired with notice requirements. While Washington does not currently have a hard statewide rent increase cap, the legislature has actively debated caps and notice requirements have been strengthened. Always verify current Washington law as this has been a rapidly changing area.

New Jersey

New Jersey does not have a statewide rent cap but allows individual municipalities to enact rent control. More than 100 NJ municipalities have local rent control ordinances, including Newark, Jersey City, Hoboken, and others.

Notice Requirements for Rent Increases

Regardless of the amount of the increase, landlords in every state must provide advance written notice before a rent increase takes effect. The required notice period depends on:

  • The type of tenancy (month-to-month vs. fixed-term lease)
  • The amount of the increase (many states require longer notice for large increases)
  • State-specific statute

States That Preempt Local Rent Control

Many states expressly prohibit cities and counties from enacting rent control or rent stabilization:

  • Arizona — State law prohibits local rent control ordinances
  • Florida — State law preempts local rent control (with limited exceptions for declared housing emergencies)
  • Georgia — Preempts local rent control
  • Idaho — Preempts local rent control
  • Indiana — Preempts local rent control
  • Iowa — Preempts local rent control
  • Minnesota — Historically prohibited local rent control; Minneapolis and St. Paul passed local ordinances in 2021, leading to ongoing litigation
  • Missouri — Preempts local rent control
  • North Carolina — Preempts local rent control
  • Ohio — Preempts local rent control
  • South Carolina — Preempts local rent control
  • Tennessee — Preempts local rent control
  • Texas — Preempts local rent control
  • Wisconsin — Preempts local rent control

If you own property in a state with preemption, you can raise rent by any amount — subject only to the notice requirements above and general anti-discrimination and retaliation laws.

Cities and Counties With Local Rent Increase Caps

Even in states without statewide rent limits, certain cities may have enacted local ordinances before preemption laws were passed or through other legal authority:

California Cities With Local Rent Control

California's statewide AB 1482 does not supersede stricter local ordinances. Major California cities with their own annual increase limits:

  • Los Angeles — Annual increases for RSO units are determined by LAHD; typically 3%-8% depending on utility passthroughs
  • San Francisco — Annual increases based on CPI; 2025-2026 increase was approximately 1.4%
  • Oakland — Annual Consumer Price Index adjustment; typically 2%-4%
  • Santa Monica — Annual increase tied to CPI with a cap; historically in the 2%-4% range
  • Berkeley — Annual increase tied to CPI

New Jersey Cities

Multiple NJ cities cap annual rent increases at 4%-6% depending on the municipality.

New York City

New York City's Rent Guidelines Board sets annual increases for rent-stabilized units. For the 2025-2026 lease year, the RGB approved increases of approximately 2.75% for one-year leases and 5.25% for two-year leases for stabilized apartments.

Rent Increases During a Fixed-Term Lease

In nearly every state, a landlord cannot raise rent during a fixed-term lease unless the lease specifically provides for it (through a rent escalation clause). A rent increase mid-lease is generally not enforceable.

At the end of a fixed-term lease, the landlord may:

  1. Offer a renewal lease at a higher rent — review lease renewal strategies to approach this effectively
  2. Convert to a month-to-month tenancy with an increased rent (with proper notice)
  3. Decline to renew the lease (subject to just cause requirements where applicable)

Rent Increases and Retaliation

Rent increases must not be retaliatory. A retaliatory rent increase — raising rent because a tenant exercised a legal right, such as reporting housing code violations to a government agency, organizing a tenant union, or requesting repairs — is illegal in virtually every state. Courts may void such increases and order the return of any amounts paid under a retaliatory increase.

Always have a documented, legitimate business reason for any rent increase (market conditions, increased operating costs, capital improvements) and ensure the timing does not correspond with protected tenant activity. Census housing data can help document market-rate trends in your area.

Above-Guideline Increases in Regulated Markets

If your property is subject to rent stabilization and you need to increase rent above the guideline amount, you must file a petition with the local rent board. Common qualifying grounds include:

  • Capital improvements — New roof, HVAC system replacement, seismic retrofitting, major plumbing or electrical work
  • Operating cost increases — Extraordinary increases in property taxes, insurance, or utility costs
  • Debt service — Some jurisdictions allow petitions based on financing costs

The petition process involves:

  1. Filing an application with the rent board and paying a filing fee
  2. Providing documentation of costs (invoices, tax bills, contracts)
  3. Attending a hearing
  4. Receiving a written decision with the approved increase amount

Processing times of 6-18 months are common. Plan capital improvement projects with this timeline in mind.

Practical Steps for Implementing a Rent Increase

Step 1: Verify Your Legal Authority

Before any increase, confirm:

  • Is your unit subject to rent stabilization (local or state)?
  • What is the maximum allowable increase for the current year?
  • What notice period is required?

Step 2: Draft a Written Notice

Your rent increase notice should include:

  • The current rent amount
  • The new rent amount
  • The effective date of the increase
  • The amount of the increase as a dollar amount and percentage
  • Your signature and contact information

Step 3: Deliver Notice Correctly

Send the notice via certified mail with return receipt requested, or deliver it in person with a signed receipt. Keep a copy in the tenant's file with proof of delivery.

Step 4: Update Your Lease Records

When the increase takes effect, update your lease records and property management software to reflect the new rent. Issue a written lease amendment or confirmation letter.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I raise rent every month on a month-to-month tenancy?

Technically, yes — with proper notice each time. But frequent increases damage your tenant relationships and may raise questions about retaliation. Most landlords implement annual increases.

What happens if I raise rent without giving proper notice?

The increase is typically voidable. The tenant can refuse to pay the increased amount until proper notice is given and the required notice period expires. In regulated markets, improper increases can result in penalties.

Can I raise rent to cover a large repair or improvement?

In unregulated markets, yes — you can raise rent for any reason with proper notice. In stabilized markets, above-guideline increases for improvements require a petition process.

How do I know if my property is rent stabilized?

In California, the local rent board or housing department maintains a registry. For other states, check the local housing authority website or consult a landlord-tenant attorney. NAR research data also publishes rental market statistics by region.

Conclusion

Rent increase rules vary dramatically by state and city. In most states, you can raise rent by any amount — but only with proper notice and never during a fixed-term lease. In states and cities with rent stabilization, annual increases are capped and above-guideline increases require a formal petition process. Understanding the rules that apply to each property in your portfolio is essential before implementing any rent increase.
