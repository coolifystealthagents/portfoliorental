Smart Thermostats for Rentals: Savings and Setup Guide

Smart thermostats are one of the easiest, most cost-effective technology upgrades a landlord can make. At $50–$250 per unit, they reduce heating and cooling costs by 10–23%, improve tenant satisfaction, and can give landlords remote visibility into HVAC performance — all with a payback period that often falls within 6–18 months.

This guide covers everything landlords need to know about smart thermostats: which scenarios deliver the most savings, how to choose the right model, how to handle installation and tenant communication, and how to manage privacy and control considerations in a rental context.

Why Smart Thermostats Deliver Strong ROI for Landlords

Standard thermostats maintain a set temperature constantly — regardless of whether anyone is home, whether it is 2 AM, or whether the unit is vacant. Smart thermostats adjust automatically based on occupancy, time of day, and tenant-programmed schedules, reducing energy waste dramatically.

Documented savings data:

  • Ecobee (based on their own data): Average savings of 23% on heating and cooling
  • Nest (Google): Claims up to 12% on heating, 15% on cooling
  • U.S. EPA ENERGY STAR estimates: 8–12% average reduction in HVAC costs (see Energy.gov thermostat guide for additional savings data)

For a unit with $1,200/year in heating and cooling costs, even a conservative 12% reduction saves $144/year per unit. At $120 device and installation cost, payback is less than 1 year.

For a 20-unit property where the landlord pays heating costs, a 15% reduction on $24,000/year in HVAC expense saves $3,600 annually.

Two Scenarios: Landlord-Paid vs. Tenant-Paid Utilities

Scenario 1: Landlord Pays Heating/Cooling

This is where smart thermostats deliver the most direct financial benefit. The landlord controls energy costs, and smart thermostats directly reduce what you spend.

Best approach: Install landlord-accessible smart thermostats that allow you to:

  • Set temperature limits (minimum and maximum) to prevent extreme usage
  • Monitor whether the unit is occupied (via motion sensing)
  • Reduce temperature during tenant absence or vacancy
  • Detect HVAC anomalies (unit running constantly = potential malfunction or open window)

Important consideration: If tenants pay a flat fee for utilities (not individually metered), they have no incentive to conserve. Smart thermostats with temperature limits become essential in this scenario.

Scenario 2: Tenant Pays Utilities

Even when tenants pay their own utility bills, smart thermostats are a marketing advantage and tenant satisfaction tool:

  • Tenants see lower monthly bills → higher satisfaction and better retention
  • Modern, energy-efficient property is a differentiator in competitive rental markets — and part of a broader set of rental property amenity upgrades that can justify higher rents
  • Ecobee and Nest both have strong consumer recognition; tenants perceive these as premium amenities
  • Lower utility costs can support higher base rents in many markets

A $150 investment that reduces a tenant's monthly utility bill by $20–$30/month is equivalent to offering a meaningful rent discount — at a one-time cost rather than an ongoing revenue sacrifice.

Choosing the Right Smart Thermostat for a Rental Property

Top Models for Landlords

Ecobee SmartThermostat Premium ($249)

  • Remote sensors detect occupancy in multiple rooms
  • Works with virtually any HVAC system including multi-stage and heat pumps
  • Landlord/manager access via app with ability to set limits
  • ENERGY STAR certified
  • Best for: Properties where you want the most robust occupancy-based savings and landlord controls

Google Nest Learning Thermostat ($129–$249)

  • Learns tenant schedule automatically within 1–2 weeks
  • Clean, widely recognized design
  • App-based landlord access
  • Best for: Tenant-paid utilities where the primary benefit is tenant satisfaction

Honeywell Home T9 ($150)

  • Multi-room sensors (purchased separately)
  • App-based controls with geofencing
  • Compatible with most HVAC systems
  • Best for: Price-conscious landlords who need multi-room sensing

Emerson Sensi Touch ($70–$100)

  • Budget-friendly
  • Simple app-based controls
  • ENERGY STAR certified
  • Best for: Landlords focused on cost-per-unit with straightforward HVAC systems

Mysa Smart Thermostat ($109) — for electric baseboard heat Many older apartment buildings use electric baseboard heating, which is not compatible with standard smart thermostats (which require a common wire "C-wire"). Mysa is designed specifically for high-voltage baseboard systems and delivers comparable energy savings.

Compatibility Check

Before purchasing, verify compatibility:

  • C-wire requirement: Most smart thermostats require a C-wire (common wire) for constant power. Older systems may not have one. Solutions include adapter kits (Nest's Power Connector, Ecobee's PEK) or hiring an electrician to run a C-wire.
  • Voltage: Standard smart thermostats are designed for 24V low-voltage systems. Electric baseboard heaters (120V or 240V) require a specialized thermostat like Mysa.
  • Heat pump compatibility: Some heat pumps require specific thermostat models. Verify before purchasing.
  • Multi-stage HVAC: Systems with multiple heating/cooling stages (more common in newer construction) need a thermostat designed for multi-stage operation.

Installation: DIY or Professional?

Smart thermostat installation is straightforward for most standard HVAC systems and takes 20–45 minutes:

  1. Turn off power to the HVAC at the breaker
  2. Remove the old thermostat and photograph the wire connections
  3. Disconnect wires and label them
  4. Mount the new thermostat base
  5. Connect wires per the new thermostat's wiring diagram
  6. Restore power and follow the setup wizard

Landlords comfortable with basic home maintenance can install smart thermostats in their rentals as DIY projects. This kind of proactive upkeep is also a practical way to reduce maintenance costs over time by catching HVAC issues before they become expensive failures. If you have an HVAC technician scheduled for annual service, ask them to install smart thermostats at the same time — many will do so for minimal additional labor cost, and you save a separate trip.

For properties where you are installing across multiple units, batch installations during turnover periods to minimize disruption.

Managing Tenant Relationships Around Smart Thermostats

Tenant communication and boundary-setting is important to avoid friction:

What to Disclose

Be transparent about:

  • The thermostat's capabilities (remote access, occupancy sensing)
  • Any temperature limits you have set
  • The privacy implications of occupancy data if applicable

Most states do not have specific laws about smart thermostat installation in rental units, but transparency prevents complaints and fosters trust. The CFPB housing resources provide guidance on tenant rights that may apply.

Setting Temperature Limits Appropriately

Setting reasonable temperature limits is appropriate and common — it prevents extreme settings that cause both excessive energy use and HVAC strain. Reasonable limits:

  • Minimum 65°F in winter (ensures pipes do not freeze; tenant comfort preserved)
  • Maximum 78–80°F in summer (prevents extreme cooling costs; still comfortable)

Set limits that are fair and focus on preventing abuse rather than micromanaging comfort. Document your temperature policy in the lease if you pay utilities.

Privacy Considerations

Motion/occupancy sensors in smart thermostats sense presence but do not identify individuals or record video or audio. Most tenants, when informed, have no objection to occupancy sensing. Be straightforward in your disclosure: "The smart thermostat uses motion sensing to reduce energy use when the unit is unoccupied."

If a tenant has a strong privacy objection, you can disable the occupancy sensing feature on most models and rely on scheduled programming instead — still a meaningful improvement over a standard thermostat.

Calculating ROI for Your Portfolio

For a quick ROI calculation:

Properties Units HVAC Cost/Unit/Year 15% Savings Device+Install Cost Payback
5 properties 20 units $800 $2,400/yr $3,000 1.25 yrs
10 properties 40 units $800 $4,800/yr $6,000 1.25 yrs

These are conservative estimates. In markets with extreme temperatures (Midwest winters, Southwest summers), actual savings often exceed 20%, improving payback further.

Smart thermostats pair well with other energy efficiency upgrades and water conservation improvements as part of an overall approach to reducing rental operating expenses. Combined, these technology-driven upgrades can reduce your utility and maintenance costs by 20–35% while simultaneously improving your properties' appeal to quality tenants — a dual benefit that directly supports the goal of maximizing rental income on every property in your portfolio.
