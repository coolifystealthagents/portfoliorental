Requiring Renters Insurance: Landlord Best Practices

Requiring renters insurance is one of the simplest and most effective risk management strategies available to landlords. It works best as part of a broader insurance framework — see the landlord insurance requirements guide for a full picture. For $15 to $30 per month, your tenants get personal property coverage and liability protection — and you get fewer claims against your own insurance, reduced litigation risk, and tenants who are better protected from financial hardship after a loss.

This guide covers the legal framework, implementation best practices, and enforcement strategies.

Why Require Renters Insurance?

Benefits for Landlords

  • Reduces claims against your policy — When a tenant has their own liability coverage, they are less likely to sue you for losses their insurance covers
  • Covers tenant negligence — If a tenant causes a fire or water damage, their renters insurance liability coverage can reimburse you for property damage
  • Additional insured status — You can require tenants to name you as an additional insured or interested party, giving you notice of cancellations
  • Lowers litigation risk — Tenants with insurance have a path to recovery that does not require suing their landlord
  • Demonstrates professionalism — Sets the expectation that you run your properties like a business

Benefits for Tenants

  • Personal property replacement — Covers furniture, electronics, clothing, and other belongings after theft, fire, or other covered events
  • Liability protection — Covers the tenant if they accidentally cause damage to the property or injure someone
  • Loss of use coverage — Pays for temporary housing if the unit becomes uninhabitable
  • Affordable — Average cost is $15 to $30 per month, less than most streaming subscriptions

Is It Legal to Require Renters Insurance?

In most states, landlords can legally require tenants to carry renters insurance as a lease condition. There are a few considerations:

General Rule

Landlords may require renters insurance and make it a condition of the lease, provided the requirement:

  • Is applied consistently to all tenants
  • Does not discriminate against protected classes
  • Specifies reasonable minimum coverage levels
  • Is clearly stated in the lease agreement

State-Specific Exceptions

A small number of jurisdictions have limitations:

  • Some local regulations may restrict renters insurance requirements in subsidized housing
  • Some states require landlords to provide tenants with information about insurance options rather than mandating coverage
  • Requirements must not conflict with source-of-income discrimination protections (e.g., cannot deny a Section 8 voucher holder solely because they cannot obtain renters insurance)

Fair Housing Considerations

Apply renters insurance requirements uniformly. Do not:

  • Require higher coverage for certain tenants based on protected characteristics
  • Use renters insurance requirements as a pretext for housing discrimination
  • Refuse reasonable accommodations related to insurance requirements for tenants with disabilities

Setting Coverage Requirements

Recommended Minimums

Coverage Type Recommended Minimum Purpose
Personal property $15,000 - $30,000 Replaces tenant belongings
Liability $100,000 Covers tenant-caused damage and injuries
Loss of use 20% of personal property Pays for temporary housing

Additional Requirements

  • Named as interested party — Requires the insurer to notify you if the policy is cancelled, lapsed, or modified
  • Additional insured — Gives you coverage under the tenant's policy for claims arising from the tenant's negligence (not all insurers offer this for renters policies)
  • No deductible requirements — Let tenants choose their own deductible level

What Not to Require

  • Excessively high coverage amounts that would be burdensome
  • Specific insurance companies (let tenants choose their provider)
  • Coverage for items that are the landlord's responsibility (building structure, landlord fixtures)

Lease Language

Include clear, specific language in your lease agreement (refer to the lease agreement template guide for sample provisions):

Required Elements

  1. Coverage requirement — Specify that tenants must maintain renters insurance throughout the lease term
  2. Minimum coverage amounts — List the minimum personal property, liability, and loss of use coverage
  3. Proof of insurance — Require a certificate of insurance or declarations page before move-in
  4. Interested party designation — Require tenants to name you as an interested party
  5. Renewal verification — State that tenants must provide updated proof of insurance annually or upon policy renewal
  6. Failure to maintain — Explain the consequences of lapsing coverage (lease violation, landlord-placed insurance)

Sample Lease Provision

"Tenant shall obtain and maintain renter's insurance throughout the lease term with minimum coverage of $100,000 personal liability and $20,000 personal property. Tenant shall provide Landlord with a certificate of insurance prior to move-in and upon each policy renewal. Tenant shall name Landlord as an interested party on the policy. Failure to maintain insurance is a lease violation subject to the cure provisions of this agreement."

Enforcement Strategies

At Lease Signing

  • Require proof of insurance before handing over keys
  • Verify coverage amounts meet your minimums
  • Confirm interested party designation
  • Set calendar reminders for policy renewal dates

During Tenancy

  • Verify coverage annually at lease renewal
  • Monitor for cancellation notices (received as interested party)
  • Send reminders 30 days before expected renewal dates

When Coverage Lapses

If a tenant's coverage lapses:

  1. Send a written notice of the lease violation
  2. Provide a cure period (typically 10-14 days) to reinstate coverage
  3. If not cured, consider landlord-placed insurance (force-placed coverage) and pass the cost to the tenant per your lease terms
  4. Document all communications and follow your standard lease violation process

Landlord-Placed Insurance

Some landlords include a provision allowing them to purchase a renters insurance policy on the tenant's behalf if the tenant fails to maintain coverage, with the premium added to the tenant's rent. This should be clearly stated in the lease and limited to the minimum required coverage.

Making It Easy for Tenants

Provide Insurance Options

While you cannot require a specific insurer, you can provide a list of options:

  • Major carriers (State Farm, Allstate, USAA, Lemonade)
  • Online platforms that offer instant quotes
  • Local insurance agents who offer competitive rates

Emphasize Affordability

Many tenants resist renters insurance because they assume it is expensive. Address this proactively:

  • Share the average monthly cost ($15-$30)
  • Note that many policies are cheaper than a single streaming service
  • Explain the financial protection against theft, fire, and liability

Integrate With Move-In Process

Make renters insurance a standard step in your move-in checklist, alongside the lease signing, deposit payment, and key handover. When it is part of the normal process, tenants are less likely to push back.

Key Takeaways

Requiring renters insurance is legal in most jurisdictions, affordable for tenants, and highly beneficial for landlords. Set reasonable minimum coverage requirements, include clear lease language, verify coverage at move-in and renewal, and enforce lapses through your standard lease violation process. The CFPB housing resources provide additional consumer-focused guidance on insurance. The modest effort to implement and maintain a renters insurance requirement significantly reduces your exposure to tenant-caused losses and litigation. For protection beyond individual policies, consider adding umbrella insurance coverage to your program.
