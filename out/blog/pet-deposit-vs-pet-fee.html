Pet Deposit vs Pet Fee: What Landlords Should Charge

Allowing pets in your rental property can expand your tenant pool significantly — approximately 70% of U.S. renters have pets, yet many struggle to find pet-friendly housing. But pets also carry real risk: odors, scratched floors, chewed trim, and carpet damage are common complaints from landlords who allowed pets without adequate financial protection.

The solution is a well-structured pet policy that uses the right financial instruments — pet deposits, pet fees, or pet rent — to offset your risk. Understanding the difference between these tools and how to deploy them correctly is essential for any landlord considering a pet-friendly policy.

The Key Distinction: Refundable vs Non-Refundable

The fundamental difference between a pet deposit and a pet fee comes down to refundability.

  • Pet deposit — A refundable amount held in trust, like a security deposit, to be returned to the tenant after move-out minus any pet-related damages
  • Pet fee — A non-refundable, one-time payment made at the start of the tenancy as compensation for allowing the pet
  • Pet rent — A recurring monthly charge added to the base rent for each pet

Each serves a different purpose, and many landlords use a combination of all three.

Pet Deposit: How It Works

A pet deposit is held alongside the security deposit and is used to cover pet-specific damage at move-out — things like:

  • Carpet replacement or deep cleaning from pet odors
  • Floor scratches or gouging
  • Damaged baseboards or trim from chewing
  • Yard damage from digging

How Much to Charge

Pet deposits typically range from $200 to $500 per pet. Some landlords charge a percentage of the monthly rent (commonly 25%–50% of one month's rent) per pet.

State-by-State Limits

Many states cap the total amount a landlord can collect in deposits — and in most states, a pet deposit counts toward that cap. Common caps include:

  • California — Total deposits (including pet deposit) cannot exceed 2 months' rent for unfurnished units (1 month for furnished)
  • New York — Maximum of 1 month's rent total for security deposits
  • Massachusetts — Maximum of 1 month's rent as security deposit
  • Virginia — Maximum of 2 months' rent

If your state has a deposit cap, adding a pet deposit may require you to reduce your standard security deposit to stay within the limit. Always check your state's specific rules through a resource like Nolo's landlord-tenant guide.

Important: Separate the Pet Deposit in Your Lease

Clearly designate in the lease that a specific portion of the total deposit is designated as the pet deposit. This makes deduction accounting at move-out cleaner and more defensible.

Pet Fee: The Non-Refundable Option

A pet fee is a one-time, non-refundable charge collected at the start of the tenancy. Unlike a deposit, you do not have to account for it at move-out — the tenant pays it and it becomes your income immediately.

Advantages of a Pet Fee

  • Immediate income — no hold period or return obligation
  • No itemized deduction requirement at move-out for the fee itself
  • Simpler accounting and administration

Disadvantages of a Pet Fee

  • Does not create a reserve for actual damage costs
  • If pet damage at move-out exceeds the security deposit, you have less cushion
  • Some states restrict or prohibit non-refundable fees

State Restrictions on Pet Fees

Not all states allow non-refundable pet fees. Notable restrictions:

  • California — Non-refundable fees of any kind (including pet fees) are generally not permitted. All deposits must be refundable.
  • Wisconsin — Non-refundable fees may be limited in certain circumstances.
  • Some local ordinances — Cities like San Francisco and Los Angeles may further restrict fee structures.

Before charging a non-refundable pet fee, verify that your state allows it. Charging a prohibited non-refundable fee can expose you to liability for the amount of the fee plus penalties.

Pet Rent: Monthly Recurring Income

Pet rent is a monthly surcharge added to the base rent for each pet — typically $25 to $100 per pet per month. This is distinct from both a deposit and a fee.

Why Pet Rent Makes Sense

  • Provides ongoing income to offset ongoing pet-related wear
  • Adjusts the unit's effective rent upward without changing the base rent
  • Legal in virtually all states — it is simply part of the rent
  • Works well in combination with a refundable pet deposit

Structuring Pet Rent in Your Lease

Make sure the lease clearly states:

  • The monthly pet rent amount per pet
  • Whether it applies to all pet types (cats, dogs, small animals) or just certain types
  • That it is part of total rent for purposes of late fees and non-payment

Combining the Three: A Sample Pet Policy

Many experienced landlords use all three tools together:

Component Amount Purpose
Refundable pet deposit $300 per pet Reserve for pet-specific damage
Non-refundable pet fee $150 per pet (where permitted) Compensation for allowing the pet
Monthly pet rent $50 per pet/month Ongoing offset for wear and tear

This structure acknowledges that pets cause both upfront and ongoing incremental wear, while ensuring you have a financial reserve for actual damage.

Assistance Animals: Critical Exception

The most important thing to understand about pet policies is that they do not apply to assistance animals. Under the Fair Housing Act and, for housing receiving federal assistance, Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act, landlords must:

  • Allow tenants with disabilities to keep emotional support animals (ESAs) and service animals
  • Waive pet deposits, fees, and pet rent for assistance animals
  • Not require the tenant to pay any additional fee related to the assistance animal

Refusing to make a reasonable accommodation for an assistance animal — or charging pet fees for one — is a federal fair housing violation subject to significant penalties.

What Documentation Can You Request?

For a service animal (trained to perform a specific task), you generally cannot request documentation. For an emotional support animal, you may request documentation from a healthcare provider verifying the tenant's disability-related need — but only if the disability is not obvious or already known to you. You may not require specific forms or particular healthcare providers.

How to Structure Your Pet Policy in the Lease

A well-written pet policy should address:

  1. Approved pets — Species, breed (avoid breed restrictions in jurisdictions that prohibit them), size limits, and maximum number of pets
  2. Financial terms — Pet deposit amount, pet fee (if permitted), monthly pet rent per pet
  3. Tenant responsibilities — Picking up waste, preventing nuisance complaints, leash requirements in common areas
  4. Damage responsibility — Explicit statement that tenant is responsible for all pet-related damage beyond normal wear
  5. Notice of pet policy changes — How and when the policy can be revised
  6. Assistance animal exception — Statement of FHA compliance and the accommodation process

Having tenants sign a pet addendum to the lease (in addition to incorporating pet terms into the main lease) creates an additional layer of documentation.

Documenting Move-In and Move-Out for Pet Tenants

Detailed documentation is especially important when pets are involved:

  • At move-in, photograph all floor surfaces, baseboards, doors, and any areas where pet damage commonly occurs
  • Note the condition of the yard or outdoor areas if applicable
  • At move-out, photograph the same areas and compare to move-in photos
  • Get contractor or cleaning company quotes for any pet-specific remediation needed before deducting from the deposit

Because pet-related damage (odor in particular) can be difficult to prove through photos alone, consider having a professional cleaner provide a written assessment of cleaning requirements at move-out.

Pet Deposit Accounting at Move-Out

When a tenant with a pet moves out:

  1. Inspect the unit thoroughly and identify any pet-specific damage
  2. Obtain quotes or invoices for pet-related cleaning and repairs
  3. Deduct documented pet-related costs from the pet deposit first, then from the general security deposit if needed
  4. Return the remaining balance within your state's required timeframe
  5. Provide an itemized statement clearly designating which costs are pet-related

Keeping the pet deposit separate in your accounting (even if held in the same trust account) makes this process cleaner.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I refuse to allow pets at all?

Yes — landlords may generally prohibit pets as a matter of policy, with the exception that they must accommodate assistance animals under the FHA regardless of a no-pets policy.

Should I charge more for large dogs vs small dogs?

Many landlords do charge a higher pet deposit for larger dogs due to the greater damage potential. This is legally permissible in most states, though you should be consistent in applying the policy. Review security deposit laws for your state's deposit caps before setting amounts.

What if a tenant brings in a pet without telling me?

An unauthorized pet is a lease violation. You may serve a cure-or-quit notice requiring the tenant to remove the pet or pay the applicable pet deposit and pet rent. In some states, an unauthorized pet after proper notice is grounds for eviction.

Can I require pet insurance?

Some landlords require tenants to carry renter's insurance (which covers pet liability in many policies) as a lease condition. This is permissible in most jurisdictions. For guidance on structuring that requirement, see pet rent and fees.

Conclusion

A thoughtful pet policy that combines a refundable pet deposit (where permitted under deposit caps), a non-refundable pet fee (where state law allows), and monthly pet rent gives you the best financial protection while remaining attractive to the large pool of pet-owning renters. The key is to comply with your state's deposit limits, never charge pet fees for assistance animals, and document the unit's condition thoroughly at both move-in and move-out.
