Not every renovation is worth your money. Some upgrades pay for themselves in higher rent and faster leasing within a year.

Others take a decade to break even, or never do at all. Knowing the best renovations for rental properties separates profitable landlords from those who over-improve and under-earn.

The key question is simple: will this renovation generate enough additional rent or property value to justify the cost? This guide ranks the top rental property renovations by ROI, gives you realistic cost estimates, and tells you what to avoid.

For a broader look at revenue optimization, see our pillar guide on how to maximize rental income.

Every dollar you spend on a renovation should serve at least one of these goals:

  • Increase monthly rent
  • Reduce vacancy time
  • Lower maintenance costs
  • Increase property value

If a renovation does not do any of these, skip it. Use your rental property ROI calculator to run the numbers before you start any project.

1. Fresh Paint: The Highest ROI Renovation

Cost: $200 to $500 per room (DIY) or $500 to $1,200 per room (professional) Rent increase: $25 to $75 per month ROI payback: 1 to 4 months

Did You Know?

Real estate has created more millionaires than any other investment type. About 90% of millionaires have invested in real estate at some point.

Fresh paint is the single best renovation for rental properties in terms of cost versus impact. Nothing transforms a space faster or cheaper.

What Works for Rentals

  • Stick to neutral colors: Greige (gray-beige), soft white, or light gray appeal to the widest range of tenants. Avoid bold colors that limit appeal.
  • Use durable paint: Choose semi-gloss or satin finish for walls. These are easier to clean and more resistant to scuffs than flat paint. Use semi-gloss for trim and doors.
  • Buy quality paint: Premium brands like Benjamin Moore or Sherwin-Williams cover better and last longer than budget options. The extra $10 to $15 per gallon saves you from repainting sooner.

Pro Tip

Pick one color for every unit in your portfolio. This simplifies touch-ups between tenants, reduces waste from leftover paint cans, and lets you buy in bulk at a discount.

2. Flooring Upgrades: Durability Meets Appeal

Cost: $3 to $8 per square foot (luxury vinyl plank) or $6 to $12 per square foot (engineered hardwood) Rent increase: $50 to $150 per month ROI payback: 6 to 18 months

Old, stained carpet is one of the top reasons tenants pass on a unit. Modern flooring changes the entire feel of a space and reduces long-term maintenance costs.

Best Flooring Options for Rentals

  • Luxury Vinyl Plank (LVP): The clear winner for rental properties. It looks like hardwood, is waterproof, scratch-resistant, and easy to install. A quality LVP floor lasts 15 to 20 years in a rental. Cost: $3 to $7 per square foot installed.
  • Engineered hardwood: A step up in perceived quality. Good for premium rentals where you charge above-market rent. Less durable than LVP but more attractive to high-end tenants. Cost: $6 to $12 per square foot installed.
  • Tile: Best for bathrooms, kitchens, and entryways. Extremely durable. Cost: $5 to $10 per square foot installed.

What to Avoid

  • Carpet: It stains, wears out fast in rentals (3 to 5 year lifespan), traps odors, and costs money to replace between tenants. The only exception is bedrooms in colder climates, where tenants expect carpet for warmth.
  • Solid hardwood: Too expensive and too easily damaged by tenants. Scratches, water damage, and dents are costly to repair.
  • Laminate: Looks cheap, is not waterproof, and does not hold up in rentals. LVP is the better choice at a similar price point.

3. Kitchen Updates: The Rent Multiplier

Cost: $3,000 to $8,000 (cosmetic update) or $15,000 to $30,000 (full remodel) Rent increase: $75 to $200 per month ROI payback: 12 to 36 months

The kitchen is the room tenants care about most. But a full kitchen remodel is rarely the right move for a rental.

Cosmetic updates deliver most of the impact at a fraction of the cost.

High-ROI Kitchen Updates

  • Paint or replace cabinet fronts: New cabinet doors or a coat of paint on existing cabinets costs $500 to $2,000 and changes the whole look. White or gray shaker-style doors are universally appealing.
  • New hardware: Replacing pulls and knobs costs $50 to $200 and takes an hour. Go with brushed nickel or matte black for a modern look.
  • Countertop upgrade: Swap old laminate for butcher block ($20 to $40 per linear foot) or quartz ($50 to $100 per linear foot). Quartz is the premium play. Butcher block is the budget-friendly option that still looks great. Check HomeAdvisor's cost guides for current pricing in your area.
  • New faucet: A modern kitchen faucet costs $100 to $250 and installs in under an hour. Choose a pull-down style in stainless or matte black.
  • Backsplash: Peel-and-stick tile costs $3 to $8 per square foot and adds visual appeal with minimal labor.
  • Appliance upgrade: Stainless steel appliances are standard in most markets. A basic stainless package (range, dishwasher, microwave, refrigerator) runs $2,000 to $4,000. Focus on reliability over features. Look for ENERGY STAR certified models to lower utility costs and appeal to energy-conscious tenants.

What to Skip

Do not gut the kitchen unless the layout is truly dysfunctional or everything is original from the 1970s. Moving plumbing and electrical is where costs explode.

Keep the existing layout and focus on surfaces and fixtures.

4. Bathroom Updates: Small Room, Big Impact

Cost: $1,500 to $5,000 (cosmetic update) or $10,000 to $20,000 (full remodel) Rent increase: $50 to $125 per month ROI payback: 12 to 36 months

Like the kitchen, the bathroom is a deal-maker or deal-breaker for tenants. Cosmetic updates go a long way.

High-ROI Bathroom Updates

  • New vanity and faucet: A modern vanity with an integrated top costs $200 to $600. A new faucet is $75 to $200. Together they change the feel of the whole bathroom.
  • New toilet: A basic low-flow toilet costs $150 to $300 installed. If the existing toilet is stained, wobbly, or inefficient, replace it.
  • Re-grout or re-caulk: This costs almost nothing in materials but makes tile look brand new. Do this between every tenant.
  • New mirror and lighting: A framed mirror ($50 to $150) and modern vanity light ($75 to $200) add a polished look.
  • Shower upgrade: A new showerhead ($30 to $100) and shower curtain rod (switch to a curved rod for $25) are quick wins. For a bigger impact, install a prefab shower surround ($500 to $1,500 installed) over old tile.

When to Do a Full Remodel

Only remodel the bathroom fully when there are structural issues (water damage, mold, failing subfloor) or when the bathroom is a clear outlier compared to the rest of the unit. If you have renovated the kitchen and living areas but the bathroom still has pink tile from 1965, it drags down the perceived value of everything else.

5. Curb Appeal and Landscaping

Cost: $500 to $3,000 Rent increase: $25 to $75 per month ROI payback: 3 to 12 months

First impressions start outside. A property with dead grass, overgrown bushes, and a faded front door loses tenants before they walk inside.

Quick Wins

  • Fresh mulch and trimmed hedges: $100 to $300 in materials. Makes the entire property look maintained.
  • Paint the front door: A bold-colored front door (navy, black, or red) costs $30 in paint and takes 2 hours. It is the first thing tenants see.
  • New house numbers and mailbox: $50 to $100 for modern-looking replacements.
  • Outdoor lighting: Solar path lights ($30 to $75 for a set) add safety and curb appeal.
  • Pressure wash: Rent a pressure washer for $75 per day and clean the driveway, walkways, and siding. The transformation is dramatic.

Ongoing Landscaping

Budget for regular lawn care and seasonal clean-up. This costs $100 to $200 per month through a service or less if you handle it yourself.

It is a maintenance expense, not a renovation, but it directly impacts how fast you fill vacancies.

6. Smart Home Features

Cost: $200 to $800 Rent increase: $25 to $50 per month ROI payback: 4 to 16 months

Smart home features are increasingly expected by younger tenants. They also reduce your operating costs and liability.

Best Smart Home Upgrades for Rentals

  • Smart lock: $150 to $250. Eliminates the need to re-key between tenants. You can provide unique access codes and revoke them remotely. This alone can save $100 per turnover in locksmith fees.
  • Smart thermostat: $100 to $250. Tenants love the convenience. If you pay utilities, a smart thermostat can cut heating and cooling costs by 10% to 15%.
  • Video doorbell: $100 to $200. Adds security and is a selling point in listings.
  • Smart smoke and CO detectors: $50 to $100 each. Some models alert you remotely, which helps you respond to emergencies even when tenants are away.

What to Skip

Avoid expensive smart home systems that require proprietary hubs, ongoing subscriptions, or complex setup. Tenants do not want to learn a new system.

Keep it simple: lock, thermostat, doorbell. These standalone devices work with any smartphone.

7. Lighting Upgrades

Cost: $100 to $500 Rent increase: $15 to $40 per month ROI payback: 3 to 12 months

Lighting is one of the most underrated renovations for rental properties. A well-lit unit feels bigger, cleaner, and more modern.

What to Upgrade

  • Replace builder-grade fixtures: Swap out the brass boob lights (yes, that is what they are called) for simple, modern flush-mount fixtures. $20 to $60 each.
  • Add under-cabinet lighting in the kitchen: LED strip lights cost $20 to $40 and install with adhesive. They make the kitchen feel upscale.
  • Switch to LED bulbs throughout: Lower energy costs and longer bulb life. If you pay utilities, this saves money. If tenants pay, it is a selling point.
  • Install dimmer switches in living areas: $15 to $25 each. A small luxury that tenants appreciate.

8. In-Unit Laundry

Cost: $1,500 to $3,000 (washer/dryer purchase and hookup) Rent increase: $75 to $150 per month ROI payback: 10 to 24 months

In-unit laundry is one of the most searched-for features on rental listing sites. If your unit has existing hookups, adding a washer and dryer is a no-brainer.

If you need to add plumbing and electrical, the cost goes up but the rent premium often justifies it.

Options

  • Full-size washer and dryer: Best for single-family homes and larger units. Cost: $800 to $2,000 for the pair.
  • Stackable or combo unit: Works in apartments and smaller spaces. Cost: $700 to $1,500.
  • Ventless dryer: Required when you cannot add exterior venting. Slower but effective. Cost: $600 to $1,200.

In-unit laundry directly impacts your rental pricing strategy. Units with in-unit laundry consistently command higher rents than those without.

It is also a strong tenant retention tool.

9. Energy Efficiency Improvements

Cost: $500 to $5,000 Rent increase: $0 to $50 per month (savings-based ROI if you pay utilities) ROI payback: 12 to 36 months

Energy efficiency upgrades may not always increase rent, but they reduce operating costs and attract environmentally conscious tenants.

Top Efficiency Upgrades

  • Attic insulation: $1,000 to $2,500. Reduces heating and cooling costs by 10% to 20%. Payback within 2 to 3 years if you pay utilities.
  • Weather stripping and caulking: $50 to $200. Seals air leaks around windows and doors. Quick, cheap, and effective.
  • Low-flow fixtures: $10 to $30 each for showerheads and faucet aerators. Reduces water costs.
  • Energy-efficient windows: $300 to $700 per window. Long payback period, so only do this when existing windows are failing.

Track these savings in your rental property expense tracking system to confirm the ROI is real.

What Renovations to Avoid

Not every improvement pays off. These common renovations typically have poor ROI for rental properties:

Over-Customization

  • High-end finishes that tenants will damage: Marble countertops, custom tile work, and expensive fixtures get scratched, chipped, and broken in rentals. Use durable mid-range materials instead.
  • Built-in features: Custom closet systems, built-in bookshelves, and window seats cost a lot and do not significantly increase rent.

Structural Changes

  • Knocking down walls: Open floor plans are trendy, but removing walls costs $3,000 to $10,000 (more if load-bearing) and rarely increases rent enough to justify the cost.
  • Adding a room: Converting a garage or basement into a bedroom can be worth it if local codes allow it and comps support the higher rent. But often the cost ($10,000 to $30,000) does not match the return.

Pool or Hot Tub

A pool increases liability, insurance costs, and maintenance expenses. Unless you are in a market where pools are standard (parts of Florida, Arizona, Southern California), skip it.

The added rent rarely covers the added cost.

Over-Landscaping

A few thousand dollars on basic landscaping pays off. Spending $10,000 on a custom hardscape, irrigation system, and exotic plants does not.

Tenants do not pay premium rent for elaborate gardens. They want clean, maintained outdoor space.

How to Focus on Your Renovation Budget

If you have a limited budget (and most landlords do), focus on renovations in this order:

  1. Safety and code compliance: Smoke detectors, handrails, electrical issues. These are non-negotiable.
  2. Paint and clean: The cheapest way to transform a unit.
  3. Flooring: Replace worn carpet with LVP.
  4. Kitchen cosmetic updates: Cabinet paint, hardware, countertops.
  5. Bathroom cosmetic updates: Vanity, faucet, lighting.
  6. Smart home basics: Lock, thermostat, doorbell.
  7. Curb appeal: Landscaping, front door, lighting.
  8. In-unit laundry: If hookups exist or can be added affordably.

Build these renovation costs into your property maintenance cost budget so they do not catch you off guard. Always calculate the expected payback period before starting.

Use this formula:

Payback Period (months) = Renovation Cost / Monthly Rent Increase

If a $3,000 kitchen update lets you charge $100 more per month, the payback is 30 months. That is a solid return on a renovation that will last 10 or more years.

For a deeper look at how renovations affect your overall property value and investment returns, read our guides on increasing rental property value and rental yield calculation.

Understanding depreciation rules also matters here. Capital improvements can be depreciated over their useful life, which creates additional tax benefits beyond the rent increase.

Final Thoughts

The best renovations for rental properties balance cost, durability, tenant appeal, and financial return. Focus on cosmetic updates that make the unit look clean and modern.

Use durable, rental-grade materials that can handle tenant use. And always run the numbers before you spend.

Paint, flooring, kitchen updates, and bathroom refreshes consistently deliver the best ROI. Smart home features and in-unit laundry round out the upgrades that today's tenants expect.

Skip the luxury finishes and structural changes that eat your budget without matching increases in rent.

Renovate with your head, not your heart. This is an investment property, not your dream home.

Every dollar should work toward higher rent, lower vacancy, or reduced maintenance costs. If it does not, save that dollar for a renovation that will.
