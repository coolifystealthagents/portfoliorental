A burst pipe is one of the most time-sensitive emergencies a rental property owner faces. Water escaping from a ruptured supply pipe flows at the full pressure of your building's water system, up to 60-80 PSI, which means even a moderate pipe failure can release hundreds of gallons before it is stopped.

A burst pipe that goes undetected for hours can cause damage measured in tens of thousands of dollars.

The landlords who minimize burst pipe damage are those who respond in minutes, not days. This guide gives you the precise emergency response protocol, the follow-up steps, and the prevention measures that reduce your risk before a pipe ever bursts.

Why Pipes Burst

Understanding the cause helps you respond correctly and prevent recurrence:

  • Frozen pipes, The most common cause in cold climates. Water expands when it freezes and cracks the pipe; the burst typically occurs when the ice thaws, not during freezing.
  • Aging pipes, Galvanized steel and polybutylene pipes are prone to corrosion and failure; copper and PEX are more durable but can still fail at fittings
  • High water pressure, Sustained pressure above 80 PSI strains joints and fittings; a pressure reducing valve (PRV) should maintain pressure at 50-60 PSI
  • Physical damage, Improper drilling or nailing through walls, especially by tenants making repairs or hanging items
  • Root intrusion, Tree roots invading outdoor supply or drain lines can cause failure
  • Manufacturing defects, Recalled polybutylene pipe (a common issue in homes built 1978-1995) is particularly prone to failure. The CPSC tracks product recalls including plumbing components.

Immediate Emergency Response Protocol

Step 1: Shut Off Water Immediately

The moment you receive a burst pipe report (or discover one yourself):

Shut off the main water supply to the property. Every property owner should know the exact location of the main shut-off. Common locations:

  • Inside the house near where the main line enters (basement, crawl space, mechanical room)
  • In an exterior utility box near the foundation
  • At the meter box at the street (requires a meter key)

If you are not at the property, call the tenant immediately and walk them through shutting off the main supply. Every tenant should be given the location of the main shut-off at move-in, this instruction can save thousands of dollars.

After shutting off the main supply, open all faucets throughout the unit to drain remaining water from the pipes and reduce further discharge.

Step 2: Turn Off Electricity to Affected Areas

Water and electricity are a lethal combination. If water is near electrical outlets, the electrical panel, or any wiring:

  • Shut off the circuit breakers to the affected areas
  • Do not enter flooded areas with active electricity
  • If in doubt, contact an electrician before re-entering the affected space

Step 3: Call a Plumber Immediately

Do not wait until business hours. Call your emergency plumber contact, you should have 24/7 plumber contacts on file for every property before any emergency occurs.

Describe the situation including:

  • The location of the burst (which pipe, which area of the house)
  • Whether the water is shut off
  • The extent of visible flooding

Step 4: Begin Water Removal

While waiting for the plumber, begin removing standing water:

  • Use a wet/dry vacuum to extract water from hard floors
  • Place buckets or containers under active drips
  • Use mops and towels to remove water from surfaces
  • Move furniture and belongings off wet floors if it is safe to do so

Step 5: Call a Water Damage Restoration Company

For significant flooding (more than 50 square feet affected or water has penetrated walls or floors), call a professional water damage restoration service immediately. Companies like SERVPRO and ServiceMaster operate 24/7 and can begin extraction and drying within hours.

Early professional drying dramatically reduces total repair costs and mold risk.

Tenant Communication During a Burst Pipe Emergency

Immediate Contact

If the tenant discovered the burst pipe:

  • Thank them for reporting immediately
  • Walk them through shutting off the main water supply if they have not done so
  • Advise them on immediate safety precautions (electricity, slip hazards)
  • Give them an estimated timeline for your arrival or the plumber's arrival

Documentation

Ask the tenant to:

  • Leave the area as is for documentation before cleanup (if safe)
  • Photograph everything they can see
  • List any personal items that were damaged

Remind them to file a claim with their renter's insurance for their personal property, your insurance covers the building, not their belongings. FEMA offers disaster recovery resources for severe water damage.

Relocation

If the unit is uninhabitable due to the burst pipe:

  • Arrange alternative housing promptly (contact local hotels or extended-stay options)
  • Provide a rent abatement for each day the unit is not habitable
  • Communicate regularly about the repair and restoration timeline

Liability: Who Pays for Burst Pipe Damage?

Liability depends on the cause:

Landlord Liability (Most Cases)

Landlords are typically responsible when:

  • The pipe burst due to aging, deferred maintenance, or pre-existing condition
  • The landlord failed to properly winterize the property (failed to insulate exposed pipes)
  • A pipe in an unheated space burst because the building was not properly configured for cold weather

Tenant Liability

Tenants may bear responsibility when:

  • The lease required the tenant to maintain heat at a minimum temperature (typically 55°F) and the tenant failed to do so
  • The tenant disabled the heat while away for an extended period
  • The tenant caused physical damage to the pipe through misuse (drilling, hammering)

Even when the tenant is at fault, the landlord typically must arrange and fund repairs first, then pursue the tenant for reimbursement through the security deposit or civil action.

Insurance Claims for Burst Pipes

Landlord Insurance Coverage

Most landlord insurance policies cover sudden and accidental water damage from burst pipes. Coverage typically includes:

  • Repair of damaged pipes
  • Water damage to building structure (flooring, walls, ceilings, insulation)
  • Contents owned by the landlord
  • Potentially, loss of rental income during repairs (if you have rental income coverage)

What Is Not Covered

  • Gradual leaks (known for weeks without reporting or repair)
  • Damage from neglected maintenance
  • Tenant's personal property (covered by renter's insurance)
  • Flooding from external water sources (requires separate flood insurance from NFIP)

Filing the Claim

  1. Document everything with photographs and video before cleanup begins
  2. Contact your insurer as soon as possible after stabilizing the emergency
  3. Request an adjuster visit before significant demolition or repair work
  4. Retain all receipts for emergency services, temporary housing, and repairs
  5. Get multiple contractor bids for repair work

Pipe Repair and Restoration

After the plumber repairs the burst pipe and the water restoration company has completed drying:

Typical repairs after a significant burst pipe event:

  • Drywall replacement in affected walls and ceilings
  • Flooring replacement (wet carpet, padding, laminate, or hardwood)
  • Insulation replacement
  • Painting
  • Plumbing repair or replacement of affected section

Timeline: A significant burst pipe event typically requires 2-4 weeks for full restoration, depending on the extent of the damage and contractor availability.

Restoring the Pipe System

After the burst is repaired:

  • Have a plumber inspect all pipes in unheated spaces for additional vulnerability
  • Test water pressure, high pressure (above 80 PSI) accelerates future failures; install a PRV if pressure is excessive
  • Insulate any pipes in unheated spaces that were not previously insulated
  • Consider replacing any aging pipe runs identified during the repair work

Burst Pipe Prevention

The best burst pipe response is the one you never have to execute. Prevention measures:

Winterization

  • Insulate all pipes in unheated spaces (attic, crawl space, garage, exterior walls)
  • Shut off and drain exterior hose bibs before freezing temperatures
  • Install heat tape on pipes at particular risk in extreme cold
  • Verify heating systems are operational before the cold season

For a full winterization approach, see our guide on winterizing your rental property.

Water Pressure Management

  • Test water pressure at the main supply connection; should be 50-60 PSI
  • Install a pressure reducing valve (PRV) if pressure exceeds 80 PSI; cost is $200-$400 installed

Pipe Age and Material Assessment

  • Identify any remaining polybutylene pipe (gray flexible plastic piping, common in homes built 1978-1995) and plan for replacement
  • Galvanized steel pipes (found in older homes) corrode from the inside and should be replaced with copper or PEX when encountered during renovation

Water Leak Detection

Install water sensors near the highest-risk locations:

  • Water heater
  • Washing machine
  • Under kitchen and bathroom sinks
  • In the basement or crawl space

Smart water sensors connect to Wi-Fi and send immediate alerts when they detect water, giving you minutes rather than hours to respond. Some systems can automatically shut off the water supply when a leak is detected.

See our guide on water leak detection systems for landlords for options.

Tenant Heating Requirements

Include in your lease:

  • Minimum indoor temperature requirement (typically 55°F at all times, even when tenant is away)
  • Requirement to notify landlord if heat is lost
  • Responsibility allocation for damage caused by failure to maintain minimum temperature

Creating a Burst Pipe Emergency Plan

Before any emergency occurs, prepare:

  1. Document main shut-off locations for every property in a format accessible from your phone
  2. Save emergency contacts: licensed 24/7 plumber, water damage restoration company
  3. Provide tenant emergency instructions at move-in, including main shut-off location
  4. Review your insurance policy so you understand coverage, deductibles, and claim procedures

Conclusion

Burst pipe emergencies are won or lost in the first hour. Knowing your main shut-off locations, having plumber and restoration company contacts saved in your phone, and being able to guide tenants through immediate response steps before you arrive can mean the difference between a $3,000 repair and a $30,000 renovation.

Prevention through proper winterization and water sensor installation eliminates most burst pipe events before they ever occur.
