Security Deposit Alternatives for Landlords and Tenants

Traditional security deposits create a significant barrier to entry for tenants and a capital management burden for landlords. Understanding the full scope of security deposit laws in your state is essential before choosing any alternative. A one-month deposit on a $1,500 apartment means the tenant needs $3,000 upfront (first month plus deposit), and the landlord must hold that $1,500 in a regulated account for the duration of the tenancy.

Security deposit alternatives have emerged as a solution that benefits both parties. Tenants get lower move-in costs, and landlords can maintain or even improve their protection against damage and unpaid rent.

Why Alternatives Are Growing

Tenant Demand

  • Average move-in costs have become prohibitive for many renters
  • Tenants often need first month, last month, and security deposit — three months of rent upfront
  • Younger renters and those in high-cost markets are particularly affected
  • Many qualified tenants are rejected or deterred by high upfront costs. The CFPB highlights how high move-in costs affect housing access

Landlord Benefits

  • Larger applicant pool when move-in costs are lower
  • Faster lease-up with reduced financial barriers
  • Elimination of deposit management compliance (escrow accounts, interest calculations, state regulations)
  • Potential for better coverage than a traditional deposit in some alternatives

Regulatory Push

Several cities and states have enacted or proposed legislation encouraging or requiring deposit alternatives:

  • Cincinnati, Atlanta, and other cities have passed deposit alternative ordinances
  • Some states have capped security deposit amounts (prompting alternative solutions)
  • Legislative trends favor expanding alternatives

Security Deposit Alternative Options

1. Surety Bonds

How it works: The tenant purchases a surety bond from a third-party provider (like Rhino or TheGuarantor) instead of paying a traditional deposit. If the tenant causes damage or owes unpaid rent, the surety company pays the landlord and then seeks reimbursement from the tenant.

Cost to tenant: Typically 1-2% of the deposit amount per month (a $1,500 deposit alternative costs $15-$30/month)

Pros:

  • Dramatically reduces move-in costs for tenants
  • Landlord receives claims coverage (often higher than a traditional deposit)
  • No escrow management required
  • Claims process handled by the surety company

Cons:

  • Monthly cost to tenants adds up over long tenancies
  • Tenant does not build equity (the money is gone)
  • Claims process may be slower than deducting from a held deposit
  • Not all surety companies are equally responsive

2. Deposit Insurance

How it works: Similar to surety bonds, deposit insurance programs (like Jetty or LeaseLock) provide coverage to the landlord. LeaseLock covers the landlord directly, often as a landlord-paid model where the cost is built into rent.

Cost: Varies by provider — tenant-paid or landlord-paid models available

Pros:

  • Can provide higher coverage limits than traditional deposits
  • Streamlines move-in process
  • Some providers guarantee payment regardless of tenant disputes
  • Eliminates deposit return disputes

Cons:

  • Monthly cost to tenants or landlords
  • Coverage terms and exclusions vary by provider
  • Relatively new products with limited track record
  • May not be accepted by all state deposit laws

3. Installment Payment Plans

How it works: Instead of paying the full deposit upfront, tenants pay the deposit in installments over several months. Some states now require landlords to offer this option.

Cost to tenant: The full deposit amount, spread over time

Pros:

  • Reduces immediate move-in burden
  • Tenant still builds deposit equity
  • Straightforward to implement
  • No third-party provider needed

Cons:

  • Landlord has reduced protection during the installment period
  • Administrative tracking of installment payments
  • Risk of tenant default on installments
  • Full deposit may not be collected if tenant leaves early

4. Lease Guarantors / Co-Signers

How it works: A third party guarantees the tenant's lease obligations. This can be a family member (traditional co-signer) or a commercial guarantor (like Insurent or TheGuarantor).

Cost to tenant: Commercial guarantors typically charge 60-110% of one month's rent as a one-time fee

Pros:

  • Landlord has additional recourse beyond the tenant's assets
  • Enables tenants with thin credit or income history to qualify
  • Commercial guarantors have deep pockets for claims

Cons:

  • Does not address the deposit itself (often used alongside deposits, not instead of)
  • Commercial guarantor fees are substantial
  • Family co-signers may be difficult to collect from
  • Adds complexity to lease enforcement

5. Last Month's Rent as Deposit

How it works: Instead of a separate security deposit, the tenant pays last month's rent upfront, which the landlord applies to the final month of tenancy.

Cost to tenant: Same as a traditional deposit, but pre-paid rent rather than a refundable deposit

Pros:

  • Simplifies move-out — no deposit return process
  • Reduces risk of tenant withholding final rent payment
  • Clear purpose for the funds

Cons:

  • Provides no coverage for damage (only covers rent) — see last month's rent explained for important distinctions
  • Some states restrict how last month's rent can be used
  • Does not solve the high move-in cost problem

6. No-Deposit Model With Higher Rent

How it works: Eliminate the deposit entirely and increase monthly rent to cover the additional risk.

Pros:

  • Simplest alternative — no deposit management at all
  • Lower move-in costs attract more applicants
  • Higher rent generates more revenue over time

Cons:

  • No dedicated fund for damage claims
  • Higher rent may reduce competitiveness
  • Difficult to recover large damage amounts from tenants without a deposit

Choosing the Right Alternative

For Large Portfolios

Surety bond or deposit insurance programs integrate well with property management software and scale across many units. Negotiate volume pricing with providers.

For High-Cost Markets

Installment plans or surety bonds help tenants in expensive markets where deposits are prohibitively high. This expands your applicant pool significantly.

For Premium Properties

Lease guarantors provide strong protection for high-end units where potential damage costs are significant.

For State-Mandated Alternatives

Some jurisdictions require landlords to offer at least one alternative. Know your local requirements and choose the option that best balances protection and simplicity.

Legal Considerations

  • Verify that your chosen alternative complies with your state's security deposit laws
  • Some states define specific alternative programs that qualify
  • Ensure your lease clearly documents the alternative arrangement
  • Maintain the same documentation standards for damage as you would with a traditional deposit — the security deposit deductions guide outlines what is permissible
  • Review your insurance coverage — some alternatives may affect your landlord insurance claims process. The FTC consumer finance resources cover regulations affecting deposit alternative products

For state-specific deposit rules, see our security deposit laws guide.

Key Takeaways

Security deposit alternatives can reduce move-in barriers for tenants while maintaining or improving your protection as a landlord. Evaluate each option based on your portfolio size, market, and risk tolerance. Surety bonds and deposit insurance are the fastest-growing alternatives, while installment plans and guarantors serve specific needs. Whichever approach you choose, maintain thorough documentation of property condition at move-in and move-out — that remains your best protection regardless of the deposit structure.
