32:T48c8,<p>Great photos are the number one thing that gets renters to click on your listing. Studies show that listings with high-quality photos get two to three times more views than those with dark, blurry, or poorly framed images. Yet many landlords rush through photos or skip them altogether.</p>
<p>You do not need expensive equipment or professional training to take great rental property photos. With the right techniques and a little preparation, you can create listing photos that attract quality tenants and help you fill vacancies faster.</p>
<p>This guide covers everything you need to know about rental property photography, from equipment and lighting to staging and editing.</p>
<h2 id="why-great-photos-matter">Why Great Photos Matter</h2>
<p>Before we get into the how-to, let us talk about why photos deserve your attention.</p>
<p>Renters scroll through dozens of listings at a time. They spend about two seconds on each listing before deciding to click or keep scrolling. Your photos are what make them stop.</p>
<p>Great listing photos help you:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Get more clicks</strong> on your online listing</li>
<li><strong>Attract serious renters</strong> who already like what they see</li>
<li><strong>Reduce wasted showings</strong> with people who would not like the property in person</li>
<li><strong>Justify your asking rent</strong> by showing the property at its best</li>
<li><strong>Fill vacancies faster</strong> by creating demand</li>
</ul>
<p>These photos become part of your broader <a href="/blog/rental-property-marketing-strategies">rental property marketing strategy</a> and work alongside your listing description to sell the property.</p>
<h2 id="phone-vs-dslr-which-should-you-use">Phone vs. DSLR: Which Should You Use?</h2>
<p>The first question most landlords ask is whether they need a fancy camera. Here is the honest answer: modern smartphones take excellent photos for rental listings.</p>
<p><strong>Use your smartphone if:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>You are on a tight budget</li>
<li>You manage just a few properties</li>
<li>You are comfortable with basic photo editing</li>
<li>Your phone is from the last three to four years</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Consider a DSLR or mirrorless camera if:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>You manage many properties and take photos regularly</li>
<li>You want the absolute best quality</li>
<li>You are willing to learn manual camera settings</li>
<li>Your properties are high-end and compete with professional listings</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Smartphone tips for better photos:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Use the wide-angle lens (usually the default on newer phones)</li>
<li>Turn on the grid overlay to keep your lines straight</li>
<li>Tap the screen to set focus, then adjust brightness</li>
<li>Use HDR mode for rooms with bright windows and dark corners</li>
<li>Hold the phone horizontally for every shot (landscape orientation)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>If you go with a DSLR:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Use a wide-angle lens (10-22mm on crop sensor or 16-35mm on full frame)</li>
<li>Shoot in aperture priority mode at f/8 to f/11 for sharp photos</li>
<li>Use a tripod for steady, consistent shots</li>
<li>Shoot in RAW format for better editing flexibility</li>
</ul>
<p>Either way, the techniques below will dramatically improve your results.</p>
<h2 id="lighting-techniques-that-make-rooms-shine">Lighting Techniques That Make Rooms Shine</h2>
<p>Lighting is the single biggest factor in photo quality. Here is how to get it right every time.</p>
<h3 id="use-natural-light">Use Natural Light</h3>
<p>Natural light makes rooms look warm, inviting, and spacious. Follow these rules:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Shoot during the day</strong> between 10 AM and 2 PM when the sun is high</li>
<li><strong>Open all blinds and curtains</strong> to let in as much light as possible</li>
<li><strong>Turn on every light in the property</strong> including lamps, overhead lights, and under-cabinet lighting</li>
<li><strong>Open interior doors</strong> to let light flow between rooms</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="avoid-common-lighting-mistakes">Avoid Common Lighting Mistakes</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Never use flash.</strong> On-camera flash creates harsh shadows and makes rooms look cold and flat.</li>
<li><strong>Watch out for mixed lighting.</strong> If a room has warm lamps and cool overhead lights, it can look odd in photos. Stick with one type when possible.</li>
<li><strong>Be careful with windows.</strong> Bright windows can make the rest of the room look dark. Use HDR mode to balance the exposure.</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="cloudy-days-are-your-friend">Cloudy Days Are Your Friend</h3>
<p>Overcast skies act like a giant softbox, spreading light evenly without harsh shadows. Many professional photographers prefer cloudy days for interior shots. If you can, schedule your photo session on a lightly overcast day.</p>
<h2 id="staging-your-property-for-photos">Staging Your Property for Photos</h2>
<p>Staging means arranging the property to look its best in photos. You do not need to hire a professional stager or buy expensive furniture. Simple staging goes a long way.</p>
<h3 id="the-basics-of-staging">The Basics of Staging</h3>
<ol>
<li><strong>Deep clean everything.</strong> Clean floors, counters, windows, and fixtures. A dirty property photographs poorly no matter what else you do.</li>
<li><strong>Declutter every surface.</strong> Remove personal items, excess furniture, and anything that makes rooms look small or messy.</li>
<li><strong>Make beds with fresh linens.</strong> A neatly made bed with matching pillows makes a bedroom look like a hotel.</li>
<li><strong>Add small touches.</strong> A bowl of fruit on the kitchen counter, fresh towels in the bathroom, or a plant on the coffee table adds warmth.</li>
<li><strong>Hide cords and cables.</strong> Loose wires make rooms look messy. Tuck them behind furniture.</li>
<li><strong>Remove trash cans, toilet brushes, and cleaning supplies.</strong> These everyday items look terrible in photos.</li>
</ol>
<h3 id="staging-an-empty-property">Staging an Empty Property</h3>
<p>Empty rooms are harder to photograph because they lack scale and feel cold. Here are some ways to handle empty units:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Add a few key pieces.</strong> A simple dining table with chairs, a bed with bedding, or a couch with throw pillows can transform an empty room.</li>
<li><strong>Use virtual staging.</strong> Software can add furniture to photos of empty rooms. This is cheaper than physical staging and looks great in listings. We will cover this more below.</li>
<li><strong>Shoot from corners.</strong> Empty rooms look bigger when photographed from a corner, showing two walls and the floor.</li>
</ul>
<p>For more detailed staging advice, see our guide on <a href="/blog/rental-property-staging-tips">rental property staging tips</a>.</p>
<h2 id="room-by-room-photography-guide">Room-by-Room Photography Guide</h2>
<p>Every room has its own challenges. Here is how to photograph each one for the best results.</p>
<h3 id="living-room">Living Room</h3>
<ul>
<li>Shoot from the corner that shows the most space</li>
<li>Include windows to show natural light</li>
<li>If furnished, arrange seating to face the camera</li>
<li>Show the view from the living room into adjacent rooms to emphasize flow</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="kitchen">Kitchen</h3>
<ul>
<li>Clean the counters completely. No dishes, no appliances, no clutter</li>
<li>Wipe down all appliances so they do not show fingerprints</li>
<li>Turn on under-cabinet lighting if available</li>
<li>Shoot from the doorway looking in and from the corners</li>
<li>Open one or two upper cabinets to show storage space</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="bedrooms">Bedrooms</h3>
<ul>
<li>Make the bed with clean, neutral bedding</li>
<li>Remove personal photos and clutter from nightstands</li>
<li>Open closet doors to show closet size (make sure the closet is organized)</li>
<li>Shoot from the doorway and from the corner opposite the bed</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="bathrooms">Bathrooms</h3>
<ul>
<li>Close the toilet lid</li>
<li>Hang fresh, matching towels</li>
<li>Remove all personal products from the counter and shower</li>
<li>Clean the mirror until it sparkles (dirty mirrors are very obvious in photos)</li>
<li>Shoot from the doorway since bathrooms are small and hard to photograph from inside</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="laundry-area">Laundry Area</h3>
<ul>
<li>Close washer and dryer doors</li>
<li>Remove detergent bottles and laundry baskets</li>
<li>Wipe down surfaces</li>
<li>Show shelving or storage if available</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="exterior-shots">Exterior Shots</h2>
<p>Exterior photos are just as important as interior ones. The outside of your property is the first thing renters see, and it sets expectations for what is inside.</p>
<h3 id="front-of-the-property">Front of the Property</h3>
<ul>
<li>Shoot on a clear day with blue sky if possible</li>
<li>Stand across the street to capture the full facade</li>
<li>Mow the lawn, trim bushes, and clean up the yard before shooting</li>
<li>Remove cars from the driveway and trash cans from the curb</li>
<li>Shoot at a slight angle rather than straight on for a more dynamic look</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="backyard-and-outdoor-spaces">Backyard and Outdoor Spaces</h3>
<ul>
<li>Photograph patios, decks, pools, and outdoor seating areas</li>
<li>Stage outdoor furniture with cushions and a few accessories</li>
<li>Clean the grill and outdoor kitchen if applicable</li>
<li>Show fencing for renters with pets or children</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="parking-and-common-areas">Parking and Common Areas</h3>
<ul>
<li>Include photos of the garage, parking spaces, or carport</li>
<li>Show common areas like lobbies, gyms, pools, and laundry rooms</li>
<li>Photograph mailboxes, storage units, and other amenities</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="editing-your-photos">Editing Your Photos</h2>
<p>Even great photos benefit from a little editing. You do not need to be a Photoshop expert. Simple adjustments make a big difference.</p>
<h3 id="free-and-easy-editing-tools">Free and Easy Editing Tools</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Google Photos</strong> has built-in editing with auto-adjust</li>
<li><strong>Snapseed</strong> is a free mobile app with powerful editing tools</li>
<li><strong>Lightroom Mobile</strong> offers professional-level editing for free</li>
<li><strong>Canva</strong> lets you add text overlays and create collages</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="basic-edits-to-make-on-every-photo">Basic Edits to Make on Every Photo</h3>
<ol>
<li><strong>Straighten the image.</strong> Make sure walls and door frames are perfectly vertical.</li>
<li><strong>Adjust brightness.</strong> Increase exposure slightly if the photo looks dark.</li>
<li><strong>Increase contrast.</strong> A small contrast boost makes the image pop.</li>
<li><strong>Correct white balance.</strong> Make sure whites look white, not yellow or blue.</li>
<li><strong>Crop for composition.</strong> Remove distracting edges and center the subject.</li>
</ol>
<h3 id="edits-to-avoid">Edits to Avoid</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Do not over-edit.</strong> Heavy filters make photos look fake and turn renters off.</li>
<li><strong>Do not misrepresent the property.</strong> Editing should enhance, not deceive. If you make a small room look huge through editing tricks, renters will be disappointed in person.</li>
<li><strong>Do not use black and white or sepia.</strong> Renters want to see accurate colors.</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="virtual-staging">Virtual Staging</h2>
<p>Virtual staging uses software to add furniture and decor to photos of empty rooms. It is a cost-effective way to help renters visualize the space.</p>
<p><strong>Benefits of virtual staging:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Costs 25 to 75 dollars per photo versus 1,000+ dollars for physical staging</li>
<li>Quick turnaround, often within 24 to 48 hours</li>
<li>Multiple style options for different target demographics</li>
<li>Easy to update or change</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Tips for virtual staging:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Use realistic furniture that fits the room proportions</li>
<li>Match the style to your target renter (modern for young professionals, traditional for families)</li>
<li>Label virtually staged photos clearly in your listing to avoid misleading renters</li>
<li>Use it for the main living areas and bedrooms but not for kitchens and bathrooms</li>
</ul>
<p>Popular virtual staging services include BoxBrownie, VirtualStagingSolutions, and Stuccco. For an even more immersive experience, consider adding <a href="/blog/virtual-tours-rental-property">virtual tours to your rental property</a> listings alongside your photos.</p>
<h2 id="drone-photography">Drone Photography</h2>
<p>Drone photos give a unique perspective that sets your listing apart. They are especially useful for:</p>
<ul>
<li>Properties with large lots or attractive surroundings</li>
<li>Multi-family buildings where you want to show the full property</li>
<li>Homes near parks, water, or scenic views</li>
<li>Properties where the neighborhood is a major selling point</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Important notes on drone photography:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>You need an FAA Part 107 license to fly drones commercially in the US</li>
<li>Hiring a licensed drone photographer costs 100 to 300 dollars</li>
<li>Check local regulations since some areas restrict drone flights</li>
<li>Do not fly over neighboring properties without permission</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="photo-order-in-your-listing">Photo Order in Your Listing</h2>
<p>The order you place photos in your listing matters more than you might think. Renters often stop scrolling after the first five to seven photos. Put your best content first.</p>
<p><strong>Recommended photo order:</strong></p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Hero shot</strong> - The best exterior photo of the property</li>
<li><strong>Living room</strong> - The main gathering space</li>
<li><strong>Kitchen</strong> - Often the most important room for renters</li>
<li><strong>Primary bedroom</strong> - Shows the main sleeping space</li>
<li><strong>Primary bathroom</strong> - Especially if it has been updated</li>
<li><strong>Additional bedrooms</strong> - One photo each</li>
<li><strong>Additional bathrooms</strong> - One photo each</li>
<li><strong>Special features</strong> - Laundry, garage, office, bonus rooms</li>
<li><strong>Outdoor spaces</strong> - Backyard, patio, pool</li>
<li><strong>Neighborhood</strong> - Parks, shops, views nearby</li>
</ol>
<h2 id="how-many-photos-to-include">How Many Photos to Include</h2>
<p>More photos generally means more interest, but there is a point of diminishing returns.</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Minimum:</strong> 10 photos for a studio or one-bedroom</li>
<li><strong>Ideal:</strong> 15 to 25 photos for a two- to three-bedroom</li>
<li><strong>Maximum:</strong> 30 to 35 photos for a large home</li>
</ul>
<p>Do not include multiple photos of the same angle or room unless they show something different. Every photo should add new information for the renter.</p>
<h2 id="creating-a-shot-list">Creating a Shot List</h2>
<p>Before you start shooting, make a list of every photo you need. This keeps you organized and ensures you do not miss anything.</p>
<p><strong>Sample shot list for a two-bedroom apartment:</strong></p>
<ul class="contains-task-list">
<li class="task-list-item"><input type="checkbox" disabled> Building exterior (2 angles)</li>
<li class="task-list-item"><input type="checkbox" disabled> Front entrance or lobby</li>
<li class="task-list-item"><input type="checkbox" disabled> Living room (2 to 3 angles)</li>
<li class="task-list-item"><input type="checkbox" disabled> Kitchen (2 to 3 angles)</li>
<li class="task-list-item"><input type="checkbox" disabled> Dining area</li>
<li class="task-list-item"><input type="checkbox" disabled> Primary bedroom (2 angles)</li>
<li class="task-list-item"><input type="checkbox" disabled> Primary bedroom closet</li>
<li class="task-list-item"><input type="checkbox" disabled> Second bedroom (2 angles)</li>
<li class="task-list-item"><input type="checkbox" disabled> Primary bathroom</li>
<li class="task-list-item"><input type="checkbox" disabled> Second bathroom</li>
<li class="task-list-item"><input type="checkbox" disabled> Laundry area</li>

</ul>
<p>Walk through the property with this list and check off each shot as you take it.</p>
<h2 id="common-photography-mistakes">Common Photography Mistakes</h2>
<p>Avoid these errors that make otherwise good properties look bad:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Shooting in portrait mode.</strong> Always hold your camera or phone horizontally.</li>
<li><strong>Including yourself in reflections.</strong> Watch for mirrors, windows, and shiny surfaces.</li>
<li><strong>Leaving toilet seats up.</strong> Always close the lid before photographing a bathroom.</li>
<li><strong>Forgetting to remove personal items.</strong> Family photos, medications, and valuables should be put away.</li>
<li><strong>Shooting at night.</strong> Even with lights on, nighttime photos look dark and uninviting.</li>
<li><strong>Tilting the camera up or down.</strong> Keep it level at about chest height for natural-looking perspectives.</li>
</ol>
<h2 id="putting-it-all-together">Putting It All Together</h2>
<p>Great rental property photography is a skill you can learn and improve over time. Start with these basics: clean the property, use natural light, shoot from corners, and make simple edits. As you get more comfortable, you can add techniques like virtual staging and drone photography.</p>
<p>Your photos work together with your <a href="/blog/writing-rental-listing-descriptions">listing description</a> to create a complete picture of your property. When both are done well, you attract better tenants and fill vacancies faster.</p>
<p>Take the time to do your photos right. The effort pays off in shorter vacancy periods, higher-quality applicants, and better rental income over time. For more marketing resources, the <a href="https://www.nar.realtor" rel="noopener" target="_blank">National Association of Realtors</a> offers guidance on real estate photography standards, and <a href="https://www.canva.com" rel="noopener" target="_blank">Canva</a> provides free templates and design tools for creating listing graphics and photo collages.</p>
