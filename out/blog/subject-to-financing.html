Subject-To Financing: How It Works for Investors

Subject-to financing is one of the most powerful — and most misunderstood — acquisition strategies in real estate investing. When a property is purchased "subject to" the existing financing, the buyer takes title to the property while the seller's mortgage remains in place, unchanged. The buyer makes the loan payments, but the loan stays in the seller's name.

For investors, this creates an extraordinary opportunity: you can potentially take over a mortgage with an interest rate locked in from a previous year, acquire properties with minimal out-of-pocket cost, and close deals in days rather than weeks. Done correctly and ethically, subject-to financing is a legitimate creative strategy that benefits both buyers and sellers in the right situations.

What Does "Subject To" Mean in Real Estate?

When you buy a property "subject to the existing financing," you're purchasing the property under the condition that the existing mortgage stays in place. You as the buyer:

  • Take legal title (the deed) to the property
  • Become responsible for making mortgage payments
  • Do not formally assume the loan (no bank approval required)
  • Do not have your name on the mortgage note

The seller:

  • Transfers title to you
  • Remains legally liable on the mortgage note
  • No longer owns the property
  • Trusts you to make the payments

This distinction — legal ownership separate from mortgage liability — is what makes subject-to transactions work, and also what creates their primary risk.

Why Sellers Agree to Subject-To Deals

This naturally raises the question: why would any seller agree to leave a mortgage in their name when they no longer own the property? Sellers in certain situations are highly motivated:

Foreclosure Prevention

A seller facing imminent foreclosure needs to sell fast. If they have little or no equity — meaning the mortgage balance is close to or exceeds the property value — a traditional sale won't work (no proceeds to pay off the mortgage after covering closing costs). A subject-to deal lets them hand over the property, stop the foreclosure process, and protect their credit from the most severe damage of a completed foreclosure. In some cases, seller financing is an alternative worth presenting alongside subject-to when sellers have more equity.

Relocation or Life Changes

Job transfers, divorce, military deployment, or family emergencies can create situations where a homeowner needs to exit a property quickly and has limited concern for maximizing the sale price. Speed and certainty matter more than squeezing every dollar.

Negative Equity

When a property is worth less than the mortgage balance (underwater), a conventional sale requires the seller to bring cash to closing to pay off the mortgage. Sellers who can't do this may welcome a subject-to arrangement that lets them exit without a cash-at-closing requirement.

No Desire for Cash (Sometimes)

Occasionally, sellers with an inherited property or a rental they're tired of managing just want the problem to go away — and getting some relief from the mortgage obligation is enough.

Why Investors Use Subject-To Financing

The advantages for buyers are significant:

Below-Market Interest Rates

This is the most compelling reason in the current environment. If a seller locked in a 3% or 4% mortgage in 2020–2022, a subject-to acquisition lets you take over that loan at that rate — rather than financing at today's 7–8%. The cash flow difference on a $300,000 loan between 3.5% and 7.5% is roughly $1,050/month. That's transformative for rental property economics.

No Qualification Required

You don't apply for a loan. There's no income verification, credit check, or bank approval. If the seller agrees to the deal and the paperwork is done correctly, you close without any institutional hurdle.

Minimal Upfront Capital

Subject-to deals often require little or no down payment — particularly in distress situations where the seller just wants out. Your primary cash requirement is closing costs (title, attorney fees, transfer taxes), any cure payments needed to bring a delinquent mortgage current, and appropriate reserves.

Speed

Without a bank loan to originate, deals can close in days. In competitive off-market situations, this speed can be decisive.

The Due-on-Sale Clause: The Primary Risk

Every investor considering subject-to financing needs to understand the due-on-sale clause — and make an informed decision about how to handle it.

Most mortgages contain a clause allowing the lender to demand full repayment of the outstanding loan balance if the property is sold or transferred without the lender's consent. This is the due-on-sale (DOS) clause, authorized by the Garn-St. Germain Depository Institutions Act of 1982.

In a subject-to transaction, when you take title while leaving the existing mortgage in place, you are technically triggering the DOS clause. The lender could — if they discovered the transfer — call the loan due immediately.

What Actually Happens in Practice

Here's the nuanced reality: lenders rarely exercise the DOS clause as long as loan payments are being made on time. From the lender's perspective, a loan being paid on schedule is a performing asset. Calling it due would require them to foreclose or refinance, which is expensive and administratively burdensome.

Lenders don't routinely scan title records to detect subject-to transfers. The risk of DOS enforcement exists, but experienced subject-to investors report it rarely occurring on performing loans.

However, "rarely" is not "never." This is a real legal risk, and any investor doing subject-to deals must:

  1. Accept and understand the risk
  2. Have a plan if the loan is called (ability to refinance or sell quickly)
  3. Never do subject-to without the seller's full informed consent
  4. Work with a real estate attorney in every transaction

Mitigating the Due-on-Sale Risk

  • Keep payments on time, every time: Lenders have no reason to review a performing loan
  • Don't advertise the structure: Avoid listing the property in ways that draw attention to the ownership structure
  • Maintain the loan escrow if possible: Keep insurance and taxes paid through the existing escrow account
  • Have an exit plan: Know how quickly you could refinance into a new loan if needed

How to Find Subject-To Deals

Subject-to opportunities come from motivated sellers in specific situations. The best lead sources:

  • Pre-foreclosure lists: Available through county courthouse records or data services, these identify homeowners who have received notice of default
  • Probate properties: Inherited properties where heirs want a quick resolution
  • Direct mail campaigns: Target homeowners with high LTV mortgages in neighborhoods with stable or rising values
  • Driving for dollars: Visually distressed properties often indicate a distressed owner
  • Real estate investor networks: Wholesalers who find distressed deals may be open to a subject-to structure
  • Expired listings: Properties that sat on the market unsold

The most effective subject-to investors build systems for consistent lead generation — because these deals require finding the right seller situation, not just the right property.

How to Structure a Subject-To Transaction

Proper structure is everything with subject-to. Cut corners on documentation and you're exposing yourself and the seller to serious risk.

Core Documents

Purchase Agreement: Explicitly states the sale is "subject to the existing financing" and details all terms. Include full disclosure of the due-on-sale risk to the seller.

Warranty Deed: Transfers title from seller to buyer (or the buyer's LLC). Recorded with the county.

Deed to Secure Debt / Agreement: Some investors use an agreement that formally spells out the payment obligations and what happens if the buyer defaults.

Seller Disclosure: A signed statement confirming the seller understands the loan remains in their name, you are not assuming legal liability for the mortgage, and they understand the due-on-sale risk.

Payment Authorization: Authorization for loan payments to be made from your account (or a servicing account) on the seller's behalf.

Using a Loan Servicing Company

Many experienced subject-to investors use a third-party loan servicing company to handle payments. Services like Dodd Frank Act-compliant servicers collect your payment, then forward the correct amount to the underlying lender on the seller's behalf. This creates a paper trail and protects the seller by ensuring payments are made correctly.

Entity Structure

Most investors acquire subject-to properties in an LLC for liability protection — review the rental property LLC benefits to understand how this structure protects you. However, take-title in an LLC may be more visible to lenders than individual ownership. Discuss entity structure with your attorney.

Subject-To vs. Loan Assumption

Subject-to and loan assumption are often confused but are fundamentally different:

Feature Subject-To Formal Loan Assumption
Mortgage name Stays in seller's name Transfers to buyer
Bank involvement None required Lender must approve
Credit check None Yes
Income verification None Yes
Seller liability Remains until payoff Released upon approval
Speed Very fast 30–45+ days

A formal loan assumption transfers the mortgage obligation to the buyer with lender approval. Most conventional and government-backed mortgages are not assumable (with exceptions for FHA and VA loans). Subject-to does not require lender approval but leaves the seller legally on the hook.

Ethical Obligations in Subject-To Deals

Subject-to financing has a problematic reputation in some circles due to investors who structured deals without fully disclosing the risks to sellers. This is both unethical and potentially fraudulent.

Always fully disclose: The seller must understand exactly what they're agreeing to — that the loan remains in their name, that they're trusting you to make payments, and that if you don't, their credit is damaged and foreclosure becomes possible.

Don't target the most vulnerable: Pre-foreclosure sellers are often in financial distress and may not fully understand complex transactions. Slow down, explain thoroughly, and give them time to consult with an attorney.

Have a real exit plan: If you acquire a property subject-to and then can't maintain payments, you've left the seller in a catastrophic situation. Only take on subject-to obligations you're confident you can service.

Ethical subject-to transactions genuinely help sellers who have no other viable exit — and build a reputation that sustains a long investing career. Predatory execution destroys both lives and reputations.

Subject-To as Part of a Creative Financing Strategy

Subject-to financing works best as one tool in a broader creative financing toolkit. It's most powerful when:

  • Interest rates are high relative to outstanding mortgages (making the assumed rate a significant economic advantage)
  • Seller distress creates motivated counterparties
  • You have the capital and discipline to service the obligation reliably

For investors building a rental property portfolio, subject-to represents an advanced strategy that requires legal support, ethical discipline, and operational reliability. But when executed correctly, it can produce acquisitions at below-market financing costs that dramatically improve your rental property ROI relative to conventional purchase approaches.

Always work with a qualified real estate attorney who specializes in creative financing transactions. The documentation requirements vary by state, and getting the paperwork right is not optional in a strategy where so much depends on clear written agreements. The National Association of Realtors has published guidance on subject-to transactions that provides additional context on state-by-state legal considerations.
