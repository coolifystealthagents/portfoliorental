2a:T3335,<p>Landscaping is often treated as an afterthought by rental property owners, yet it has a direct and measurable impact on rental income. Properties with well-maintained landscaping rent faster, attract better tenants, and often command higher rents — studies consistently show that curb appeal improvements can <a href="/blog/increase-rental-property-value">increase rental property value</a> by 5-15%. At the same time, overly complex landscaping is a maintenance burden that can drain your operating budget.</p>
<p>The goal for a rental property landscape is simple: maximum curb appeal with minimum maintenance cost. This guide covers practical landscaping strategies that achieve that balance, from plant selection to tenant responsibility structures.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="the-business-case-for-good-landscaping">The Business Case for Good Landscaping</h2>
<p>Before diving into tactics, understand why landscaping matters financially:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Faster leasing</strong> — A well-maintained exterior is the first impression prospective tenants see. Poor curb appeal increases vacancy time.</li>
<li><strong>Higher rents</strong> — Attractive outdoor spaces justify premium pricing, especially in competitive markets.</li>
<li><strong>Better tenants</strong> — Tenants who choose a well-maintained property tend to take better care of it.</li>
<li><strong>Reduced liability</strong> — Overgrown shrubs, dead trees, and poor drainage create hazards and legal exposure.</li>
<li><strong>Property value</strong> — Well-maintained landscaping contributes to appraised property value.</li>
</ul>
<hr>
<h2 id="designing-for-low-maintenance">Designing for Low Maintenance</h2>
<p>The best time to invest in landscaping is when a unit turns over. Use the vacancy period to redesign toward lower maintenance requirements.</p>
<h3 id="choose-the-right-plants">Choose the Right Plants</h3>
<p>Select plants that are:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Native or drought-tolerant</strong> — Native plants are adapted to local rainfall and soil conditions and require minimal watering and care once established. The <a href="https://www.epa.gov/watersense">EPA WaterSense program</a> offers guidance on water-efficient landscaping</li>
<li><strong>Slow-growing</strong> — Fast-growing plants require more frequent pruning and create more debris</li>
<li><strong>Disease and pest resistant</strong> — Choose varieties known to thrive in your region with minimal treatment</li>
<li><strong>Proportionate to the space</strong> — Plant at mature size, not planting size — a shrub planted 2 feet from a foundation will become a problem as it grows</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="replace-high-maintenance-features">Replace High-Maintenance Features</h3>
<p>High-maintenance landscaping elements to remove or avoid:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Annual flower beds</strong> — Require replanting every year; replace with perennials or low-growing shrubs</li>
<li><strong>Formal hedges requiring precise shearing</strong> — Replace with informal naturalistic plantings</li>
<li><strong>Large mulched beds with sparse plants</strong> — Weeds fill the gaps quickly; use dense ground cover plantings instead</li>
<li><strong>Ornamental features requiring seasonal storage</strong> — Fountains, decorative pots, and seasonal decorations add labor costs</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="ground-cover-alternatives-to-lawn">Ground Cover Alternatives to Lawn</h3>
<p>Lawn is the most maintenance-intensive landscaping element. Where possible, reduce lawn area by:</p>
<ul>
<li>Installing mulched planting beds with dense shrubs or ground cover plants</li>
<li>Using pea gravel or decomposed granite in low-traffic areas</li>
<li>Installing hardscape (patios, walkways) in high-traffic areas</li>
<li>Replacing narrow strips of grass (between sidewalk and street) with low-growing perennials or native ground covers</li>
</ul>
<hr>
<h2 id="tenant-vs-landlord-landscaping-responsibilities">Tenant vs Landlord: Landscaping Responsibilities</h2>
<p>Clarifying who is responsible for landscaping maintenance is one of the most important decisions a landlord makes. The answer depends on property type and your management model.</p>
<h3 id="for-single-family-homes-and-duplexes">For Single-Family Homes and Duplexes</h3>
<p>Most landlords assign lawn and basic landscaping maintenance to tenants in single-family homes. This arrangement works well when:</p>
<ul>
<li>The lease clearly specifies what tenants are responsible for (lawn mowing, leaf raking, light weeding, snow removal)</li>
<li>Expectations are set at move-in with a walkthrough of outdoor areas</li>
<li>The yard is simple enough that a typical tenant can maintain it</li>
<li>Failure to maintain is documented and addressed promptly</li>
</ul>
<p>Even when tenants are responsible for basic maintenance, the landlord typically retains responsibility for:</p>
<ul>
<li>Tree trimming and removal</li>
<li>Sprinkler system maintenance and repair</li>
<li>Major landscaping improvements</li>
<li>Any features requiring professional equipment</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="for-multi-family-properties">For Multi-Family Properties</h3>
<p>For apartment buildings, landscaping is almost always the landlord's responsibility. Contract with a professional landscaping service for:</p>
<ul>
<li>Weekly or bi-weekly lawn care (mowing, edging, blowing)</li>
<li>Monthly or seasonal pruning</li>
<li>Seasonal color (annual plantings at entry areas for curb appeal)</li>
<li>Snow removal (where applicable)</li>
</ul>
<p>Get multiple bids and negotiate annual contracts for better pricing than per-visit rates. Reliable, contracted landscape crews tend to provide more consistent service than ad-hoc mow-and-go operations.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="lawn-care-basics-for-rental-properties">Lawn Care Basics for Rental Properties</h2>
<p>If you maintain the lawn yourself or oversee tenant maintenance, these fundamentals apply:</p>
<h3 id="mowing">Mowing</h3>
<ul>
<li>Mow at the correct height for the grass species (3-4 inches for most cool-season grasses, 2-3 inches for warm-season grasses)</li>
<li>Never remove more than one-third of the grass blade in a single mowing</li>
<li>Keep blades sharp — dull blades tear grass rather than cut cleanly, increasing disease susceptibility</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="fertilization">Fertilization</h3>
<ul>
<li>Apply fertilizer 2-4 times per year depending on grass type and region</li>
<li>A basic lawn fertilizer program (spring, summer, fall) maintains density and green color</li>
<li>Consider organic slow-release fertilizers — they are lower-risk for <a href="https://www.epa.gov/watersense">water contamination</a> and require less frequent application</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="weed-control">Weed Control</h3>
<ul>
<li>Pre-emergent herbicide applied in early spring prevents annual weed germination</li>
<li>Spot-treat weeds that break through rather than broadcasting herbicide indiscriminately</li>
<li>In problem areas, dense planting and mulch are more sustainable than ongoing herbicide application</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="irrigation">Irrigation</h3>
<ul>
<li>If the property has an irrigation system, test all heads at the start of the season and repair broken or misaligned heads</li>
<li>Program controllers for efficiency — deep infrequent watering (1 inch per week) produces deeper root growth than shallow daily watering</li>
<li>Winterize irrigation systems before freezing temperatures</li>
</ul>
<hr>
<h2 id="tree-maintenance">Tree Maintenance</h2>
<p>Trees are valuable assets on a rental property but require active management:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Annual inspection</strong> — Look for dead or dying branches, signs of disease or pest damage, and proximity to structures or utility lines</li>
<li><strong>Pruning</strong> — Hire a certified arborist for any pruning beyond basic dead-wooding. Improper pruning damages trees and can create liability.</li>
<li><strong>Dead tree removal</strong> — Dead or dying trees adjacent to buildings, walkways, or parking areas should be removed promptly — a fallen tree or limb creates serious <a href="https://www.nolo.com/legal-encyclopedia/landlord-tenant-law">premises liability</a> exposure</li>
<li><strong>Root considerations</strong> — Tree roots can damage foundations, sewer lines, and hardscape over time. Consider this when planting new trees or when purchasing properties with large established trees.</li>
</ul>
<p>For more on tree-related emergency risks, see our guide on <a href="/blog/tree-damage-rental-property">storm and tree damage at rental properties</a>.</p>
<hr>
<h2 id="seasonal-landscaping-schedule">Seasonal Landscaping Schedule</h2>
<h3 id="spring-march-may">Spring (March–May)</h3>
<ul>
<li>Clean up winter debris — remove dead plant material, rake leaves from beds</li>
<li>Apply pre-emergent weed control to lawn and beds</li>
<li>Divide and transplant overgrown perennials</li>
<li>Fertilize lawn</li>
<li>Inspect and start irrigation system</li>
<li>Mulch planting beds (2-3 inches of fresh mulch suppresses weeds and retains moisture)</li>
<li>Plant warm-season annuals at entry areas for seasonal color</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="summer-june-august">Summer (June–August)</h3>
<ul>
<li>Maintain mowing schedule (weekly during active growth)</li>
<li>Water deeply and infrequently during drought periods</li>
<li>Monitor for pest and disease problems</li>
<li>Deadhead flowering plants to extend bloom period</li>
<li>Trim shrubs that are encroaching on walkways or buildings</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="fall-september-november">Fall (September–November)</h3>
<ul>
<li>Core aerate and overseed thin lawn areas</li>
<li>Apply fall fertilizer</li>
<li>Plant cool-season annuals (pansies, ornamental kale) for fall color</li>
<li>Rake and remove fallen leaves (matted leaves smother grass)</li>
<li>Cut back perennial plants after frost</li>
<li>Winterize irrigation system</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="winter-december-february">Winter (December–February)</h3>
<ul>
<li>Apply de-icing materials to walkways and entry areas as needed</li>
<li>Remove snow from walkways promptly — ice on tenant walkways creates significant liability</li>
<li>Inspect trees for storm damage after major wind or ice events</li>
<li>Plan spring improvements during the off-season</li>
</ul>
<hr>
<h2 id="budget-landscaping-improvements-with-high-roi">Budget Landscaping Improvements With High ROI</h2>
<p>If your rental property needs a landscaping refresh, these improvements deliver the most curb appeal per dollar. Factor them into your <a href="/blog/property-maintenance-cost-budgeting">maintenance cost budget</a> to avoid surprises:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<p><strong>Fresh mulch</strong> — Two to three inches of dark bark mulch in planting beds dramatically improves appearance for $3-$5 per cubic yard. A typical front yard improvement costs $150-$400.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Seasonal color at the entry</strong> — A few flats of seasonal flowers at the front entry can transform the first impression for less than $100.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Defined bed edges</strong> — Clean, defined edges between lawn and planting beds create a well-maintained look. A steel or plastic edging product installs easily and lasts for years.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Shrub replacement</strong> — Removing overgrown or dying foundation shrubs and replacing with appropriate-sized, healthy plants is one of the highest-impact landscaping investments.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Walkway repair</strong> — Cracked or heaved walkways are both an eyesore and a liability. Repairing or replacing a front walkway ($500-$2,000) significantly improves curb appeal and reduces trip-and-fall risk.</p>
</li>
</ol>
<hr>
<h2 id="landscaping-lease-provisions">Landscaping Lease Provisions</h2>
<p>Your lease should address landscaping clearly:</p>
<ul>
<li>Specify which maintenance tasks are the tenant's responsibility (mowing, watering, weeding, leaf removal, snow removal)</li>
<li>State the standard to which the lawn must be maintained (mowed to no more than 4 inches, for example)</li>
<li>Specify that tenant cannot remove existing plants, trees, or hardscape without written permission</li>
<li>Address approval requirements for tenant-added landscaping</li>
<li>Include consequences for failure to maintain assigned landscaping tasks</li>
</ul>
<hr>
<h2 id="conclusion">Conclusion</h2>
<p>Effective rental property landscaping is about designing for durability, assigning responsibilities clearly, and maintaining consistently. A low-maintenance landscape designed with native plants, minimal lawn, and defined beds will cost less to maintain and look better than high-maintenance alternatives. Curb appeal drives faster leasing and higher rents — making landscaping one of the most financially impactful maintenance categories for rental property owners.</p>
