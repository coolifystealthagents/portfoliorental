28:T24a9,<p>Familial status is one of the seven federally protected classes under the <a href="https://www.justice.gov/crt/fair-housing-act-1">Fair Housing Act</a>, yet it remains one of the most frequently violated. Many landlords unknowingly discriminate against families with children through occupancy policies, advertising language, or lease terms that create different standards for households with kids.</p>
<p>This guide explains what familial status protections cover, how to set legal occupancy standards, and the limited exemptions available for senior housing. For the full legal text, see the <a href="https://www.nolo.com/legal-encyclopedia/landlord-tenant-law">DOJ Fair Housing overview</a>.</p>
<h2 id="what-familial-status-means-under-fair-housing-law">What Familial Status Means Under Fair Housing Law</h2>
<p>Familial status protects:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Families with children under 18</strong> living with a parent or legal guardian</li>
<li><strong>Pregnant women</strong></li>
<li><strong>Anyone in the process of securing custody</strong> of a child under 18</li>
</ul>
<p>This means you cannot refuse to rent, set different terms, or treat tenants differently because they have children or are expecting children.</p>
<h3 id="what-it-does-not-protect">What It Does Not Protect</h3>
<p>Familial status protections do not require landlords to:</p>
<ul>
<li>Make properties child-safe at their own expense (though reasonable modifications by the tenant must be allowed)</li>
<li>Waive legitimate safety policies that apply to all tenants</li>
<li>Accept applicants who do not meet lawful screening criteria unrelated to family status</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="common-familial-status-violations">Common Familial Status Violations</h2>
<h3 id="refusing-to-rent-to-families">Refusing to Rent to Families</h3>
<p>The most obvious violation is declining an application because the household includes children. This includes:</p>
<ul>
<li>Telling families a unit is not available when it is</li>
<li>Steering families away from certain units or buildings</li>
<li>Adding extra requirements for families that do not apply to other applicants</li>
<li>Requiring a higher deposit because children might cause damage</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="occupancy-restrictions-that-target-families">Occupancy Restrictions That Target Families</h3>
<p>Occupancy limits that are unreasonably restrictive can serve as a proxy for familial status discrimination. A policy of "one person per bedroom" effectively excludes most families with children and has been found discriminatory by courts and HUD.</p>
<h3 id="restrictive-rules-targeting-children">Restrictive Rules Targeting Children</h3>
<p>Rules that single out children are discriminatory:</p>
<ul>
<li>"Children must be supervised at all times in common areas" (unless all tenants must follow the same rule)</li>
<li>"No playing on the lawn"</li>
<li>"Pool hours restricted for children"</li>
<li>Noise policies applied more strictly to families</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="different-lease-terms">Different Lease Terms</h3>
<p>Charging families higher rent, requiring larger deposits, or offering only shorter lease terms because they have children are all violations.</p>
<h2 id="setting-legal-occupancy-standards">Setting Legal Occupancy Standards</h2>
<p>You are allowed to set reasonable occupancy limits to prevent overcrowding and property damage. The key is ensuring those limits are based on legitimate factors, not family size.</p>
<h3 id="hud-s-keating-memo">HUD's Keating Memo</h3>
<p>The most widely referenced guideline is HUD's 1998 Keating Memo, which establishes a general standard of two persons per bedroom as a reasonable occupancy limit. However, this is a starting point, not an absolute rule. HUD evaluates occupancy standards based on:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Size of bedrooms and living spaces</strong></li>
<li><strong>Configuration of the unit</strong> (some layouts accommodate more people comfortably)</li>
<li><strong>State and local occupancy codes</strong> (which may allow more or fewer occupants)</li>
<li><strong>Age of occupants</strong> (infants may not count the same as adults)</li>
<li><strong>Other relevant factors</strong> specific to the property</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="safe-occupancy-policies">Safe Occupancy Policies</h3>
<p>To stay compliant:</p>
<ol>
<li>Base occupancy limits on square footage or building codes, not number of children</li>
<li>Apply the same standard to every applicant regardless of household composition</li>
<li>Document the basis for your occupancy limits (building code, fire code, utility capacity)</li>
<li>Allow exceptions when local law requires it</li>
<li>Never count an unborn child or infant as a full occupant for occupancy purposes</li>
</ol>
<p>For more on building compliant screening processes, see our <a href="/blog/tenant-screening-best-practices">tenant screening best practices guide</a>.</p>
<h2 id="the-senior-housing-exemption">The Senior Housing Exemption</h2>
<p>The Fair Housing Act provides a narrow exemption from familial status protections for qualifying senior housing. Two categories qualify:</p>
<h3 id="62-and-older-housing">62-and-Older Housing</h3>
<p>If every occupant in the community is 62 or older, the property is exempt from familial status requirements. No additional conditions apply.</p>
<h3 id="55-and-older-housing-hopa">55-and-Older Housing (HOPA)</h3>
<p>Under the Housing for Older Persons Act (HOPA), a community qualifies if:</p>
<ul>
<li>At least 80% of occupied units have at least one resident who is 55 or older</li>
<li>The community publishes and adheres to policies demonstrating intent to be 55+ housing</li>
<li>The community complies with HUD verification procedures</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="what-the-exemption-allows">What the Exemption Allows</h3>
<p>Qualifying senior housing may:</p>
<ul>
<li>Refuse to rent to families with children</li>
<li>Advertise as "55+" or "62+" housing</li>
<li>Set age-based occupancy requirements</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="what-the-exemption-does-not-allow">What the Exemption Does Not Allow</h3>
<p>Even with the senior housing exemption, you cannot discriminate based on other protected classes. A 55+ community must still comply with fair housing rules regarding race, religion, national origin, disability, and other protected categories.</p>
<h2 id="steering-and-familial-status">Steering and Familial Status</h2>
<p>Steering occurs when you direct families toward or away from specific units, buildings, or neighborhoods based on familial status. Examples include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Showing families only ground-floor units because they have children</li>
<li>Directing families away from "adult" buildings within a complex</li>
<li>Suggesting a family would be "happier" in a different neighborhood with more children</li>
<li>Assigning families to specific sections of a community</li>
</ul>
<p>Even if well-intentioned, steering is illegal. Present all available options and let the applicant choose.</p>
<h2 id="advertising-compliance">Advertising Compliance</h2>
<p>Rental advertising must not indicate a preference regarding familial status. Avoid:</p>
<ul>
<li>"Adults only" or "No children" (unless qualifying senior housing)</li>
<li>"Perfect for couples" or "Professional building"</li>
<li>"Not suitable for children" or "Quiet, mature community"</li>
<li>Images showing only adults with no representation of families</li>
</ul>
<p>For detailed advertising guidance, see our <a href="/blog/fair-housing-advertising-rules">fair housing advertising rules guide</a>.</p>
<h2 id="handling-complaints-between-tenants">Handling Complaints Between Tenants</h2>
<p>When tenants without children complain about noise from families with children, handle the situation carefully:</p>
<ul>
<li>Apply noise policies equally to all tenants</li>
<li>Document complaints and responses consistently</li>
<li>Do not single out the family for enforcement that other tenants would not face</li>
<li>Recognize that normal sounds of children playing are not lease violations</li>
<li>Use mediation before escalation</li>
</ul>
<p>Retaliating against a family because of complaints from other tenants is a fair housing violation.</p>
<h2 id="penalties-for-familial-status-discrimination">Penalties for Familial Status Discrimination</h2>
<p>HUD and courts take familial status discrimination seriously:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>HUD complaints</strong> can result in settlements exceeding $50,000 — file through <a href="https://www.hud.gov/program_offices/fair_housing_equal_opp">HUD's fair housing office</a></li>
<li><strong>Federal lawsuits</strong> may include compensatory and punitive damages</li>
<li><strong>Pattern and practice cases</strong> brought by DOJ can result in six-figure penalties</li>
<li><strong>Testing organizations</strong> actively test properties for familial status discrimination</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="key-takeaways">Key Takeaways</h2>
<p>Familial status discrimination is preventable with clear, consistent policies. Base occupancy limits on objective criteria, apply rules equally to all tenants, and train staff to avoid steering. Unless you operate qualifying senior housing, every policy and practice must treat families with children the same as any other household.</p>
<p>For the complete picture, review our <a href="/blog/fair-housing-compliance-rental">fair housing compliance guide</a>.</p>
