What Does a Property Manager Do? Daily Responsibilities Explained

You have heard that hiring a property manager can make your life easier. But what does a property manager do, exactly? If you are going to hand over the keys to your investment property, you should know exactly what you are paying for.

A property manager wears many hats. They are part marketer, part accountant, part handyman coordinator, and part legal advisor. Their job is to protect your investment while keeping tenants happy, following industry standards set by the National Association of Residential Property Managers. That balance is harder than it sounds.

This guide walks through every responsibility a property manager handles so you can decide if professional management is right for you. If you are already sold on the idea, head over to our complete guide to property management services for the full picture.

The Core Responsibilities of a Property Manager

A property manager's job breaks down into six main areas. Each area requires specific skills, knowledge, and systems. Let us walk through them one by one.

1. Marketing and Filling Vacancies

Every vacant day costs you money. If your unit rents for $1,800 per month, that is $60 per day in lost income. A property manager's first job is to minimize that lost income by filling vacancies fast.

Here is what that looks like in practice:

Setting the right rental price. A property manager runs a comparative market analysis (CMA) to determine the best price for your unit. They look at similar properties in the area, current market trends, and seasonal demand. Price it too high and the unit sits empty. Price it too low and you leave money on the table.

Creating the listing. Professional photos, detailed descriptions, and accurate floor plans make a listing stand out. A good property manager knows how to highlight your property's best features while being honest about its limitations.

Distributing the listing. Your property needs to appear where renters are looking. That means Zillow, Apartments.com, Realtor.com, Facebook Marketplace, Craigslist, and sometimes the local MLS. A property manager syndicates your listing across all major platforms.

Conducting showings. Whether it is individual tours or open houses, the property manager handles all showings. They answer questions, point out features, and follow up with interested prospects. Many managers now offer self-guided tours with lockbox access for added convenience.

Tracking results. How many inquiries did the listing generate? How many showings? What feedback are prospects giving? A data-driven property manager tracks these numbers and adjusts the strategy if the unit is not getting traction.

The goal is to fill the unit with a qualified tenant within 2-3 weeks. Top property managers consistently achieve this benchmark. To learn how vacancy impacts your bottom line, check out our rental property cash flow analysis guide.

2. Tenant Screening and Selection

Finding a tenant is one thing. Finding the right tenant is another. This is where a property manager's screening process becomes critical.

A thorough screening process includes:

Credit check. The property manager pulls the applicant's credit report and reviews their score, payment history, outstanding debts, and any collections or judgments. Most managers require a minimum credit score between 620 and 680, though this varies by market.

Background check. A criminal background check identifies any past convictions. Property managers must follow fair housing laws when evaluating criminal history. They cannot use a blanket ban on all criminal records. Instead, they assess each case individually based on the nature, severity, and recency of the offense.

Income verification. The standard requirement is that a tenant's gross monthly income should be at least three times the monthly rent. The property manager verifies this through pay stubs, tax returns, bank statements, or employment letters.

Rental history. The manager contacts previous landlords to ask about payment history, lease compliance, property condition at move-out, and whether they would rent to the tenant again. This is often the most revealing part of the screening process.

Eviction history. A search of court records reveals any past evictions. While a single eviction years ago may not disqualify someone, a pattern of evictions is a serious red flag.

The property manager evaluates all of this information against consistent, documented criteria to ensure fair housing compliance. They then present their recommendation to you for approval.

3. Rent Collection and Financial Management

Consistent cash flow is the foundation of a profitable rental property. A property manager builds systems that make rent collection predictable and efficient.

Online rent payment. Most property managers set up online portals where tenants pay rent electronically. This reduces late payments because tenants can set up autopay. It also eliminates the hassle of chasing checks or cash.

Late payment enforcement. When rent is late, the property manager follows a clear process. They send a reminder on day one. They issue a formal late notice on day three or five, depending on the lease terms. They assess late fees as specified in the lease. If the tenant does not pay, the manager begins the legal process.

Security deposit handling. The property manager collects, holds, and returns security deposits in compliance with state law. Many states have specific rules about where deposits must be held, how much interest to pay, and how quickly to return them after move-out. Getting this wrong can result in penalties.

Monthly financial statements. You should receive a detailed owner statement every month. This statement shows:

  • Rent collected
  • Management fees deducted
  • Maintenance expenses
  • Any other income or expenses
  • Net proceeds deposited to your account

Year-end reporting. At tax time, the property manager provides a year-end summary and IRS Form 1099. This makes tax preparation much simpler for you and your accountant.

Reserve fund management. Many managers maintain a reserve fund for each property to cover unexpected expenses. A typical reserve is $500-$1,000 per unit. This prevents delays when urgent repairs arise.

Good financial management gives you a clear view of your rental property cash flow and helps you make informed investment decisions.

4. Maintenance and Repairs

Maintenance is the responsibility that drives most landlords to hire a property manager. Dealing with repair requests, coordinating contractors, and handling emergencies takes a huge amount of time and energy.

Here is how a property manager handles maintenance:

24/7 emergency response. Burst pipes, gas leaks, and electrical failures do not wait for business hours. A property manager has systems in place to respond to emergencies around the clock. They maintain a list of trusted contractors who can respond quickly at any hour.

Work order management. Tenants submit maintenance requests through an online portal or by phone. The property manager creates a work order, prioritizes it based on urgency, and dispatches the appropriate contractor. They track the request from submission to completion.

Vendor relationships. Property managers maintain a network of vetted contractors including plumbers, electricians, HVAC technicians, handymen, and cleaners. Because they send steady business, they often negotiate better rates than an individual landlord could get.

Spending authorization. Most management agreements include a spending threshold. The manager can approve repairs up to a set amount, usually $300-$500, without contacting you first. Anything above that amount requires your approval. This speeds up routine repairs while keeping you in control of bigger expenses.

Preventive maintenance. Smart property managers schedule regular inspections and preventive maintenance. This includes HVAC filter changes, gutter cleaning, annual furnace inspections, and seasonal property walkthroughs. Catching problems early saves money. A $150 drain cleaning prevents a $3,000 sewer backup.

Capital improvements. When a major upgrade is needed, such as a new roof, updated appliances, or a bathroom renovation, the property manager gets multiple bids, presents options, and oversees the project. They ensure the work meets quality standards and stays within budget.

Understanding maintenance costs is important when evaluating property management fees and the total cost of professional management.

5. Legal Compliance and Risk Management

Rental property law is complex and varies by state, county, and city. A property manager stays current on all relevant regulations so you do not accidentally break the law.

Fair housing laws. Federal, state, and local fair housing laws prohibit discrimination based on protected characteristics. A property manager ensures that all marketing, screening, and tenant interactions comply with these laws. Violations can result in lawsuits and fines exceeding $16,000 for a first offense.

Lease agreements. The property manager drafts and maintains lease agreements that protect your interests while complying with state law. They include all required disclosures, proper language about security deposits, and clear terms about tenant responsibilities.

Eviction procedures. When an eviction becomes necessary, the property manager follows the exact legal process required by your jurisdiction. They serve proper notice, file the correct paperwork, attend court hearings, and coordinate the move-out. Skipping a single step can result in the case being thrown out, costing you months of additional lost rent.

Safety and habitability standards. Local codes require landlords to maintain properties to certain standards. This includes working smoke detectors, carbon monoxide detectors, proper locks, lead paint disclosures, and more. The property manager makes sure your property stays compliant.

Local ordinances. Many cities have specific landlord regulations like rental registration requirements, annual inspections, or rent control laws. A property manager with local expertise knows these rules and ensures you follow them.

6. Tenant Relations and Communication

Happy tenants stay longer. Longer tenancies mean less turnover, less vacancy, and lower costs. A property manager builds positive tenant relationships through professional communication.

Move-in process. The manager conducts a detailed move-in inspection with the tenant, documenting the condition of every room with photos and notes. They review the lease terms, explain how to submit maintenance requests, and answer questions. A smooth move-in sets the tone for the entire tenancy.

Ongoing communication. Good property managers respond to tenant inquiries within 24 hours. They send clear notices about inspections, policy changes, or community updates. They treat tenants with respect while enforcing lease terms consistently.

Lease renewals. About 60-90 days before a lease expires, the property manager reaches out to the tenant about renewal. They assess whether a rent increase is appropriate based on market conditions, prepare the renewal agreement, and work to retain good tenants. Retention saves the significant cost of turnover.

Move-out process. When a tenant leaves, the property manager conducts a move-out inspection and compares it to the move-in documentation. They assess any damage beyond normal wear and tear, calculate deductions, and return the security deposit within the legally required timeframe.

Conflict resolution. Noise complaints, neighbor disputes, and lease violations happen. The property manager addresses these issues professionally and documents everything. This protects you from liability and keeps the community peaceful.

What a Property Manager Does NOT Do

It is just as important to understand the limits of property management. A property manager typically does not:

  • Make investment decisions for you. They can advise, but you decide whether to buy, sell, or renovate.
  • Guarantee occupancy. No manager can promise zero vacancy.
  • Handle HOA disputes. While they may coordinate with an HOA, they are not your HOA representative.
  • Perform repairs themselves. Most managers coordinate contractors rather than doing the physical work.
  • Act as your attorney. They follow legal procedures but cannot provide legal advice.

How to Tell If Your Property Manager Is Doing a Good Job

Once you hire a property manager, look for these performance indicators:

  • Vacancy rate. Is it at or below the market average?
  • Time to fill. Are vacancies being filled within 2-4 weeks?
  • Rent collection rate. Are they collecting 95%+ of rent on time?
  • Maintenance response time. Are routine requests handled within 48 hours?
  • Communication. Are you receiving monthly statements and prompt responses to your questions?
  • Tenant retention. Are good tenants renewing their leases?

If your property manager falls short on these metrics, it may be time to evaluate other options. Our guide on how to choose a property manager explains what to look for in a replacement.

Is Hiring a Property Manager Worth It?

The answer depends on your situation. Here is a quick framework:

Hire a property manager if:

  • You own 3+ units or plan to scale your portfolio
  • Your properties are more than an hour away
  • You have a demanding full-time job
  • You are not confident in landlord-tenant law
  • You want to focus on acquisitions, not operations

Self-manage if:

  • You have 1-2 nearby units
  • You enjoy the hands-on work
  • You have solid systems and contractor relationships
  • You understand local rental laws
  • Your time is flexible

Either way, understanding what a property manager does helps you run your rental business better. Even if you self-manage, you can model your operations after professional management practices.

The Bottom Line

A property manager does far more than collect rent. They market your property, screen tenants, manage finances, coordinate maintenance, ensure legal compliance, and maintain tenant relationships. Each of these responsibilities requires expertise, systems, and time.

When done well, professional property management protects your investment and frees you to focus on growing your portfolio. Organizations like the Institute of Real Estate Management (IREM) publish best practices that define what excellent property management looks like. When done poorly, it can cost you more than self-managing. That is why choosing the right property manager matters so much.
