Using Home Equity to Buy a Rental Property

For homeowners who have built up significant equity in their primary residence, that equity represents more than just net worth on paper — it's deployable capital that can be used to purchase income-producing rental property. Using home equity to invest in rental property is one of the most accessible paths to becoming a landlord, particularly for first-time investors who haven't yet accumulated cash reserves for a traditional down payment.

This guide covers the two primary methods for accessing home equity, how to evaluate whether this strategy makes financial sense, and how to structure the move to protect your primary residence while building rental income.

What Is Home Equity and How Much Can You Access?

Home equity is the difference between your home's current market value and what you owe on your mortgage. For example, if your home is worth $400,000 and you owe $220,000, your equity is $180,000.

However, lenders don't let you access all of your equity. Most programs limit you to accessing up to 80–85% of your home's value, minus what you owe.

Maximum accessible equity formula: (Home Value × 80%) − Remaining Mortgage Balance = Maximum Accessible Equity

Using the example above: ($400,000 × 80%) − $220,000 = $320,000 − $220,000 = $100,000

That $100,000 is the theoretical maximum you could access — in practice, lenders set limits based on creditworthiness, income, and program specifics.

Two Ways to Access Home Equity for Rental Investment

Option 1: Home Equity Line of Credit (HELOC)

A HELOC is a revolving line of credit secured by your home's equity. You're approved for a maximum credit limit and can draw from it as needed during the "draw period" (typically 5–10 years), then repay principal during the "repayment period" (10–20 years).

HELOC features for rental property investors:

  • Variable interest rate tied to prime rate (rates fluctuate)
  • Draw as needed: You only pay interest on what you use
  • Revolving credit: As you repay, you can redraw
  • Typical rates: Prime + 0–2% (approximately 7–9% in 2026)
  • No closing costs on many HELOC programs (or minimal fees)

HELOCs work particularly well as short-term acquisition capital. Use the HELOC to fund the down payment on a rental property, then use the rental income to pay down the HELOC balance, eventually recycling the credit for the next deal.

Option 2: Cash-Out Refinance on Primary Residence

A cash-out refinance replaces your existing primary mortgage with a new, larger loan, and you receive the difference in cash. This is a lump-sum approach: you receive all the equity upfront and immediately begin repaying it via the new (higher) mortgage payment.

Cash-out refinance features:

  • Fixed interest rate available (rate security vs. HELOC's variability)
  • Lump sum: Full proceeds at closing
  • Closing costs: Typically 2–4% of the new loan amount
  • Primary residence rates: Lower than investment property rates since your home secures the loan
  • Maximum LTV: 80% for conventional cash-out refinances

The key advantage of a cash-out refinance over a HELOC is rate stability. If rates are favorable, locking in a fixed rate on the accessed equity provides predictable carrying costs.

HELOC vs. Cash-Out Refinance: Which Is Right for You?

Feature HELOC Cash-Out Refinance
Rate type Variable Fixed available
Rate level Usually lower short-term Often higher long-term
Flexibility Draw as needed Lump sum
Closing costs Low/none 2–4%
Best for Active investors, multiple deals Single large acquisition
Risk Rate can rise significantly Rate locked in

For investors planning to do multiple deals over the next 2–3 years, a HELOC's flexibility is hard to beat. For a single, larger rental property purchase where you want payment certainty, a cash-out refinance may be more appropriate.

Running the Numbers: Does Using Home Equity Make Financial Sense?

This strategy only works if the rental property generates enough income to cover the equity borrowing costs and still produce meaningful cash flow or appreciation. Let's model a real example.

Scenario:

  • HELOC amount: $80,000 at 8.5% (interest-only payment = $567/month)
  • Down payment on rental property: $80,000 (20% of $400,000 purchase)
  • Rental property mortgage: $320,000 at 7.25% (30-year fixed) = $2,183/month
  • Property taxes and insurance: $400/month
  • Maintenance/vacancy reserve: $300/month
  • Total monthly cost: $3,450/month

Rental income needed to break even: $3,450/month

If the property rents for $3,800/month, you're generating $350/month in cash flow after covering both the HELOC and the rental property mortgage. That's a solid start, and as you pay down the HELOC, your cash flow improves.

Additionally, the property is building equity through tenant principal paydown and (ideally) appreciation. Always run this analysis before pulling equity from your home — the rental property cash flow analysis is non-negotiable.

The Risk Profile: What Could Go Wrong?

Using home equity to invest in rental property is a powerful strategy, but it comes with real risks that must be understood before proceeding.

Your Home Is at Stake

This is the most significant risk. If your rental investment goes wrong — the property sits vacant, a major repair drains your reserves, or a bad tenant causes damage — and you can't cover the HELOC or cash-out payments, your primary residence is ultimately at risk. Lenders can foreclose.

Mitigation: Maintain 6 months of reserves covering both your primary mortgage and the HELOC payment before drawing. Never tap your emergency fund to invest.

Rental Income Isn't Guaranteed

Vacancies, problem tenants, and economic downturns can all reduce rental income. A property that seemed to cash flow positively can quickly become a money-losing asset.

Mitigation: Underwrite conservatively. Use 10% vacancy, realistic maintenance reserves, and 8% property management even if you self-manage. Learn to analyze rental property before you commit.

Rising HELOC Rates

HELOCs are variable rate. If the prime rate climbs 2–3%, your carrying cost increases. A HELOC that costs $567/month at 8.5% costs $745/month at 11%.

Mitigation: Model worst-case rate scenarios before drawing. Consider converting to a fixed-rate option if your HELOC lender offers it, or refinance into a fixed home equity loan.

Over-Leveraging

Using home equity to buy rental properties increases your total debt load significantly. If the rental market softens or you face unexpected expenses, being heavily leveraged amplifies the financial pressure.

Mitigation: Start with one rental property before using home equity for multiple acquisitions. Make sure each deal stands on its own financially.

Step-by-Step: Using Home Equity to Buy Your First Rental Property

Step 1: Get Your Equity Position Assessed

Contact your lender or use an online home equity estimator to determine how much equity you have and what you can access. A formal appraisal may be required for larger HELOC amounts.

Step 2: Choose Your Equity Access Method

Decide between a HELOC and a cash-out refinance based on your plans (one deal vs. multiple, rate preference, flexibility needs).

Step 3: Apply for the HELOC or Refinance

You'll need good credit (680+ minimum, 720+ for best terms), sufficient income to service both your existing mortgage and the new obligation, and an acceptable debt-to-income ratio.

Step 4: Find a Rental Property that Cash-Flows

Use the equity as a down payment on a rental property that generates positive cash flow after all expenses, including your equity borrowing costs. Focus on analyzing deals carefully using realistic assumptions.

Step 5: Structure the Rental Property Financing

Most investors use the home equity for the down payment (20–25% of purchase price) and finance the remainder with a conventional mortgage or DSCR loan on the rental property. This keeps the primary residence loan and the rental property loan separate.

Step 6: Build Cash Flow to Repay the HELOC

If you used a HELOC, prioritize repaying it with rental income and any surplus cash flow. Paying down the HELOC restores your credit line for future deals. Many successful investors cycle through this pattern repeatedly to build their rental property portfolio.

Tax Implications

The interest on a HELOC or cash-out refinance used to invest in rental property may be deductible as a business expense against your rental income — not as home mortgage interest. IRS Publication 936 on home mortgage interest deduction and Publication 527 on rental income both apply here, and the rules changed after the 2017 Tax Cuts and Jobs Act.

Work with a CPA who understands real estate investment to ensure you're deducting these costs correctly. Also review the full picture of rental property tax deductions to understand how this fits your overall tax strategy.

Is This Strategy Right for You?

Using home equity to invest in rental property is best suited for homeowners who:

  • Have substantial equity (at least $75,000+ accessible)
  • Carry a primary mortgage with a rate well below current market (protecting it from a cash-out refinance is often smart)
  • Live in markets where rental property cash flow is achievable
  • Have stable income to service increased debt obligations
  • Have 6+ months of liquid reserves before and after the investment

The CFPB's home equity guidance provides useful consumer-facing context for understanding HELOC terms and risks before signing.

This strategy has launched thousands of successful rental portfolios. When approached with discipline, conservative underwriting, and adequate reserves, leveraging home equity to buy rental property is one of the most efficient paths from homeowner to landlord. Just remember: the goal is to protect your primary residence while building wealth through additional properties — never the reverse.
