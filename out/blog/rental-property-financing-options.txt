22:T4b42,<p>Finding the right financing is one of the biggest challenges for rental property investors. The wrong loan can turn a great deal into a bad investment. The right loan can make a marginal deal profitable.</p>
<p>Most people only know about conventional bank loans. But there are at least ten different ways to finance a rental property. Each one has unique advantages, drawbacks, and ideal use cases.</p>
<p>This guide compares every major financing option available to rental property investors. By the end, you will know which option works best for your situation and your next investment.</p>
<h2 id="conventional-loans">Conventional Loans</h2>
<p>Conventional loans are the most common way to finance rental properties. These are standard mortgages offered by banks, credit unions, and mortgage lenders.</p>
<p><strong>How they work.</strong> You apply with a lender, provide documentation of your income, assets, and credit history, and the lender underwrites the loan based on your personal financial profile.</p>
<p><strong>Down payment.</strong> Expect to put down 20-25% for an investment property. Some lenders require 25% for multi-unit properties.</p>
<p><strong>Interest rates.</strong> Rates for investment properties are typically 0.5% to 0.75% higher than primary residence rates. As of early 2026, expect rates in the 6.5-7.5% range for well-qualified borrowers based on current <a href="https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/MORTGAGE30US" rel="noopener" target="_blank">Federal Reserve mortgage rate data</a>.</p>
<p><strong>Pros:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Lowest interest rates for investment properties</li>
<li>30-year fixed terms available</li>
<li>Widely available from many lenders</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Cons:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Strict income and credit requirements (usually 680+ credit score)</li>
<li>Conventional lenders typically cap you at 10 financed properties</li>
<li>Full documentation required (tax returns, pay stubs, bank statements)</li>
<li>Slower closing (30-45 days)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Best for:</strong> Investors with strong W-2 income, good credit, and fewer than ten financed properties.</p>
<h2 id="fha-loans-and-house-hacking">FHA Loans and House Hacking</h2>
<p>FHA loans are government-backed mortgages designed for owner-occupants. But smart investors use them to get started through house hacking.</p>
<p><strong>How they work.</strong> You buy a property with one to four units, live in one unit, and rent out the rest. The FHA loan covers the entire property. You must live there for at least 12 months.</p>
<p><strong>Down payment.</strong> Just 3.5% with a credit score of 580 or higher. That means you can buy a $300,000 property for about $10,500 down.</p>
<p><strong>Interest rates.</strong> FHA rates are typically lower than conventional investment property rates. You get owner-occupied pricing on an income-producing property.</p>
<p><strong>Pros:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Very low down payment (3.5%)</li>
<li>Lower credit score requirements (580+)</li>
<li>Lower interest rates than investment property loans</li>
<li>Rental income from other units helps you qualify</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Cons:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>You must live in the property for one year</li>
<li>Mortgage insurance premium required (MIP)</li>
<li>Property must meet FHA condition standards</li>
<li>Limited to four units or fewer</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Best for:</strong> First-time investors who are willing to live in the property. This is one of the best ways to start <a href="/blog/build-rental-property-portfolio">building a rental property portfolio</a> with very little money.</p>
<h2 id="dscr-loans">DSCR Loans</h2>
<p>DSCR stands for Debt Service Coverage Ratio. These loans are underwritten based on the property's income, not your personal income. They are a game changer for self-employed investors and those who already own many properties.</p>
<p><strong>How they work.</strong> The lender looks at the property's rental income compared to its monthly debt payment. If the rent covers the mortgage (usually at a 1.0 to 1.25 ratio), you qualify. No tax returns, pay stubs, or employment verification needed.</p>
<p><strong>Down payment.</strong> Typically 20-25%.</p>
<p><strong>Interest rates.</strong> Rates are 1-2% higher than conventional loans. Expect 7.5-9% in the current market.</p>
<p><strong>Pros:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>No personal income verification</li>
<li>No limit on the number of properties</li>
<li>Fast closing (2-3 weeks)</li>
<li>Works for LLCs and business entities</li>
<li>Great for self-employed borrowers</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Cons:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Higher interest rates</li>
<li>Higher fees and closing costs</li>
<li>Minimum credit score (usually 660-680)</li>
<li>Some lenders require six months of reserves</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Best for:</strong> Self-employed investors, investors with many properties, and anyone who cannot document traditional income. DSCR loans are excellent for <a href="/blog/scaling-rental-property-business">scaling your rental property business</a>. For a list of lenders that specialize in this product, see our guide on <a href="/blog/dscr-lenders-small-portfolios">DSCR lenders for small portfolios</a>.</p>
<h2 id="portfolio-lenders">Portfolio Lenders</h2>
<p>Portfolio lenders are banks or credit unions that keep loans on their own books instead of selling them to Fannie Mae or Freddie Mac. This gives them flexibility to set their own rules.</p>
<p><strong>How they work.</strong> You work directly with a local bank or credit union. They evaluate your overall financial picture and the property. Their guidelines are more flexible than conventional lenders.</p>
<p><strong>Down payment.</strong> Varies by lender, typically 20-30%.</p>
<p><strong>Interest rates.</strong> Usually between conventional and DSCR rates. Some portfolio lenders offer competitive rates for strong borrowers.</p>
<p><strong>Pros:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>More flexible underwriting</li>
<li>Can finance properties that conventional lenders reject</li>
<li>Relationship-based lending</li>
<li>May offer blanket loans covering multiple properties</li>
<li>No Fannie Mae property limits</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Cons:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Harder to find</li>
<li>Terms may not be as favorable (shorter amortization, balloon payments)</li>
<li>Less standardized than conventional loans</li>
<li>May require personal guarantees and cross-collateralization</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Best for:</strong> Experienced investors with a local banking relationship and properties that do not fit conventional guidelines.</p>
<h2 id="hard-money-loans">Hard Money Loans</h2>
<p>Hard money loans are short-term, asset-based loans from private lending companies. They are designed for investors who need to move fast or cannot qualify for traditional financing.</p>
<p><strong>How they work.</strong> The lender focuses primarily on the property's value, not your income or credit. Loans are typically short-term (6 to 24 months) with high interest rates.</p>
<p><strong>Down payment.</strong> Usually 10-30% of the purchase price, depending on the property's after-repair value.</p>
<p><strong>Interest rates.</strong> High. Expect 10-14% interest plus 1-3 points in origination fees.</p>
<p><strong>Pros:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Very fast closing (7-14 days)</li>
<li>Less focus on borrower credit and income</li>
<li>Works for properties in poor condition</li>
<li>Flexible terms for experienced borrowers</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Cons:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Very expensive (high rates and fees)</li>
<li>Short terms require a clear exit strategy</li>
<li>Monthly payments can be high</li>
<li>Not a long-term financing solution</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Best for:</strong> Flippers and BRRRR investors who plan to refinance into permanent financing within 6-18 months. For the full breakdown of this approach, read our <a href="/blog/brrrr-strategy-complete-guide">BRRRR strategy complete guide</a>. Not ideal for long-term buy and hold unless you refinance.</p>
<h2 id="private-money-loans">Private Money Loans</h2>
<p>Private money comes from individuals rather than lending companies. These are loans from people you know or find through your network: friends, family, colleagues, or other investors.</p>
<p><strong>How they work.</strong> You negotiate terms directly with the lender. There are no standard guidelines. Everything is negotiable: rate, term, down payment, and repayment structure.</p>
<p><strong>Down payment.</strong> Completely negotiable. Some private lenders will finance 100% of the purchase.</p>
<p><strong>Interest rates.</strong> Typically 6-12%, depending on the relationship and the deal.</p>
<p><strong>Pros:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Highly flexible terms</li>
<li>No credit or income requirements</li>
<li>Fast closing</li>
<li>Relationship-based</li>
<li>Creative deal structures possible</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Cons:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Can strain personal relationships</li>
<li>Less professional than institutional lending</li>
<li>May have unclear terms if not properly documented</li>
<li>Limited capital (one lender can only fund so many deals)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Best for:</strong> Investors with strong personal networks and the ability to present professional investment packages. Always use proper legal documents even with friends and family.</p>
<h2 id="seller-financing">Seller Financing</h2>
<p>Seller financing means the property seller acts as the bank. Instead of getting a mortgage from a lender, you make payments directly to the seller.</p>
<p><strong>How they work.</strong> You and the seller agree on a price, down payment, interest rate, and term. The seller holds a note secured by the property. You make monthly payments to the seller.</p>
<p><strong>Down payment.</strong> Negotiable. Typically 10-20%, but some sellers accept less.</p>
<p><strong>Interest rates.</strong> Usually 5-8%, depending on the deal and the seller's motivation.</p>
<p><strong>Pros:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>No bank qualification needed</li>
<li>Flexible terms</li>
<li>Faster closing (no lender underwriting)</li>
<li>Potential for creative deal structures</li>
<li>Win-win for both buyer and seller</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Cons:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Not all sellers will offer financing</li>
<li>Sellers may want a balloon payment in 3-5 years</li>
<li>Due-on-sale clauses on the seller's existing mortgage can be an issue</li>
<li>Less standardized than institutional loans</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Best for:</strong> Deals where the seller owns the property free and clear and wants passive income from the note. Also great for properties that do not qualify for traditional financing.</p>
<h2 id="commercial-loans">Commercial Loans</h2>
<p>Commercial loans are designed for larger properties (usually five or more units) or for investors who buy through business entities.</p>
<p><strong>How they work.</strong> Commercial lenders evaluate both the borrower and the property's income. Terms are different from residential loans. Amortization periods of 20-25 years are common, with balloon payments after 5-10 years.</p>
<p><strong>Down payment.</strong> Typically 25-30%.</p>
<p><strong>Interest rates.</strong> Vary widely based on the property, borrower, and market. Expect 6.5-9%.</p>
<p><strong>Pros:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Designed for larger properties</li>
<li>Property income is a major factor in approval</li>
<li>Can finance through LLCs and business entities</li>
<li>No limit on number of properties</li>
<li>Higher loan amounts available</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Cons:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Higher down payments</li>
<li>Balloon payments create refinance risk</li>
<li>More complex underwriting</li>
<li>Higher closing costs</li>
<li>Shorter amortization periods</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Best for:</strong> Investors buying apartment buildings, commercial properties, or portfolios of properties. Essential once you move beyond small residential properties.</p>
<h2 id="heloc-home-equity-line-of-credit">HELOC (Home Equity Line of Credit)</h2>
<p>A HELOC lets you borrow against the equity in a property you already own. It works like a credit card secured by your real estate.</p>
<p><strong>How they work.</strong> Your lender gives you a credit line based on your property's equity. You draw funds as needed and pay interest only on what you borrow. Most HELOCs have a 10-year draw period followed by a 20-year repayment period.</p>
<p><strong>Down payment.</strong> Not applicable. A HELOC provides funds for down payments on other properties.</p>
<p><strong>Interest rates.</strong> Variable rates, typically prime rate plus 0.5-2%. Currently around 8-9%.</p>
<p><strong>Pros:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Access cash without selling a property</li>
<li>Pay interest only on what you use</li>
<li>Reusable: pay it down and borrow again</li>
<li>Fast access to funds once approved</li>
<li>No closing costs in many cases</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Cons:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Variable interest rates (payments can increase)</li>
<li>Risk of losing your property if you cannot repay</li>
<li>Lenders can freeze or reduce your line</li>
<li>Interest may not be deductible for investment purposes in all cases</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Best for:</strong> Investors who own properties with significant equity and want flexible access to capital for down payments on new acquisitions.</p>
<h2 id="cash-out-refinance">Cash-Out Refinance</h2>
<p>A cash-out refinance replaces your existing mortgage with a larger one and gives you the difference in cash. This lets you access your property's equity while keeping the property.</p>
<p><strong>How they work.</strong> You refinance your property at its current market value. The new loan pays off the old mortgage, and you receive the remaining funds as cash. Most lenders let you borrow up to 75-80% of the property's value.</p>
<p><strong>Down payment.</strong> Not applicable. You are accessing equity you have already built.</p>
<p><strong>Interest rates.</strong> Standard mortgage rates. Investment property cash-out refinance rates are slightly higher than purchase rates.</p>
<p><strong>Pros:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Access large amounts of cash</li>
<li>Fixed-rate options available</li>
<li>Keep your property and its cash flow</li>
<li>Tax-free proceeds (it is a loan, not income)</li>
<li>Reset your mortgage term</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Cons:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Closing costs (2-5% of the loan amount)</li>
<li>Higher monthly payment</li>
<li>Reduces your equity cushion</li>
<li>Takes 30-45 days to close</li>
<li>Must qualify based on income and credit</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Best for:</strong> Investors who have built significant equity and want to use it for additional property purchases. This is the "refinance" step in the BRRRR strategy.</p>

<h2 id="how-to-choose-the-right-financing-option">How to Choose the Right Financing Option</h2>
<p>Picking the right loan depends on your situation. Ask yourself these questions:</p>
<p><strong>What is your income situation?</strong> If you have strong W-2 income, conventional loans give you the best rates. If you are self-employed, DSCR or portfolio loans may work better.</p>
<p><strong>How many properties do you own?</strong> If you have fewer than ten, conventional loans are your cheapest option. Beyond ten, look at DSCR or portfolio lenders.</p>
<p><strong>How fast do you need to close?</strong> If speed matters, hard money or private money can close in days. Conventional loans take a month or more.</p>
<p><strong>What is the property condition?</strong> Banks will not finance a property in poor condition. Hard money and private money will.</p>
<p><strong>What is your exit strategy?</strong> Short-term financing works if you plan to refinance or sell quickly. Long-term holds need long-term financing.</p>
<p><strong>How much cash do you have?</strong> If cash is limited, FHA house hacking or seller financing can get you started with less money down. For more ideas on getting started with limited capital, check out our <a href="/blog/real-estate-investing-beginners">beginner's guide to real estate investing</a>.</p>
<h2 id="stacking-multiple-financing-strategies">Stacking Multiple Financing Strategies</h2>
<p>Experienced investors often combine multiple financing strategies for a single deal or across their portfolio.</p>
<p>For example, you might use an FHA loan for your first house hack, then a conventional loan for your next two properties. When you run out of conventional loan slots, switch to DSCR loans. Use a HELOC on an existing property to fund the down payment.</p>
<p>This layered approach lets you keep buying even as your situation changes. When you sell a property, a <a href="/blog/1031-exchange-rental-property-guide">1031 exchange</a> lets you defer capital gains taxes and reinvest the full proceeds into your next acquisition. Each financing tool has its place. The best investors know all of them and use the right one for each deal.</p>
<h2 id="final-thoughts">Final Thoughts</h2>
<p>There is no single best way to finance a rental property. The right choice depends on your income, credit, experience, property type, and investment goals.</p>
<p>Start with the option that fits your current situation. Use tools like <a href="https://www.bankrate.com/mortgages/mortgage-calculator/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">Bankrate's mortgage calculator</a> to compare monthly payments across loan types. As your portfolio grows, you will naturally need different financing tools. The investors who build the biggest portfolios are the ones who master multiple financing strategies.</p>
<p>Do not let financing be the reason you never start. There is a loan product for almost every situation. Talk to lenders, explore your options, and find the right fit for your next deal.</p>
