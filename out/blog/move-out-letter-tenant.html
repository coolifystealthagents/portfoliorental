How to Write a Move-Out Letter to Tenants

When a tenant gives notice that they are leaving, the clock starts immediately on a process that determines how quickly your unit turns over, how much your security deposit disputes cost you, and how the tenant relationship ends — a matter that affects your reputation and potentially your ability to pursue claims later.

A professional, detailed move-out letter to tenants is the document that governs this process. It tells tenants exactly what you expect, what the inspection and deposit return timeline looks like, and what they need to do before handing over the keys. This guide covers everything that should be in a move-out letter, the right timing, how to handle the inspection process, and a complete template you can adapt.

Why a Move-Out Letter Matters

A move-out letter serves three distinct purposes:

Expectation setting: Tenants who know exactly what is expected of them before moving out are more likely to meet those expectations. Ambiguity leads to tenants leaving a unit in condition they believe is acceptable — and to disputes when you disagree.

Legal protection: A written, delivered move-out letter creates a record that you communicated your expectations and procedures clearly. This is valuable evidence if a deposit dispute goes to small claims court or a state housing authority.

Relationship management: Even when a tenant is leaving, how the move-out is handled affects your reputation. A professional, fair process — communicated clearly — leaves the landlord-tenant relationship on positive terms. Tenants who leave satisfied are more likely to leave positive reviews and refer future tenants.

This document is a natural complement to your overall rental property turnover process and should be used alongside your jurisdiction's requirements under security deposit laws.

When to Send the Move-Out Letter

Immediately upon receiving notice: As soon as a tenant provides move-out notice — whether in writing or verbally — send a formal move-out letter within 24 to 48 hours. Do not wait until close to the move-out date. Tenants need time to prepare, and the letter gives them the information they need to do so.

A reminder two weeks before move-out: A brief follow-up reminder two weeks before the move-out date reinforces key deadlines and gives tenants a chance to ask clarifying questions before it is too late.

Day before or day of move-out: A brief confirmation of the key return process and inspection scheduling.

Some landlords send a comprehensive move-out letter at the beginning of the tenancy — bundled with the lease and the welcome letter — so tenants understand the process from day one. This is excellent practice. It removes surprises and sets a professional tone for the entire tenancy.

What to Include in a Move-Out Letter

1. Header Information

Include the basic identifying information:

  • Date of the letter
  • Tenant name(s)
  • Property address and unit number
  • Lease end date and the official last day of occupancy
  • Your contact information

2. Acknowledgment of Notice

Confirm that you have received the tenant's notice to vacate and that the information in the letter applies to their specific move-out date.

Example: "This letter confirms receipt of your notice to vacate and outlines the move-out procedures and expectations for your tenancy at [Address], effective [Date]."

3. Key Return Instructions

Specify:

  • What keys, fobs, garage openers, and access cards must be returned
  • Where and how to return them (in person, drop slot, mailbox, etc.)
  • The specific deadline for return (often the final day of the lease, before a specific time)
  • Consequences of not returning keys on time (re-keying costs, holdover charges)

Be explicit. "All keys must be returned to the management office drop box by 11:59 PM on [Date]" is clearer than "keys should be returned on move-out day."

4. Cleaning Expectations

This section prevents the most common source of security deposit disputes. Be specific about what "clean" means:

Interior:

  • All rooms swept, mopped, or vacuumed
  • Appliances cleaned inside and out (oven, refrigerator, dishwasher, microwave)
  • Refrigerator emptied and defrosted
  • Bathrooms scrubbed (toilet, tub/shower, sink, mirrors)
  • Cabinets and drawers emptied and wiped out
  • Windows cleaned on interior surfaces
  • Light fixtures cleaned, all bulbs functional
  • Closets emptied and swept

Exterior (if applicable):

  • Balcony or patio cleared and swept
  • Outdoor storage emptied
  • Yard in maintained condition (for single-family rentals)
  • Garage swept and cleared

Trash:

  • All garbage removed from the unit
  • No items left in common areas, hallways, or on the property
  • No furniture or large items left at the curb without prior arrangement

Provide a reference to your cleaning standard: "The unit should be returned in the same clean condition in which it was received at move-in." If you use a professional cleaning service and charge cleaning fees, state this clearly and note any circumstances under which cleaning fees will be deducted from the deposit.

5. Damage Repair Expectations

Distinguish between normal wear and tear (which is not chargeable to the tenant) and damage (which is). This distinction is legally significant in every state under landlord-tenant law, and being clear about it upfront reduces disputes.

Normal wear and tear (not chargeable):

  • Small nail holes from standard picture hanging
  • Minor carpet wear in normal traffic areas
  • Faded paint from sunlight
  • Minor scuffs on walls

Damage (potentially chargeable):

  • Large holes in walls
  • Stained or heavily soiled carpet
  • Broken fixtures, doors, or windows
  • Unauthorized modifications
  • Pet damage beyond normal wear
  • Burns, deep scratches, or gouges in floors or countertops

Some landlords include a reference to the original move-in inspection report: "Your security deposit deductions will be assessed relative to the condition documented on your move-in inspection form dated [Date]."

6. Move-In Inspection Report Reference

Remind the tenant that the move-in inspection form documents the pre-existing condition of the unit. Encourage them to review this document before the move-out inspection.

If you have digital records stored in a tenant portal, provide the link. If they were provided a physical copy, remind them where it would be filed. This step protects you from claims that damage was pre-existing — and it is fair to the tenant.

7. Move-Out Inspection Process

Explain exactly how the inspection works:

  • Whether a joint inspection will be conducted (in some states, tenants have the right to be present)
  • How to schedule the inspection
  • When the inspection will take place relative to the move-out date
  • Who will conduct the inspection (you, a property manager, a contractor)
  • Whether photos or video will be taken
  • How the tenant will receive the results

Many states require landlords to offer tenants the opportunity to be present for the move-out inspection, as outlined in HUD fair housing guidance. Even where not required, it is good practice — it reduces disputes by allowing tenants to see and respond to any issues in real time. Check your state's specific requirements under applicable security deposit laws.

8. Security Deposit Return Timeline and Process

This section must comply with your state's specific rules. Security deposit return timelines vary from 14 days to 45 days depending on the jurisdiction. The CFPB housing resources can help tenants and landlords understand their rights.

Include:

  • The deadline by which you will return the deposit (based on your state's law)
  • The address to which you will mail the check (request their forwarding address)
  • Whether an itemized deduction statement will be provided (required in most states when deductions are made)
  • What documentation will accompany any deductions (photos, receipts, contractor invoices)

A clear, professional statement of this process significantly reduces deposit disputes. Tenants who understand the timeline and the documentation you will provide are less likely to assume the worst about deductions.

9. Forwarding Address Request

Request the tenant's forwarding address for:

  • Security deposit return
  • Any final utility statements
  • Future correspondence

Many landlords miss this step and then face delays returning the deposit. Include it in the letter explicitly and provide a way for the tenant to submit their address (a reply email, a form in the portal, or a line to write it in and return).

10. Utilities and Final Billing

If utilities are in the tenant's name, remind them to:

  • Keep utilities on through the last day of occupancy (so the unit can be inspected with power and water)
  • Schedule disconnection for the day after move-out, not the last day
  • Provide the utility company with their forwarding address for final billing
  • Transfer any trash service or internet accounts

If utilities are in your name, confirm the transition timeline.

11. Remaining Personal Property

State your policy on any items left behind after the move-out date. Most states have specific rules about how landlords must handle abandoned property. Include a brief statement:

"Any personal property remaining in the unit after [Date] will be treated as abandoned and may be disposed of in accordance with [State] law. Disposal costs may be deducted from the security deposit."

12. Early Move-Out and Holdover Procedures

If the tenant might leave before the lease end date, clarify whether early return of possession affects rent obligations. If they might stay past the end date, explain holdover rent procedures.

Move-Out Letter Template

Use this template as a foundation and adapt it to your state's specific requirements:

[Property Name or Your Name] [Your Address] [City, State, ZIP] [Phone] | [Email]

[Date]

[Tenant Name(s)] [Property Address, Unit] [City, State, ZIP]

Re: Move-Out Procedures — [Property Address, Unit]

Dear [Tenant Name],

Thank you for your notice to vacate. This letter confirms that your tenancy at [Property Address] will end on [Lease End Date]. Please review the following information carefully to ensure a smooth move-out.

Your Last Day of Occupancy: [Date] Key Return Deadline: All keys, fobs, and access devices must be returned by [Time] on [Date] via [Method/Location].

Move-Out Inspection A move-out inspection will be scheduled for [Date/Time or "please contact us to schedule"]. You are welcome to be present. Please contact us at [Phone/Email] to confirm your inspection time. The unit should be fully vacated and cleaned before the inspection.

Cleaning Expectations Please return the unit in clean condition, including:

  • All appliances cleaned inside and out
  • Bathrooms scrubbed (toilet, tub, sink, mirrors)
  • All floors swept, mopped, or vacuumed
  • Cabinets and drawers emptied and wiped
  • All trash removed from the unit and property
  • Outdoor areas (balcony, patio, yard) cleared and swept

Security Deposit Your security deposit of $[Amount] will be returned, along with an itemized statement of any deductions, within [X days — per state law] of your move-out date. To ensure timely return, please provide your forwarding address below or email it to [Address].

Deductions will be documented with photos and receipts. Normal wear and tear will not be charged. Please review your original move-in inspection form dated [Date] as a reference.

Forwarding Address: Name: ___________________________ Address: ___________________________ City, State, ZIP: ___________________________

Utilities Please ensure all utilities remain active through your last day. Schedule disconnection for [Day After Move-Out Date].

Personal Property Any belongings remaining in the unit after [Date] may be treated as abandoned under [State] law. Please ensure all personal property is removed by your move-out date.

If you have any questions about this process, please contact us at [Phone] or [Email]. We appreciate your tenancy at [Property Address] and wish you well in your next home.

Sincerely,

[Your Name] [Title, if applicable] [Contact Information]

Common Mistakes in Move-Out Letters

Being vague about cleaning standards. "Broom clean" or "clean condition" is subjective. The more specific your cleaning list, the fewer disputes you will have.

Failing to reference move-in documentation. Without this reference point, tenants can claim any damage was pre-existing. Always connect the move-out expectations to the documented move-in condition.

Missing the forwarding address request. This simple omission creates unnecessary delays in returning the security deposit.

Not sending it promptly. A move-out letter sent the week before move-out gives tenants no time to address issues. Send it within 48 hours of receiving notice.

Using threatening language. A move-out letter that leads with warnings about charges and legal consequences sets a combative tone. Lead with a professional, neutral framing and present requirements as information, not threats.

Integrating Move-Out Letters into Your Turnover Process

The move-out letter is the first step in your unit turnover process, not the last. After it goes out, the sequence continues: inspection, deposit accounting, repairs, cleaning, re-listing, tenant screening, and onboarding. For a complete framework for this process, see our guide to rental property turnover.

For guidance on communicating professionally throughout the tenant lifecycle — not just at move-out — see our resource on tenant communication tips and lease agreement best practices.

Summary

A well-crafted move-out letter to tenants includes: confirmation of notice, key return instructions, specific cleaning expectations, damage versus normal wear and tear distinctions, inspection scheduling details, security deposit return timeline and process, forwarding address request, and utility transfer instructions. Send it within 48 hours of receiving a tenant's notice, reference the original move-in documentation, and follow your state's specific security deposit requirements.

The landlords who do this consistently see fewer deposit disputes, smoother turnovers, and more cooperative tenant behavior in the final weeks of a tenancy. A professional move-out process is as important as a professional move-in process — both shape the overall tenant experience and your reputation as a landlord.
