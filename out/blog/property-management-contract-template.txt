24:T5962,<p>A property management contract template gives you a clear framework for the legal agreement between you and your property manager. Whether you are drafting a new contract or reviewing one a management company has handed you, knowing what should be in it and what the language should look like puts you in a much stronger position.</p>
<p>This resource covers every essential section of a property management contract template. You will find sample clause language, negotiation tips, common pitfalls to avoid, and a complete review checklist. Use this as your reference guide before you sign anything.</p>
<p><strong>Important note:</strong> This guide provides educational information and sample language, not legal advice. For additional legal context, <a href="https://www.nolo.com/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">Nolo's contract and landlord guides</a> offer helpful background on property management agreements. Every state has different landlord-tenant laws and property management regulations. Have a licensed real estate attorney in your state review any contract before you sign.</p>
<h2 id="why-you-need-a-strong-contract">Why You Need a Strong Contract</h2>
<p>Your property management contract defines the rules of your business relationship. It controls how your money is handled, who makes decisions about your property, and what happens when problems arise.</p>
<p>A strong contract:</p>
<ul>
<li>Prevents misunderstandings by putting every agreement in writing</li>
<li>Protects your investment by defining the manager's obligations</li>
<li>Limits your liability by clearly allocating responsibilities</li>
<li>Provides an exit strategy if the relationship does not work out</li>
<li>Creates accountability with measurable performance standards</li>
</ul>
<p>A weak contract, or no written contract at all, leaves you exposed to hidden fees, poor service, and legal disputes with no clear resolution. For a clause-by-clause explanation of what each section means, see our <a href="/blog/property-management-agreement-explained">property management agreement explained</a> guide.</p>
<h2 id="essential-contract-sections">Essential Contract Sections</h2>
<p>Every property management contract template should include these sections. We will go through each one with sample language and practical advice.</p>
<h3 id="section-1-parties-and-property-identification">Section 1: Parties and Property Identification</h3>
<p>This section identifies who is entering the agreement and what property is being managed.</p>
<p><strong>Sample clause:</strong></p>
<blockquote>
<p>This Property Management Agreement ("Agreement") is entered into as of [DATE] by and between [OWNER NAME/ENTITY], hereinafter referred to as "Owner," and [MANAGEMENT COMPANY NAME], hereinafter referred to as "Manager," for the management of the property located at [FULL PROPERTY ADDRESS], including [NUMBER] units, hereinafter referred to as "Property."</p>
</blockquote>
<p><strong>Tips:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>If you own the property through an LLC or trust, use the entity name as the Owner, not your personal name. This maintains your liability protection.</li>
<li>Include the complete legal address and a description of all structures and units covered.</li>
<li>If you are adding properties later, include a provision for amending the agreement to add or remove properties without rewriting the entire contract.</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="section-2-term-and-renewal">Section 2: Term and Renewal</h3>
<p>This defines how long the contract lasts and what happens when it expires.</p>
<p><strong>Sample clause:</strong></p>
<blockquote>
<p>This Agreement shall commence on [START DATE] and continue for an initial term of twelve (12) months. Thereafter, this Agreement shall automatically renew for successive twelve (12) month periods unless either party provides written notice of non-renewal at least sixty (60) days prior to the expiration of the then-current term.</p>
</blockquote>
<p><strong>Negotiation tips:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Avoid initial terms longer than one year, especially with a new manager. You need time to evaluate their performance.</li>
<li>Make sure the auto-renewal notice period is reasonable. Sixty days is standard. Ninety days or more can catch you off guard if you forget to send notice.</li>
<li>Consider negotiating a six-month initial term if this is your first time working with the company.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Common pitfall:</strong> Signing a multi-year contract with a steep cancellation penalty. This locks you in even if the service is poor.</p>
<h3 id="section-3-manager-s-duties-and-authority">Section 3: Manager's Duties and Authority</h3>
<p>This is the longest and most important section. It defines exactly what the manager will do.</p>
<p><strong>Sample clause for leasing authority:</strong></p>
<blockquote>
<p>Manager is authorized to advertise the Property for rent, show the Property to prospective tenants, screen applicants according to Manager's established and legally compliant criteria, and execute lease agreements on behalf of Owner for terms not exceeding twelve (12) months. Manager shall use commercially reasonable efforts to maintain occupancy and minimize vacancy periods.</p>
</blockquote>
<p><strong>Sample clause for maintenance authority:</strong></p>
<blockquote>
<p>Manager is authorized to make or cause to be made repairs, maintenance, and improvements to the Property without prior approval of Owner for any single expenditure not exceeding $[AMOUNT]. For expenditures exceeding this amount, Manager shall obtain Owner's prior written approval except in cases of emergency where delay would result in damage to the Property or risk to tenant health and safety. Manager shall provide Owner with written notice of any emergency expenditure within twenty-four (24) hours.</p>
</blockquote>
<p><strong>Sample clause for rent collection:</strong></p>
<blockquote>
<p>Manager shall collect all rents and other charges due from tenants. Manager shall enforce the collection of rent in accordance with the lease terms, including the assessment of late fees. Manager shall deposit all collected funds into the designated trust account within two (2) business days of receipt.</p>
</blockquote>
<p><strong>Tips:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>List every service you expect. If it is not written down, assume the manager will not provide it or will charge extra for it.</li>
<li>Define the maintenance spending limit clearly. $300 to $500 per incident is typical.</li>
<li>Specify emergency response expectations, including response time and tenant communication.</li>
<li>For a full picture of what management services should include, see our guide on <a href="/blog/what-does-a-property-manager-do">what a property manager does</a> and our overview of <a href="/blog/full-service-property-management">full service property management</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="section-4-owner-s-obligations">Section 4: Owner's Obligations</h3>
<p>The contract should also define your responsibilities as the property owner.</p>
<p><strong>Sample clause:</strong></p>
<blockquote>
<p>Owner shall maintain the Property in compliance with all applicable building codes, health and safety regulations, and habitability requirements. Owner shall maintain adequate property and liability insurance coverage naming Manager as an additional insured. Owner shall maintain a reserve fund of $[AMOUNT] in the trust account for routine expenses and emergencies. Owner shall provide Manager with timely responses to requests for authorization when required.</p>
</blockquote>
<p><strong>Tips:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>The reserve fund requirement is standard. Typical amounts range from $500 to $2,000 per property depending on age and condition.</li>
<li>Make sure the insurance requirement is reasonable and that you understand what coverage you need.</li>
<li>Clarify response time expectations. If the manager needs your approval for a $2,000 repair, how quickly must you respond?</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="section-5-compensation-and-fee-schedule">Section 5: Compensation and Fee Schedule</h3>
<p>Every fee must be clearly stated. No surprises.</p>
<p><strong>Sample clause:</strong></p>
<blockquote>
<p>Owner shall compensate Manager as follows:</p>
<p>a) <strong>Monthly Management Fee:</strong> [X]% of gross rent collected from the Property, due and payable monthly. No management fee shall be charged during periods when the Property is vacant and producing no rental income.</p>
<p>b) <strong>Leasing Fee:</strong> A fee equal to [X]% of the first month's rent for each new tenant placed by Manager. If a tenant placed by Manager vacates within the first six (6) months of their lease, Manager shall re-lease the Property at no additional leasing fee.</p>
<p>c) <strong>Lease Renewal Fee:</strong> A flat fee of $[AMOUNT] for each lease renewal executed by Manager.</p>
<p>d) <strong>No Additional Fees:</strong> Manager shall not charge any fees beyond those explicitly listed in this Agreement without Owner's prior written consent.</p>
</blockquote>
<p><strong>Negotiation tips:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Always insist that the management fee is based on collected rent, not scheduled rent. This aligns incentives.</li>
<li>Negotiate a tenant placement guarantee. If their screening fails and the tenant leaves early, you should not pay another leasing fee.</li>
<li>The "no additional fees" clause is powerful. It prevents the manager from adding charges that were not disclosed upfront.</li>
<li>Compare fee structures against market norms using our <a href="/blog/property-management-fees-breakdown">property management fees breakdown</a> and <a href="/blog/property-management-cost-per-door">cost per door analysis</a>. The <a href="https://www.narpm.org/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">NARPM contract standards and resources</a> can also serve as a useful benchmark.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Common pitfall:</strong> Agreements that reference a separate "fee schedule" document that can be changed without amending the contract. All fees should be in the contract itself.</p>
<h3 id="section-6-trust-account-and-financial-management">Section 6: Trust Account and Financial Management</h3>
<p>This section governs how your money is handled. It is legally regulated in every state.</p>
<p><strong>Sample clause:</strong></p>
<blockquote>
<p>Manager shall deposit all rents, security deposits, and other funds collected on behalf of Owner into a federally insured trust account maintained by Manager at [BANK NAME]. Security deposits shall be held in a separate account as required by [STATE] law. Manager shall not commingle trust funds with Manager's operating funds. Manager shall disburse Owner's net proceeds on or before the [DAY] of each month for the preceding month's activity. Manager shall maintain a minimum reserve balance of $[AMOUNT] in the trust account for property-related expenses.</p>
</blockquote>
<p><strong>Tips:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Verify that the manager actually maintains separate trust accounts. This is a legal requirement, not a negotiable term.</li>
<li>Know your state's security deposit laws. Some states require interest-bearing accounts. Some require specific disclosures to tenants.</li>
<li>Clarify the disbursement schedule so you know when to expect your money each month.</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="section-7-reporting-and-communication">Section 7: Reporting and Communication</h3>
<p>Define what information you receive and how often.</p>
<p><strong>Sample clause:</strong></p>
<blockquote>
<p>Manager shall provide Owner with the following reports:</p>
<p>a) Monthly financial statement detailing all income received, expenses paid, and net proceeds, delivered by the fifteenth (15th) day of the following month.</p>
<p>b) Year-end financial summary and IRS Form 1099, delivered by January 31 of each year.</p>
<p>c) Property inspection reports with photographs, conducted no less than [semi-annually/quarterly].</p>
<p>d) Vacancy and marketing reports during any period the Property is unoccupied.</p>
<p>e) Online portal access for real-time viewing of financial data, maintenance requests, and property documents.</p>
<p>Manager shall respond to Owner communications within one (1) business day.</p>
</blockquote>
<p><strong>Tips:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Specific dates and deadlines create accountability. Vague language like "regular reports" is meaningless.</li>
<li>Portal access is standard in modern property management. If a manager does not offer it, they are behind the industry.</li>
<li>The one-business-day response time is reasonable. You are not asking for instant replies, but you deserve timely communication.</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="section-8-insurance-and-liability">Section 8: Insurance and Liability</h3>
<p>This section allocates risk between you and the manager.</p>
<p><strong>Sample clause:</strong></p>
<blockquote>
<p>Manager shall maintain the following insurance coverage throughout the term of this Agreement: (a) Commercial General Liability insurance with limits of not less than $1,000,000 per occurrence; (b) Professional Liability (Errors and Omissions) insurance with limits of not less than $500,000; (c) Fidelity Bond or Crime insurance with limits of not less than $250,000; (d) Workers' Compensation insurance as required by law.</p>
<p>Manager shall indemnify and hold Owner harmless from any claims, damages, or expenses arising from Manager's negligent acts or omissions in the performance of this Agreement. Owner shall indemnify and hold Manager harmless from any claims arising from the physical condition of the Property or Owner's failure to comply with applicable laws and regulations.</p>
</blockquote>
<p><strong>Tips:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Make sure the indemnification is mutual and balanced. You should not be holding the manager harmless for their own negligence.</li>
<li>Request certificates of insurance before signing and annually thereafter.</li>
<li>Verify that fidelity bond coverage is adequate to cover the maximum amount of your funds the manager will hold at any given time.</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="section-9-termination">Section 9: Termination</h3>
<p>This section defines how either party can end the agreement.</p>
<p><strong>Sample clause:</strong></p>
<blockquote>
<p>Either party may terminate this Agreement without cause upon sixty (60) days' written notice to the other party. No early termination fee shall apply. Either party may terminate this Agreement immediately for cause, including but not limited to: material breach of this Agreement, fraud, misappropriation of funds, loss of required licenses, or failure to maintain required insurance.</p>
<p>Upon termination, Manager shall: (a) transfer all trust account funds to Owner or Owner's designee within thirty (30) days; (b) provide all financial records, tenant files, and property documents to Owner; (c) assign all existing tenant leases to Owner or Owner's designee; (d) return all keys, access codes, and property-related items to Owner.</p>
</blockquote>
<p><strong>Negotiation tips:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Push for no early termination fee, or at minimum a fee that decreases over time.</li>
<li>The 60-day notice period gives both parties time to prepare for a smooth transition.</li>
<li>The transition obligations are critical. Without them, a departing manager could delay transferring your funds and documents. This is one of the most common issues landlords face when switching managers.</li>
<li>For more on evaluating whether you need a manager at all, see our comparison of <a href="/blog/property-management-vs-self-managing">property management vs self managing</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="section-10-dispute-resolution">Section 10: Dispute Resolution</h3>
<p><strong>Sample clause:</strong></p>
<blockquote>
<p>Any dispute arising under this Agreement shall first be submitted to mediation administered by [MEDIATION ORGANIZATION] in [COUNTY, STATE]. If mediation does not resolve the dispute within sixty (60) days, either party may pursue binding arbitration or litigation in the courts of [COUNTY, STATE]. Each party shall bear its own costs and attorney fees unless a court or arbitrator awards fees to the prevailing party.</p>
</blockquote>
<p><strong>Tips:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Mediation first is always a good idea. It is cheaper and faster than arbitration or court.</li>
<li>Make sure the venue is convenient for you. If the manager's contract specifies arbitration in a different state, negotiate to change it.</li>
<li>"Each party bears its own costs" is standard. A "prevailing party" attorney fee provision can be either helpful or risky depending on the outcome.</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="state-specific-requirements-to-watch-for">State-Specific Requirements to Watch For</h2>
<p>Property management contracts must comply with state law. Here are areas where requirements differ significantly:</p>
<p><strong>Security deposits:</strong> States set maximum deposit amounts, required holding methods (separate accounts, interest-bearing accounts), and return timelines. Your contract must comply.</p>
<p><strong>Eviction procedures:</strong> State law dictates notice periods, grounds for eviction, and court filing requirements. The contract should reference compliance with state eviction law rather than specifying procedures that might conflict.</p>
<p><strong>Licensing requirements:</strong> Some states require specific property management licenses. Others allow management under a real estate broker's license. Make sure the contract references the correct license type.</p>
<p><strong>Trust account regulations:</strong> State real estate commissions regulate trust accounts. The contract should comply with these specific requirements.</p>
<p><strong>Rent control and tenant protections:</strong> In states and cities with rent control, the contract must address compliance with rent increase limitations and tenant protection ordinances.</p>
<p><strong>Consult a local attorney</strong> who knows your state's requirements. A template from the internet, including the sample language in this guide, may not comply with your state's specific laws.</p>
<h2 id="what-to-customize-in-any-template">What to Customize in Any Template</h2>
<p>No template is ready to sign without customization. Here is what you should always tailor:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Fee amounts and percentages</strong> based on your negotiations and market norms</li>
<li><strong>Maintenance spending limits</strong> based on your property's age, condition, and your risk tolerance</li>
<li><strong>Reporting requirements</strong> based on your information needs</li>
<li><strong>Insurance minimums</strong> based on your property value and risk exposure</li>
<li><strong>Termination terms</strong> based on your comfort level with the manager</li>
<li><strong>Reserve fund amounts</strong> based on your property's maintenance history</li>
<li><strong>Specific services included</strong> based on your management needs</li>
<li><strong>Communication expectations</strong> based on your preferred level of involvement</li>
</ol>
<p>Use the template as a starting point, not a finished product.</p>
