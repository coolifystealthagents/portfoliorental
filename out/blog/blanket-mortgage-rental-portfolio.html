As your rental property portfolio grows, managing multiple individual mortgages becomes increasingly complex and expensive. Each property requires its own loan application, appraisal, title search, and closing, a time-consuming and costly process that multiplies with every purchase.

Blanket mortgages offer a different approach: a single loan that covers multiple properties simultaneously, streamlining your financing and potentially unlocking better terms.

For landlords managing five, ten, or twenty-plus properties, understanding blanket mortgages for rental portfolios is essential knowledge. This guide covers how they work, who offers them, how to qualify, and when they make more sense than financing each property individually.

What Is a Blanket Mortgage?

A blanket mortgage (also called a blanket loan or portfolio loan) is a single mortgage that covers two or more properties. Instead of taking out separate loans for each rental, you consolidate them under one loan with one payment, one interest rate, and one set of terms.

The defining feature of most blanket mortgages is the release clause (sometimes called a partial release clause). This provision allows you to sell or refinance individual properties within the portfolio without having to pay off the entire loan, you simply pay down a portion of the principal that corresponds to the released property.

Blanket mortgages are most commonly used by:

  • Landlords with 5+ properties looking to simplify their financing
  • Investors buying a package of properties from a single seller
  • Real estate operators transitioning from scattered individual loans to portfolio-level financing
  • Investors who've hit conventional loan limits (typically 10 properties)

How Blanket Mortgages Work

The mechanics are straightforward. A lender evaluates the combined value and income of all properties included in the loan and issues a single loan secured by all of them.

The lender files liens on all included properties.

Example Structure

Suppose you own six rental properties with a combined appraised value of $1.2 million and combined gross rents of $9,600/month. A blanket lender might offer:

  • Loan amount: $840,000 (70% LTV)
  • Interest rate: 7.5% (30-year amortization)
  • Monthly payment: ~$5,875
  • Loan type: Commercial or portfolio loan

If you later sell one property appraised at $150,000, the release clause would require you to pay down the loan by a negotiated percentage of that value (often 110, 120% of the allocated loan amount for that property) before the lender releases the lien on that specific property.

Cross-Collateralization

A critical characteristic of blanket mortgages is cross-collateralization. All properties in the loan serve as collateral for the entire debt.

This means if you default on the loan, the lender can pursue any or all of the properties, not just one. This is a significant risk to understand and accept before entering a blanket loan arrangement.

When a Blanket Mortgage Makes Sense

A blanket mortgage for your rental portfolio makes strategic sense in specific situations.

You're Buying a Package of Properties

When a seller is offering a portfolio of properties as a bundle, and won't sell individually, a blanket mortgage lets you finance the package in one transaction. This is common in buying rental portfolios in bulk from departing landlords, institutional sellers, or estate sales.

You've Hit Conventional Loan Limits

Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac conventional guidelines limit most investors to 10 financed properties. Once you've hit that ceiling, blanket loans and portfolio lenders become your primary financing options.

Commercial blanket mortgages have no such limits.

You Want to Simplify Your Finances

Managing 10 separate loans, each with different lenders, due dates, interest rates, and term expirations, is an administrative burden. A blanket loan consolidates all of this into a single monthly payment, one relationship, and one set of renewal terms.

You're Building Efficiently

Rather than paying closing costs on five separate loans, consolidating into one blanket loan reduces origination and closing costs. This is particularly valuable when building a rental property portfolio aggressively.

Types of Lenders Offering Blanket Mortgages

Traditional banks, especially large national banks, generally don't offer blanket mortgages for residential rental portfolios. You'll need to look at alternative lender types.

Community Banks and Credit Unions

Local community banks and credit unions are often the best source for blanket loans on smaller portfolios (2, 10 properties). They have relationship-based underwriting, keep loans in-house, and have flexibility that national banks lack.

Building a relationship with a community banker who understands your market is invaluable.

Commercial Mortgage Lenders

For larger portfolios or higher loan amounts, commercial mortgage lenders (sometimes called commercial real estate lenders) specialize in multi-property and apartment financing. They underwrite deals based on property income rather than personal income.

Portfolio Lenders and Non-QM Lenders

Many non-bank lenders now offer blanket loan products specifically designed for residential rental portfolios. Companies like Kiavi, Lima One Capital, and Visio Lending have programs for portfolios of single-family and small multifamily properties.

These are essentially aggregated DSCR loans structured as a blanket product.

Hard Money and Bridge Lenders

Some hard money lenders offer portfolio bridge loans for short-term purchase of multiple properties. These carry higher rates but allow you to acquire a package quickly and then refinance into permanent financing.

Qualification Requirements for a Blanket Mortgage

Blanket mortgage underwriting differs significantly from conventional loan underwriting. Here's what lenders typically evaluate:

Combined Net Operating Income (NOI)

The primary qualifier for a blanket loan is the income the portfolio generates. Lenders calculate the combined NOI of all included properties (gross rent minus vacancy, taxes, insurance, and maintenance) and compare it to the proposed debt service.

Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR) is the key metric. Most blanket lenders require a DSCR of 1.20, 1.30, meaning the portfolio generates 20, 30% more income than required to service the debt. A portfolio generating $8,000/month in NOI with $6,000/month in debt payments would have a DSCR of 1.33, a strong position.

Combined Loan-to-Value (LTV)

Blanket lenders typically lend at 65, 75% of the combined appraised value. Getting accurate appraisals of all included properties is part of the application process, and properties with deferred maintenance or poor condition may be valued lower than expected.

Borrower Experience

Lenders want to know you have a track record managing rental properties. Be prepared to document your rental history, provide rent rolls, and show operational expertise.

First-time investors rarely qualify for blanket loan programs.

Credit and Liquidity

While blanket lenders focus more on property income than personal financials, they still want to see:

  • Credit scores of 660+ (680+ preferred)
  • Adequate liquid reserves (often 6, 12 months of payments)
  • Clean payment history on existing loans

Blanket Mortgage Terms and Rates

Blanket mortgage rates are typically higher than conventional investment property rates, reflecting the commercial underwriting and portfolio risk:

Feature Typical Range
Interest Rate 6.5, 9.5%
Loan Term 5, 30 years
Amortization 20, 30 years
LTV 65, 75%
DSCR Requirement 1.20, 1.30x
Minimum Loan Size $500,000, $1M
Minimum Properties 2, 5

Many blanket loans are structured as 5/1, 7/1, or 10/1 ARMs, meaning the rate is fixed for an initial period and then adjusts annually. Be aware of rate adjustment caps and worst-case payment scenarios before committing.

Pros and Cons of Blanket Mortgages

Advantages

  • Simplified administration: One payment, one lender, one set of terms
  • Reduced closing costs: One appraisal and title process instead of many
  • No conventional loan count limits: Finance 20, 30, or more properties
  • Faster purchases: Adding properties to an existing blanket loan can be faster than originating new individual loans
  • Release clause flexibility: Sell individual properties without repaying the full loan

Disadvantages

  • Cross-collateralization risk: Default on one property's income, lose them all
  • Higher rates: Blanket loans typically carry higher rates than conventional investment property loans
  • Less competitive than individual financing: For properties you plan to hold long-term with no need to sell, individual loans may be cheaper overall
  • Complexity on exit: Selling or refinancing individual properties requires lender coordination

Blanket Mortgages vs. Individual DSCR Loans

For many investors with 5, 15 properties, the question isn't blanket vs. conventional, it's blanket vs. individual DSCR loans. Here's how they compare:

Blanket Mortgage Individual DSCR Loans
Admin burden Low (one loan) High (multiple loans)
Rate Slightly higher Slightly lower per loan
Cross-collateral risk Yes No
Flexibility to sell Release clause required Sell anytime
Minimum portfolio Often 5+ properties One at a time

For investors who intend to hold all properties long-term and want simplicity, blanket mortgages win on administration. For investors who may sell selectively or want the flexibility of individual ownership, multiple DSCR loans may be preferable.

Fannie Mae's rental housing programs offer additional context on how the secondary market treats aggregated rental portfolios, which affects what private lenders can offer.

Getting Started with a Blanket Mortgage

If you're ready to explore blanket mortgage financing for your rental portfolio:

  1. Compile your portfolio data: Gather current leases, rent rolls, property tax statements, and insurance policies for all properties you want to include.
  2. Get appraisals or BPOs: Know about what each property is worth.
  3. Calculate your combined DSCR: Estimate whether your portfolio's income supports the loan you need.
  4. Shop lenders: Approach 3, 5 portfolio lenders or community banks. Blanket loan terms vary significantly between lenders.
  5. Negotiate the release clause: This is critical. Make sure the release clause allows you to sell or refinance individual properties at a reasonable cost.

A blanket mortgage for your rental portfolio is a sophisticated financing tool that makes the most sense once you've built meaningful scale. Combined with other rental property financing options and a clear understanding of your portfolio performance benchmarks, it can be a cornerstone of your long-term investment strategy.
