26:T2676,<p>The <a href="https://www.justice.gov/crt/fair-housing-act-1">Fair Housing Act</a> requires landlords to make reasonable accommodations and allow reasonable modifications for tenants with disabilities. These are not optional courtesies — they are legal obligations that apply to nearly every residential rental property in the country.</p>
<p>Understanding the difference between accommodations and modifications, knowing what you must pay for versus what the tenant pays for, and having a clear process in place will keep you compliant and protect your business.</p>
<h2 id="accommodations-vs-modifications-key-differences">Accommodations vs Modifications: Key Differences</h2>
<p>These two terms are often confused, but they have distinct meanings under fair housing law.</p>
<h3 id="reasonable-accommodations">Reasonable Accommodations</h3>
<p>A reasonable accommodation is a change to a rule, policy, practice, or service. No physical changes to the property are required.</p>
<p>Examples:</p>
<ul>
<li>Allowing an assistance animal despite a no-pet policy</li>
<li>Reserving a parking space for a tenant with mobility limitations</li>
<li>Permitting a live-in aide</li>
<li>Adjusting rent payment schedules</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Cost responsibility:</strong> The landlord bears the cost of reasonable accommodations because they involve policy changes, not physical work.</p>
<p>For a deep dive into accommodations specifically, see our <a href="/blog/reasonable-accommodation-rental-housing">reasonable accommodation guide</a>.</p>
<h3 id="reasonable-modifications">Reasonable Modifications</h3>
<p>A reasonable modification is a physical change to the unit, common areas, or premises that allows a tenant with a disability to fully use the property.</p>
<p>Examples:</p>
<ul>
<li>Installing grab bars in the bathroom</li>
<li>Widening doorways for wheelchair access</li>
<li>Building a ramp at the entrance</li>
<li>Lowering countertops or shelving</li>
<li>Adding visual fire alarms for deaf tenants</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Cost responsibility in private housing:</strong> In most private rental housing, the tenant pays for modifications. However, the landlord must permit the modifications if they are reasonable.</p>
<p><strong>Cost responsibility in federally funded housing:</strong> If the property receives federal financial assistance, the landlord must pay for and complete reasonable modifications.</p>
<h2 id="what-the-law-requires">What the Law Requires</h2>
<h3 id="properties-covered">Properties Covered</h3>
<p>The Fair Housing Act covers virtually all residential housing, including:</p>
<ul>
<li>Apartment complexes</li>
<li>Single-family homes (when rented through a broker or with more than three units)</li>
<li>Condominiums and townhomes</li>
<li>Mobile home parks</li>
<li>Public and subsidized housing</li>
</ul>
<p>Limited exemptions exist for owner-occupied buildings with four or fewer units and single-family homes rented without a broker and without discriminatory advertising.</p>
<h3 id="disability-definition">Disability Definition</h3>
<p>Fair housing law defines disability broadly:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Physical impairment</strong> that substantially limits one or more major life activities</li>
<li><strong>Mental impairment</strong> that substantially limits one or more major life activities</li>
<li><strong>A record of</strong> such impairment</li>
<li><strong>Being regarded as</strong> having such impairment</li>
</ul>
<p>This includes mobility impairments, visual and hearing impairments, mental health conditions, intellectual disabilities, chronic illnesses, and many other conditions. The <a href="https://www.ada.gov">ADA.gov resource center</a> provides additional guidance on disability definitions and accessibility standards.</p>
<h2 id="common-modification-requests-and-how-to-handle-them">Common Modification Requests and How to Handle Them</h2>
<h3 id="grab-bars-and-bathroom-modifications">Grab Bars and Bathroom Modifications</h3>
<p>Among the most common requests. The tenant typically pays for installation. You may require that a licensed contractor perform the work and that the tenant agree to restore the bathroom to its original condition at move-out if the modifications affect the next tenant's use.</p>
<p>However, think carefully before requiring restoration. Grab bars and accessible features often benefit future tenants and add value to the property.</p>
<h3 id="ramps-and-entrance-modifications">Ramps and Entrance Modifications</h3>
<p>A tenant who uses a wheelchair may request permission to build a ramp. You must allow it if it is reasonable. You may:</p>
<ul>
<li>Require the modification to meet building codes</li>
<li>Require the work to be done by a qualified contractor</li>
<li>Require the tenant to carry liability insurance during construction</li>
<li>Request restoration at move-out if the modification is for a single unit entrance (but not if it benefits all tenants)</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="wider-doorways">Wider Doorways</h3>
<p>Doorway widening is a significant modification. You may require professional installation and may request restoration for interior doorways. Common area doorways that are widened typically benefit all tenants and should not require restoration.</p>
<h3 id="service-and-assistance-animals">Service and Assistance Animals</h3>
<p>While technically an accommodation (not a modification), assistance animal requests are the most common disability-related request landlords receive. You must waive no-pet policies, pet fees, and breed restrictions for verified assistance animals. See our <a href="/blog/emotional-support-animal-landlord-guidelines">ESA landlord guidelines</a> for detailed procedures.</p>
<h2 id="the-interactive-process">The Interactive Process</h2>
<p>When you receive a disability-related request, engage in good faith dialogue with the tenant.</p>
<h3 id="steps-in-the-interactive-process">Steps in the Interactive Process</h3>
<ol>
<li><strong>Acknowledge the request</strong> promptly and in writing</li>
<li><strong>Verify the disability</strong> if not apparent (request documentation from a healthcare provider)</li>
<li><strong>Discuss the specific need</strong> — understand what barrier the tenant faces</li>
<li><strong>Evaluate feasibility</strong> — is the requested modification or accommodation reasonable?</li>
<li><strong>Propose alternatives</strong> if the specific request is not feasible</li>
<li><strong>Reach agreement</strong> and document the outcome</li>
<li><strong>Implement</strong> the accommodation or permit the modification</li>
</ol>
<h3 id="when-you-can-deny-a-request">When You Can Deny a Request</h3>
<p>Denial is appropriate only when:</p>
<ul>
<li>The request would impose an <strong>undue financial or administrative burden</strong></li>
<li>The request would <strong>fundamentally alter</strong> the nature of the housing operation</li>
<li>The modification would create a <strong>structural hazard</strong> that cannot be mitigated</li>
<li>The person does not meet the legal definition of disability and cannot provide documentation</li>
</ul>
<p>Document your reasoning thoroughly if you deny any request.</p>
<h2 id="design-and-construction-requirements">Design and Construction Requirements</h2>
<p>Properties built after March 1991 with four or more units must meet accessibility design requirements under the Fair Housing Act. These include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Accessible common areas</li>
<li>Doors wide enough for wheelchairs</li>
<li>Accessible routes into and through units</li>
<li>Reinforced walls in bathrooms for later grab bar installation</li>
<li>Accessible light switches and thermostats</li>
<li>Kitchens and bathrooms usable by wheelchair users</li>
</ul>
<p>If your property was built after 1991 and does not meet these standards, you may face design and construction complaints regardless of whether a specific tenant requests modifications. HUD's <a href="https://www.hud.gov/program_offices/fair_housing_equal_opp">fair housing office</a> enforces these design and construction requirements.</p>
<h2 id="insurance-and-liability-considerations">Insurance and Liability Considerations</h2>
<p>When allowing modifications:</p>
<ul>
<li>Require tenants to use licensed, insured contractors</li>
<li>Verify that modifications meet local building codes</li>
<li>Maintain documentation of approved modifications and contractor credentials</li>
<li>Update your property records to reflect structural changes</li>
<li>Review your <a href="/blog/landlord-insurance-requirements">landlord insurance coverage</a> to ensure modifications are covered</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="record-keeping-requirements">Record-Keeping Requirements</h2>
<p>Maintain records of all disability-related requests, including:</p>
<ul>
<li>Date and nature of the request</li>
<li>Documentation received and verification steps taken</li>
<li>Interactive process notes and communications</li>
<li>Decision and reasoning</li>
<li>Modification specifications and contractor information</li>
<li>Restoration agreements if applicable</li>
</ul>
<p>Keep these records confidential and separate from general tenant files. See our <a href="/blog/rental-property-record-keeping">record-keeping guide</a> for best practices.</p>
<h2 id="key-takeaways">Key Takeaways</h2>
<p>Disability accommodations and modifications are a legal requirement under the Fair Housing Act. Allow reasonable modifications, engage in the interactive process in good faith, and document every step. The cost of compliance is almost always lower than the cost of a fair housing complaint, and many modifications improve your property's accessibility for all tenants.</p>
<p>For the complete framework of your obligations, review our <a href="/blog/fair-housing-compliance-rental">fair housing compliance guide</a>.</p>
