21:T37a2,<p>Running a background check on a rental applicant is one of the most legally complex steps in the screening process. Done correctly, it gives landlords meaningful information about an applicant's history. Done carelessly, it exposes landlords to liability under federal law, state statute, and fair housing regulations — even when the landlord's intent was simply to protect their property.</p>
<p>This guide covers the legal framework for background checks in residential rentals, including FCRA compliance obligations, ban-the-box laws, HUD guidance on the use of criminal records, how to conduct individualized assessments, what actually justifies a denial, and how to issue adverse action notices when required.</p>
<h2 id="the-legal-framework-what-governs-background-checks">The Legal Framework: What Governs Background Checks</h2>
<p>Three major bodies of law govern background checks on rental applicants:</p>
<p><strong>The Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA)</strong> — federal law administered by the Federal Trade Commission (FTC) — governs how consumer reports (including criminal background checks obtained through third-party screening agencies) can be obtained and used. The <a href="https://www.ftc.gov/tips-advice/business-center/guidance/using-consumer-reports-what-landlords-need-know">FTC's guide on tenant background checks</a> is the primary compliance reference for landlords.</p>
<p><strong>Fair Housing Act and HUD Guidance</strong> — while the Fair Housing Act does not name criminal history as a protected class, HUD has issued guidance stating that blanket bans on applicants with criminal records can constitute disparate impact discrimination based on race and national origin, which are protected classes. The <a href="https://www.hud.gov/sites/documents/HUD_OGCGUIDAPPFHASTANDCR.PDF">HUD guidance on criminal records</a> requires landlords to conduct individualized assessments rather than categorical exclusions.</p>
<p><strong>State and Local Law</strong> — many states and cities have enacted ban-the-box laws, restrictions on the lookback period for criminal records, and limitations on what types of convictions can be considered. These vary significantly by jurisdiction and in some cases go further than federal requirements.</p>
<h2 id="fcra-compliance-the-basics-for-landlords">FCRA Compliance: The Basics for Landlords</h2>
<p>The FCRA applies whenever you use a third-party consumer reporting agency (CRA) to obtain a background check. If you run the check yourself through court records, the FCRA does not apply to that specific search, though fair housing laws still do.</p>
<p>When using a CRA for a background check rental applicant screening:</p>
<h3 id="1-disclose-and-obtain-written-authorization">1. Disclose and Obtain Written Authorization</h3>
<p>Before ordering a background check, you must provide the applicant with a clear, standalone written disclosure informing them that a consumer report may be obtained. This disclosure cannot be buried in the rental application — it must be a separate document. You must then obtain the applicant's written authorization before ordering the report.</p>
<h3 id="2-certify-your-permissible-purpose-to-the-cra">2. Certify Your Permissible Purpose to the CRA</h3>
<p>When ordering a report, you certify to the CRA that you have a permissible purpose (evaluating a housing application), that you have obtained authorization, and that you will comply with FCRA adverse action requirements.</p>
<h3 id="3-follow-adverse-action-procedures">3. Follow Adverse Action Procedures</h3>
<p>If you deny an applicant or take other adverse action (such as requiring a larger deposit or a co-signer) based on information in a background check, FCRA requires a specific process:</p>
<p><strong>Pre-adverse action</strong>: Before making a final adverse decision, provide the applicant with a copy of the background report and a summary of their rights under the FCRA. Give the applicant a reasonable opportunity — typically five business days — to review the report and dispute inaccuracies.</p>
<p><strong>Final adverse action notice</strong>: If you proceed with denial after the pre-adverse action period, send a formal adverse action notice that includes the name, address, and phone number of the CRA; a statement that the CRA did not make the adverse decision; notice of the applicant's right to a free copy of the report within 60 days; and notice of the right to dispute report accuracy.</p>
<p>Failure to follow these procedures can result in actual damages, statutory damages of $100–$1,000 per violation, punitive damages, and attorney fees.</p>
<p>Review our <a href="/blog/tenant-screening-best-practices">tenant screening best practices guide</a> for a broader overview of screening procedures that integrate FCRA compliance.</p>
<h2 id="hud-guidance-on-criminal-records-individualized-assessment">HUD Guidance on Criminal Records: Individualized Assessment</h2>
<p>The most significant shift in background check practice for landlords over the past decade is HUD's 2016 guidance that blanket policies excluding all applicants with criminal records likely violate the Fair Housing Act through disparate impact.</p>
<p>The reasoning: because Black and Hispanic Americans are incarcerated at higher rates than white Americans, policies that exclude all applicants with any criminal record tend to disproportionately exclude applicants in protected racial classes, even when the landlord has no discriminatory intent.</p>
<p>This does not mean landlords cannot consider criminal records. It means they cannot apply categorical exclusions without conducting individualized assessments for each applicant.</p>
<h3 id="what-individualized-assessment-means">What Individualized Assessment Means</h3>
<p>An individualized assessment requires landlords to consider:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>The nature of the crime</strong>: A violent offense, offense against a person, or offense involving property damage is more relevant to housing than a financial crime or drug possession charge.</li>
<li><strong>How long ago the offense occurred</strong>: The passage of time significantly reduces the predictive weight of most offenses. A conviction from 15 years ago tells you less about current risk than one from 18 months ago.</li>
<li><strong>The number of offenses</strong>: A pattern of repeated offenses is more significant than a single isolated incident.</li>
<li><strong>Evidence of rehabilitation</strong>: Completion of treatment programs, stable employment, stable housing history since release, and character references are all relevant.</li>
<li><strong>Whether the offense directly relates to tenancy</strong>: An offense involving arson, property destruction, or violence against a neighbor is directly relevant to residential tenancy. A fraud conviction or DUI has less obvious bearing.</li>
</ul>
<p>Document your individualized assessment in writing for every applicant whose criminal record you consider as part of a denial or adverse action. This documentation is your defense if a fair housing complaint is filed.</p>
<h2 id="ban-the-box-laws-in-rental-housing">Ban-the-Box Laws in Rental Housing</h2>
<p>Ban-the-box laws originated in employment law but have expanded into rental housing in several jurisdictions. These laws generally restrict when and how landlords can ask about criminal history during the application process.</p>
<p>Common requirements in jurisdictions with rental ban-the-box laws:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Prohibit criminal history questions on the initial application</strong>: Landlords cannot ask "Have you ever been convicted of a crime?" until after the applicant has been conditionally approved or screened on other criteria.</li>
<li><strong>Limit the lookback period</strong>: Some jurisdictions limit consideration of criminal history to offenses within the past three, five, or seven years.</li>
<li><strong>Prohibit consideration of arrests without convictions</strong>: Arrests that did not result in conviction cannot be used as a basis for denial in most jurisdictions with these laws.</li>
<li><strong>Require written notice and opportunity to respond</strong>: Before denying an applicant based on criminal history, some jurisdictions require the landlord to notify the applicant, provide the records considered, and allow the applicant to provide additional information.</li>
</ul>
<p>Notable jurisdictions with rental-specific ban-the-box or criminal record restriction laws include Seattle, San Francisco, New York City, Newark, and the state of Oregon. Check with a local landlord-tenant attorney or your local housing authority for the current rules in your area.</p>
<p>See our state-by-state resource on <a href="/blog/landlord-tenant-law-by-state">landlord-tenant law</a> for jurisdiction-specific information.</p>
<h2 id="what-criminal-records-can-legitimately-disqualify-an-applicant">What Criminal Records Can Legitimately Disqualify an Applicant</h2>
<p>Given the requirements above, what can landlords actually use as a basis for denial?</p>
<p><strong>Convictions that may legitimately justify denial:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Manufacturing or distributing controlled substances (federal law requires landlords to deny applicants with drug manufacturing convictions)</li>
<li>Violent crimes, particularly recent ones or patterns of violence</li>
<li>Crimes directly involving rental property or neighbors (destruction of property, arson, stalking, domestic violence convictions where the victim was a co-resident)</li>
<li>Sex offender registration, depending on jurisdiction and nature of offense</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Items that generally should not be the sole basis for denial:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Arrests without conviction</li>
<li>Offenses that were expunged or sealed (in most states, expunged records cannot be used against an applicant)</li>
<li>Very old convictions (10+ years) without subsequent criminal history</li>
<li>Juvenile adjudications (in most jurisdictions)</li>
<li>Minor drug possession charges, particularly in states that have decriminalized or legalized the substance in question</li>
</ul>
<p>For guidance on how criminal background checks interact with tenant rights, review our article on <a href="/blog/tenant-rights-responsibilities">tenant rights and responsibilities</a>.</p>
<h2 id="building-a-written-criminal-background-check-policy">Building a Written Criminal Background Check Policy</h2>
<p>To protect yourself legally and apply background checks consistently, your written screening policy should include:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>A statement that you conduct criminal background checks</strong>: Disclose this upfront in your listing and application materials.</li>
<li><strong>The CRA you use</strong>: Name the screening service and confirm it is a licensed CRA.</li>
<li><strong>The types of offenses you consider</strong>: Be specific. "Any criminal history" is not a defensible standard; "convictions within the past seven years for violent felonies, drug manufacturing, or crimes against property or persons" is.</li>
<li><strong>Your individualized assessment process</strong>: Document that applicants will not be denied solely on the existence of a criminal record but will receive individualized review.</li>
<li><strong>Your lookback period</strong>: Define how far back in an applicant's criminal history you look.</li>
<li><strong>Your adverse action procedure</strong>: Document the pre-adverse action and final adverse action notice process in compliance with FCRA.</li>
</ol>
<p>A written policy does three things: it guides your staff or process to consistent decisions, it demonstrates good faith in a fair housing investigation, and it gives applicants a fair understanding of what to expect.</p>
<p>For the broader screening framework this policy should sit within, see our <a href="/blog/fair-housing-compliance-rental">fair housing compliance guide</a>.</p>
<h2 id="practical-steps-for-running-a-background-check">Practical Steps for Running a Background Check</h2>
<ol>
<li><strong>Choose an FCRA-compliant CRA</strong>: Use a reputable tenant screening service that is certified as an FCRA-compliant consumer reporting agency. Avoid relying solely on free or unofficial internet searches, which are not FCRA-compliant and often inaccurate.</li>
<li><strong>Provide the standalone disclosure and get written consent</strong>: Do this before ordering any report. Keep a copy of the signed authorization in the applicant's file.</li>
<li><strong>Review the full report, not just a summary</strong>: Background check reports often include misidentifications, duplicate records, and errors. Confirm that records belong to the applicant before acting on them.</li>
<li><strong>Cross-reference with your written criteria</strong>: Evaluate findings against your documented policy. If the offense falls outside your stated criteria, do not use it as a basis for denial.</li>
<li><strong>Conduct the individualized assessment</strong>: For any disqualifying record, document the nature, recency, and context of the offense and your analysis.</li>
<li><strong>Follow adverse action procedures</strong>: If you deny or take other adverse action based on the report, follow the two-step FCRA process.</li>
</ol>
<h2 id="conclusion">Conclusion</h2>
<p>Background checks are a legitimate and important tool in tenant screening, but they come with significant legal obligations. Landlords who run checks carelessly — using non-FCRA-compliant sources, applying blanket criminal history bans, or skipping adverse action notices — face exposure under federal law and fair housing regulations.</p>
<p>The solution is not to avoid background checks; it is to do them correctly. Use a licensed CRA, disclose and get consent, review the full report, apply your written criteria consistently, conduct individualized assessments for criminal history, and follow FCRA adverse action procedures every time.</p>
<p>Landlords who treat background checks as a systematic, documented process rather than an informal judgment call protect themselves legally while gathering the information they actually need to make sound tenant decisions.</p>
