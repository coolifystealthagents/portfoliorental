Cap Rate Explained: How to Calculate and Use It

Capitalization rate, universally called "cap rate" in real estate circles, is one of the most frequently cited and frequently misunderstood metrics in investment property analysis. Brokers use it to price properties.

Investors use it to compare deals. Lenders use it to assess risk.

But for it to be useful, you need to understand not just how to calculate it, but what it actually measures and, critically, what it doesn't.

This guide explains cap rate from first principles: the formula, what the number means, how to use it in practice, and the limitations that every investor needs to understand before relying on it.

What Is Capitalization Rate?

Cap rate is the ratio of a property's net operating income (NOI) to its current market value (or purchase price). It represents the return you'd earn on an all-cash purchase, no financing.

Cap Rate = Net Operating Income ÷ Property Value

Or stated differently:

Property Value = Net Operating Income ÷ Cap Rate

This second formula reveals one of cap rate's most powerful applications: it gives you a way to estimate property value from income, which is how commercial real estate is priced.

The Cap Rate Formula: Step by Step

Step 1: Calculate Gross Rental Income

Start with total annual rent if every unit were occupied 100% of the year.

Example: A six-unit apartment building with each unit renting for $1,100/month.

  • Annual gross rent = $1,100 × 6 units × 12 months = $79,200

Step 2: Subtract Vacancy

Apply a realistic vacancy factor based on the local market (typically 5-10%).

  • Vacancy (7%): $79,200 × 0.07 = $5,544
  • Effective Gross Income = $79,200 - $5,544 = $73,656

Step 3: Calculate Net Operating Income

Subtract all operating expenses, but NOT mortgage payments.

Operating expenses typically include:

  • Property taxes
  • Insurance
  • Property management fees
  • Maintenance and repairs
  • Capital cost reserves
  • Utilities (if landlord-paid)
  • Lawn care, snow removal, trash
  • Accounting and legal fees

Example operating expenses: $28,000/year

  • Net Operating Income (NOI) = $73,656 - $28,000 = $45,656

Step 4: Divide NOI by Property Value

If the property is listed at $600,000:

  • Cap Rate = $45,656 ÷ $600,000 = 7.6%

What Does the Cap Rate Number Mean?

Cap rate represents the return you'd earn if you paid all cash for the property. A 7.6% cap rate means you'd earn 7.6% of your total investment annually from operations, before financing and taxes.

Higher cap rates generally indicate:

  • Higher potential return
  • Higher perceived risk
  • Less competitive markets (or lower-demand markets)
  • Older or more management-intensive properties

Lower cap rates generally indicate:

  • Lower return (or lower risk premium)
  • High-demand, high-quality markets
  • Newer or turnkey properties
  • More competition among buyers

As a frame of reference:

  • Cap rates in major gateway markets (NYC, San Francisco, Boston) often range from 3.5-5%
  • Suburban and secondary markets typically range from 5.5-7.5%
  • Tertiary and rural markets may range from 7.5-10%+

These ranges shift with interest rates. When borrowing costs rise, cap rates tend to follow, because investors demand higher returns to justify the investment relative to risk-free alternatives like Treasury bonds.

Cap Rate vs. Cash-on-Cash Return: Understanding the Difference

This is where many new investors get confused. Cap rate and cash-on-cash return are related but distinctly different:

Metric What It Measures Includes Financing?
Cap Rate Return on asset value, pre-financing No
Cash-on-Cash Return on actual cash invested Yes

If you finance a purchase with a mortgage, your cash-on-cash return will differ from the cap rate depending on how your interest rate compares to the cap rate:

  • If your interest rate is below the cap rate → positive use (financing increases your return)
  • If your interest rate is above the cap rate → negative use (financing hurts your return)

Example: Property at 7.6% cap rate, 30-year mortgage at 7.25% interest rate. Because your cost of debt approaches the cap rate, using the property adds minimal benefit and actually compresses your cash-on-cash return when accounting for principal payments and vacancy risk.

This dynamic became particularly important as interest rates rose significantly from 2022-2025. Deals that made sense at 4% interest rates often stopped making sense at 7%+ without cap rate expansion.

Using Cap Rate to Determine Property Value

The income approach to valuation uses cap rate to estimate what a property is worth based on its income:

Property Value = NOI ÷ Market Cap Rate

If you know:

  • The property generates $50,000 NOI annually
  • Properties in this market typically trade at 6.5% cap rates

Then the implied value = $50,000 ÷ 0.065 = $769,231

This is how commercial real estate buyers and brokers think about pricing. Every dollar of NOI increase drives a multiple improvement in property value based on the prevailing cap rate.

This principle underpins the value-add real estate strategy, increase NOI through rent increases or expense reduction, and the property value rises in proportion.

Market Cap Rates: How to Find Comparable Data

You can't evaluate a property's cap rate in isolation, you need to know what the prevailing cap rate is for similar properties in that market. Sources for market cap rate data:

  • Commercial brokers: The most reliable local source; they see actual transaction data
  • CoStar and LoopNet: National commercial real estate databases with market-level cap rate tracking (subscription required for full data)
  • CBRE Cap Rate Survey: Published semiannually, covers major markets and property types
  • Local investor communities: Real estate investment clubs and investor networks often share recent transaction cap rates

Understanding prevailing market cap rates lets you assess whether a listed property is priced fairly, at a discount, or at a premium.

Common Cap Rate Mistakes Investors Make

Using Seller-Provided NOI Without Verification

Sellers (and their brokers) have an incentive to present the highest possible NOI, which produces the lowest cap rate, which justifies the highest price. Always verify:

  • Actual rent rolls vs. claimed rents
  • All operating expenses (sellers frequently omit management fees or understate maintenance)
  • Vacancy assumptions

A "6% cap rate" presented in marketing materials may be 4.5% or 5% when you replace optimistic assumptions with realistic ones.

Applying Cap Rates Across Different Property Types

A 7% cap rate for a Class B multifamily apartment is very different from a 7% cap rate for a single-tenant net lease retail property. Different property types carry different risk profiles and thus have different appropriate cap rates.

Never compare cap rates across property types without adjusting for the underlying risk characteristics.

Ignoring What Happens After You Buy

Cap rate measures current income relative to current value. It tells you nothing about:

  • Rent growth potential
  • Market appreciation
  • Renovation upside
  • Future exit cap rate (what cap rate buyers will apply when you sell)

A property with a 5% cap rate in a market with strong rent growth and low vacancy may outperform a property with an 8% cap rate in a stagnant market over a 5-10 year horizon.

Cap Rate in the Context of a Full Investment Analysis

Cap rate is a starting point for analysis, not the ending point. Use it as a screening tool and for market pricing comparison, then layer in the full analysis:

  • Cash-on-cash return (with your actual financing terms)
  • Total return (including appreciation and principal paydown)
  • Internal rate of return over your projected hold period

The buy and hold real estate strategy depends on understanding both current income (captured by cap rate) and long-term total return. Strong investors use cap rate as one lens among several, not as the sole determinant of deal quality.

When evaluating whether a specific market offers good cap rates for your strategy, combine this analysis with full market research covering economic fundamentals, vacancy trends, and rent growth potential. Numbers are most useful when they're grounded in a real understanding of the market behind them.
