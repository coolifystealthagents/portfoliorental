Carbon monoxide (CO) is called the "silent killer" for good reason. It is odorless, colorless, and tasteless — and at sufficient concentrations, it kills within minutes. According to the CDC carbon monoxide data, approximately 400 Americans die from accidental CO poisoning each year, and many thousands more are treated in emergency rooms. Many of these incidents occur in residential properties during the heating season, when fuel-burning appliances are operating in insufficiently ventilated spaces.

For rental property owners, carbon monoxide detectors are not just a good idea — they are legally required in most states. Understanding exactly what is required, where detectors must be placed, and how to maintain compliance is a critical landlord obligation.

What Produces Carbon Monoxide in Rental Properties

CO is produced by incomplete combustion of any carbon-based fuel. Sources in rental properties include:

  • Gas furnaces — The most common source; a cracked heat exchanger can allow CO from combustion to enter the living space
  • Gas water heaters — Malfunctioning or improperly vented water heaters
  • Gas stoves and ovens — Particularly with chronic incomplete combustion (yellow flames)
  • Fireplaces and wood stoves — Blocked or restricted flues
  • Attached garages — Vehicle exhaust from idling cars can penetrate into living spaces
  • Portable generators — Never use inside or in attached garages; generates lethal CO concentrations rapidly. The CPSC warns about generators and CO risks.
  • Gas dryers — Venting failures can allow combustion byproducts to recirculate
  • Outdoor grills used indoors — Extremely dangerous; never bring gas or charcoal grills inside

State CO Detector Requirements for Rental Properties

Most states now require CO detectors in residential rental properties that have combustion appliances or attached garages. The following is a general overview — always verify current law for your specific state and locality.

States With CO Detector Laws Requiring Detectors in Rentals

California — CO detectors required in all dwelling units on each floor and near sleeping areas. Applies to all residential rentals (Civil Code 1013).

New York — CO detectors required within 15 feet of each sleeping area in one- and two-family homes and multi-family dwellings with fuel-burning appliances or attached garages.

New Jersey — Required in all residential rental properties with fuel-burning appliances, fireplaces, or attached garages.

Illinois — Required within 15 feet of each sleeping room in all residential buildings.

Massachusetts — Required in residential units with fuel-burning appliances, fireplaces, or attached garages. Battery backup required even in hardwired units.

Washington — Required in any dwelling containing a carbon monoxide alarm trigger (fuel-burning appliances, garages, fireplaces).

Oregon — Required in all dwellings with fuel-burning appliances.

Florida — Required in all new construction and applicable to rental properties; local ordinances may apply more broadly.

Texas — Required in single-family homes; multi-family requirements vary by local ordinance.

Pennsylvania — Required in all new residential construction and recommended for all rentals with combustion appliances.

Colorado — Required in all residential properties.

Minnesota — Required within 10 feet of each bedroom in all residential dwellings.

Maryland — Required in all residential dwellings.

Connecticut — Required in dwellings with fuel-burning appliances or attached garages.

Virginia — Required in all dwellings.

North Carolina — Required in all new residential construction; increasingly required in existing rentals under local codes.

States Still Updating CO Requirements

Some states have less specific or more recently enacted laws. Always verify current law with a local landlord association or legal counsel.

Placement Requirements

Even where exact state law varies, the following placement standards reflect NFPA 720 (the CO alarm standard) and most states' specific requirements:

Required Placement

  • Within 15 feet of each sleeping area (typically in the hallway adjacent to bedrooms)
  • On each level of the dwelling, including the basement
  • Near (but not directly adjacent to) combustion appliances — Do not install directly next to a furnace or water heater; CO detectors detect CO in the breathing zone, not at the source

Recommended Additional Placement

  • In each bedroom for maximum protection
  • In an attached garage (and mounted near the door to the living space)
  • Near a fireplace if present

Avoid Installing Near

  • Cooking appliances — Can cause false alarms from combustion products during cooking
  • Humid areas (within 5 feet of a shower or bathtub)
  • Forced-air vents that could dilute detector readings
  • Windows and exterior doors

Types of CO Detectors

By Power Source

Battery-operated — Simplest to install; requires battery replacement. Many states now require 10-year sealed lithium battery units to prevent disabling.

Hardwired with battery backup — Preferred for reliability; connected to building electrical system with battery backup for power outages. Required in some jurisdictions.

Plug-in with battery backup — Plugs into standard outlet with battery backup; easiest installation.

By Technology

Electrochemical — Most common and most accurate; responds to CO concentration accurately across a wide range. This is the preferred technology for residential use.

Biomimetic — Uses a gel that changes color in the presence of CO and triggers an alarm. Less common.

Semiconductor — Can be affected by humidity and other gases; less preferred for residential use.

Combination Smoke/CO Alarms

Combination smoke/CO alarms satisfy both requirements with one device. They are an efficient solution for rental properties, particularly when replacing aging smoke-only detectors. Verify that the combination unit meets your state's requirements for both smoke and CO detection.

CO Detector Lifespan

CO detectors have a defined service life:

  • Most CO detectors: 5-7 year lifespan
  • Combination smoke/CO detectors: Typically 10-year lifespan
  • End-of-life indication: Most units have an end-of-life chirp or indicator to signal that the sensor has reached its designed lifespan

The expiration date is typically printed on the back of the detector or in the documentation. Landlords must replace detectors that have reached their expiration date — an expired CO detector may not function when needed.

Landlord Obligations

At Tenant Move-In

  • Install functioning CO detectors in all required locations
  • Test each detector before tenant occupancy
  • Document the installation and testing in writing
  • Provide tenant with instructions for how to test the detectors

During the Tenancy

  • Respond to any tenant report of CO alarm activation immediately
  • Replace any CO detector that a tenant reports as malfunctioning
  • Replace detectors that reach their expiration date

At Each Unit Turnover

  • Test all CO detectors and confirm they are functioning
  • Replace any that fail to respond or are expired
  • Confirm placement meets current code requirements

What to Do When a CO Alarm Activates

For Tenants (Include in Move-In Materials)

  1. Leave the building immediately — do not investigate the source
  2. Call 911 from outside the building
  3. Do not re-enter until cleared by the fire department
  4. Seek medical attention if anyone has symptoms (headache, dizziness, nausea)

For Landlords

When notified of a CO alarm:

  1. Treat as an emergency — contact the tenant to confirm everyone has evacuated
  2. Contact the gas company and/or fire department — they can test the CO level in the unit and identify the source
  3. Do not allow re-occupancy until the source is identified and corrected
  4. Have the combustion appliance(s) inspected and serviced by a licensed technician before allowing re-entry
  5. Document the incident, response, and repair

CO Detection and HVAC Maintenance

The most common cause of CO problems in rental properties is a malfunctioning or aging furnace, specifically a cracked heat exchanger. A cracked heat exchanger allows combustion gases — including CO — to enter the air distribution system and circulate throughout the building.

This is why annual HVAC service is not just about efficiency — it is a CO safety measure. Annual furnace service includes heat exchanger inspection. If a technician identifies a cracked heat exchanger, the furnace must be shut down immediately and replaced before re-occupancy.

For a complete HVAC maintenance schedule, see our guide on HVAC maintenance for rental properties.

CO Detector Requirements and Fire Safety Compliance

CO detector compliance is typically part of a broader fire safety compliance program. Many states require both smoke detectors and CO detectors, and landlords should evaluate both systems together at each inspection.

For a complete overview of fire safety requirements, see our article on fire safety requirements for rental properties.

Penalties for Non-Compliance

The consequences of failing to provide required CO detectors in a rental property include:

  • Housing code violations — Local inspectors can cite landlords and impose fines for missing or non-functional detectors
  • Civil liability — If a tenant is injured or killed due to CO poisoning in a unit without required detectors, the landlord faces severe civil liability
  • Criminal exposure — In cases of tenant death from CO in a unit without required safety equipment, criminal charges against landlords are possible
  • Insurance implications — Insurance companies may deny or reduce claims where required safety equipment was absent

Frequently Asked Questions

Does my property need CO detectors if it has only electric appliances?

Most laws require CO detectors based on the presence of combustion appliances, fireplaces, or attached garages. An all-electric building with no attached garage may not require CO detectors under the law — but check your specific state and local ordinance. Installing them voluntarily is always a sound safety practice.

Can tenants be held responsible for replacing CO detector batteries?

In some states, tenants can be assigned responsibility for replacing batteries in battery-operated detectors. However, the landlord's primary responsibility is to provide functioning detectors. Regardless of lease provisions, a landlord cannot cite a tenant's failure to replace batteries as a defense to a habitability claim if the detector was not functioning.

How often should I test CO detectors?

Test at least annually (at each lease renewal or during your annual maintenance inspection). Monthly testing is recommended. Replace immediately if any test fails.

Conclusion

Carbon monoxide detectors are a non-negotiable fire and safety compliance requirement for rental properties in most states. The combination of annual HVAC servicing, proper CO detector placement, and consistent testing creates a defense against one of the most serious hazards in residential housing. Stay current on your state's specific requirements and treat CO detector compliance with the same seriousness as smoke detectors.
