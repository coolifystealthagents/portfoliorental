Short-Term Rental Tax Rules Landlords Must Know

Short-term rental properties — whether listed on Airbnb, VRBO, Hipcamp, or directly — operate in a fundamentally different tax environment than traditional long-term rentals. The rules are more complex, the opportunities are often greater, and the consequences of getting it wrong are significant.

Understanding short-term rental tax rules is essential for any investor in this space. Whether you are a first-time host or a multi-property operator, this guide covers the key distinctions, filing requirements, deduction opportunities, and strategic planning considerations that apply specifically to short-term rental income.

The Critical Distinction: How the IRS Classifies Your Rental

The IRS classification of your rental activity depends primarily on the average rental period — specifically, the average number of days each unit is rented per rental period. This determines which tax rules apply, where you report income, and whether losses are passive.

Average Rental Period of 7 Days or Fewer

If the average rental period per customer is 7 days or fewer, your activity is not treated as rental activity under IRC Section 469. Instead, it falls under the general passive activity rules for businesses.

The important implication: if you materially participate in this activity, your income and losses are non-passive — regardless of whether you qualify as a real estate professional. You can deduct losses against ordinary income without the passive activity loss limitations that apply to traditional rentals.

This is often called the "short-term rental loophole." But it comes with a catch: it may also mean your income is subject to self-employment tax.

Average Rental Period of 8–30 Days

For rentals averaging 8–30 days, the property is still treated as rental activity if you do not provide substantial services. The passive activity loss rules apply, and the real estate professional status requirements govern whether losses are non-passive.

Average Rental Period Over 30 Days

Traditional rental rules apply. Income and losses go on Schedule E, passive activity loss rules govern deductibility, and depreciation follows standard 27.5-year residential recovery periods.

Schedule E vs. Schedule C: Where to Report Your Income

Most traditional landlord income goes on Schedule E. Short-term rental income may go on Schedule C depending on the services you provide:

Report on Schedule E if:

  • You provide minimal services comparable to long-term rental (property access, basic utilities)
  • Your rental activity is investment-focused with no hotel-like services

Report on Schedule C if:

  • You provide substantial services typically associated with a hotel (daily maid service, concierge, meals, linen changes during the stay)
  • The IRS views the activity as a trade or business rather than investment rental

The critical difference: Schedule C income is subject to self-employment tax (15.3% on net income up to the Social Security wage base), while Schedule E income is not. Most Airbnb and VRBO hosts who do not provide hotel-level services should report on Schedule E, not Schedule C.

The line can be blurry. Providing a welcome basket, one-time cleaning at arrival/departure, and local recommendations does not rise to the level of "substantial services." Daily housekeeping and concierge services likely does. Consult a rental property CPA if you are unsure.

The Personal Use Rule: The 14-Day / 10% Test

If you use your short-term rental property personally, the IRS applies special rules that limit your deductions:

If personal use exceeds 14 days OR 10% of total rental days (whichever is greater):

  • The property is treated as a "personal residence"
  • You can still deduct rental expenses, but only up to rental income (no deductible loss)
  • Personal use expenses and shared expenses must be allocated between rental and personal days
  • You cannot create or deduct a loss from the rental activity

If personal use does not exceed 14 days OR 10% of rental days:

  • Full rental expense deductions are available (including losses, subject to passive activity rules)
  • The property is treated as a pure rental, not a personal residence

For most serious short-term rental investors, limiting personal use is important for preserving the deduction value of the property.

What Counts as Personal Use

  • Days you use the property for personal recreation
  • Days used by family members at less than fair market rent
  • Days used by friends at less than fair market rent
  • Days traded under home exchange programs

Days you or family members are there for repair and maintenance work do not count as personal use days.

Short-Term Rental Deductions

Short-term rentals can take the same categories of deductions as traditional rentals, plus some additional ones relevant to the hospitality nature of the business:

Standard rental deductions:

  • Depreciation (27.5-year for residential structure, plus cost segregation on components)
  • Mortgage interest
  • Property taxes
  • Insurance
  • Repairs and maintenance
  • HOA fees
  • Property management or platform fees

Short-term rental-specific deductions:

  • Cleaning fees (between guests)
  • Toiletries, linens, and consumable supplies
  • Welcome basket and guest amenity costs
  • Smart locks, security cameras, and keypad systems
  • Streaming service subscriptions provided to guests
  • Interior design and photography costs
  • Host liability insurance
  • Platform fees (Airbnb's host service fees are deductible)
  • Local lodging permits and licensing fees

For the most comprehensive list, see our rental property tax deductions guide.

Depreciation and Cost Segregation for Short-Term Rentals

Depreciation rules for short-term rentals are similar to traditional rentals: residential buildings are depreciated over 27.5 years, commercial over 39 years.

However, cost segregation studies are particularly powerful for furnished vacation rentals because:

  • Furniture, appliances, and personal property (5-year assets) is often a larger component of a vacation rental than a traditional rental
  • Specialty items like smart TVs, hot tubs, fire pits, and outdoor furniture can all be identified as shorter-life assets
  • Bonus depreciation on these components can generate substantial first-year deductions

If you are operating multiple short-term rental properties, a cost segregation study on each one can significantly reduce your tax liability.

Self-Employment Tax: The Short-Term Rental Trade-Off

The ability to deduct losses against ordinary income (for 7-day average rentals with material participation) sounds ideal. But there is a trade-off: the IRS may view materially participating short-term rental operators as running a business — which triggers self-employment tax on net income.

This creates a planning decision:

  • If you have losses (from depreciation), the non-passive treatment is beneficial because losses offset other income
  • If you have profits, the self-employment tax exposure reduces the net benefit

Work with your CPA to evaluate whether your short-term rental profile makes the business classification advantageous or costly, and to structure accordingly.

Transient Occupancy Taxes: The Non-Income Tax Obligation

Beyond federal and state income taxes, most short-term rental hosts must collect and remit transient occupancy taxes (TOT), also called hotel taxes, lodging taxes, or short-term rental taxes. These vary dramatically by location:

  • Airbnb and VRBO collect and remit these taxes automatically in many jurisdictions, but not all
  • Where platforms do not collect, you are responsible for collection and remittance
  • Rates typically range from 2% to 18% of rental income
  • Registration and licensing requirements vary by city and county

Check your local jurisdiction's requirements carefully. Failure to collect and remit TOT can result in penalties and back taxes. The MyLodgeTax by Avalara platform is a popular tool for tracking and remitting multi-jurisdiction lodging taxes.

State Income Tax Considerations

Short-term rental income is subject to state income taxes in the states where the property is located. For out-of-state investors:

  • You are generally required to file a nonresident return in every state where you have rental income
  • State rules for short-term rentals vary — some conform to federal passive activity rules, others do not
  • Some states have specific short-term rental licensing and tax registration requirements

This is particularly relevant for investors building a diversified rental portfolio across multiple states.

Record-Keeping for Short-Term Rentals

Good records are non-negotiable for short-term rental tax compliance. Maintain:

  • Rental calendar: Every rental period with check-in/out dates and guest names
  • Platform payout statements: Download annually from Airbnb, VRBO, etc.
  • Personal use log: Track all personal use days contemporaneously
  • Expense receipts: All supply, cleaning, maintenance, and improvement costs
  • Occupancy tax records: Documentation of taxes collected and remitted
  • Material participation log: If claiming non-passive status, document your hours managing the property

The rental calendar is especially important — average rental period calculations and personal use tracking both depend on it. Use a dedicated rental property bookkeeping system to centralize these records.

Short-Term Rental Tax Planning Strategies

Maximize the Non-Passive Loophole

If your average rental period is 7 days or fewer and you materially participate (500+ hours/year), you can potentially deduct losses against all income. This is a powerful strategy when combined with cost segregation-driven depreciation losses.

Time Improvements and Purchases Strategically

Capital improvements placed in service in the current year qualify for current-year depreciation (including any applicable bonus depreciation). If you are planning renovations, completing them before December 31 maximizes your current-year deductions.

Manage Personal Use Days Carefully

Keep personal use under the 14-day or 10% threshold to preserve full deductibility. Track personal use days rigorously throughout the year, not just at tax time.

Consider Entity Structure

If you operate multiple short-term rentals as a business, an LLC or S-Corp may offer tax efficiency advantages. An S-Corp can help separate active income (subject to self-employment tax) from passive returns if structured correctly.

Short-term rental taxation is genuinely complex, and the strategies available to well-informed hosts are significant. Working with a specialized CPA and maintaining excellent records allows you to take full advantage of the deductions available while staying compliant — a combination that supports the goal of maximizing rental income and portfolio returns over the long run.
