2b:T4126,<p>A rental application form is the foundation of your tenant screening process. It is the document that collects the information you need to evaluate whether an applicant qualifies for your property — and if it is poorly designed or legally incomplete, it can expose you to liability, produce unusable information, or cause you to miss red flags that would have disqualified an applicant before they moved in.</p>
<p>This guide provides a complete tenant application form template with every section landlords need, explains what questions are legally permissible, covers the legal requirements around application fees and disclosures, and addresses the growing shift toward digital rental applications.</p>
<h2 id="why-your-rental-application-form-matters">Why Your Rental Application Form Matters</h2>
<p>The rental application is not just a formality. It serves several distinct purposes in your screening process:</p>
<ul>
<li>It collects the data needed to run credit, background, and eviction checks</li>
<li>It creates a written record of representations the applicant made under penalty of perjury</li>
<li>It is evidence that you screened applicants systematically and consistently</li>
<li>It triggers your FCRA disclosure and authorization obligations</li>
<li>It begins the paper trail you need if an adverse action complaint is filed against you</li>
</ul>
<p>A weak or incomplete application creates gaps in your screening process and leaves you without documentation you may need later. A well-structured one makes the process faster, more consistent, and more defensible.</p>
<h2 id="what-a-rental-application-must-include">What a Rental Application Must Include</h2>
<h3 id="section-1-property-and-application-information">Section 1: Property and Application Information</h3>
<p>Every application should begin with identifying information about the specific unit and application:</p>
<ul>
<li>Property address and unit number</li>
<li>Desired move-in date</li>
<li>Lease term requested</li>
<li>Monthly rent amount for the unit</li>
<li>Application date</li>
</ul>
<p>This section documents which unit the application relates to and creates a timestamp for when the application was received — important in jurisdictions that require first-come, first-served processing.</p>
<h3 id="section-2-applicant-personal-information">Section 2: Applicant Personal Information</h3>
<p>Collect the following for each applicant:</p>
<ul>
<li>Full legal name</li>
<li>Date of birth</li>
<li>Social Security Number or Individual Taxpayer Identification Number (ITIN)</li>
<li>Current address and how long they have lived there</li>
<li>Prior address (last 2–3 years)</li>
<li>Phone number and email address</li>
<li>Driver's license or government-issued ID number and state</li>
</ul>
<p>The SSN or ITIN is required to run a credit check. The ID information allows you to verify identity and is standard practice.</p>
<p><strong>What you cannot ask</strong>: Do not ask about national origin, citizenship status, place of birth, or religion. Do not ask about immigration status — this is heavily regulated and in many jurisdictions prohibited as a basis for a housing decision. Do not ask questions that would reveal a protected class under the Fair Housing Act, such as family status, disability, or sex.</p>
<h3 id="section-3-occupants">Section 3: Occupants</h3>
<p>List all individuals who will live in the unit:</p>
<ul>
<li>Name</li>
<li>Date of birth (to verify minor occupant status for occupancy standard purposes)</li>
<li>Relationship to applicant</li>
</ul>
<p>Note that every adult occupant (typically 18 and over) should be required to complete their own application and consent to screening. Children are occupants but do not need separate applications.</p>
<p><strong>Familial status caution</strong>: Do not set occupancy standards that effectively exclude families with children. HUD's general guidance is that two persons per bedroom is a reasonable starting point, but there is no federal standard, and the analysis is fact-specific. Review our <a href="/blog/fair-housing-compliance-rental">fair housing compliance guide</a> for the full picture.</p>
<h3 id="section-4-employment-and-income-information">Section 4: Employment and Income Information</h3>
<p>Collect the following for each applicant:</p>
<ul>
<li>Current employer name, address, and phone number</li>
<li>Position/title and supervisor name</li>
<li>Date of employment start</li>
<li>Employment type (full-time, part-time, self-employed, contract)</li>
<li>Gross monthly income</li>
<li>Prior employer (if employed less than two years at current job)</li>
</ul>
<p>If the applicant is self-employed, retired, or receives income from other sources (investments, alimony, disability, Social Security, rental income), include fields for documenting those sources:</p>
<ul>
<li>Income source type</li>
<li>Monthly gross amount</li>
<li>Contact or documentation available for verification</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>What you can ask</strong>: You can ask about income sources. You cannot ask about the source in a way designed to identify protected classes. For example, asking "Do you receive disability benefits?" in a way intended to screen out disabled applicants violates the Fair Housing Act. The appropriate question is about the amount of verifiable income, not its type.</p>
<h3 id="section-5-rental-history">Section 5: Rental History</h3>
<p>Request the following for the past two to three years:</p>
<ul>
<li>Current and prior landlord names, addresses, and phone numbers</li>
<li>Monthly rent amount at each address</li>
<li>Lease start and end dates</li>
<li>Reason for leaving</li>
<li>Permission to contact each landlord</li>
</ul>
<p>Include a question asking whether the applicant has ever been evicted, had an eviction filed against them, or broken a lease early. Require the applicant to explain any "yes" answers.</p>
<p>This section is where experienced landlords invest the most time during verification. Calling prior landlords directly — rather than relying solely on the written application — reveals information that does not appear in screening reports.</p>
<h3 id="section-6-references">Section 6: References</h3>
<p>Request two to three personal or professional references:</p>
<ul>
<li>Full name</li>
<li>Relationship to applicant</li>
<li>Phone number and email</li>
<li>How long they have known the applicant</li>
</ul>
<p>Specify that references cannot be family members or romantic partners. Character references from employers, colleagues, or long-term acquaintances provide useful supplemental context.</p>
<h3 id="section-7-vehicle-information">Section 7: Vehicle Information</h3>
<p>Collect information about vehicles that will be parked on the property:</p>
<ul>
<li>Make, model, year, and color</li>
<li>License plate number and state</li>
</ul>
<p>This is relevant for properties with assigned or limited parking and for enforcing lease terms about unauthorized vehicles.</p>
<h3 id="section-8-pet-information">Section 8: Pet Information</h3>
<p>If your property permits pets, collect:</p>
<ul>
<li>Type and breed of pet</li>
<li>Weight and age</li>
<li>Whether the pet is spayed or neutered</li>
<li>Vaccination records availability</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Assistance animal note</strong>: Emotional support animals (ESAs) and service animals are not pets under fair housing law. Landlords with no-pet policies must make reasonable accommodations for ESA and service animal requests from tenants with documented disabilities. Do not include ESA or service animal questions in the general pet section — handle those requests through a separate, compliant accommodation request process.</p>
<h3 id="section-9-background-and-screening-disclosures">Section 9: Background and Screening Disclosures</h3>
<p>This is the legally critical section. It must include:</p>
<p><strong>FCRA Disclosure</strong>: A clear, standalone statement that the landlord will obtain a consumer report (credit, background, or both) from a consumer reporting agency (name the CRA or state it will be disclosed prior to ordering). This disclosure must be separate from the rest of the application in a format that draws attention to it.</p>
<p><strong>Written Authorization</strong>: The applicant's signature authorizing the landlord to obtain consumer reports, contact employers and landlords listed in the application, and verify the information provided.</p>
<p><strong>Acknowledgment of Accuracy</strong>: A statement that the applicant certifies all information is true and complete, and understands that misrepresentation may be grounds for denial or lease termination.</p>
<p>For the full text of required FCRA disclosure language, see the <a href="https://www.ftc.gov/legal-library/browse/statutes/fair-credit-reporting-act">FTC's resources on the Fair Credit Reporting Act</a>.</p>
<h3 id="section-10-signature-and-date">Section 10: Signature and Date</h3>
<p>Each adult applicant must sign and date the completed application. Unsigned applications are not valid. If collecting applications digitally, your platform must support legally binding electronic signatures.</p>
<h2 id="application-fees-what-the-law-allows">Application Fees: What the Law Allows</h2>
<p>Application fees exist to cover the cost of running credit and background checks and reviewing the application. They are common practice but heavily regulated in many states.</p>
<h3 id="state-by-state-fee-limits">State-by-State Fee Limits</h3>
<p>Many states limit how much landlords can charge:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>California</strong>: Maximum of the actual cost of the screening, up to a state-set limit (updated annually; approximately $65 as of recent years)</li>
<li><strong>Washington</strong>: Capped at the actual cost of the screening</li>
<li><strong>New York City</strong>: Capped at $20</li>
<li><strong>Oregon</strong>: Capped at the actual cost of the consumer report(s)</li>
<li><strong>Colorado</strong>: No fee for applicants who provide their own screening report (obtained within the past 30 days)</li>
</ul>
<p>In states without specific caps, reasonable fees are generally acceptable, but charging amounts far above actual screening costs can create legal risk.</p>
<h3 id="fee-receipts-and-itemization">Fee Receipts and Itemization</h3>
<p>Some jurisdictions require landlords to provide a written receipt for application fees. Several states require itemization of what the fee covers. Keep records of what you spent on screening for each application.</p>
<h3 id="refund-of-application-fees">Refund of Application Fees</h3>
<p>Most states do not require landlords to refund application fees if an applicant is denied. However, many require landlords to refund fees if the unit is rented to someone else before the application is processed, or if the landlord decides not to rent the unit at all.</p>
<h2 id="legal-questions-what-landlords-cannot-ask">Legal Questions: What Landlords Cannot Ask</h2>
<p>Beyond the items noted in individual sections above, here is a consolidated list of questions that are legally prohibited or high-risk on a rental application:</p>
<ul>
<li>Questions about race, color, national origin, religion, sex, familial status, or disability (federally protected classes under the Fair Housing Act)</li>
<li>Questions about pregnancy or whether an applicant plans to have children</li>
<li>Questions about immigration or citizenship status (in many jurisdictions)</li>
<li>Questions about a prior bankruptcy (you may review bankruptcy history through a credit report, but asking about it directly can create complications in some jurisdictions)</li>
<li>Questions about arrests without convictions (in many ban-the-box jurisdictions)</li>
<li>Questions about medical conditions or disability-related needs (separate from a disability accommodation request process)</li>
</ul>
<p>When in doubt, ask whether a question is necessary to evaluate the applicant's ability to pay rent and comply with the lease. If the answer is no, remove it.</p>
<h2 id="digital-vs-paper-applications">Digital vs. Paper Applications</h2>
<p>The shift toward digital rental applications has accelerated significantly. Most landlords now use some form of online application, whether through a dedicated property management platform, a tenant screening service, or a standalone form tool.</p>
<h3 id="advantages-of-digital-applications">Advantages of Digital Applications</h3>
<ul>
<li>Applications can be submitted from anywhere, speeding up the process</li>
<li>Identity verification, credit checks, and background checks can be integrated and run automatically</li>
<li>Signatures are captured electronically and stored with the application</li>
<li>Documents (pay stubs, bank statements, IDs) can be uploaded directly</li>
<li>Applications are stored in a searchable, organized database rather than paper files</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="what-to-look-for-in-a-digital-application-platform">What to Look for in a Digital Application Platform</h3>
<p>A digital application tool should:</p>
<ul>
<li>Produce FCRA-compliant disclosures and consent forms</li>
<li>Support legally binding electronic signatures</li>
<li>Integrate with at least one FCRA-compliant CRA for credit and background checks</li>
<li>Store applications securely and allow you to retrieve them if a dispute arises</li>
<li>Allow you to configure your screening criteria questions and disclosures</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="paper-applications">Paper Applications</h3>
<p>Paper applications remain appropriate for landlords who manage a small number of units or who prefer physical documentation. If you use paper, maintain organized files, make copies before returning originals, and retain completed applications (accepted and denied) for at least three years.</p>
<h2 id="integrating-the-application-into-your-screening-process">Integrating the Application into Your Screening Process</h2>
<p>The application is the first step, not the whole process. After receiving a completed application:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Verify identity</strong>: Confirm that the name on the application matches the government-issued ID provided.</li>
<li><strong>Run credit and background checks</strong>: Obtain written authorization before ordering (confirm this is in the application) and use a licensed CRA.</li>
<li><strong>Verify income</strong>: Call employers or request recent pay stubs and bank statements. Verify self-employment through tax returns.</li>
<li><strong>Contact prior landlords</strong>: Call each landlord listed by phone. Ask directly whether they would rent to this applicant again.</li>
<li><strong>Review the application for consistency</strong>: Compare what the applicant listed with what the reports and verifications show. Unexplained discrepancies are a red flag.</li>
<li><strong>Apply your written screening criteria</strong>: Evaluate the applicant against your documented standards. Document the evaluation.</li>
<li><strong>Issue adverse action notices as required</strong>: If you deny the application based on a consumer report, follow FCRA procedures.</li>
</ol>
<p>For a complete screening process framework, see our <a href="/blog/tenant-screening-best-practices">tenant screening best practices guide</a>. For the lease agreement that follows an approved application, review our <a href="/blog/lease-agreement-template-guide">lease agreement template guide</a>.</p>
<p>For guidance on the property management contract context this fits within, see our <a href="/blog/property-management-contract-template">property management contract template guide</a>.</p>
<h2 id="conclusion">Conclusion</h2>
<p>A tenant application form template is a starting point, not a finished product. Every landlord should review their application against the laws of their specific jurisdiction, their property type, and their screening process before using it.</p>
<p>The sections covered here — personal information, employment and income, rental history, references, occupants, pets, vehicles, FCRA disclosures, and signatures — represent the standard components of a complete and legally sound rental application. Adding questions beyond these should be done carefully, with attention to fair housing law.</p>
<p>The best application forms are clear, complete, and consistent. They collect what you need to make a sound decision, document the applicant's consent to be screened, and create a paper trail that protects you if a dispute arises later. Pair a strong application with systematic verification and written screening criteria, and you have the foundation of a professional tenant selection process.</p>
