Screening Section 8 Tenants: What Landlords Need to Know

Section 8 tenant screening is one of the most misunderstood areas of landlord-tenant law. Some landlords assume they can apply entirely different criteria to voucher holders, while others refuse to accept them at all without realizing they may be breaking state or local law. Getting this wrong can result in fair housing complaints, fines, and legal liability.

This guide walks through everything landlords need to know about screening tenants who hold Housing Choice Vouchers (HCV), formerly known as Section 8 vouchers — including what the program actually involves, where source-of-income protections apply, what screening is still legally permitted, and why accepting vouchers can make sense for your portfolio.

What Is the Section 8 / Housing Choice Voucher Program?

The Housing Choice Voucher (HCV) program is administered by the U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD) and run at the local level by Public Housing Authorities (PHAs). The program provides rental assistance to low-income families, the elderly, and people with disabilities.

Here is how it works in practice:

  • A tenant receives a voucher from their local PHA.
  • They find a privately-owned rental unit that meets program requirements.
  • The landlord and PHA enter into a Housing Assistance Payments (HAP) contract.
  • The PHA pays a portion of rent directly to the landlord each month.
  • The tenant pays the remainder, which is typically 30% of their adjusted gross income.

For landlords, this means a portion of the rent is guaranteed by a government agency every month — a meaningful benefit in markets where tenant income instability is a concern.

For more information on program structure and eligibility, visit the HUD Housing Choice Vouchers fact sheet.

Source-of-Income Discrimination Laws: Know Your State

The most critical thing landlords must understand before screening Section 8 applicants is whether their state, city, or county prohibits source-of-income (SOI) discrimination.

At the federal level, the Fair Housing Act does not list source of income as a protected class. However, many states and municipalities have enacted their own laws that do.

States with Source-of-Income Protections

As of 2026, states that prohibit landlords from refusing to rent to Section 8 voucher holders (or recipients of lawful income sources) include:

  • California
  • Colorado
  • Connecticut
  • Illinois
  • Maine
  • Maryland
  • Massachusetts
  • Minnesota
  • New Jersey
  • New York
  • Oregon
  • Vermont
  • Virginia
  • Washington

Many cities in states not on this list — including Chicago, Philadelphia, Austin, and others — have local ordinances that provide similar protections. Check with your local housing authority or a landlord-tenant attorney before posting a listing that says "No Section 8."

In states without SOI protections, refusing voucher holders is legally permissible at the state level, but you must still comply with all federal fair housing requirements. You cannot, for example, advertise "No Section 8" as a proxy to discriminate based on race or national origin — classes that are federally protected.

To understand the full scope of fair housing obligations, review our guide on fair housing compliance for rental properties.

What Section 8 Tenant Screening Looks Like in Practice

When SOI protections apply, landlords must apply the same screening criteria to voucher holders as to any other applicant. This means the same credit checks, criminal background checks, rental history verification, and income requirements.

Where landlords get confused is around income verification. Voucher holders often have low earned income, but their effective ability to pay rent is a combination of their portion (30% of adjusted income) and the PHA subsidy. Courts and housing agencies have generally held that landlords should factor in the total housing payment — not just the tenant's share — when applying income-to-rent ratios.

What You Can Still Screen For

Even where SOI protections apply, you retain the right to screen Section 8 applicants on:

  • Credit history: Review credit reports for patterns of delinquency, collections, or prior evictions. Establish a written minimum credit score policy and apply it to all applicants. See our article on tenant screening best practices for guidance on setting defensible standards.
  • Rental history: Contact prior landlords, verify lease compliance, and check for prior evictions or complaints.
  • Criminal background: Subject to ban-the-box laws and individualized assessment requirements in your jurisdiction (covered in more detail in our background check article).
  • Income verification: Confirm the applicant's portion of rent can be covered by their income or assets.
  • References: Speak with prior landlords about the applicant's conduct, maintenance of the unit, and communication.

The key is consistency. Apply your written screening criteria the same way to every applicant, regardless of whether they hold a voucher.

The HQS Inspection Requirement

Before a voucher can be used at your property, the unit must pass a Housing Quality Standards (HQS) inspection conducted by the PHA. This is a practical requirement landlords need to plan for.

HQS inspections cover:

  • Sanitation and plumbing
  • Heating and ventilation
  • Electrical systems
  • Structural soundness
  • Safety equipment (smoke detectors, window guards where required)
  • Lead-based paint compliance for units built before 1978

If your unit fails inspection, you will have a defined period to make corrections before the PHA will approve the HAP contract. For landlords with well-maintained properties, this is rarely an obstacle. For those with deferred maintenance, it can delay move-in or disqualify the unit entirely.

This is one reason good property maintenance practices matter not just for tenant satisfaction but for keeping your units voucher-eligible.

Benefits of Accepting Housing Choice Vouchers

Many landlords who have experience with the HCV program report that voucher tenants are no more problematic than unassisted tenants — and that the guaranteed government payment is a meaningful advantage.

Predictable Partial Rent Payments

The PHA's portion of rent is paid directly to the landlord, typically on a fixed schedule. This eliminates the risk of non-payment for that portion of rent. In high-vacancy or economically volatile markets, this stability has real value.

Reduced Vacancy

Voucher holders are often highly motivated to find and keep a unit. Losing housing assistance is a serious consequence of a lease violation or voluntary move, which creates an incentive for compliance. Voucher holders also tend to stay longer, reducing turnover costs.

Access to a Larger Applicant Pool

In markets with SOI protections, refusing voucher holders narrows your applicant pool. Accepting them widens it. During periods of high vacancy, this can meaningfully reduce time-to-lease.

For more strategies on minimizing vacancy and improving rental income, see our guide on maximizing rental income strategies.

Common Misconceptions About Section 8

"I Can't Evict a Section 8 Tenant"

False. Landlords retain the right to evict voucher holders for lease violations, non-payment of their portion of rent, or lease expiration under applicable state law. You follow the same eviction process as with any tenant. You must also notify the PHA of an eviction proceeding.

"The PHA Will Pay Regardless of Tenant Conduct"

Not entirely accurate. The PHA can terminate assistance if a tenant violates program rules. If a tenant is terminated from the program mid-lease, you are no longer receiving the subsidy and must pursue collection of the full rent from the tenant — or follow your lease terms for non-payment.

"Section 8 Means Lower Rents"

PHAs set Payment Standards — the maximum subsidy they will pay for a unit of a given size in a given area. These are typically based on fair market rents (FMRs) set by HUD. In many markets, Payment Standards are competitive with or close to market rate. You can charge market rent; the PHA will only subsidize up to its Payment Standard, and the tenant covers the difference.

State-Specific Considerations

Beyond SOI laws, landlords need to be aware of state-specific rules that intersect with Section 8 screening. Some states, for example, have:

  • Written screening criteria requirements: Oregon, Washington, and others require landlords to provide written screening criteria upfront.
  • Application fee limits: Several states cap or prohibit application fees.
  • First-come, first-served requirements: Some jurisdictions require landlords to process applications in the order received rather than selecting among a pool.

Our guide to landlord-tenant law by state provides a state-by-state overview of these requirements.

For detailed information on tenant rights in your jurisdiction, also review tenant rights and responsibilities to understand what applicants are entitled to during the screening process.

Building a Legally Sound Section 8 Screening Policy

To screen Section 8 applicants effectively and legally, take the following steps:

  1. Determine your SOI obligations: Consult a landlord-tenant attorney or your local housing authority to understand what laws apply in your jurisdiction.
  2. Write a screening criteria document: Put your minimum requirements for credit, income, rental history, and criminal background in writing. Apply them uniformly.
  3. Review your income calculation method: Decide in advance how you will treat the PHA subsidy when calculating income-to-rent ratios and document that method.
  4. Understand the HAP contract: Before accepting your first voucher tenant, review a sample HAP contract with your local PHA. Know your obligations under it.
  5. Plan for the HQS inspection: Make sure your unit is in good repair and meets HQS before marketing to voucher holders.
  6. Document everything: Keep records of all applications received, how each was evaluated, and the basis for any denial.

Conclusion

Section 8 tenant screening requires landlords to balance legitimate screening criteria with a growing body of state and local law that limits when and how you can decline voucher holders. The fundamentals are straightforward: understand whether SOI protections apply in your market, apply consistent written criteria to all applicants, and evaluate the practical benefits of participating in the HCV program.

Landlords who engage thoughtfully with the program often find it adds stability to their rental income and broadens their tenant pool. Those who dismiss it without understanding the legal landscape may find themselves exposed to fair housing complaints — or simply leaving a reliable income source on the table.
