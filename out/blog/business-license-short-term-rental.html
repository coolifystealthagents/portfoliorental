A growing number of cities and counties require short-term rental hosts to obtain a general business license in addition to, or as part of, their STR permit. Failing to secure the right business license can result in fines, back taxes, and platform listing removal.

Yet many hosts don't realize this requirement exists until they receive a compliance notice.

This guide explains what a business license is, when it's required for STR hosting, how to obtain one, and what happens if you skip this step.

Our short-term rental management guide covers the full compliance and operational framework every host needs.

What Is a Business License?

A business license is a government-issued authorization to conduct commercial activity within a specific jurisdiction. Unlike an STR-specific permit, which authorizes the use of your property for short-term rental purposes, a business license acknowledges that you are running a business and subjects you to local business regulations and taxes.

Business licenses are typically issued by:

  • City or municipal governments
  • County governments
  • Sometimes both (in unincorporated areas or multi-jurisdictional locations)

The SBA covers licensing basics for small businesses. The cost ranges from under $50 to several hundred dollars annually.

Many are renewed yearly and tied to a business tax return or gross receipts filing.

Do You Need a Business License to Host on Airbnb?

The answer depends on your jurisdiction, and it varies significantly. Here's how to think about it:

Jurisdictions That Require a Business License for STRs

Many major cities require a general business license for all STR operators, either as a standalone requirement or as part of the STR permit application. Cities in this category include:

  • Los Angeles, California: Hosts must obtain a Business Tax Registration Certificate (BTRC) from the city's Office of Finance, separate from the Home-Sharing registration
  • Atlanta, Georgia: All STR operators must have a current City of Atlanta business license
  • New Orleans, Louisiana: STR operators register for a business license through the city's One Stop App, which processes STR permits alongside business registrations
  • Portland, Oregon: STR hosts are required to register with the city and obtain a business license
  • Philadelphia, Pennsylvania: The city requires a Commercial Activity License and a Housing Rental License

Jurisdictions That Don't Require a Standalone Business License

Some cities issue STR-specific permits without requiring a separate general business license. In these markets, the STR permit is the primary regulatory mechanism.

However, hosts may still need to register for local tax purposes separately.

The Only Way to Know for Certain

Regulations change frequently. The only reliable way to know your specific requirements is to:

  1. Check your city's official business licensing website
  2. Check your county's requirements if the property is in an unincorporated area
  3. Call the licensing department directly
  4. Consult a local attorney or business formation service

Also review str permits and licensing to understand how business licensing fits alongside STR-specific permit requirements.

State-Level Business Registration

In some states, running an STR business, especially through a legal entity like an LLC, requires registration at the state level. This typically includes:

  • Registering your LLC or corporation with the Secretary of State's office (required when you form the entity)
  • Obtaining a state sales tax or transaction privilege tax permit to collect and remit applicable taxes
  • Filing for a state business license in states that issue them (Washington State, for example, requires a state business license for virtually all businesses)

For hosts operating through an LLC, these state registrations are handled during the entity formation process. See our short term rental llc setup guide for a step-by-step walkthrough.

Common Business License Application Requirements

Most business license applications for STR operators require:

Basic business information:

  • Business name (your legal name if operating as a sole proprietor, or your LLC/corporation name)
  • Business address (typically the rental property address)
  • Business type/activity (short-term rental or vacation rental)
  • EIN (Employer Identification Number), required if operating through an LLC; available free from the IRS

Owner/applicant information:

  • Legal name and contact information
  • Government-issued ID
  • Social Security Number (for sole proprietors) or EIN (for entities)

Property information:

  • Property address
  • Proof of ownership or authority to rent
  • Type of unit (single-family, condo, multi-family)

Fees:

  • Typically $50, $300 depending on the jurisdiction, paid annually

Some jurisdictions also require proof of STR permit (if a separate permit is required) or proof of insurance before issuing a business license.

Operating as a Sole Proprietor vs. an LLC

The legal structure of your business affects your licensing obligations.

Sole Proprietor

If you operate as a sole proprietor (no formal business entity), you typically license the business under your own name. A DBA ("doing business as") registration may be needed if you want to operate under a business name.

Pros:

  • Simpler setup
  • No separate entity to maintain

Cons:

  • No personal liability protection
  • Personal assets at risk in a lawsuit

LLC (Limited Liability Company)

Operating through an LLC requires registering the entity with the state Secretary of State, obtaining an EIN from the IRS, and then obtaining local business licenses in the LLC's name.

Pros:

  • Separates personal and business liability
  • More professional structure
  • Potential tax advantages

Cons:

  • More paperwork and maintenance
  • Annual state fees and reporting requirements

Our rental property llc benefits article explores why many property investors choose the LLC structure.

The EIN: Your Business Tax ID

Whether you form an LLC or not, obtaining an Employer Identification Number (EIN) from the IRS is a smart step for STR hosts. An EIN:

  • Is required for LLCs (except single-member LLCs in some cases)
  • Allows you to open a business bank account
  • Is required on many business license applications
  • Keeps your Social Security Number off business documents
  • Is needed to issue 1099s to service providers you pay over $600/year

An EIN is free and can be obtained in minutes through the IRS EIN application page. It does not require you to have employees.

Business Bank Account and Record-Keeping

Once you have a business license (and especially if you operate through an LLC), opening a dedicated business bank account is essential. This account:

  • Receives all rental income
  • Pays all business expenses (cleaning, supplies, maintenance, insurance)
  • Makes bookkeeping and tax preparation dramatically easier
  • Maintains the legal separation between your personal and business finances, critical for preserving LLC liability protection

Commingling personal and business funds is one of the most common mistakes STR investors make, and it can expose your personal assets to business liabilities.

Business License and Tax Obligations

Obtaining a business license often triggers local business tax obligations. Common taxes associated with licensed STR businesses:

Local Business Tax (Gross Receipts Tax) Many cities impose a local business tax based on gross receipts, your total rental revenue. This is separate from income tax and is typically a small percentage of revenue or a flat annual fee based on revenue brackets.

Lodging/Occupancy Tax Most jurisdictions require STR hosts to collect and remit a lodging tax (also called transient occupancy tax or TOT) from guests. Some cities handle this automatically through platform collection agreements with Airbnb; others require hosts to remit independently. See our short-term rental tax guide for full details.

State Income Tax STR income is taxable at the state and federal level. The IRS rental income guide explains reporting requirements. Operating through an LLC does not eliminate income tax, it changes how the income is reported and potentially how it's taxed.

Home Occupation Permits: A Related Requirement

In some jurisdictions, if you are managing your STR from your primary residence (i.e., your home is also your office), you may need a home occupation permit in addition to a business license. Home occupation permits ensure that business activities from a residential address don't create traffic, noise, or signage problems for the neighborhood.

In many markets, simply managing an STR from your home qualifies as a home occupation. Requirements are usually minimal, a simple registration and fee, but the requirement exists and can carry fines if ignored.

Consequences of Operating Without a Business License

Beyond the moral and civic obligations, the practical consequences of operating without a required business license can be significant:

  • Fines: Typically $50, $500 for initial violations, escalating for repeat violations
  • Back taxes: The licensing department can assess all business taxes that would have been owed from the date your business started
  • License denial: Some jurisdictions deny STR permits or licenses to applicants with a history of unlicensed operation
  • Platform removal: As cities share enforcement data with platforms, unlicensed listings are increasingly subject to removal
  • Difficulty with financing: Business lenders and some mortgage products require proof of proper licensing and business registration

Renewals and Ongoing Compliance

A business license is not a one-time task. Ongoing obligations typically include:

  • Annual renewal: Most business licenses expire annually and must be renewed with updated information and fee payment
  • Change notifications: If your business name, address, or structure changes, you typically must notify the licensing authority
  • Revenue reporting: Some business tax systems require annual gross receipts reporting tied to the license renewal
  • Permit coordination: Keep your STR permit and business license renewal dates aligned, losing one may affect the validity of the other

Build these renewals into your business calendar and set reminders 60, 90 days before expiration.

Summary and Key Takeaways

Business license requirements for short-term rentals vary by city and state, but they are an increasingly common compliance obligation for STR hosts. Here's what to take away:

  • Many major cities require a general business license separate from, or as part of, the STR permit process
  • Operating as an LLC requires additional state registration and an EIN
  • A business bank account is essential for maintaining liability protection and clean records
  • Business licensing triggers local business tax and gross receipts tax obligations
  • Operating without required licenses can result in fines, back taxes, and listing removal
  • Licenses must be renewed annually, build this into your compliance calendar
