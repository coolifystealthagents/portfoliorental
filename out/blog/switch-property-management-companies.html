How to Switch Property Management Companies Smoothly

Deciding to switch property management companies is rarely easy. You may have an existing relationship to navigate, active leases in progress, and legitimate concerns about disrupting your tenants. But staying with an underperforming manager because the transition feels complicated is a costly mistake. Poor management compounds over time—through higher vacancy, declining tenant quality, deferred maintenance, and eroding cash flow.

The good news is that a property management transition can be handled professionally and systematically with minimal disruption to tenants and operations. This guide walks through every step of the process, from reviewing your current contract to completing the handoff.

Before starting the transition process, read our guide on the property management agreement explained to understand your rights and obligations under your current contract.

Step 1: Review Your Current Management Agreement

The management agreement governs the terms of your relationship with your current manager—including the conditions under which you can terminate it. Before you do anything else, read the agreement carefully and identify:

The termination clause: Most agreements require 30 to 90 days' written notice. Some require more. The termination clause may also specify whether you can terminate mid-lease or only at lease expiration, and whether a termination fee applies.

The performance clause: Some agreements include performance-based termination provisions. If the manager has failed to meet stated service standards, this may give you the right to terminate with shorter notice or without a penalty.

Fees payable upon termination: Check whether there are any outstanding fees, unpaid maintenance charges, or advance leasing commissions that become due upon termination.

Transition obligations: Some agreements include provisions requiring the outgoing manager to cooperate with a transition—providing tenant files, transferring security deposits, and surrendering keys and access codes. Understand what is required of them before you give notice.

If the agreement is unclear on any of these points, consult a real estate attorney before proceeding. The cost of a one-hour legal consultation is far less than a disputed termination.

Step 2: Find and Hire Your New Manager Before Giving Notice

One of the most common mistakes landlords make when switching managers is giving notice to the old company before the new one is ready to take over. This creates a gap in management that leaves tenants without a point of contact, maintenance requests unaddressed, and rent collection in limbo.

The correct sequence is:

  1. Evaluate and select a new property manager
  2. Sign the new management agreement
  3. Establish a transition start date with the new manager
  4. Give formal written notice to the current manager based on your contract's termination requirements
  5. Work backward from the transition date to ensure the notice period aligns with the new manager's start date

For guidance on selecting a new manager, see how to choose a property manager and the hiring property manager checklist. Review the property management contract template to understand what the new agreement should cover.

Step 3: Give Formal Written Notice to Your Current Manager

Once your new manager is secured, give formal written notice to your current manager in accordance with the agreement. Written notice should include:

  • The date of the notice
  • Your intention to terminate the management agreement
  • The termination date (calculated from the notice requirement in the agreement)
  • A request for confirmation of receipt

Send notice by a method that provides proof of delivery—certified mail with return receipt or email with read receipt. Keep a copy of the notice and confirmation for your records.

Be professional and factual in the notice. You do not need to enumerate every grievance. A simple, clear statement of termination is sufficient.

Step 4: Request All Owner Documents From Your Current Manager

Once notice has been given, begin requesting the documents and information you will need to complete the transition. This typically includes:

  • Copies of all current leases, including any addenda or side agreements
  • Tenant contact information
  • Security deposit amounts held per tenant
  • Move-in inspection reports for current tenants
  • Maintenance history for each unit
  • Outstanding maintenance work orders (open or pending)
  • Vendor contact information and any outstanding vendor invoices
  • Keys, access codes, and gate/entry credentials for each property
  • Utility account information
  • Insurance certificates
  • Any pending court filings or active eviction proceedings

Request this information in writing and give a specific deadline for delivery. Your management agreement may specify a timeframe for document transfer—if not, 10 to 14 business days is a reasonable request.

Step 5: Transfer Security Deposits in Compliance With State Law

Security deposit transfer is one of the most legally sensitive parts of a management transition. Nolo's landlord-tenant law encyclopedia covers security deposit handling rules by state. State law governs how security deposits must be held (typically in a separate trust account), who holds them during a transition, and how the transfer must be documented.

In most states:

  • Security deposits must be transferred to the new manager or directly to the owner within a specified period after management changes
  • Tenants must be notified in writing of the change in who holds their deposit
  • The deposit amounts must be accurately documented and reconciled

Work with both your outgoing and incoming manager to ensure the transfer is documented correctly. A gap or discrepancy in security deposit records is one of the most common sources of legal disputes in property management transitions.

Step 6: Notify Tenants of the Management Change

Tenants should be notified of the management change promptly and professionally. A written notice—delivered by email, postal mail, or through the new owner portal—should include:

  • The name and contact information of the new management company
  • The effective date of the change
  • Where to send rent payments going forward
  • Who to contact for maintenance requests
  • Any new tenant portal login information

Frame the communication professionally and positively. Tenants who feel uncertain about what the change means for them are more likely to question their tenancy. Reassure them that their lease terms remain unchanged and that the transition is designed to improve their experience.

In some states, landlord-tenant law requires formal written notice of a change in management—typically within a specified number of days. Check your state's requirements.

Step 7: Update Rent Payment Instructions

If tenants were paying rent through the old manager's platform or to a bank account controlled by the old manager, you need to update payment instructions immediately. Establish the new payment method—whether that is a new tenant portal, ACH instructions, or a physical address—and communicate it clearly to all tenants before the first rent due date under the new management.

Do not let a single rent payment go to the wrong destination. Once money enters the wrong account, recovering it can be slow and contentious.

Step 8: Confirm Open Items Are Resolved at Transition

Before the formal handoff date, conduct a reconciliation with the outgoing manager to confirm:

  • All outstanding maintenance work orders have been addressed or clearly documented for the incoming manager
  • All vendor invoices have been paid or transferred
  • Owner financial accounts are reconciled through the transition date
  • All property access credentials have been transferred
  • Security deposits are accounted for and transferred
  • Tenant files are complete and transferred

Get written confirmation of this reconciliation. If disputes arise later about the condition of accounts, open maintenance issues, or missing documents, you want documentation that the handoff was properly completed.

How Long Does the Transition Take?

A well-coordinated transition typically takes 30 to 60 days from notice to full handoff. The timeline is driven primarily by the notice period in your existing agreement. If your agreement requires 60 days' notice, plan accordingly so that your new manager is ready to take over the day the old manager's services end.

Common Transition Pitfalls to Avoid

  • Giving notice before the new manager is ready: Creates a management gap that harms tenants and properties
  • Failing to document the handoff: Leaves you without evidence if disputes arise later
  • Missing the security deposit transfer deadline: Creates legal liability and potential claims from tenants
  • Neglecting vendor relationships: Outstanding invoices or contracts with vendors need to transfer cleanly
  • Not updating bank accounts: Rent or deposits going to the wrong account can be very difficult to recover

Summary

Switching property management companies is a manageable process when approached systematically. Review your current contract, secure a new manager before giving notice, request all necessary documents, transfer security deposits in compliance with state law, notify tenants professionally, and document the entire handoff.

The key is sequencing everything correctly so that neither your tenants nor your cash flow experience any disruption. For help evaluating your new manager before you make the switch, use the how to choose a property manager guide and review the hiring property manager checklist.
