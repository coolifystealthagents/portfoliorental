Security cameras at rental properties protect against theft, vandalism, and liability disputes — but they also raise serious privacy concerns that landlords must navigate carefully. Installing a camera in the wrong location, or without proper disclosure, can violate tenant privacy rights and create significant legal liability. Understanding the rules before you install is essential.

This guide covers where landlords can and cannot install security cameras, disclosure requirements, tenant rights, and how to build a legally compliant camera system that protects your property without violating privacy.

The Core Legal Principle: Privacy Expectation

Security camera law is built on the concept of "reasonable expectation of privacy." People have a reasonable expectation of privacy:

  • Inside their homes and apartments
  • In private areas like bedrooms and bathrooms
  • In private conversations

People generally do NOT have a reasonable expectation of privacy:

  • On public sidewalks and streets
  • In common areas of buildings (lobbies, hallways, parking lots)
  • In the exterior of their own home when viewed from a public area

For landlords, this translates into clear rules about where cameras are permissible.

Where Landlords Can Install Security Cameras

The following locations are generally permissible for landlord-installed security cameras:

Exterior of the Building

  • Exterior entry points (front door, back door, side doors) — Permitted; these face areas without privacy expectation
  • Driveways and parking areas — Permitted; these are common or semi-public areas
  • Exterior walls and corners — Permitted when aimed at the exterior, parking, or street

Common Areas in Multi-Family Properties

  • Lobby and main entrance — Widely permitted; common in apartment buildings
  • Mail area and package room — Permitted; package theft is a primary use case
  • Laundry rooms — Generally permitted (but aim cameras at entry/equipment, not changing areas)
  • Pool and recreation areas — Permitted as long as cameras do not capture private spaces
  • Parking garages and lots — Permitted
  • Stairwells and corridors — Permitted; primarily for security documentation
  • Elevator interiors — Widely permitted in larger buildings

Where Landlords Cannot Install Security Cameras

The following locations are generally prohibited for landlord cameras — these are areas where tenants have a reasonable expectation of privacy:

  • Inside rental units — Placing cameras inside a tenant's unit (living room, bedroom, kitchen, bathroom) without explicit consent is a serious privacy violation and is illegal in virtually every jurisdiction
  • Bathrooms or changing areas — Any camera in or aimed at a bathroom, shower, or changing area is illegal, full stop
  • Bedroom windows — Cameras should not be positioned to look through the windows of a tenant's bedroom or other private areas
  • Common laundry room changing areas — Where people change, cameras violate privacy

A Specific Rule: Cameras Must Not Peer Into Private Spaces

Even exterior cameras that are technically positioned on common or landlord property can create legal issues if they are positioned or angled to view into private spaces — for example, a camera aimed such that it captures the interior of a ground-floor tenant's bedroom through the window. The camera's position does not override the privacy expectation of the space being viewed.

State-Specific Laws

Laws governing surveillance cameras vary by state. Key considerations:

California

California's Penal Code Section 647(j)(1) prohibits any installation of a device to observe, record, or amplify a person in "private area of another person" without consent. California courts take a broad view of privacy expectations. Landlords in California should be conservative about camera placement and always disclose camera locations to tenants.

New York

New York prohibits unauthorized installation of observation devices in rooms where privacy is expected. Camera placement in common areas is generally permissible.

Illinois

Illinois has audio recording restrictions under the Illinois Eavesdropping Act. If cameras capture audio, two-party consent may be required for any audio recording. Consider disabling audio on cameras in common areas.

Washington

Washington requires all parties to a private conversation to consent to recording. Cameras capturing audio in any setting may require consent.

Federal Laws

The federal Video Voyeurism Prevention Act prohibits capturing video of intimate areas. While primarily aimed at individual voyeurism, the principle applies to any surveillance.

Audio Recording: An Additional Complication

Many modern security cameras capture audio as well as video. Audio recording adds a layer of legal complexity because most states have wiretapping or eavesdropping statutes that govern audio recording.

  • Two-party/all-party consent states — California, Illinois, Washington, Massachusetts, and others require all parties to a conversation to consent before audio is recorded. In these states, audio-recording cameras in common areas (where conversations may be captured) can violate state eavesdropping law.
  • One-party states — Many states only require one party to consent. Even here, recording private conversations has limitations.

Best practice: Disable audio recording on exterior and common area cameras. You gain little operational benefit from audio at these locations, and you eliminate the audio recording legal complexity entirely.

Disclosure Requirements

Even where cameras are legally permissible, disclosure to tenants is both a legal requirement and best practice. Review tenant privacy laws for guidance.

Lease Disclosure

Include a provision in your lease or a camera addendum that:

  • Lists all camera locations on the property
  • States that cameras are for security purposes only
  • Confirms that cameras are not placed in any private areas
  • States your data retention and access policy

Posting Notice

In common areas of multi-family buildings, post visible notices indicating that security cameras are in use. This is required by law in some states and is best practice regardless.

No Hidden Cameras

Never install hidden cameras anywhere on a rental property. Even in legally permissible areas, hidden cameras create significant legal and ethical liability. Always install cameras visibly and disclose them.

Camera Data: Storage, Retention, and Access

Retention Period

Most landlords retain camera footage for 7-30 days on a rolling basis. Longer retention requires more storage. Best practices:

  • 7-14 days is standard for exterior and common area cameras
  • Retain specific footage related to incidents as long as it may be needed for legal purposes

Who Has Access?

Limit access to camera footage to property management personnel with a legitimate need. Understand landlord liability protection before sharing footage.

Responding to Subpoenas and Law Enforcement Requests

The FTC's privacy guidelines apply to surveillance data. If law enforcement requests camera footage, work with counsel before providing it. In general, police with a valid warrant are entitled to footage; informal requests should be handled carefully.

Tenant Requests for Footage

Tenants may request camera footage related to incidents involving them (package theft from the lobby, for example). You are generally not legally required to provide footage unless compelled by a court, but cooperating with reasonable requests demonstrates good faith.

What You Can Do With Camera Systems

Landlords with compliant camera installations can:

  • Monitor common areas for security incidents
  • Document access to the property for evidence in disputes
  • Deter and document property vandalism, theft, and trespassing
  • Verify vendor and contractor access (particularly useful with keyless entry integration)
  • Provide footage to law enforcement following documented incidents

Ring and Doorbell Cameras

Video doorbell cameras (Ring, Nest Hello, Eufy Video Doorbell) are popular and increasingly common at rental property entry points. Rules:

  • Landlord-installed doorbells — You own and control the device; rules above apply
  • Tenant-installed doorbells — Tenants may install their own doorbell cameras but the same legal rules apply (cannot record audio without consent in two-party states, etc.)
  • Field of view — Doorbell cameras with a wide field of view may capture footage of neighboring properties, public sidewalks, or adjacent tenant areas; be aware of what they capture

Some landlords include a provision in the lease addressing tenant-installed cameras (requiring disclosure, prohibiting placement aimed at other units, etc.).

Smart Camera Systems for Rental Properties

Self-Managed Systems

For small portfolios, self-managed systems work well:

  • Arlo Pro 4 — Wireless, weather-resistant, clear 2K video, good app
  • Eufy Security 2K — Local storage, no monthly fee
  • Ring Stick Up Cam — Widely compatible with Ring ecosystem, subscription required for cloud storage

Professionally Monitored Systems

For larger properties or landlords who want professional monitoring:

  • SimpliSafe and ADT offer camera-integrated security systems
  • Commercial systems (Avigilon, Hikvision) are appropriate for larger multi-family buildings

Conclusion

Security cameras are a valuable protection tool for rental properties when installed and used in compliance with privacy law. The key rules are simple: cameras in private spaces (inside units, bathrooms) are prohibited; cameras in exterior and common areas with proper disclosure are generally permissible; audio recording requires extra care in consent-required states. Always disclose camera locations in your lease and visibly post notice in common areas.
