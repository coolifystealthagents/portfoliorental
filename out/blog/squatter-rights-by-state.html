Squatter Rights by State: What Landlords Must Know

Squatters occupying your vacant property is not just a nuisance — it is a legal situation that requires careful handling. In many states, you cannot simply change the locks or remove a squatter's belongings. Depending on how long the occupation lasts and the circumstances, squatters may gain legal protections that force you through a formal eviction process.

This guide explains squatter rights, adverse possession laws, and practical steps to protect your properties.

What Is a Squatter?

A squatter is someone who occupies a property without the owner's permission or a legal right to be there. Squatters differ from trespassers in a legally significant way:

  • Trespasser — Someone who enters property without permission and typically does not remain. Can be removed by police as a criminal matter.
  • Squatter — Someone who occupies property without permission but establishes a degree of residency. May require a civil eviction process to remove.

The line between trespasser and squatter often comes down to duration and the appearance of residency. A squatter who has personal belongings in the property, receives mail there, or has been present for an extended period may be treated as an unauthorized occupant requiring formal eviction.

Adverse Possession: How Squatters Can Gain Legal Rights

Adverse possession is a legal doctrine that allows someone to claim ownership of property they have occupied continuously for a statutory period. Cornell Law Institute provides a helpful overview of landlord-tenant law and related property rights concepts. While adverse possession claims on rental properties are rare, understanding the requirements helps you prevent them.

Elements of Adverse Possession

To claim adverse possession, the occupant must typically prove:

  1. Actual possession — Physically present and using the property
  2. Open and notorious — Occupation is visible and obvious, not hidden
  3. Exclusive — Not sharing possession with the owner
  4. Continuous — Uninterrupted for the statutory period
  5. Hostile — Without the owner's permission (this is a legal term, not necessarily confrontational)

Some states add requirements like:

  • Payment of property taxes during the occupation period
  • Color of title (some document, even if invalid, claiming ownership)
  • Good faith belief of ownership

Statutory Periods by State

The time required for an adverse possession claim varies widely:

  • Short periods (5-7 years) — California (5 years with taxes paid), Florida (7 years with color of title)
  • Medium periods (10-15 years) — New York (10 years), Texas (10 years), Illinois (20 years)
  • Long periods (15-21+ years) — Pennsylvania (21 years), New Jersey (30 years)

These long timelines make adverse possession claims against actively managed rental properties extremely unlikely, but vacant or neglected properties are vulnerable.

Removing Squatters: State-by-State Approaches

States Requiring Formal Eviction

Most states treat squatters who have established residency as unauthorized tenants, requiring a formal eviction process. Follow the eviction process step by step to avoid procedural errors that can delay removal:

  • File an unlawful detainer or eviction action in court
  • Serve proper notice (varies by state)
  • Attend a court hearing
  • Obtain a judgment and writ of possession
  • Have the sheriff execute the removal

This process can take weeks to months depending on the jurisdiction and court backlog.

States With Expedited Processes

Some states have enacted laws specifically targeting squatters:

  • Florida (2024 law) — Allows property owners to request immediate law enforcement removal of squatters upon providing proof of ownership
  • Georgia — Criminal trespass charges can expedite removal
  • Several states are considering or have recently passed similar expedited removal laws

Self-Help Removal Risks

In most states, landlords cannot legally:

  • Change locks to remove a squatter who has established residency
  • Remove a squatter's belongings from the property
  • Shut off utilities to force a squatter out
  • Physically remove or threaten a squatter

These self-help actions can result in liability for wrongful eviction, property damage, and assault claims — even against a squatter. Nolo's landlord-tenant law encyclopedia explains the legal risks of self-help eviction in detail.

Preventing Squatters

Prevention is far easier and cheaper than removal. For vacant properties:

Physical Security

  • Secure all entry points — Deadbolts, window locks, padlocks on gates
  • Board up properties undergoing extended renovation
  • Install security cameras with remote monitoring
  • Use smart locks that log entry attempts
  • Maintain the exterior — Overgrown yards signal vacancy

Regular Monitoring

  • Inspect vacant properties weekly — Drive by at minimum, walk through monthly (see property inspection requirements for best practices)
  • Use property monitoring services for remote properties
  • Set up utility alerts — Unexpected water or electricity usage can indicate unauthorized occupation
  • Monitor mail delivery — Redirect mail and check for unauthorized mail at the property
  • Coordinate with neighbors — Ask them to report unusual activity

Legal Protections

  • Post no-trespassing signs — Required in some states to establish criminal trespass
  • File vacant property registrations where required by local ordinance
  • Maintain current insurance that covers vacant property (standard policies may not; the FEMA flood insurance program may also be relevant for vacant properties in flood zones)
  • Consider a property caretaker for long-term vacancies

Minimize Vacancy Duration

The best prevention is having a tenant:

  • Price vacant units competitively to reduce vacancy time
  • Begin marketing before current tenants move out
  • Streamline your rental property turnover process to minimize gaps

Dealing With Holdover Tenants vs Squatters

A holdover tenant — someone who stays after their lease expires — is different from a squatter:

  • Holdover tenants had a legal right to be there initially
  • The eviction process for holdovers is typically faster and more straightforward
  • Holdover tenants may owe rent for the holdover period

Do not confuse holdover tenant situations with squatter situations. The legal procedures may differ.

Key Takeaways

Squatter situations are preventable with basic property security and monitoring. If a squatter does occupy your property, act immediately — the longer they stay, the harder removal becomes. Follow your state's legal process for removal rather than resorting to self-help, which creates liability. For vacant properties, invest in security, conduct regular inspections, and minimize vacancy periods.
