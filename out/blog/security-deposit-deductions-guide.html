Security Deposit Deductions: What Landlords Can Withhold

Security deposit deductions are one of the most frequent sources of landlord-tenant disputes. Tenants feel they left the property in good condition; landlords see damage beyond normal wear and tear. The difference between a smooth move-out and a small claims court date often comes down to documentation, clear standards, and following your state's rules precisely.

This guide explains what you can deduct, how to document it, and how to handle the process correctly. For a legal overview of the landlord-tenant framework governing deposits, see the Cornell Law Institute.

What You Can Deduct

Most states allow security deposit deductions for:

Unpaid Rent

Any rent owed at the time of move-out, including the final month's rent if the tenant did not pay.

Damage Beyond Normal Wear and Tear

This is the most common deduction category and the most disputed. The key distinction:

  • Normal wear and tear — Gradual deterioration from ordinary use that you cannot charge for
  • Damage — Loss or destruction caused by negligence, carelessness, abuse, or misuse that you can charge for
Normal Wear and Tear Tenant Damage
Faded paint from sunlight Crayon marks or large holes in walls
Worn carpet from foot traffic Stains, burns, or pet damage to carpet
Loose door handles from use Broken door from forced entry
Minor scuffs on hardwood Deep gouges or water damage on floors
Fading of countertops Burns or cuts in countertops
Dusty blinds Broken or missing blinds
Nail holes from normal picture hanging Large anchor holes or damaged drywall

Cleaning Costs

You can deduct cleaning costs if the tenant left the unit dirtier than when they moved in, beyond normal cleaning expectations. Deductions are appropriate for:

  • Heavy grease buildup in kitchen
  • Mold or mildew from poor maintenance (the EPA lead and toxics resources cover related environmental hazards in housing)
  • Heavily soiled carpets requiring professional cleaning
  • Trash or debris left behind
  • Bathroom requiring deep cleaning beyond normal turnover

You generally cannot deduct for routine turnover cleaning that you would perform between any tenants.

Lease Violation Charges

If your lease specifies charges for certain violations and your state allows it:

  • Unauthorized pet fees or damage
  • Early termination fees (where legally permitted)
  • Key replacement costs
  • Charges for removing unauthorized fixtures or modifications

Utility Charges

Unpaid utility balances that the tenant was responsible for under the lease.

Documentation Requirements

Move-In Inspection

Your documentation starts at move-in, not move-out:

  • Conduct a thorough move-in inspection with the tenant present
  • Photograph or video every room, wall, floor, appliance, and fixture
  • Note existing damage on a signed move-in checklist
  • Both parties should sign and date the inspection report
  • Provide the tenant with a copy

Without a move-in inspection, proving that damage occurred during the tenancy becomes much harder.

Move-Out Inspection

At move-out:

  • Conduct the inspection with the tenant present if possible (some states require this)
  • Photograph and video the same areas documented at move-in
  • Compare conditions side by side
  • Note any damage beyond normal wear and tear
  • Document cleaning needs
  • Record any items left behind

Repair Documentation

For each deduction, maintain:

  • Dated photographs showing the damage
  • Contractor or vendor invoices for repairs
  • Receipts for materials if you did the work yourself
  • Comparison photos from move-in showing prior condition
  • Written description of the damage and repair

The Itemized Statement

Most states require landlords to provide an itemized statement of deductions when returning the security deposit. Your statement should include:

  • Each deduction listed separately with a description
  • The amount charged for each item
  • Copies of invoices or receipts (required in some states)
  • The total amount deducted
  • The remaining deposit balance being returned
  • A check for the balance owed to the tenant

State Return Deadlines

Deadlines for returning the deposit and itemized statement vary by state:

  • 14 days — Hawaii, Montana, Washington
  • 21 days — California
  • 30 days — Common in many states (Colorado, Florida, Illinois, Texas, and others)
  • 45-60 days — A few states allow longer periods

Missing the deadline can result in forfeiture of your right to deduct, penalty fees, or liability for the full deposit amount plus damages.

Check your state's security deposit laws for specific deadlines.

Common Deduction Disputes

Painting

  • You can charge for painting if the tenant damaged walls beyond normal wear (crayon, markers, excessive holes, unauthorized paint colors)
  • You generally cannot charge for repainting that would have been needed anyway due to age
  • Some states use a depreciation model — if the paint was already 3 years old and has a 5-year life, you can only charge for the remaining 2 years of useful life

Carpet

  • Pet stains, burns, and tears are deductible — for more on documenting and recovering costs, see our property damage by tenant guide
  • Normal traffic patterns and fading are not
  • Apply depreciation — if carpet has a 10-year life and the tenant lived there for 7 years, you can only charge 30% of replacement cost
  • Professional cleaning costs are often deductible if the tenant left carpets excessively dirty

Appliances

  • Damage from misuse is deductible
  • Mechanical failure from age and normal use is not
  • Missing parts or accessories are deductible
  • Normal wear on surfaces is not

Cleaning

  • Deep cleaning beyond normal turnover standards is deductible
  • Basic turnover cleaning that you would do regardless is generally not
  • Document the condition with photos to support cleaning charges

Avoiding Disputes

Set Clear Expectations

  • Provide a move-out letter to tenants before they leave
  • Explain the move-out inspection process in advance
  • Define your cleaning standards in the lease
  • Include specific examples of normal wear vs damage

Use the Pre-Move-Out Inspection

Some states require or allow a pre-move-out inspection where you identify issues the tenant can fix before the final move-out. This reduces disputes and helps tenants get their full deposit back.

Be Reasonable and Fair

Courts scrutinize landlords who deduct aggressively. Nolo's landlord-tenant guide provides state-specific guidance on what courts consider reasonable. Reasonable deductions for genuine damage are upheld; excessive charges for minor issues or normal wear are not. When in doubt, err on the side of returning more of the deposit.

Key Takeaways

Security deposit deductions must be documented, itemized, and returned within your state's deadline. Start documentation at move-in with thorough photos and a signed checklist. At move-out, compare conditions, photograph damage, get repair estimates, and provide a detailed itemized statement. Follow your state's specific rules precisely — procedural errors can cost you the entire deposit regardless of the damage.
