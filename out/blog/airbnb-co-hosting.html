Running a short-term rental can be incredibly rewarding, but it is also time-intensive. Between managing guest communications, coordinating cleaners, handling check-ins, and keeping your listing improved, many hosts find themselves overwhelmed.

That is where Airbnb co-hosting comes in.

Co-hosting is a flexible arrangement that allows property owners to bring on a trusted partner, often a local property manager, friend, or professional co-host, to share operational responsibilities. It is one of the most practical tools available to hosts who want to scale without burning out.

This guide covers everything you need to know: how Airbnb co-hosting works, what co-hosts actually do, how fees are structured, and how to find or become a co-host.

What Is Airbnb Co-Hosting?

Airbnb co-hosting is an official feature on the Airbnb platform that lets a primary host add one or more co-hosts to help manage their listing. Co-hosts get access to the listing dashboard and can perform tasks like responding to guests, updating calendars, managing pricing, and coordinating housekeeping.

Unlike a traditional property management company, co-hosting keeps the listing under the primary host's account. The original host retains ownership and control of the listing, while the co-host operates with a defined set of permissions.

Who Uses Co-Hosting?

  • Property owners who travel frequently or have day jobs and cannot manage day-to-day operations
  • Investors who own multiple properties and need operational support
  • Remote hosts managing listings in cities they don't live in
  • New hosts who want an experienced partner to guide their early days

What Does an Airbnb Co-Host Do?

Co-hosts can take on as many or as few responsibilities as the primary host delegates. Airbnb's platform gives primary hosts granular control over what each co-host can access and manage.

Common Co-Host Responsibilities

Guest Communication

  • Responding to inquiries and booking requests
  • Sending pre-arrival messages with check-in instructions
  • Handling mid-stay issues and guest complaints
  • Coordinating post-checkout reviews

Listing Management

  • Updating availability calendars
  • Adjusting pricing (with or without pricing tools)
  • Editing listing descriptions, photos, and amenities
  • Managing minimum stay requirements and booking settings

Operations Coordination

  • Scheduling and confirming cleaning crews
  • Overseeing maintenance requests
  • Conducting property inspections between stays
  • Restocking consumables and supplies

Financial Reporting

  • Tracking income and expenses
  • Communicating earnings to the primary host
  • Assisting with documentation for taxes

Airbnb Co-Hosting Permissions: What Hosts Can Control

When you add a co-host on Airbnb, you choose their permission level. As of 2026, Airbnb allows primary hosts to grant co-hosts access to specific features:

Permission Level What Co-Host Can Do
Full Access Manage everything including payouts
Limited Access Manage bookings and communications, no financials
Custom Pick and choose individual permissions

This flexibility is important. If you are bringing on a co-host for the first time, starting with limited access and expanding permissions as trust builds is a smart approach.

How Much Do Airbnb Co-Hosts Earn?

Co-host compensation varies widely depending on the scope of work, local market, and the agreement between parties. There is no fixed Airbnb co-hosting fee set by the platform, it is negotiated between the primary host and co-host.

Typical Co-Hosting Fee Structures

Percentage of Revenue (Most Common) Most co-hosts charge between 10% and 30% of gross rental revenue, depending on responsibilities:

  • 10, 15%: Light duties (guest messaging, calendar management only)
  • 15, 25%: Full operations including cleaning coordination and inspections
  • 25, 30%+: Fully managed, including furnishing and setup services

Flat Monthly Fee Some co-hosts charge a flat rate regardless of occupancy. This works well for hosts in high-season markets where the property is almost always booked.

Per-Task Pricing Less common but used for specific one-off services like a deep clean after a long-term stay or a seasonal property refresh.

Who Pays the Cleaning Fee?

In most arrangements, the cleaning fee collected from guests goes directly to cover cleaning costs. If the co-host manages the cleaning crew, they may handle payment and simply reconcile costs with the primary host monthly.

How to Find an Airbnb Co-Host

Finding the right co-host is critical. A bad fit can lead to poor guest experiences, damaged reviews, and financial disputes.

Where to Look

Airbnb's Co-Host Network Airbnb has introduced tools to connect hosts with experienced co-hosts in their area. Check your Airbnb hosting dashboard for the co-host marketplace or network feature.

Local STR Communities Facebook groups, Reddit communities (r/airbnb, r/airbnbhosts), and local real estate investor meetups are excellent places to find vetted co-hosts.

Professional Property Managers Many traditional property management companies also offer co-hosting services. Compare what they offer versus standalone co-hosts, a vacation rental property management company may offer more robust systems but at higher cost.

Personal Network Trusted friends or family members who are local to your property can make excellent co-hosts, especially if they already have hospitality or real estate experience.

What to Look for in a Co-Host

  • Strong communication skills and fast response times
  • Experience managing short-term rentals (check their own host profile and reviews)
  • Knowledge of local regulations and STR compliance requirements
  • Reliability, especially for last-minute issues like broken appliances or early arrivals
  • Alignment on hosting style and guest experience standards

How to Become an Airbnb Co-Host

If you are on the other side of this equation, interested in becoming a co-host as a business, here is how to build that career.

Step 1: Build Your Own Hosting Experience

The best co-hosts have firsthand experience running their own Airbnb listing. Start by hosting a room or a small property to understand the workflow from the inside.

Step 2: Get to Know Your Local Market

Understand average daily rates, occupancy trends, local regulations, and what guests in your area expect. Tools like AirDNA can help you benchmark performance across your market.

Step 3: Develop Your Systems

Successful co-hosts run tight operational systems. This means:

  • Standardized guest messaging templates
  • Reliable cleaning and maintenance teams
  • A process for handling emergencies at any hour
  • Familiarity with airbnb automation tools that save time

Step 4: Set Your Rate and Services

Define your service packages clearly:

  • What tasks are included in your base fee?
  • What is billed additionally (deep cleans, emergency callouts, restocking runs)?
  • How will you report earnings and expenses to property owners?

Step 5: Market Yourself

Create a professional profile, gather testimonials from hosts you have worked with, and list yourself on co-host marketplaces. Word of mouth is powerful in this industry, delivering exceptional results for one host often leads to referrals.

Co-Hosting vs. Property Management: What's the Difference?

Many hosts confuse co-hosting with hiring a full property management company. There are meaningful differences:

Factor Co-Host Property Management Company
Listing Ownership Stays with primary host May be transferred to PM account
Platform Airbnb native feature Often third-party tools
Flexibility Highly customizable Standard service packages
Cost Typically 10, 25% Often 20, 40%
Scale Usually 1, 5 properties Can manage dozens or hundreds
Accountability Informal agreements Formal contracts

Legal and Tax Considerations for Co-Hosts

Written Agreements Are Essential

Even if you co-host with a friend, always document the arrangement in writing, a practice the SBA recommends for all businesses. Your agreement should cover:

  • Fee structure and payment schedule
  • Scope of responsibilities
  • How liability is handled for property damage or guest incidents
  • Termination conditions and notice periods

Tax Treatment of Co-Host Income

Co-hosts who earn income from hosting services are generally classified as self-employed by the IRS. This means:

  • Co-hosting income is reportable to tax authorities
  • Business expenses related to co-hosting may be deductible
  • Depending on volume, co-hosts may need to register as a business entity

Consult a tax professional familiar with short-term rental income. Our short-term rental tax guide covers the basics for STR operators.

Common Co-Hosting Mistakes to Avoid

1. Not Setting Clear Expectations Ambiguity breeds conflict. Define who handles what before the first guest arrives.

2. Skipping the Written Agreement Handshake deals go wrong. A simple one-page agreement protects both parties.

3. Choosing the Wrong Person A co-host who is slow to respond or unreliable will tank your reviews. Treat the selection process like hiring an employee.

4. Micromanaging If you hired a co-host to take things off your plate, let them. Trust your systems and check in on performance metrics rather than every decision.

5. Ignoring Performance Data Track your response rate, review scores, occupancy, and revenue before and after bringing on a co-host. Data tells you whether the arrangement is working.

Is Co-Hosting Right for You?

Co-hosting is an excellent option if:

  • You own a property but lack time for daily management
  • You want to scale your portfolio without becoming a full-time operator
  • You are entering a new market and want local expertise
  • You are burning out from self-managing

It may not be the right fit if:

  • You are highly hands-on and have difficulty delegating
  • Your property generates modest income and a co-host fee would significantly cut margins
  • You cannot find a trustworthy co-host in your area

Conclusion

Airbnb co-hosting is one of the most underused strategies in short-term rental investing. Whether you are a property owner looking for operational relief or an experienced host ready to monetize your skills, co-hosting opens up a scalable, flexible path forward.

The keys to success are clear communication, a well-defined agreement, and the right systems in place. With those foundations, co-hosting can transform how you manage, and grow, your short-term rental portfolio.

Quick Reference: Airbnb Co-Hosting Essentials

  • Co-hosts earn 10, 30% of revenue depending on scope
  • Primary hosts control all permission levels on the platform
  • Always use a written co-hosting agreement
  • Look for co-hosts with verified STR experience and strong communication skills
  • Track performance data to measure the value of your co-hosting arrangement
