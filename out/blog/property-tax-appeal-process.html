How to Appeal Your Property Tax Assessment

Property taxes are typically a rental investor's second-largest expense after the mortgage. Yet most landlords pay their assessment without question — even when the assessment is inaccurate, outdated, or based on incorrect property data. The property tax appeal process exists precisely to correct these errors, and successful appeals can save landlords hundreds or even thousands of dollars annually.

This guide walks you through the complete property tax appeal process: understanding assessments, identifying grounds for appeal, gathering evidence, and navigating the hearing process to get your assessment reduced.

Why Property Tax Assessments Are Often Wrong

Local governments reassess properties on varying schedules — some annually, others every few years. Assessors rely on mass appraisal models that use limited data and comparable sales, often without physically inspecting each property. The result is that a meaningful percentage of properties are over-assessed at any given time.

Common reasons for inaccurate assessments include:

  • Incorrect property data: Square footage, bedroom count, bathroom count, or lot size recorded incorrectly in the assessor's records
  • Outdated comparable sales: Using comps from a rising market during a subsequent market correction
  • Failing to account for property condition: Assessors cannot inspect every property; they may not know about deferred maintenance, functional obsolescence, or needed repairs
  • Incorrect property classification: Zoning or use classification errors that affect the applicable assessment method
  • Unequal assessment: Your property assessed at a higher percentage of value than similar nearby properties

Studies consistently show that 30–60% of properties are over-assessed in many jurisdictions. The problem is that most owners never appeal.

Step 1: Review Your Assessment Notice Carefully

When your assessment notice arrives — typically by mail annually or during reassessment years — do not simply file it away. Review:

  • Assessed value vs. market value: Some jurisdictions assess at full market value; others use a percentage (assessment ratio). Know your local rules.
  • Property description: Verify square footage, bedroom/bathroom count, lot size, and property type. Errors here are surprisingly common.
  • Assessment date: This tells you which market period the assessment reflects.
  • Appeal deadline: This is critical. Deadlines vary widely (30 to 90 days after notice is typical) and missing them forfeits your appeal rights for that year.

Mark the appeal deadline on your calendar the moment you receive the notice.

Step 2: Calculate Whether an Appeal Is Worth Pursuing

Before spending time preparing a case, estimate the potential savings:

  • Determine what you believe the correct assessed value should be
  • Calculate the difference in taxes using your local tax rate
  • Compare that savings to the time and cost of an appeal

Most informal appeals take only a few hours of preparation. Even a $30,000 reduction in assessed value at a 2% tax rate saves $600/year — every year until the next reassessment. That recurring savings compounds significantly over a typical hold period.

For a property assessed at $400,000 that you believe should be $350,000:

  • Tax reduction: $50,000 × 2% = $1,000/year
  • Over a 7-year hold: $7,000 in total savings

The math almost always favors attempting an appeal.

Step 3: Pull the Assessor's Property Record Card

Every assessor's office maintains a property record card (sometimes called a data card or property detail sheet) for each parcel. This is the foundation of your assessment and is usually available:

  • Online through your county assessor's or auditor's website
  • In person at the assessor's office

Review the card carefully for errors in:

  • Living area square footage
  • Number of bedrooms and bathrooms
  • Garage type and size
  • Year built
  • Quality rating or grade assigned by the assessor
  • Basement finish status
  • Any features listed that the property does not actually have

A simple factual error — such as listing 3 bathrooms when you only have 2 — is the easiest type of appeal to win. These are corrected as a matter of administrative process, often without a formal hearing.

Step 4: Research Comparable Sales

If your property data is accurate but the assessed value is still too high, you need to demonstrate that comparable properties sold for less than your assessed value. This is the valuation approach to an appeal.

To find comparable sales (comps):

  • Use your county assessor's online search tool for recent sales
  • Access your local MLS through a real estate agent or Zillow/Redfin for sold prices
  • Look for properties that are similar in size, age, condition, and location that sold in the 12 months preceding your assessment date

For each comp, note:

  • Sale price
  • Sale date
  • Square footage (calculate price per square foot)
  • Address and proximity to your property
  • Key differences (more bathrooms, renovated kitchen, better location)

If the average price per square foot of your comps suggests a value below your assessment, you have a valuation case.

Step 5: Document Property Condition

If your property has deferred maintenance, significant repairs needed, or functional issues that reduce its market value, document this thoroughly:

  • Photographs of problem areas (dated)
  • Contractor estimates for needed repairs
  • Independent appraisal (if the stakes are high enough)
  • Inspection reports
  • Insurance loss history

A property that requires $50,000 in roof and foundation work is worth less than a comparable property in turnkey condition. The assessor's model may not reflect this.

Step 6: File Your Informal or Formal Appeal

Most jurisdictions have a two-stage process:

Informal Review

The first step is usually an informal meeting or written request for review with the assessor's office. This is often the fastest and most painless path to a reduction.

Submit:

  • Your completed appeal form (available at the assessor's office or online)
  • A brief explanation of your grounds for appeal
  • Supporting documentation (comps, photos, property card corrections)

Many appeals are resolved at this stage. Assessors have incentive to correct clear errors or accept well-supported valuation challenges without going to a formal hearing.

Formal Hearing Before the Review Board

If the informal review does not result in an acceptable reduction, you can escalate to a formal hearing before a board of review, assessment appeals board, or equivalent body. At this stage:

  • Present your evidence formally
  • Be prepared to explain your comparable sales analysis
  • Bring printed copies of all documentation for the board members
  • Stay factual and unemotional — assessors and boards respond to data, not frustration

The hearing process and specific procedures vary by state and county. The National Tax Lien Association and your state's department of revenue typically publish guidance on local appeal procedures.

Step 7: Consider Hiring a Property Tax Consultant

For commercial properties or high-value residential rentals, professional property tax consultants can be worth the cost. They:

  • Know local assessors and appeal board members
  • Have access to comparable data and appraisal expertise
  • Often work on contingency (a percentage of the tax savings achieved)

A contingency arrangement means you pay nothing unless they win. This removes most of the risk and makes professional representation accessible for properties where the savings potential is meaningful.

State-by-State Considerations

The property tax appeal process is governed entirely by state and local law. Key variations include:

  • Deadlines: Range from 30 to 120+ days after notice
  • Hearing format: Some boards allow informal discussions; others are more formal quasi-judicial proceedings
  • Burden of proof: In most states, the assessor's value is presumed correct and you must overcome that presumption with evidence
  • Income approach: For commercial and larger rental properties, many jurisdictions allow income-based valuation evidence. Presenting your actual rental income and expense data (NOI) and applying market capitalization rates can support a lower value.

Research your specific state's procedures before filing. Your state's department of taxation or revenue website is the authoritative source.

After a Successful Appeal: Next Steps

If your appeal results in a reduction:

  • Verify the change appears on your next tax bill: Follow up if you do not see it reflected
  • Request a refund of any overpaid taxes: Many jurisdictions will refund the difference for the current year
  • Calendar the next reassessment: Many assessments revert to market value at the next general reassessment, so plan to review and appeal again

A successful appeal compounds over the full reassessment cycle — every year until the next assessment, you benefit from the lower tax bill. For a rental property held long-term, this recurring savings meaningfully improves your rental property cash flow and overall returns.

Integrating Property Tax Appeals into Your Portfolio Strategy

Property tax reduction is one component of a broader property tax reduction strategies approach that includes challenging assessments, seeking available exemptions, and structuring ownership to minimize exposure.

For landlords focused on reducing rental operating expenses and improving net operating income, an annual review of your property assessments should be standard practice — not a one-time exercise.

Track your property taxes carefully in your rental property expense tracking system, and flag any year-over-year increase greater than inflation for immediate review. Regular engagement with the appeal process is one of the simplest high-return activities available to any landlord looking to maximize rental income through expense control.
