Raising rent is one of the most direct ways to increase rental income—but doing it wrong can cost you a good tenant or, worse, create legal liability. The right rent increase notice is professional, legally compliant, and delivered with enough advance notice to preserve the landlord-tenant relationship.

This guide provides a free, customizable rent increase notice template, explains the required notice periods by state, and gives you practical guidance on setting the right amount and timing.

Free Rent Increase Notice Template

Use this template as a starting point. Customize it to reflect your local laws, the specific property, and your relationship with the tenant:

[Date]

[Tenant Name(s)] [Property Address] [City, State, ZIP]

RE: Notice of Rent Increase – Effective [New Rent Effective Date]

Dear [Tenant Name(s)],

This letter serves as formal notice that effective [New Rent Effective Date], the monthly rent for the above-referenced property will increase from $[Current Rent] to $[New Rent], an increase of $[Dollar Amount] per month.

This increase reflects [choose one or more: current market rental rates / increased operating costs / property improvements that have been made / general cost increases]. Your new monthly rent of $[New Rent] will be due on the [1st / 15th] of each month, per the terms of your existing lease agreement.

If you have any questions regarding this notice, please contact me at:

[Landlord/Property Manager Name] [Phone Number] [Email Address]

Please sign and return one copy of this notice to acknowledge receipt.

Tenant Signature: _________________________ Date: ___________

Landlord Signature: _________________________ Date: ___________

Sincerely,

[Landlord/Property Manager Name] [Address] [Phone | Email]

State Notice Requirements: How Much Time Do You Have to Give?

Notice requirements vary significantly by state. Providing insufficient notice can render the rent increase unenforceable and may violate tenant protection laws. Here are requirements for major states:

State Notice Required Notes
California 30 days (under 10% increase) / 90 days (10%+ increase) Rent control applies in many cities
New York 30–90 days depending on tenancy length ETPA may apply
Texas 30 days No statewide rent control
Florida 30 days (month-to-month) 60 days recommended
Illinois 30 days (Chicago: 30 days) Chicago has additional requirements
Washington 60 days Increased from 30 days in 2023
Oregon 90 days Statewide rent stabilization applies
Colorado 60 days HB22-1287 increased from 21 days
Arizona 30 days No statewide rent control
Georgia 60 days No statewide rent control

Important: These are minimum state requirements. Local ordinances—especially in cities with rent stabilization or rent control—may impose stricter limits. Always verify current requirements with a local attorney or your state's housing authority before issuing a rent increase.

HUD's state resource page provides links to each state's housing laws and tenant protection statutes.

When Can You Raise Rent?

Timing depends on your lease type:

Fixed-Term Lease (e.g., 12-month lease)

You generally cannot raise rent mid-lease unless the lease explicitly allows it. Rent increases typically apply at lease renewal. Deliver the notice at least 30–90 days before the lease ends (check your state's requirement).

Month-to-Month Tenancy

You can raise rent with proper advance notice (usually 30–60 days), effective at the start of the next rent period. Some states require the increase to align with the tenant's rent payment cycle.

During a Lease Renewal Negotiation

This is the most common and least disruptive time to implement an increase. Frame it as part of the lease renewal offer rather than a standalone notice.

How Much Should You Raise Rent?

Setting the right increase amount is as important as the legal requirements. Here's a framework:

Step 1: Research Current Market Rents

Run a rent comps analysis to understand what comparable units in your area are renting for. If your unit is below market, you have more room to increase; if it's at or above market, large increases risk vacancy.

Step 2: Consider Your Operating Cost Increases

If your property taxes, insurance, or maintenance costs have risen, factoring this into your increase communicates a legitimate business reason to the tenant.

Step 3: Weigh Tenant Retention Value

A reliable, long-term tenant is worth something concrete:

  • Vacancy typically costs 1–2 months of lost rent ($1,800–$3,600 on a $1,800/month unit)
  • Turnover costs: cleaning, repairs, advertising, leasing = $500–$2,000+
  • Risk of new unknown tenant: potentially higher

If your current rent is $50–$100 below market, raising it $75 may be smarter than raising it $200 to full market, which risks losing a great tenant. The rental pricing strategy guide covers this trade-off in detail.

Typical Rent Increase Benchmarks

  • Annual inflationary increase: 3–5% (maintains real value without shocking tenants)
  • Market-realignment increase: Up to 10% (bringing below-market rents in line)
  • Post-renovation increase: 8–15% (justified by specific improvements)
  • Rent control limits: Varies—often CPI + 5% or 5% flat cap

Tips for Delivering a Rent Increase Notice

Do It in Writing, Always

Verbal rent increases are unenforceable in most states and create disputes. Always use a written notice, even for informal month-to-month situations.

Send via Certified Mail + Email

Certified mail creates a legal record of delivery. Email provides a quick confirmation and is what tenants actually read. Doing both ensures documentation.

Give Context, Not Apologies

Explaining the reasons for the increase (rising costs, market adjustments, recent improvements) helps tenants understand it's a business decision. Avoid over-apologizing, which can signal weakness and invite negotiation you're not prepared for.

Time It Right

  • Avoid increases during difficult months for tenants (January, around holidays)
  • Give yourself runway: send notice well before the required deadline, not the day of
  • Pair increases with positive news when possible (renewed commitment to the property, upcoming improvement project)

Be Prepared to Negotiate

Some tenants will push back. Know your floor (the minimum increase you'll accept to avoid turnover) and your walk-away position (the point at which you'd prefer turnover). Have your market research ready to justify your ask.

Rent Increase Notice vs. Rent Increase Addendum

A rent increase notice is appropriate for month-to-month tenants and lease renewals where you're changing only the rent.

A rent increase addendum formally modifies an existing fixed-term lease and should be signed by both parties. Use this when you're extending a lease and updating rent terms simultaneously.

For new leases at higher rent, simply draft a new lease agreement at the new rate. No addendum needed.

What Happens if the Tenant Refuses?

If a tenant won't accept the rent increase and won't leave, you have limited options mid-lease (the increase isn't enforceable without their agreement during a fixed term). At lease end:

  1. Issue proper notice of non-renewal if the tenant refuses the new rate
  2. Begin the eviction/holdover process if the tenant stays past the lease end without accepting new terms (follow your state's specific procedures carefully)

This is why large increases at lease renewal require extra care—you need the tenant to either accept the new rate or vacate, and the process takes time. Planning well in advance is essential.

Special Situations

Rent-Controlled Properties

Many cities have rent stabilization or control ordinances that cap annual increases (often at CPI or 5%, whichever is lower). Research whether your property falls under local rent control before issuing any increase. Violations can result in the tenant recovering overpaid rent plus penalties.

Section 8 / HCV Tenants

Rent increases for Housing Choice Voucher tenants must be approved by the local housing authority. Submit an increase request at least 60 days before the desired effective date, using the housing authority's specific process.

Below-Market Legacy Rents

If you've inherited tenants with rents significantly below market, consider phasing increases over 2–3 years rather than implementing a single large jump. This preserves tenant goodwill while steadily moving toward market rate.

Sample Rent Increase Communication Email

For current tenants, a brief, friendly email before the formal notice maintains the relationship:

Subject: Upcoming Lease Renewal – Rent Adjustment

Hi [Tenant Name],

Your lease at [address] is coming up for renewal on [date], and I wanted to reach out personally before sending the formal renewal offer.

The market in our area has seen significant rent increases, and I'll be adjusting the rent to $[new amount]/month for the new term—an increase of $[amount] from your current rate of $[current amount]. I've enclosed a formal notice, and your official renewal offer will follow.

You've been a wonderful tenant, and I hope you'll choose to continue. If you have any questions, please don't hesitate to reach out.

Best regards, [Your Name]

This kind of personal communication—before the formal notice—often improves acceptance rates and preserves the relationship.

Connecting Rent Increases to Your Income Strategy

Consistent, well-managed rent increases are the most reliable way to maximize your rental income over time. A property that starts at $1,500/month and increases 4% annually reaches $2,220/month in 10 years—a 48% income growth that dramatically improves your cap rate and property value.

The key is consistency: small annual increases are far better than years of stagnation followed by a large, relationship-damaging jump. Build annual rent reviews into your property management calendar, use this template as your standard process, and benchmark against fair market rent each year to ensure you're optimizing income without unnecessary vacancy risk.
