Depreciation is one of the most powerful tax benefits available to rental property owners. It lets you deduct the cost of your property over time, even while that property is gaining value. This "paper loss" reduces your taxable rental income, which means you keep more of your cash flow.

Yet many landlords either do not understand depreciation or fail to use it fully. Some miss it entirely. Others make mistakes that trigger penalties or unexpected tax bills when they sell.

This rental property depreciation guide explains everything you need to know. You will learn how depreciation works, which method to use, how cost segregation can accelerate your deductions, what happens when you sell, and how to keep records that hold up to IRS scrutiny.

What Is Rental Property Depreciation?

Depreciation is a tax deduction that accounts for the wear and tear on your rental property over time. The IRS recognizes that buildings do not last forever. Roofs age, systems break down, and structures eventually need replacement. Depreciation lets you deduct a portion of the property's cost each year to reflect this decline.

Here is what makes depreciation unique: it is a non-cash deduction. You do not actually spend any money to claim it. You already spent the money when you bought the property. Depreciation simply spreads that cost over the useful life of the asset, reducing your taxable income each year.

A Simple Example

You buy a rental property for $250,000. The land is worth $50,000 and the building is worth $200,000. You can only depreciate the building, not the land, because land does not wear out.

Using the standard method, you depreciate the $200,000 building over 27.5 years. That gives you a deduction of $7,273 per year ($200,000 / 27.5).

If your rental income is $18,000 per year and your operating expenses are $8,000, your taxable income before depreciation is $10,000. After the $7,273 depreciation deduction, your taxable income drops to $2,727.

At a 22% tax bracket, depreciation saves you $1,600 in federal taxes every year. Over 27.5 years, the total tax savings from this single deduction exceeds $44,000.

This deduction is available on top of every other expense you track. It stacks with your rental property expense tracking deductions for repairs, insurance, property taxes, and management fees.

The Straight-Line Depreciation Method

The straight-line method is the standard approach for residential rental property. It is what most landlords use and what the IRS requires for the building itself.

How It Works

  • Residential rental property: Depreciated over 27.5 years as outlined in IRS Publication 946
  • Commercial property: Depreciated over 39 years
  • Method: Equal deduction each year (hence "straight-line")
  • Starting point: The month the property is placed in service (available for rent, not necessarily rented)

Calculating Your Depreciable Basis

Your depreciable basis is the value of the building and improvements, minus land. Here is how to find it:

Step 1: Determine your cost basis. This is typically the purchase price plus certain closing costs (title insurance, attorney fees, recording fees, transfer taxes). It does not include prepaid items like insurance or property taxes.

For example: You buy a property for $300,000. Eligible closing costs total $5,000. Your cost basis is $305,000.

Step 2: Subtract the land value. The IRS does not allow depreciation on land. You can determine land value using:

  • The assessed value ratio from your property tax bill (if the tax assessment shows land at 20% of total value, use 20% of your purchase price)
  • An independent appraisal that separates land and building values
  • Comparable land sales in the area

If land is valued at $60,000, your depreciable basis is $305,000 - $60,000 = $245,000.

Step 3: Divide by 27.5 years. $245,000 / 27.5 = $8,909 per year in depreciation.

Mid-Month Convention

The IRS uses a mid-month convention for the first and last year of depreciation. If you place the property in service in June, you get 6.5 months of depreciation that year (half of June plus July through December). This slightly reduces your first-year deduction but increases the final year.

Most tax software and CPAs handle this calculation automatically. Just make sure you know the exact date the property was placed in service.

Cost Segregation: Accelerate Your Deductions

Cost segregation is an advanced strategy that can dramatically increase your depreciation deductions in the early years of ownership. It is one of the most valuable tools in this rental property depreciation guide.

What Is Cost Segregation?

A cost segregation study breaks your property into its individual components and assigns each component a shorter depreciation life. Instead of depreciating the entire building over 27.5 years, certain components are reclassified into shorter categories:

  • 5-year property: Carpeting, appliances, certain fixtures, landscaping
  • 7-year property: Furniture, office equipment, certain mechanical components
  • 15-year property: Land improvements like parking lots, sidewalks, fencing, landscaping structures
  • 27.5-year property: The building structure itself (walls, roof, foundation)

These recovery period classifications are defined under IRC Section 168 of the federal tax code.

The Impact on Your Tax Bill

Consider a property with a $200,000 depreciable basis. Under straight-line depreciation, you deduct $7,273 per year.

With a cost segregation study, you might reclassify $50,000 into shorter-life categories:

  • $20,000 in 5-year property (annual deduction: $4,000 per year for 5 years)
  • $10,000 in 7-year property (annual deduction: $1,429 per year for 7 years)
  • $20,000 in 15-year property (annual deduction: $1,333 per year for 15 years)
  • $150,000 in 27.5-year property (annual deduction: $5,455 per year)

In year one, your total depreciation jumps to $12,217 compared to $7,273 under the standard method. That is $4,944 in additional deductions, which saves you over $1,000 in taxes at a 22% rate.

When Cost Segregation Makes Sense

A cost segregation study typically costs $5,000 to $15,000, depending on the property size and complexity. It makes financial sense when:

  • The property's depreciable basis is $500,000 or more
  • You plan to hold the property for at least 5 years
  • You have rental income (or other passive income) to offset with the deductions
  • You are in a tax bracket of 22% or higher

For a $1 million property, a cost segregation study can generate $100,000 or more in accelerated deductions over the first 5 years. That is real money back in your pocket.

How to Get a Cost Segregation Study

Work with a firm that specializes in cost segregation. They will:

  1. Review your purchase documents and property details
  2. Conduct a site visit or review property blueprints and photos
  3. Identify and classify all components
  4. Deliver a detailed report that your CPA uses to file your taxes

Your CPA should review the study and apply it to your return. If your CPA is not familiar with cost segregation, consider finding one who specializes in real estate.

Bonus Depreciation

Bonus depreciation allows you to deduct a large percentage of certain asset costs in the first year, rather than spreading them over their useful life.

Current Rules

Under the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act (TCJA), bonus depreciation rates are phasing down:

  • 2022: 100%
  • 2023: 80%
  • 2024: 60%
  • 2025: 40%
  • 2026: 20%
  • 2027 and beyond: 0% (unless Congress extends it)

Bonus depreciation applies to property with a recovery period of 20 years or less. This includes the 5-year, 7-year, and 15-year components identified in a cost segregation study. It does not apply to the 27.5-year building structure.

Practical Application

Using the earlier example, you identified $50,000 in short-life property through cost segregation. In 2026, with 20% bonus depreciation, you can deduct 20% of that amount ($10,000) in the first year on top of the normal depreciation.

This is a powerful accelerator. Even at the reduced 2026 rate, combining cost segregation with bonus depreciation can front-load significant deductions. Consult your CPA about how this interacts with your overall tax situation.

Interaction With Cost Segregation

Cost segregation and bonus depreciation work together. The cost segregation study identifies the assets eligible for bonus depreciation. Without the study, those assets would be lumped into the 27.5-year building category and would not qualify.

This is why cost segregation studies have become so popular among real estate investors. They unlock bonus depreciation on components that would otherwise depreciate slowly.

Depreciation on Improvements

When you make capital improvements to your rental property, each improvement starts its own depreciation schedule. This is separate from the building's original depreciation.

Examples

  • You install a new roof for $12,000. This is depreciated over 27.5 years, starting in the month installed. Annual deduction: $436.
  • You replace the HVAC system for $8,000. Depreciated over 27.5 years. Annual deduction: $291.
  • You add new appliances for $3,000. Depreciated over 5 years (or eligible for bonus depreciation). Annual deduction: $600 without bonus depreciation.

Track every improvement with its date, cost, and useful life. This information feeds directly into your rental property expense tracking system and your annual tax return.

Understanding which improvements have the best ROI helps you make smarter renovation decisions. Our guide on the best renovations for rental properties ranks upgrades by financial return so you can prioritize.

Depreciation Recapture: What Happens When You Sell

Depreciation gives you tax savings while you own the property. But the IRS gets some of that back when you sell. This is called depreciation recapture, and it is one of the most important concepts in this rental property depreciation guide.

How Recapture Works

When you sell a rental property, the IRS taxes the total depreciation you claimed (or should have claimed) at a recapture rate of up to 25%. This is separate from capital gains tax on the property's appreciation.

Example

You bought a property for $200,000 (building value) and claimed $72,727 in depreciation over 10 years. You sell the property for $300,000 (total, including land).

  • Adjusted basis: $200,000 - $72,727 = $127,273 (building) + $50,000 (land) = $177,273
  • Total gain: $300,000 - $177,273 = $122,727
  • Depreciation recapture: $72,727 taxed at 25% = $18,182
  • Remaining capital gain: $122,727 - $72,727 = $50,000, taxed at long-term capital gains rate (0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income)

Key Points About Recapture

  • You owe recapture whether you claimed depreciation or not. The IRS calculates recapture based on the depreciation you were "allowed or allowable." If you forgot to claim depreciation, you still owe recapture on the amount you should have claimed. This is a common and costly mistake.
  • A 1031 exchange defers recapture. If you sell your rental and exchange it for another investment property under Section 1031, you defer both capital gains and depreciation recapture. The depreciation carries forward to the new property.
  • Plan ahead. Factor recapture into your sell-versus-hold analysis. Use your rental property ROI calculator to model the after-tax proceeds from a sale.

Record Keeping for Depreciation

The IRS can audit your depreciation claims going back to the year you purchased the property. Solid records are essential.

What to Keep

  • Purchase settlement statement (HUD-1 or Closing Disclosure): Shows the purchase price, closing costs, and date of acquisition.
  • Property tax assessments: Documents the land-to-building value ratio.
  • Appraisal reports: If you used an appraisal to determine land and building values.
  • Cost segregation study: The full report and any supporting documentation.
  • Improvement records: Invoices, receipts, and contractor agreements for every capital improvement, with dates and amounts.
  • Depreciation schedules: Your annual depreciation calculations, usually generated by your tax software or CPA.
  • Prior tax returns: Keep copies of every return that includes rental property depreciation (Schedule E).

Organization Tips

  • Store everything digitally in a cloud-based system organized by property and year.
  • Keep a running depreciation schedule that shows the original basis, annual deductions taken, and remaining basis for each property and improvement.
  • Update the schedule each time you add a capital improvement.

This documentation is part of your broader rental property expense tracking system. Keep it in the same place so everything is accessible at tax time.

Working With a CPA: When and Why

Depreciation is one area where professional tax advice pays for itself. A good CPA who specializes in real estate will:

  • Correctly determine your depreciable basis and land allocation
  • Apply the mid-month convention accurately
  • Advise on whether a cost segregation study makes sense for your properties
  • Handle bonus depreciation calculations
  • Plan for depreciation recapture when you sell
  • Ensure you claim all depreciation you are entitled to (remember, unclaimed depreciation still triggers recapture)

Finding the Right CPA

Look for a CPA with these qualifications:

  • Real estate specialization: Not all CPAs understand rental property depreciation deeply. Ask specifically about their experience with rental properties, cost segregation, and 1031 exchanges.
  • Proactive approach: The best CPAs do not just file your return. They help you plan purchases, improvements, and sales to optimize your tax position.
  • Familiarity with your market: Local CPAs understand state and local tax implications that affect your overall return.

The cost of a real estate CPA ($500 to $2,000 per year for a small portfolio) is itself a deductible expense. And the tax savings they generate typically far exceed their fees.

Your CPA should work alongside your property management company to ensure all financial data flows smoothly. If you handle management fees and expenses on your own, make sure your records are clean before handing them to your CPA.

Common Depreciation Mistakes

Avoid these errors that cost landlords money or create problems with the IRS:

Not Claiming Depreciation

This is the biggest mistake. Some landlords skip depreciation because they do not understand it or think it is optional. It is not optional in practical terms: the IRS will calculate recapture as if you claimed it, whether you did or not. Failing to claim depreciation means you pay taxes twice: once on the income you did not offset, and again on the recapture when you sell.

If you have not been claiming depreciation, file amended returns (Form 1040-X) or use Form 3115 to correct the error. Your CPA can advise on the best approach.

Depreciating Land

Land is not depreciable. If you do not separate land value from building value, and you depreciate the entire purchase price, you will have problems in an audit. Always document the land-building split.

Using the Wrong Recovery Period

Residential rental property uses 27.5 years. Commercial property uses 39 years. Mixing these up changes your annual deduction significantly.

Forgetting to Depreciate Improvements

Every capital improvement starts its own depreciation schedule. If you spend $5,000 on a new HVAC system and do not depreciate it, you lose $182 per year in deductions for the next 27.5 years.

Ignoring Partial Dispositions

When you replace a component (like a roof), you can write off the remaining undepreciated value of the old component. This is called a partial disposition. Many landlords miss this and end up depreciating both the old and new components simultaneously, which the IRS does not allow.

How Depreciation Fits Your Overall Strategy

Depreciation is one piece of your rental property financial picture. It works alongside:

  • Cash flow management: Depreciation does not affect cash flow directly, but the tax savings it generates do. Track your rental property cash flow with and without tax benefits to see the full picture.
  • Income maximization: Every dollar you save on taxes through depreciation adds to your net income. Combine depreciation with strategies to maximize rental income for the best results.
  • Property value growth: Ironically, while you depreciate your property for tax purposes, it may be appreciating in market value. This creates a powerful wealth-building dynamic. Learn how to increase your rental property value while claiming depreciation on it.
  • Portfolio-level planning: Depreciation benefits vary across properties in your portfolio. Understanding how each property contributes to your overall tax position helps you make better buy, hold, and sell decisions. Use your rental yield calculations alongside depreciation analysis.

Final Thoughts

Rental property depreciation is not a bonus. It is a fundamental part of your investment strategy. It reduces your tax bill every year you own the property, and when used with cost segregation and bonus depreciation, the savings can be substantial.

The key is to claim it correctly from day one, keep impeccable records, plan for recapture when you sell, and work with a CPA who knows real estate. Do these things, and depreciation will be one of the most valuable financial tools in your rental property portfolio.

Do not leave money on the table. If you own rental property and are not maximizing depreciation, start today. Review your records, talk to a CPA, and make sure every dollar of deduction is working for you. The cost per door of professional tax advice is one of the best investments you can make.
