Conventional Loans for Investment Properties: Requirements

For most real estate investors, conventional loans are the first stop when financing a rental property. They typically offer the most competitive interest rates among institutional lending products, fixed-rate options, and the flexibility to be used across a wide range of property types. But conventional loans for investment properties come with significantly stricter requirements than loans for primary residences — and understanding those requirements upfront can save you months of frustration.

This guide covers everything you need to know about qualifying for a conventional investment property loan: down payment minimums, credit score thresholds, income documentation, reserve requirements, and the critical limit on how many properties you can conventionally finance.

What Is a Conventional Loan for Investment Property?

A conventional loan is any mortgage that isn't backed by a government agency (FHA, VA, or USDA). For investment properties, the dominant conventional loan programs are underwritten to guidelines set by Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac — the government-sponsored enterprises (GSEs) that purchase conforming mortgages from lenders.

Because Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac guidelines govern most conventional investment property loans, understanding their rules is essential. Lenders who sell loans to the secondary market must follow these guidelines, which is why you'll see similar requirements across most banks, credit unions, and mortgage companies.

Fannie Mae's Selling Guide is the definitive source for conventional investment property underwriting rules and is updated regularly.

Conventional Loan Investment Property Requirements

Down Payment Requirements

Investment properties require substantially higher down payments than primary residences. Under Fannie Mae guidelines:

Property Type Minimum Down Payment
Single-family investment property 15% (85% LTV)
2-4 unit investment property 25%
Single-family (with certain risk factors) 25%

Important: While 15% is technically the minimum for a single-family rental, most lenders require 20–25% to qualify for the best rates and avoid additional risk-based pricing adjustments. In practice, investors budgeting for 25% down are in a much stronger position.

The down payment must come from your own funds or approved sources. Gift funds are generally not allowed for investment property down payments under conventional guidelines.

Credit Score Requirements

Investment property loans demand higher credit scores than primary residence mortgages:

  • Minimum: 620 (for some programs, with significant pricing penalties)
  • Competitive threshold: 720+
  • Best rates: 740–760+

Every 20-point drop in your credit score below 720 triggers additional rate premiums. At a 660 score, the rate adjustment can add 0.5–0.75% to your interest rate — on a $300,000 loan, that's $1,500–$2,250/year in additional interest. Prioritize getting your score above 720 before applying.

Income and Employment Documentation

Conventional lenders verify income thoroughly. For W-2 employees:

  • 2 most recent pay stubs
  • 2 years of W-2 forms
  • Most recent 2 months of bank statements

For self-employed borrowers:

  • 2 years of federal tax returns (personal and business)
  • Year-to-date profit and loss statement
  • Business bank statements (often 12–24 months)

The lender calculates your debt-to-income ratio (DTI), which compares your total monthly debt obligations to your gross monthly income. Most conventional investment property programs require a DTI below 45%.

Using Rental Income to Qualify

One of the most important nuances of conventional investment property lending is how rental income factors into your qualification. Fannie Mae allows lenders to use 75% of the subject property's gross rental income to offset the mortgage payment when calculating DTI — but only under certain conditions:

  • The property must already be rented (or rent must be documented via a market rent appraisal)
  • You must be purchasing or have existing documentation if refinancing
  • The income appears on Schedule E of your tax returns for existing properties

If you're buying a new rental property, lenders may use an appraiser's market rent analysis to estimate income. But until you have 1–2 years of reported rental income on your taxes, lenders may count the full mortgage payment against your DTI rather than netting it against rental income. This is a critical limitation for new investors with W-2 income.

Reserve Requirements

Conventional investment property loans require significantly larger cash reserves than primary residence mortgages. Fannie Mae's current guidelines:

  • 2–4 unit investment property: 6 months of PITI (principal, interest, taxes, and insurance) for that property
  • 1-unit investment property: 2–6 months of PITI depending on overall loan risk profile
  • If you own other financed properties: Additional reserves may be required for each

If you own five rental properties and are applying for a sixth conventional loan, your reserve requirements can stack up to dozens of months of mortgage payments across all properties. Many investors find this reserve requirement — not the down payment — is the primary bottleneck as they scale.

The 10-Property Limit

Perhaps the most significant restriction on conventional investment property loans is the 10-financed-property limit. Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac guidelines prohibit purchasing or refinancing investment properties when the borrower already has 10 or more financed properties (including their primary residence).

This means conventional financing effectively has a ceiling for growing investors. Once you've accumulated 9–10 financed properties, you must shift to:

  • DSCR loans
  • Portfolio lenders
  • Blanket mortgages
  • Commercial lending programs
  • Creative financing approaches

Many investors plan for this ceiling from the beginning, reserving their conventional loan capacity for the properties with the highest quality and most favorable economics.

Loan-to-Value and Risk Pricing Adjustments

Conventional investment property loans are subject to Loan-Level Price Adjustments (LLPAs) — pricing add-ons imposed by Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac based on credit score, LTV, loan purpose, and property type. These adjustments directly affect your interest rate.

For investment properties, LLPAs are substantially higher than for primary residences. As a result, even though conventional rates are advertised at historically attractive levels, investment property borrowers typically pay 0.5–1.0% more than owner-occupants at the same credit score and LTV.

Rate differential example (approximate, 2026):

  • Primary residence, 780 credit, 80% LTV: 6.50%
  • Investment property, 780 credit, 75% LTV: 7.25–7.50%
  • Investment property, 700 credit, 75% LTV: 7.75–8.25%

This is why maintaining excellent credit and making a 25% down payment are so important for investment property borrowers — they minimize LLPA penalties.

Conventional vs. DSCR Loans: Which Is Better?

Many investors face this choice: go conventional (lower rate, stricter income requirements) or go DSCR (income-flexible, slightly higher rate)?

Feature Conventional DSCR
Rate Lower (typically) Higher by 0.5–1%
Income qualification Personal income required Property income only
Property count limit 10 None
Credit score minimum 620 (720+ competitive) 640–680
Down payment minimum 15–25% 20–25%
Documentation burden High Low
Entity (LLC) allowed Rarely Yes

For investors early in their career who have strong personal income and haven't maxed out their conventional loan count, conventional loans are almost always the better choice due to lower rates. For self-employed investors, those with complex financials, or those who've hit the 10-property ceiling, DSCR loans become the tool of choice.

The Mortgage Shopping Process for Investment Properties

Step 1: Know Your Numbers

Before approaching any lender, know your credit score, your DTI (without rental income), and exactly how much liquid capital you have for down payment and reserves. Lenders will pull these numbers — you should know them first.

Step 2: Compare at Least 3–5 Lenders

Investment property loans have significant rate variation between lenders. The same borrower profile can see 0.5–0.75% differences in quoted rates across institutions. Always get competing quotes. Even a 0.25% difference on a $300,000 loan means $750/year in interest.

Step 3: Get Pre-Approved Before Making Offers

A pre-approval letter strengthens your offer and ensures you understand your borrowing capacity before you're under contract. For competitive properties, sellers want confidence that financing won't fall through.

Step 4: Lock Your Rate at the Right Time

Mortgage rates move daily. Once you have a property under contract and your financing is largely confirmed, locking your rate protects you from market movement during underwriting.

Step 5: Move Quickly Through Underwriting

Investment property loans require thorough documentation. Respond to lender requests within 24 hours and have your documents organized in advance. Delays in underwriting can push closing dates and frustrate sellers.

Common Mistakes with Conventional Investment Property Loans

Making large financial moves before closing. Any new debt, large cash withdrawal, or change in employment status before closing can derail your approval. Don't buy a car, open new credit cards, or change jobs during the loan process.

Underestimating reserve requirements. Many investors have the down payment but forget to account for the reserve requirement. Make sure you have both — and make sure the reserves are in accounts you can document.

Not accounting for LLPAs in your rate comparison. When comparing lender quotes, ask each one to include all origination fees and discount points in the APR. The "best rate" headline often has costs baked in that make it less competitive.

Failing to optimize credit before applying. Pay down revolving balances to below 30% utilization, dispute errors, and avoid hard inquiries in the months before applying. A 20-point credit score improvement can meaningfully lower your rate.

Building a Rental Portfolio with Conventional Financing

The strategic approach to using conventional loans in portfolio building is to use them wisely while you have access to them. Each conventional loan you can get is essentially a low-cost, long-term financing slot that will be hard to replicate later through other channels.

Focus your conventional loans on:

  • Long-term buy-and-hold properties with strong fundamentals
  • Markets with proven rental demand and appreciation history
  • Properties that maximize cash flow at the lower conventional rates

As you approach the 10-property ceiling, begin building relationships with portfolio lenders and DSCR lenders so you're ready to transition smoothly. The rental property financing options overview covers how to plan for this transition as part of a broader portfolio strategy.

Conventional loans for investment properties remain the gold standard for investors who qualify — offering the best combination of rates, terms, and stability available in the institutional lending market.
