Setting Pet Rent and Pet Fees as a Landlord

Pets are in more than 66% of American households. For landlords, the question isn't whether to allow pets—it's how to allow them in a way that protects your property and increases your income. The right pet policy balances three financial tools: pet deposits, one-time pet fees, and monthly pet rent.

Understanding the legal distinctions between these tools, and how to price them appropriately, can add $600–$1,500+ per year in additional income per pet-owning tenant.

Pet Deposit vs. Pet Fee vs. Pet Rent: Key Differences

These three tools are often confused but serve distinct purposes—and have different legal treatment:

Pet Deposit

A pet deposit is a refundable security deposit held to cover pet-related damage. It's returned to the tenant (minus documented pet damage) at move-out.

  • Typically: $200–$500 per pet
  • Legal status: Refundable; regulated by security deposit laws in your state
  • When to use: When you want financial protection against specific damage but don't want to add ongoing income

One-Time Pet Fee (Non-Refundable)

A pet fee is a non-refundable, one-time payment made at move-in to compensate for general increased wear associated with pets (deodorizing, deep cleaning at turnover).

  • Typically: $100–$500 per pet
  • Legal status: Non-refundable; check your state—some states prohibit non-refundable fees
  • When to use: To cover predictable turnover costs without the complexity of itemized deductions

Pet Rent

Pet rent is an additional monthly charge added to base rent for each approved pet.

  • Typically: $25–$100/month per pet
  • Legal status: Part of rent; generally unrestricted where pets are allowed
  • When to use: To generate ongoing income and reflect the ongoing, cumulative wear pets create

The winner for income: Pet rent outperforms a one-time fee over time. A $50/month pet rent on a 2-year tenancy = $1,200—compared to a $300 one-time fee. Longer-stay tenants make pet rent increasingly valuable.

How Much to Charge for Pet Rent

Pet rent varies by market, property type, and pet type:

Pet Type Typical Monthly Pet Rent
Cat $20–$50/month
Small dog (under 25 lbs) $35–$75/month
Medium dog (25–60 lbs) $50–$100/month
Large dog (60+ lbs) $75–$150/month

Research your local market by checking competing rental listings. Sites like Zillow, Apartments.com, and Rent.com show pet policies and fees for comparable units. If competitors charge $40/month per pet, charging $70 may price you out of the market; charging $30 leaves money on the table.

To see how this fits into your overall pricing strategy, use a rent comps analysis to benchmark your total rent + pet fees against the market.

How Much to Charge for Pet Deposits and Fees

Pet Deposit Guidelines:

  • Single cat: $150–$300
  • Single dog: $250–$500
  • Multiple pets: Often 1.25–1.5x the single-pet deposit
  • Maximum: Most states cap total security deposits (including pet deposits) at 1–2 months' rent

One-Time Pet Fee Guidelines:

  • Cat: $100–$250
  • Small dog: $150–$350
  • Large dog: $250–$500

A common and effective structure combines a one-time fee with monthly pet rent:

  • One-time fee: $250 (to cover deep cleaning at turnover)
  • Monthly pet rent: $50/month (ongoing income)

Over a 2-year tenancy, this generates $250 + $1,200 = $1,450 per pet in additional revenue.

State Laws That Affect Pet Fees

Pet fee and deposit laws vary significantly by state:

States with notable restrictions:

  • California: Non-refundable fees are generally not permitted for tenants in need of service/assistance animals; total security deposits capped at 2 months' rent (unfurnished)
  • Oregon: No separate non-refundable pet deposits; pet fees must be clearly distinguished from security deposits
  • Minnesota: Prohibits landlords from charging a pet security deposit if the tenant has an emotional support animal
  • New York: Caps combined security deposit at 1 month's rent under recent Housing Stability Act

Critical note on service animals and emotional support animals: Under the Fair Housing Act, service animals and emotional support animals (ESAs) are not "pets." You cannot charge pet fees or pet rent for assistance animals, regardless of your pet policy. Charging these fees to tenants with documented disabilities is illegal discrimination.

HUD's guidance on assistance animals clarifies what documentation landlords can request and what accommodations must be made.

Always consult a local real estate attorney to ensure your pet policy complies with current state and local law.

Should You Allow Pets? The Financial Case

Many landlords default to "no pets" to avoid damage risk. But this policy has real costs:

The cost of no-pets policy:

  • You exclude 66%+ of potential tenants from consideration
  • Pet-owning tenants tend to be older, more stable, and longer-staying (they can't easily move with pets)
  • You likely rent for less and experience more vacancy while waiting for non-pet tenants

The financial math:

  • Pet-friendly units typically rent for $50–$100/month more than comparable no-pet units
  • Pet owners stay an average of 12–18 months longer than non-pet renters
  • The extra rent + pet fees often exceed the incremental cost of pet-related damage

The key is the right pet policy, not a blanket ban.

Building a Strong Pet Policy

A comprehensive pet policy protects your property while generating maximum income:

1. Set Pet Limits and Restrictions

  • Maximum number of pets (typically 1–2)
  • Weight limits for dogs (common: under 50 lbs, or per-unit maximums)
  • Breed restrictions (consult your insurance carrier—some policies exclude certain breeds)
  • Pet age requirements (adult pets only, or puppies/kittens with higher deposit)

2. Require Pet Screening

Services like PetScreening.com provide formal pet applications including vaccination records, behavioral history, and owner references. This creates a documented basis for approvals/denials.

3. Conduct Pet Inspections

Quarterly or semi-annual property inspections let you catch pet damage early—before it becomes a major repair project. This is your early warning system.

4. Use a Pet Addendum to the Lease

Your pet policy should be formalized in a signed pet addendum that specifies:

  • Approved pets (name, breed, weight, age)
  • Pet deposit, fee, and rent amounts
  • Tenant responsibility for pet damage beyond normal wear
  • Conduct requirements (leash rules, noise, waste disposal)
  • Consequences for unauthorized pets

5. Require Renters Insurance

Require tenants with pets to carry renters insurance with a minimum liability coverage amount (typically $100,000). This protects you if a tenant's dog bites someone on your property.

Maximizing Pet Income Across Your Portfolio

If you own multiple units, standardizing your pet policy and fees across your portfolio creates consistency and predictability:

Annual pet revenue calculation for a 10-unit building:

  • Assume 6 pet-owning tenants (60% occupancy by pet owners)
  • Average monthly pet rent: $50/unit
  • Annual pet rent revenue: 6 × $50 × 12 = $3,600/year
  • One-time pet fees (assuming 2 new pet tenants per year): 2 × $250 = $500/year
  • Total annual pet revenue: $4,100

At a 6.5% cap rate, this $4,100 in additional NOI adds $63,077 in property value—simply from having a well-structured pet policy.

Pet-Friendly Amenities That Command Higher Pet Rent

The premium you can charge for pet rent increases when your property has amenities that attract pet owners:

  • Fenced yard or dog run: $10–$20 additional monthly pet premium
  • Dog washing station: Strong differentiator, supports $15–$30 premium
  • Direct backyard access for ground-floor units: Significant value to dog owners
  • On-site pet waste stations: Expected; absence is a negative
  • Proximity to dog parks or trails: Leverage in marketing

Some of our amenity upgrades that justify higher rent have outsized appeal for pet owners and can justify both higher base rent and higher pet rent.

Handling Pet Damage at Move-Out

If a pet causes damage beyond normal wear and tear, document it thoroughly:

  1. Conduct move-out inspection with written checklist and photos
  2. Obtain written estimates from qualified contractors
  3. Provide itemized damage statement within your state's required timeframe (typically 14–30 days)
  4. Deduct documented damage from the pet deposit
  5. Return remaining deposit balance within required timeframe

Common pet damage items: carpet replacement (urine damage), flooring scratches, wall scuffs, yard damage. Keep receipts for all cleaning and repairs—you may need them if the tenant disputes deductions.

Summary

A well-designed pet policy is one of the easiest ways to increase your rental property income without adding units or making major improvements. The formula is straightforward:

  • Charge a reasonable one-time pet fee to cover turnover costs
  • Add monthly pet rent to generate ongoing income
  • Require pet screening, rental insurance, and a signed addendum to minimize risk
  • Know your state's laws—especially regarding assistance animals

By welcoming responsible pet owners with the right protections in place, you expand your tenant pool, reduce vacancy, increase retention, and generate meaningful additional income from a policy change that costs you nothing to implement.
