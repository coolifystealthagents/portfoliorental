Before you take a single booking, one of the most fundamental questions you must answer is: does your local zoning law allow short-term rentals at your property? Zoning compliance is not optional, and the consequences of operating an STR in a zone where it's not permitted can be severe — from daily fines to forced closure of your listing.

Many investors discover zoning problems after they've already purchased, renovated, and launched a property. Avoiding that mistake requires understanding how zoning works, how to research your property's classification, and what your options are if your current zone doesn't permit STRs.

This guide covers all of it. For the broader compliance framework, see our short-term rental management guide.

What Is Zoning and Why Does It Matter for STRs?

Zoning is the legal framework by which local governments divide land within their jurisdiction into districts — called zones — and regulate what uses are permitted in each zone. HUD's land use resources provide federal planning guidance. Common zone categories include:

  • Residential zones (R-1, R-2, R-3, etc.): Single-family, multi-family, or mixed residential use
  • Commercial zones (C-1, C-2, etc.): Retail, office, and commercial activity
  • Mixed-use zones: Combination of residential and commercial uses
  • Agricultural zones: Farming and rural land uses
  • Industrial zones: Manufacturing and warehousing

Short-term rentals occupy an ambiguous position in zoning law. Historically, zoning codes did not contemplate the Airbnb model — short stays in private homes transacted through an online platform. As STRs have grown, municipalities have responded in one of several ways:

  1. Explicitly permitting STRs in certain zones, often with conditions
  2. Explicitly prohibiting STRs in residential zones as a "commercial use"
  3. Staying silent — leaving interpretation to zoning administrators
  4. Creating STR-specific overlay zones or use categories

The treatment varies enormously, even within a single metropolitan area.

The Residential Zone Problem

The most common zoning issue for STR hosts is operating in a residential zone where STRs are classified as a prohibited commercial use. The legal theory is that a traditional residential zone is designed for long-term occupancy — neighbors, families, people with community ties. Short-term transient occupancy is more akin to hotel use, which belongs in commercial zones.

When a host operates an STR in a residential zone that prohibits commercial activity (including STRs), they are operating an illegal use of land — a zoning violation. This is separate from and in addition to any permit or business license issues.

"Transient Occupancy" Defined

Many zoning codes define transient occupancy as stays of fewer than 30 days. This is the threshold that separates residential use (long-term tenants) from transient use (STR guests). Properties that primarily serve transient occupancy may be classified as hotels or lodging facilities under the zoning code — uses not permitted in residential zones.

How STR Zoning Works: The Main Regulatory Models

Cities have adopted several approaches to STR zoning:

Model 1: Owner-Occupied Only

STRs are permitted in residential zones only when the host lives on-site as their primary residence. The theory is that an owner-occupant STR is more residential in character — a homeowner occasionally renting out a room or their home while traveling. Non-owner-occupied "investor STRs" are treated as commercial activity and zoned out of residential areas.

Examples: Denver, Portland, San Francisco, Austin (with nuance)

Impact on investors: Investment properties purchased specifically for STR use may not be permitted in residential zones. This significantly limits the viable market for non-owner-occupied STR investment.

Model 2: Permitted with Conditions

STRs are permitted in residential zones subject to registration, operating standards (noise, parking, guest limits), and sometimes density caps. Both owner-occupied and investor-owned properties may be eligible.

Examples: Many suburban and rural markets, tourism-heavy communities

Impact on investors: More favorable environment for non-owner-occupied STR investment, though compliance with conditions is required.

Model 3: Prohibited in Residential Zones

STRs are prohibited in residential zones entirely. They may be permitted in commercial, mixed-use, or specific tourism zones.

Examples: Some municipalities in New Jersey, New York state, and others

Impact on investors: Properties in residential zones cannot be used as STRs. Zoned commercial or mixed-use properties may be viable.

Model 4: No STR-Specific Zoning

The municipality has not addressed STRs in its zoning code. Interpretation is left to zoning administrators, and outcomes can be unpredictable.

Impact on investors: Higher risk due to ambiguity. A favorable interpretation today can change if the municipality adopts explicit regulations.

How to Research Your Property's Zoning

Step 1: Identify Your Zone Classification

Most municipalities provide zoning maps and property lookup tools on their official websites. You can typically search by address to find your property's zone designation (e.g., "R-1," "RS-2," "C-3").

Search: "[Your City] zoning map" or "[Your City] property zoning lookup"

Step 2: Find the Zoning Code

The zoning code (also called the municipal code, land use code, or development code) defines what uses are permitted in each zone. Look for:

  • A table of permitted uses by zone
  • The definition of "short-term rental," "vacation rental," or "transient occupancy"
  • Any STR-specific regulations, conditions, or overlay districts

Most municipalities publish their zoning codes online through sites like Municode or American Legal Publishing. The NAR zoning resource page also tracks STR-related legislation.

Step 3: Look for STR-Specific Regulations

Many cities have added STR-specific chapters to their zoning codes. Search the code for:

  • "Short-term rental"
  • "Vacation rental"
  • "Transient lodging"
  • "Home sharing"

Step 4: Contact the Zoning Department

If the code is unclear — which it often is — call or email your local zoning or planning department. Ask specifically: "Is a short-term rental of less than 30 days permitted at [address] in the [zone designation] zone?" Get the answer in writing if possible.

Step 5: Check for Overlay Districts or Special Zones

Some cities create STR overlay districts that modify the base zone regulations. A property in a residential zone that falls within a tourism overlay district may have different STR permissions than its base zone would suggest.

Zoning and the Purchase Decision

For investors considering a property purchase specifically for STR use, zoning research must happen before purchase. Due diligence should include:

  1. Confirming the property's current zone classification and permitted uses
  2. Reviewing the full zoning code for STR-specific regulations
  3. Checking for pending zoning amendments or regulatory changes (review city council agendas)
  4. Consulting with a local real estate attorney to interpret ambiguous language
  5. Confirming with the zoning department in writing

A property with STR-unfavorable zoning is not necessarily disqualified — but the purchase price and investment thesis must account for the regulatory risk.

What Happens If Your Property Doesn't Comply With Zoning?

Operating an STR in a zone where it's not permitted is a zoning violation. Enforcement mechanisms vary by municipality:

Neighbor complaints: Most zoning enforcement is complaint-driven. A neighbor who objects to your guests may file a complaint with the zoning department, triggering an investigation.

Cease-and-desist orders: A zoning notice or order requiring you to stop STR operations immediately.

Daily fines: Many jurisdictions impose per-day fines for continued violations after a cease-and-desist order. These can accumulate rapidly.

Listing removal: Cities increasingly share enforcement data with platforms like Airbnb. Non-compliant listings are removed.

Revocation of permits: Other permits (business license, STR permit) may be revoked when a zoning violation is discovered.

Legal action: The municipality can seek injunctive relief in court to force cessation of the illegal use.

Applying for a Variance or Special Use Permit

If your property is in a zone where STRs are not permitted as of right, you may be able to pursue:

Variance

A variance is an official exception to the zoning code for a specific property, typically granted when strict application of the code would cause undue hardship. Variances for STR use are difficult to obtain and are not available in all jurisdictions.

Special Use Permit (SUP) or Conditional Use Permit (CUP)

A Special Use Permit or Conditional Use Permit allows a use that is not automatically permitted in a zone but may be approved with conditions. The process typically involves:

  • An application to the planning department
  • A public hearing where neighbors can comment
  • A vote by the planning commission or zoning board
  • Conditions attached to the approval (guest limits, parking requirements, noise rules)

This process can take several months and is not guaranteed to succeed. However, for properties in markets where STRs have strong demand but limited zoning permissions, this approach may be worth pursuing.

State Preemption Laws and Zoning

Several states have passed laws that limit the ability of local governments to use zoning to prohibit STRs entirely. These state preemption laws represent a political response to cities becoming overly restrictive.

Notable states with STR preemption laws:

  • Arizona: Prevents cities from banning STRs altogether; local regulation is limited to health, safety, and neighborhood standards
  • Tennessee: Restricts retroactive bans (cities cannot eliminate STR rights for properties that were legally operating)
  • Texas: Similar protections against retroactive bans in certain circumstances

Even in preemption states, significant regulation is still permitted — cities just can't outright prohibit STRs. Always verify the current legal landscape in your state, as these laws evolve. SBA's local regulation guide helps identify applicable requirements.

HOA Zoning Restrictions vs. Government Zoning

It's important to understand that HOA rules and government zoning are separate legal frameworks:

  • Government zoning: Public law that applies to all properties in a jurisdiction
  • HOA rules: Private contractual obligations on properties within the HOA

Even if your city's zoning permits STRs, your HOA may prohibit them. Even if your HOA allows STRs, your city's zoning may not. Both must be satisfied simultaneously. See our airbnb hoa rules restrictions guide for the HOA side of this equation.

The Intersection of Zoning and Short-Term Rental Regulations

Zoning compliance is one component of a broader STR regulatory framework. Complete compliance for most hosts involves:

  1. Zoning: Is the use permitted in my zone?
  2. STR permit: Does the city require a specific STR permit?
  3. Business license: Is a general business license required?
  4. Tax registration: Am I registered to collect and remit occupancy taxes?
  5. HOA approval: Do my CC&Rs permit STRs?
  6. Insurance: Do I have proper coverage?

For full details on each element, visit our short-term rental regulations guide.

Summary and Key Takeaways

STR zoning compliance is a foundational requirement that every host and investor must verify before listing. Here's what to remember:

  • Zoning law governs whether STRs are legally permitted at your property at all
  • Major regulatory models include owner-occupied-only, permitted with conditions, and outright prohibition
  • Research your property's zoning by checking the official zoning map, reading the code, and confirming with the planning department
  • For investment purchases, zoning research must happen during due diligence — before closing
  • Zoning violations can result in cease-and-desist orders, daily fines, and listing removal
  • Variances and special use permits may be available in some cases
  • State preemption laws in some states limit how aggressively cities can restrict STRs
  • HOA rules and government zoning are separate frameworks — both must be satisfied
