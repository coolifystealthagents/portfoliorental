Americans are running out of storage space. With an average of 10 square feet of commercial storage per person—more than any other country—and demand still outpacing supply in many markets, storage has become one of the most predictable ancillary income streams available to property owners.

The best part: you may already have space that could be generating this income. Basements, utility rooms, crawl spaces, and outbuildings can all be converted into profitable storage units for your existing tenants or, in some cases, the general public.

The Business Case for Storage Rental Income

Self-storage is one of the most resilient real estate sectors because demand is driven by life events that happen in both good economies and bad: people moving, downsizing, divorcing, inheriting belongings, starting businesses, and simply accumulating too much stuff.

For rental property owners, the opportunity is simpler than owning a standalone storage facility—you're converting underutilized space that you already own into a new income stream with minimal capital outlay.

Key metrics:

  • Self-storage average rent: $87–$180/month per 10×10 unit nationally (varies significantly by market)
  • Average occupancy: 92–94% in most markets
  • Typical ROI on storage conversion: 20–40%+

Types of Storage You Can Offer at Rental Properties

1. Basement Storage Cages

The most common option for multifamily buildings. Wire mesh cages (4×8 or 5×10 ft) installed in basement or utility areas.

  • Cost per cage: $200–$500 installed
  • Monthly rent per cage: $30–$100
  • Best for: Apartment buildings where tenants need overflow storage

2. Standalone Storage Units

Converted outbuildings, cargo containers, or simple structures offering individual lockable units.

  • Cost: $1,500–$8,000 depending on construction method
  • Monthly rent: $50–$200 per unit depending on size and access
  • Best for: Properties with underutilized outbuildings or large lots

3. Garage Storage Rental

As covered in our garage rental income guide, unused garage bays can serve as storage for vehicles, furniture, seasonal items, or business equipment.

4. Dedicated Storage Rooms

Shared utility rooms or former storage closets converted into rentable, lockable space.

  • Cost: $500–$3,000 (shelving, lighting, locks)
  • Monthly rent: $40–$120 depending on size

5. Climate-Controlled Storage

Premium storage with temperature and humidity control for sensitive items (wine, artwork, electronics, documents).

  • Premium over non-climate storage: 30–50%
  • Investment: HVAC/mini-split addition to existing space
  • Best for: Higher-income properties in markets where premium storage demand exists

Pricing Your Storage Space

Storage pricing varies dramatically by market. Research local self-storage rates first:

National benchmarks (2026):

Unit Size Non-Climate Average Climate-Controlled Average
5×5 (25 sq ft) $40–$75/month $55–$95/month
5×10 (50 sq ft) $60–$120/month $85–$150/month
10×10 (100 sq ft) $100–$180/month $135–$230/month
10×20 (200 sq ft) $150–$300/month $200–$380/month

Positioning strategy: Price your tenant-facing storage 10–20% below local self-storage facilities. Your tenants already live on-site, so the convenience value is higher—they're paying for one-stop convenience, not the cheapest option in town.

For basement storage cages (smaller, less private, more basic): Price at 40–60% of full self-storage rates. A $50–$75/month cage for a 4×8 space is typically well-received by apartment tenants.

Step-by-Step: Converting Basement Space to Storage Cages

This is the most common and cost-effective storage income project for multifamily landlords:

Step 1: Assess Available Space

Measure your basement or utility area. Identify space not required for mechanical systems, maintenance access, or building services. 200–400 square feet can typically yield 4–8 cage units.

Step 2: Plan the Layout

Design your cage layout to maximize units while maintaining access aisles (minimum 36 inches wide) and access to all mechanical systems. Each 4×8 cage provides approximately 32 square feet of floor storage—useful for most household overflow needs.

Step 3: Purchase and Install Cages

Wire mesh storage cages are available from industrial suppliers:

  • Basic wire mesh kit (4×8×8 ft): $180–$280
  • Professional installation (if outsourcing): $100–$200 per cage
  • Add-on: Individual padlock hasps (included in most kits)

Install with anchor bolts into the concrete floor for stability. Most cage kits can be assembled by a handy landlord in 2–3 hours per unit.

Step 4: Improve Lighting and Access

  • Install LED shop lights on the ceiling above the cage area
  • Ensure the space is secure (keypad or lock on the basement door)
  • Add a dehumidifier if the basement has moisture issues

Step 5: Set Rental Terms and Market to Tenants

  • First offer storage to existing tenants (easiest transactions)
  • Add a storage addendum to their lease
  • For vacant units, advertise storage availability in your rental listing

Financial Example: 8-Unit Apartment Building

Project: Install 6 basement storage cages

Item Cost
6 wire mesh cage kits $1,560
Hardware and anchoring $200
LED lighting upgrade $350
Dehumidifier $280
Total investment $2,390

Revenue:

  • 6 cages × $60/month × 11 months (occupancy rate: 92%) = $3,960/year

Return metrics:

  • Cash-on-cash return: $3,960 ÷ $2,390 = 166%
  • Payback period: 7.2 months

At a 6.5% cap rate, $3,960 in annual NOI adds $60,923 in property value from a $2,390 investment. This is the highest-return project in this guide by far.

Renting Storage to the General Public

If your property has substantial unused outdoor space, a separate outbuilding, or cargo containers, you can rent storage to people beyond your tenants—capturing a larger market.

Options:

  • Neighbor.com: A peer-to-peer platform where property owners list storage space for rent. Handles payments and provides basic liability protection.
  • StoreAtMyHouse.com: Similar peer-to-peer model
  • Craigslist/Facebook Marketplace: Direct listings for local storage seekers

Legal considerations: Renting storage to the public typically doesn't require a special license for small-scale operators, but check local business licensing requirements. Your landlord insurance policy may need a rider for commercial storage operations.

Income potential (outdoor storage, 1,000 sq ft):

  • 5 vehicles/boats/RVs: $150/month each = $750/month = $9,000/year

This is one of the best returns available on underutilized rural or suburban property with large lots.

Storage Income for Single-Family Rental Properties

Single-family landlords can also generate storage income:

Backyard shed/outbuilding: If your property has an outbuilding not needed by the tenant, consider retaining it as a separately-rented storage unit. Add a separate padlock and rent it independently for $50–$100/month.

Garage with extra space: If the tenant only needs one bay and the house has a two-car garage, rent the second bay as storage. See our garage rental income guide for pricing guidance.

Basement storage room: A separate, lockable basement room (separate from the tenant's general basement access) can be a profitable storage rental if the layout allows.

Legal, Insurance, and Tax Considerations

Lease Agreement

Use a clear storage addendum or separate storage rental agreement covering:

  • Space dimensions and location
  • Monthly rent and payment terms
  • Permitted and prohibited items (flammables, perishables, hazardous materials)
  • Access hours (if relevant)
  • No liability for stored items (tenant stores at own risk)
  • Landlord's right to inspect with notice
  • Termination provisions

Insurance

Review your landlord insurance policy. Most residential policies don't cover tenant-stored property, but you should ensure your liability coverage extends to injuries in storage areas. A commercial umbrella policy may be appropriate for larger storage operations.

Self-Storage Lien Rights

If a storage tenant defaults on payments, many states have specific lien statutes for self-storage operators that allow for auction of stored goods after proper notice. Residential landlords offering storage typically use standard eviction/enforcement processes rather than storage lien laws—clarify with your attorney.

Taxes

Storage rental income is taxable as rental income. Storage structures are depreciable over their useful life (typically 15–20 years for metal structures; 27.5–39 years for permanent structures). See our rental property tax deductions guide and depreciation guide for complete treatment.

Tracking Storage Revenue in Your Property Financials

Track storage income separately in your expense tracking system:

  • "Storage income" line item in your rent roll
  • Separate from base rent but included in gross income for NOI calculation
  • Document storage-specific expenses (cage maintenance, dehumidifier electricity, etc.)

This level of tracking lets you accurately calculate the ROI of your storage investment and present clean financials to lenders when refinancing or selling.

For more ideas on maximizing every dollar of income from your rental property, visit our comprehensive guide to rental income maximization strategies.

The Self Storage Association's industry statistics provide market occupancy and rent data that can help you benchmark your storage pricing and assess local demand.

Summary

Storage rental income is one of the simplest, highest-ROI ancillary income strategies available to property owners. Whether you're converting basement space to cages, renting an outbuilding, or listing spare outdoor space on Neighbor.com, the potential returns—often 50–150%+ on invested capital—are remarkable.

Key takeaways:

  • Start with existing underutilized space before investing in new construction
  • Basement storage cages offer the fastest payback (often under 12 months)
  • Price 10–20% below local self-storage to attract tenants while maximizing revenue
  • Use proper lease agreements and ensure your insurance coverage is adequate
  • Track storage income separately to measure ROI accurately

Combined with garage rental income, laundry revenue, and pet rent, a comprehensive ancillary income program can add 10–20% to your property's total revenue.
