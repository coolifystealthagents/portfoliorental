29:T2968,<p>A written service agreement protects both you and your contractor. Without one, disputes over scope, payment, timelines, and liability become a matter of he-said-she-said. For landlords managing multiple properties, a standard contractor agreement ensures consistency and reduces legal exposure across your portfolio.</p>
<p>This guide explains the essential clauses every contractor service agreement should include and provides a framework you can adapt to your needs.</p>
<h2 id="why-you-need-a-written-agreement">Why You Need a Written Agreement</h2>
<p>Verbal agreements are legally enforceable in many jurisdictions but nearly impossible to prove. A written contractor service agreement:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Defines scope clearly</strong> so both parties understand what work is included</li>
<li><strong>Sets payment terms</strong> to avoid disputes about amounts and timing</li>
<li><strong>Establishes timelines</strong> with consequences for delays</li>
<li><strong>Allocates liability</strong> for damage, injuries, and defective work</li>
<li><strong>Protects against liens</strong> by requiring lien waivers</li>
<li><strong>Creates documentation</strong> for insurance claims, tax deductions, and legal disputes</li>
</ul>
<p>Even for small jobs, a simple written agreement is better than none. Scale the complexity of your agreement to the size and risk of the project. Nolo's <a href="https://www.nolo.com/legal-encyclopedia/landlord-tenant-law">landlord-tenant law encyclopedia</a> covers the legal basis for contractor agreements in a rental context.</p>
<h2 id="essential-clauses-for-landlord-contractor-agreements">Essential Clauses for Landlord-Contractor Agreements</h2>
<h3 id="1-parties-and-property-identification">1. Parties and Property Identification</h3>
<p>Clearly identify:</p>
<ul>
<li>Your legal entity name (LLC, trust, or individual)</li>
<li>The contractor's legal business name and entity type</li>
<li>The property address where work will be performed</li>
<li>The specific unit(s) if applicable</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="2-scope-of-work">2. Scope of Work</h3>
<p>The most important clause in any contractor agreement. A well-written scope includes:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Detailed description</strong> of the work to be performed</li>
<li><strong>Materials specifications</strong> — Who provides materials? What quality level?</li>
<li><strong>What is explicitly excluded</strong> from the scope</li>
<li><strong>Change order process</strong> — How are scope changes requested, approved, and priced?</li>
</ul>
<p>Be specific. "Renovate bathroom" is too vague. "Remove existing tile, install new ceramic tile (owner-selected, owner-purchased), replace toilet, install new vanity (contractor-supplied, model XYZ), paint walls with two coats of primer and two coats of semi-gloss paint" is clear.</p>
<h3 id="3-payment-terms">3. Payment Terms</h3>
<p>Specify:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Total contract price</strong> or rate structure (flat fee, hourly, cost-plus)</li>
<li><strong>Deposit amount</strong> (if any) — typically 10-30% of total</li>
<li><strong>Progress payment schedule</strong> tied to milestones, not calendar dates</li>
<li><strong>Final payment</strong> due upon satisfactory completion and inspection</li>
<li><strong>Retainage</strong> — Consider holding 10% for 30 days after completion for defect claims</li>
<li><strong>Payment method</strong> — Check, ACH, or other method</li>
<li><strong>Late payment terms</strong> if applicable</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="4-timeline-and-scheduling">4. Timeline and Scheduling</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Start date</strong> or trigger for start (e.g., within 5 business days of deposit)</li>
<li><strong>Estimated completion date</strong></li>
<li><strong>Penalties for unreasonable delays</strong> — Per-day charges or right to terminate</li>
<li><strong>Excusable delays</strong> — Weather, material shortages, tenant access issues</li>
<li><strong>Tenant access coordination</strong> — Who schedules entry with tenants? What notice is required?</li>
</ul>
<p>For rental properties, tenant coordination is critical. Your agreement should specify whether the contractor or you handle <a href="/blog/landlord-right-of-entry">tenant notification and access scheduling</a>.</p>
<h3 id="5-insurance-and-licensing">5. Insurance and Licensing</h3>
<p>Require the contractor to:</p>
<ul>
<li>Maintain general liability insurance with minimum coverage amounts (typically $1 million per occurrence)</li>
<li>Maintain workers' compensation insurance if they have employees</li>
<li>Provide certificates of insurance before work begins</li>
<li>Name you as an additional insured on their general liability policy</li>
<li>Hold all required state and local licenses for the work being performed</li>
</ul>
<p>See our <a href="/blog/contractor-insurance-verification">contractor insurance verification guide</a> for the verification process.</p>
<h3 id="6-permits-and-code-compliance">6. Permits and Code Compliance</h3>
<p>Specify who is responsible for:</p>
<ul>
<li>Obtaining necessary building permits</li>
<li>Scheduling required inspections</li>
<li>Ensuring all work meets current building codes and <a href="https://www.osha.gov/workers">OSHA safety standards</a></li>
<li>Correcting code violations found during inspections</li>
</ul>
<p>In most cases, the contractor should handle permits and inspections as part of their scope. Include the cost of permits in the contract price.</p>
<h3 id="7-liability-and-indemnification">7. Liability and Indemnification</h3>
<p>Include clauses that:</p>
<ul>
<li>Require the contractor to indemnify you against claims arising from their work</li>
<li>Address damage to your property during the project</li>
<li>Cover damage to tenant property during the project</li>
<li>Specify the contractor's obligation to repair defective work</li>
<li>Limit your liability for contractor injuries or subcontractor disputes</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="8-warranty">8. Warranty</h3>
<p>Define:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Workmanship warranty period</strong> — Typically one year from completion</li>
<li><strong>What the warranty covers</strong> — Defects in workmanship, not normal wear</li>
<li><strong>Response time</strong> for warranty claims</li>
<li><strong>Exclusions</strong> — Damage from tenant misuse, acts of nature, etc.</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="9-lien-waiver-requirements">9. Lien Waiver Requirements</h3>
<p>Protect yourself from mechanics' liens:</p>
<ul>
<li>Require partial lien waivers with each progress payment</li>
<li>Require a final lien waiver before releasing final payment</li>
<li>If the contractor uses subcontractors, require sub-tier lien waivers as well</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="10-termination-clause">10. Termination Clause</h3>
<p>Both parties should have the right to terminate under defined circumstances:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>For cause</strong> — Breach of contract, failure to perform, safety violations</li>
<li><strong>For convenience</strong> — With reasonable notice and payment for completed work</li>
<li><strong>Contractor abandonment</strong> — Define what constitutes abandonment (e.g., failure to work for 5 consecutive business days without notice)</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="11-dispute-resolution">11. Dispute Resolution</h3>
<p>Specify how disputes will be resolved:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Negotiation first</strong> — Require good-faith discussion before escalating</li>
<li><strong>Mediation</strong> — Lower cost than arbitration or litigation</li>
<li><strong>Arbitration or litigation</strong> — Choose one and specify the governing jurisdiction</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="12-independent-contractor-status">12. Independent Contractor Status</h3>
<p>Confirm that the contractor is an independent contractor, not an employee. This clause protects you from employment tax and benefits obligations. Include language stating:</p>
<ul>
<li>The contractor controls how and when work is performed</li>
<li>The contractor provides their own tools and equipment</li>
<li>The contractor is responsible for their own taxes (see <a href="https://www.irs.gov/publications/p527">IRS rental property guidance</a> for reporting contractor payments)</li>
<li>No employment relationship is created</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="adapting-the-agreement-for-different-project-types">Adapting the Agreement for Different Project Types</h2>
<h3 id="small-repairs-under-1-000">Small Repairs (Under $1,000)</h3>
<p>Use a simplified one-page agreement covering scope, price, timeline, and insurance verification. Excessive contract length for small jobs may deter good contractors.</p>
<h3 id="turnover-renovations-1-000-10-000">Turnover Renovations ($1,000 - $10,000)</h3>
<p>Use the full agreement with detailed scope, milestone payments, and tenant coordination clauses. Turnover timelines are tight, so include daily delay penalties if the project affects your ability to lease the unit.</p>
<h3 id="major-renovations-over-10-000">Major Renovations (Over $10,000)</h3>
<p>Full agreement plus detailed specifications, architectural drawings if applicable, bonding requirements for very large projects, and a more detailed change order process. Consider having an attorney review the agreement.</p>
<h2 id="common-mistakes-to-avoid">Common Mistakes to Avoid</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Scope too vague</strong> — Creates disputes about what was and was not included</li>
<li><strong>No change order process</strong> — Leads to scope creep and cost overruns</li>
<li><strong>Paying too much upfront</strong> — Reduces the contractor's incentive to finish</li>
<li><strong>Missing insurance verification</strong> — Exposes you to liability for on-site injuries</li>
<li><strong>No lien waiver requirement</strong> — Leaves you vulnerable to mechanics' liens from unpaid subcontractors</li>
<li><strong>Forgetting tenant coordination</strong> — Tenant access issues delay projects and create complaints</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="key-takeaways">Key Takeaways</h2>
<p>A solid contractor service agreement sets expectations, allocates risk, and provides legal protection for both parties. Invest time upfront to create a standard template that covers the essential clauses, then adapt it for each project. The agreement should be firm enough to protect you but practical enough that quality contractors are willing to sign it.</p>
