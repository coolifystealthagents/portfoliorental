Just Cause Eviction Laws: Landlord Compliance Guide

Just cause eviction laws represent one of the most significant legal shifts in residential landlord-tenant relations over the past decade. These statutes require landlords to cite a legally recognized reason before terminating a tenancy — eliminating the traditional "no-fault" eviction in covered jurisdictions. For rental property owners managing portfolios in multiple markets, understanding where just cause laws apply and how to operate within them is no longer optional — it is a core compliance requirement.

This guide explains what just cause eviction laws are, which states and cities have enacted them, what qualifies as acceptable cause, and how to structure your leasing and property management practices to minimize legal exposure.

What Are Just Cause Eviction Laws?

Under traditional landlord-tenant law, a landlord could decline to renew a lease or issue a notice to vacate without providing any reason — as long as proper notice was given. Just cause eviction laws change this dynamic by requiring the landlord to establish a legitimate, enumerated reason before terminating a tenancy.

These laws are designed to protect long-term renters from arbitrary displacement and are frequently linked to rent control or rent stabilization frameworks. The Fair Housing Act also intersects with eviction practices by prohibiting discriminatory enforcement. In many jurisdictions, just cause protections kick in only after a tenant has occupied the unit for a specified period — commonly 12 months.

Common Accepted Just Causes for Eviction

Most just cause statutes recognize two broad categories: at-fault just cause and no-fault just cause. Understanding the eviction process step by step is essential before attempting to remove a tenant under either category.

At-fault just cause typically includes:

  • Nonpayment of rent
  • Violation of lease terms (after written notice and opportunity to cure)
  • Illegal activity on the premises
  • Subletting without landlord permission
  • Material damage to the property
  • Refusal to sign a substantially similar renewal lease

No-fault just cause typically includes:

  • Owner move-in (landlord or immediate family)
  • Substantial renovation requiring the unit to be vacant
  • Withdrawal of the property from the rental market (Ellis Act in California)
  • Government order requiring the unit to be vacated

Even when no-fault grounds exist, many jurisdictions require landlords to pay relocation assistance — sometimes equal to two to three months' rent — before the tenant is required to leave.

States and Cities With Just Cause Eviction Protections

Just cause eviction laws are spreading at both state and local levels. Understanding your specific jurisdiction is critical.

Statewide Just Cause Protections

Several states have enacted statewide just cause eviction requirements:

  • California — AB 1482 (effective 2020) applies to most rental units statewide that are not otherwise exempt. Tenants must occupy the unit for 12 months before protections apply. Covered landlords must have just cause for any eviction and pay relocation assistance for no-fault terminations.
  • Oregon — Oregon was the first state to pass statewide just cause protections (HB 2001, 2019). After 12 months of tenancy, landlords must have qualifying cause to end a tenancy.
  • Washington — Enacted statewide just cause eviction protections in 2023 covering most residential tenancies.
  • New Jersey — Has long-standing just cause eviction requirements that apply after the initial lease term expires.
  • New Hampshire — Enacted protections effective 2023 that limit no-cause evictions.

Cities and Counties With Local Just Cause Laws

Dozens of municipalities have enacted local ordinances that may be stricter than state law:

  • New York City — The Housing Stability and Tenant Protection Act significantly expanded just cause protections.
  • San Francisco, Oakland, Los Angeles — All have local ordinances with robust just cause requirements, often broader than California's statewide AB 1482.
  • Seattle — The Just Cause Eviction Ordinance applies citywide.
  • Chicago — Passed expanded tenant protections that include just cause requirements in many circumstances.
  • Denver, Boulder (CO) — Local protections supplement state law.

Always verify whether local ordinances apply in addition to state law. Local rules often prevail where they are more protective of tenants.

Exemptions: When Just Cause Laws May Not Apply

Most just cause statutes include exemptions. Common exemptions include:

  • Single-family homes — Some laws (including California's AB 1482) exempt single-family homes not owned by a corporation or LLC, provided proper notice is given to tenants.
  • New construction — Units built within the last 15 years are often exempt (the "15-year rolling exemption" in California).
  • Owner-occupied small buildings — A landlord living in a unit of a duplex or small building may be exempt in certain states.
  • Short-term rentals — Transient occupancies under 30 days are typically excluded.
  • Subsidized housing — Units subject to other regulatory frameworks sometimes have separate rules.

Understanding whether your properties fall within an exemption is just as important as understanding the law itself. Consult a qualified real estate attorney to review your portfolio's compliance status.

How Just Cause Laws Affect Lease Management

Documenting Violations Carefully

If you ever need to evict a tenant for an at-fault reason, your documentation becomes the foundation of your legal case. Best practices include:

  • Sending all lease violation notices in writing via certified mail
  • Keeping dated copies of all communications
  • Photographing lease violations (unauthorized pets, property damage, etc.)
  • Maintaining a log of noise complaints and neighbor reports

Using Cure-or-Quit Notices Correctly

Many at-fault just cause situations require you to give the tenant an opportunity to fix ("cure") the violation before you can proceed with eviction. A three-day notice to pay rent or quit and a five-day notice to cure or quit are common instruments. Consult your state's specific timelines — errors in notice periods are a leading cause of dismissed eviction cases.

Structuring Move-In Documentation

Because disputes often arise about the original condition of a unit, move-in condition reports with photographs signed by the tenant protect you if damage later becomes a just cause for eviction.

Relocation Assistance Requirements

No-fault evictions in just cause jurisdictions often trigger mandatory relocation assistance payments to displaced tenants. Key facts:

  • Amount typically ranges from one to three months' rent, depending on the jurisdiction and reason for termination.
  • California AB 1482 requires one month's rent as relocation assistance or an equivalent rent waiver for the last month of tenancy.
  • San Francisco and Oakland require substantially higher relocation assistance — sometimes reaching $10,000 or more for long-term tenants.
  • Some cities require relocation assistance even for at-fault evictions in certain circumstances.

Budget for relocation assistance as an operating cost when you acquire properties in highly regulated markets.

Practical Compliance Steps for Landlords

Step 1: Identify Your Jurisdiction's Rules

Before purchasing or managing a rental in any market, research whether state or local just cause protections apply. Resources include:

  • Your state's landlord-tenant statute (usually found on the state legislature's website)
  • Local housing department websites
  • A licensed real estate attorney familiar with landlord-tenant law in that jurisdiction

Step 2: Update Your Lease Agreements

Your lease should clearly reference the applicable just cause law, list acceptable termination reasons, and include notice period requirements. Using a lease template from a local landlord association or attorney is advisable rather than a generic online form.

Step 3: Create a Termination Paper Trail

Never verbally agree to terminate a tenancy. Every notice, demand, or agreement relating to the end of a tenancy should be in writing, dated, and retained in the tenant's file.

Step 4: Train Your Property Managers

If you use a property management company or in-house manager, ensure they understand the specific requirements in each jurisdiction you operate in. A manager who issues a termination notice without just cause — even in good faith — can expose you to liability.

Step 5: Consult Counsel Before Acting

Before serving any eviction notice, have your documentation reviewed by a licensed attorney. The cost of a brief consultation is negligible compared to a dismissed case, a tenant's attorney fee award, or a wrongful eviction claim.

Penalties for Non-Compliance

Violating just cause eviction laws can be costly:

  • Wrongful eviction lawsuits — Tenants can sue for actual damages, emotional distress, and in some jurisdictions punitive damages. The cost of eviction for landlords can escalate quickly when wrongful eviction claims are involved.
  • Attorney fee awards — Many just cause statutes include prevailing party attorney fee provisions, meaning a landlord who loses must pay the tenant's legal costs.
  • Reinstatement orders — Courts may order the tenant reinstated to the unit, particularly in egregious cases.
  • Fines from housing agencies — Local rent boards and housing departments may impose administrative fines for violations. HUD's fair housing enforcement office may also investigate complaints involving discriminatory eviction patterns.

Just Cause Laws and Portfolio Strategy

If you are actively building or expanding a rental portfolio, just cause regulations should factor into your acquisition analysis:

  • Market selection — Highly regulated cities (San Francisco, New York, Los Angeles) have lower eviction flexibility, which can affect your ability to reposition assets.
  • Asset type — Single-family homes often have more exemptions than multi-family properties.
  • Underwriting assumptions — In just cause jurisdictions, budget for longer vacancy periods when transitioning units and for relocation assistance costs.
  • Exit strategy — Understand whether the Ellis Act or equivalent withdrawal procedures apply if you plan to convert a property.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I evict a month-to-month tenant without just cause?

In states and cities with just cause protections, no — once the required tenancy period is met (usually 12 months), you must have a qualifying reason even for month-to-month tenants.

Does just cause apply to commercial rentals?

Most just cause eviction statutes apply only to residential tenancies. Commercial leases are generally governed by different rules.

Can I include a buyout clause in my lease?

Many jurisdictions allow landlords to negotiate a voluntary tenant buyout, but the process is regulated. In San Francisco, for example, buyout agreements must be registered with the Rent Board.

What if my property is exempt — do I still need to give a reason?

Even exempt landlords should document reasons for termination. Some courts and housing agencies look critically at serial evictions that suggest retaliation or discrimination, even where just cause technically does not apply.

Conclusion

Just cause eviction laws are now a permanent feature of the rental landscape in a growing number of states and cities. Landlords who understand the specific requirements of their jurisdictions, document tenancy issues carefully, and follow proper notice procedures will be positioned to manage their portfolios effectively even in heavily regulated markets.

The key is not to view just cause laws as an obstacle but as a framework that, when followed correctly, provides a clear and legally defensible path for managing your rental units. Building compliance into your operational systems from day one is far less costly than navigating a wrongful eviction claim after the fact.
