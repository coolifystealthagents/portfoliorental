For short-term rental operators, unauthorized parties and excessive noise are among the most serious liability and neighbor relations problems. A single loud party can result in fines from the city, noise ordinance violations, neighbor complaints that threaten your STR permit, and property damage. And because you are not present at the property, you may not even know it happened until the damage is done.

Noise monitoring devices offer a proactive solution: they measure sound levels at the property in real time and alert you when noise exceeds a set threshold — giving you the opportunity to contact guests before the situation escalates into a code violation or neighbor complaint.

This guide covers the best noise monitoring devices for short-term rentals, the legal rules you must follow, how to disclose devices to guests, and how to integrate noise monitoring into your STR management workflow.

How Noise Monitors Work

Noise monitoring devices are sound-level meters connected to the internet. They measure ambient sound levels in decibels (dB) continuously and:

  • Alert the host when noise exceeds a configurable threshold (typically 70-85 dB, set by the operator)
  • Do not record audio — they measure sound volume only, not conversations or identifiable sounds
  • Send alerts via SMS, email, or app notification
  • Some devices also monitor the number of people at the property (using Wi-Fi device counting or IR sensors) and temperature/humidity

The FTC guides consumer privacy expectations for smart devices. The distinction that noise monitors measure decibel levels rather than recording audio is legally significant — it distinguishes them from audio surveillance devices, which have significant legal restrictions.

Top Noise Monitoring Products for STR Operators

Minut

Minut is the leading purpose-built STR noise monitor and is officially endorsed by Airbnb:

  • Measures sound level, temperature, humidity, and the number of devices connected to Wi-Fi (as a proxy for occupancy)
  • No audio recording — dB measurement only
  • Integration with Airbnb, VRBO, Booking.com, and major property management platforms (Hostaway, Guesty, Lodgify)
  • Automated guest messaging when thresholds are exceeded
  • Monthly SaaS pricing: approximately $10-$15/month per property

Best for: Airbnb hosts and STR operators of all sizes who want the most integrated solution.

Noise Aware

NoiseAware is the other major STR-focused noise monitoring brand:

  • Indoor and outdoor sensors available
  • Measures sound in real time; no audio recording
  • Alerts via app and can trigger automated guest messages
  • Integration with major STR platforms
  • Provides a "noise score" that tracks patterns over time
  • Monthly pricing: approximately $10-$15/month per property

Best for: Hosts who want outdoor coverage (NoiseAware offers outdoor units, while Minut is primarily indoor)

Alertify

Alertify (formerly Party Squasher) focuses specifically on party prevention:

  • Noise monitoring plus cigarette smoke detection
  • Occupancy monitoring (counts active Wi-Fi devices as a proxy for guest count)
  • Tamper detection (alerts if device is removed)
  • Integration with STR platforms
  • Monthly pricing: approximately $15-$25/month per property

Best for: Higher-risk properties in party-prone locations or operators who need cigarette smoke monitoring

Roomonitor

A European-origin solution with growing U.S. market presence:

  • Real-time noise monitoring with mobile alerts
  • No audio recording
  • Tamper alerts
  • Available for both indoor and outdoor monitoring

Legal Rules for Noise Monitors

What Is Permitted

Noise-level monitoring devices (decibel meters) that do not record audio or identifiable sounds are generally permissible with proper disclosure. Because they measure volume rather than content, they do not constitute wiretapping or audio surveillance under federal or state wiretapping laws.

What Is Not Permitted

Audio recording devices that capture identifiable speech require:

  • Compliance with the Electronic Communications Privacy Act (federal)
  • Compliance with state wiretapping and eavesdropping laws
  • In two-party/all-party consent states (California, Illinois, Washington, and others), all parties being recorded must consent

Noise level monitors are not audio recorders and do not implicate these laws, as long as the device does not record or transmit identifiable audio.

Placement Restrictions

Even though noise monitors are not audio recorders, they must not be placed in areas where guests have a heightened privacy expectation:

  • Not in bedrooms — Guests have a clear privacy expectation in sleeping areas
  • Not in bathrooms — Privacy expectation is highest here; never install any sensor in a bathroom
  • Common areas are appropriate — Living rooms, kitchens, and common gathering areas are appropriate placement

Placing a noise monitor in a bedroom would likely violate guest privacy expectations even if it only measures decibels. Review tenant privacy laws before installing devices.

Platform Rules

Major STR platforms have their own rules:

  • Airbnb — Requires hosts to disclose all noise monitoring devices in the listing; requires that devices be in common areas only (not bedrooms or bathrooms); prohibits audio or video surveillance inside the property
  • VRBO — Similar requirements: disclosure required, must not be in bedrooms or bathrooms

Disclosure Requirements

Full disclosure is both a legal obligation and a best practice:

In Your Listing

Every STR listing should specifically mention that the property has a noise monitoring device:

"This property is equipped with a noise monitoring device in the [living room/common area] that measures sound levels to ensure compliance with neighborhood quiet hours. The device does not record audio."

In Your House Rules

Your house rules should include:

  • Reference to the noise monitoring device
  • The quiet hours applicable at the property
  • What happens if noise thresholds are exceeded (you will contact guests)
  • Maximum occupancy limits

At Check-In

Many hosts include information about the noise monitor in their check-in message or the welcome book at the property.

Setting Appropriate Thresholds

Noise monitors allow you to configure alert thresholds. Setting these correctly prevents both under-alerting (too high a threshold) and alert fatigue (too low a threshold):

Reference decibel levels:

  • 60 dB — Normal conversation
  • 70 dB — Loud conversation; TV at normal volume
  • 80 dB — Raised voices; party noise beginning
  • 90 dB — Loud party; shouting
  • 100 dB+ — Concert levels; very loud party

Recommended STR settings:

  • Daytime (9 AM–10 PM): Alert at 80-85 dB sustained for 5-10 minutes
  • Nighttime (10 PM–9 AM): Alert at 70-75 dB sustained for 3-5 minutes

The "sustained for X minutes" trigger is important — it prevents single events (a TV program, a door slamming) from triggering false alerts while catching sustained party noise.

Responding to Noise Alerts

When your device triggers:

  1. Check the alert details — Confirm the noise level and which sensor triggered
  2. Message the guest immediately — A polite reminder is usually sufficient to resolve the situation: "Hi, we've noticed elevated noise levels at the property. Please be mindful of the quiet hours (10 PM - 9 AM) and your neighbors. Thank you!"
  3. If noise continues — Follow up with a direct call to the guest; remind them of the consequences (early checkout, additional charges per house rules)
  4. If noise remains — Contact local authorities if the situation constitutes a noise ordinance violation; document everything

Most situations are resolved at Step 2. Guests who know they are being monitored typically self-regulate when reminded.

Occupancy Monitoring

Some noise monitoring devices (Minut, Alertify) also monitor occupancy by counting devices connected to your property's Wi-Fi network. This is a useful proxy for guest count, alerting you when the number of connected devices significantly exceeds the number of registered guests.

Limitations:

  • Not all guests connect to Wi-Fi
  • Each person may have multiple devices
  • It is an estimate, not a precise count

Best use: Trigger for a follow-up communication if occupancy appears significantly above the booking count, not as definitive proof of a violation.

Integrating Noise Monitoring with Your STR Operations

Noise monitoring is most effective as part of an integrated short-term rental management approach:

Automated Guest Messaging

Configure your noise monitor to trigger automated messages through your PMS (property management software) when thresholds are exceeded. Minut, for example, integrates directly with Hostaway, Guesty, and other platforms to automatically send a noise alert message.

House Rules and Security Deposit

Your house rules should explicitly address noise, parties, and the consequences of violations:

  • Maximum occupancy
  • Quiet hours
  • Prohibition on parties and events
  • Noise monitoring disclosure
  • Additional cleaning fees and security deposit forfeiture terms for violations

Consider pairing noise monitoring with security cameras for exterior coverage.

Local Ordinance Compliance

Many cities with STR permit programs require operators to maintain quiet hours, respond to neighbor complaints within a set time frame, and prevent excessive gatherings. Your noise monitoring system provides both the early warning to respond before a violation and the documentation that you took proactive steps.

Noise Monitoring for Long-Term Rentals

While noise monitors are primarily associated with short-term rentals, some long-term landlords use them in multi-family properties for:

  • Common area noise monitoring (hallways, lobby)
  • Shared laundry rooms
  • After-hours amenity spaces (pool, rooftop)

For long-term rental applications, the same disclosure requirements apply, and devices should never be placed inside units. Review landlord right of entry rules for your state.

Conclusion

Noise monitoring devices are an essential tool for short-term rental operators who need visibility into their properties between check-in and check-out. By measuring — not recording — sound levels and alerting hosts when thresholds are exceeded, they enable proactive guest management that prevents noise violations, neighbor complaints, and property damage. The keys to compliant deployment are choosing a device that does not record audio, disclosing the device clearly in your listing, and placing sensors only in common areas.
