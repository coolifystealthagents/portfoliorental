2c:T2963,<p>A series LLC lets you create multiple protected "series" within a single LLC, each shielding its assets from the liabilities of other series. For rental property investors, this means you can hold each property in its own series without forming a separate LLC for each one — reducing paperwork, filing fees, and administrative complexity.</p>
<p>But series LLCs are not available in every state, and their interstate recognition remains uncertain. This guide covers how they work, where they are available, and whether they are right for your portfolio.</p>
<h2 id="how-a-series-llc-works">How a Series LLC Works</h2>
<h3 id="structure">Structure</h3>
<p>A series LLC consists of:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>The master LLC</strong> — The parent entity filed with the state</li>
<li><strong>Individual series</strong> — Separate divisions within the master LLC, each with its own assets, liabilities, members, and business purpose</li>
</ul>
<p>Each series operates as a separate liability shield. If a tenant sues over a property in Series A, only the assets in Series A are at risk. Series B, C, and the master LLC's assets are protected.</p>
<h3 id="key-features">Key Features</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Internal liability barriers</strong> — Assets in one series are shielded from claims against another series</li>
<li><strong>Single state filing</strong> — One LLC formation with the state, not one per property</li>
<li><strong>Separate books and records</strong> — Each series must maintain its own accounting</li>
<li><strong>Flexible membership</strong> — Different series can have different members and ownership percentages</li>
<li><strong>Separate bank accounts</strong> — Each series should have its own bank account to maintain the liability shield</li>
</ul>

<h2 id="states-that-allow-series-llcs">States That Allow Series LLCs</h2>
<p>As of 2026, the following states authorize series LLCs through statute:</p>
<ul>
<li>Alabama</li>
<li>Arkansas</li>
<li>Delaware (the originator of the series LLC concept)</li>
<li>Illinois</li>
<li>Indiana</li>
<li>Iowa</li>
<li>Kansas</li>
<li>Missouri</li>
<li>Montana</li>
<li>Nebraska</li>
<li>Nevada</li>
<li>North Dakota</li>
<li>Oklahoma</li>
<li>South Dakota</li>
<li>Tennessee</li>
<li>Texas</li>
<li>Utah</li>
<li>Virginia</li>
<li>Wisconsin</li>
<li>Wyoming</li>
<li>District of Columbia</li>
</ul>
<p>Several other states have introduced or are considering series LLC legislation.</p>
<h3 id="the-interstate-recognition-problem">The Interstate Recognition Problem</h3>
<p>The biggest risk with a series LLC is that states without series LLC statutes may not recognize the internal liability barriers. If you own property in a state that does not authorize series LLCs, a court in that state might treat all series as one entity for liability purposes.</p>
<p><strong>Practical implication:</strong> If you form a Texas series LLC but own property in Ohio (which does not have a series LLC statute), an Ohio court might not respect the liability separation between series.</p>
<h3 id="strategies-for-multi-state-portfolios">Strategies for Multi-State Portfolios</h3>
<ul>
<li>Form the series LLC in a state that recognizes the structure</li>
<li>For properties in non-recognizing states, consider holding them in traditional separate LLCs</li>
<li>Register the series LLC as a foreign entity in states where you own property (requirements vary)</li>
<li>Consult an attorney familiar with the laws of each state where you own property</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="setting-up-a-series-llc-for-rental-properties">Setting Up a Series LLC for Rental Properties</h2>
<h3 id="step-1-form-the-master-llc">Step 1: Form the Master LLC</h3>
<p>File articles of organization in a state that authorizes series LLCs. The <a href="https://www.sba.gov/business-guide/launch-your-business">SBA business launch guide</a> covers general LLC formation steps. The articles must include language authorizing the creation of series.</p>
<h3 id="step-2-create-the-operating-agreement">Step 2: Create the Operating Agreement</h3>
<p>The master operating agreement should:</p>
<ul>
<li>Authorize the creation of individual series</li>
<li>Define the process for creating new series</li>
<li>Establish that each series has separate assets, liabilities, and members</li>
<li>Set governance rules for the master LLC and individual series</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="step-3-create-individual-series">Step 3: Create Individual Series</h3>
<p>For each property, create a series designation that:</p>
<ul>
<li>Identifies the series (e.g., "Series A — 123 Main Street")</li>
<li>Lists the assets assigned to that series</li>
<li>Defines the members and their ownership percentages</li>
<li>Establishes separate accounting</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="step-4-maintain-separate-records">Step 4: Maintain Separate Records</h3>
<p>Each series must maintain its own:</p>
<ul>
<li>Bank account</li>
<li>Accounting records</li>
<li>Books and records</li>
<li>Contracts (leases signed in the series name)</li>
<li>Insurance policies (or properly named on a shared policy)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Critical:</strong> Commingling assets between series undermines the liability separation and can cause a court to disregard the series structure entirely.</p>
<h2 id="tax-treatment">Tax Treatment</h2>
<h3 id="irs-guidance">IRS Guidance</h3>
<p>The IRS has not issued comprehensive guidance on series LLC taxation. Landlords should review the <a href="https://www.irs.gov/publications/p527">IRS rental property guidance</a> for general rental income reporting rules. Current practice:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Single-member series</strong> — Generally treated as a disregarded entity, reported on the owner's personal return</li>
<li><strong>Multi-member series</strong> — May need a separate EIN and partnership tax return</li>
<li><strong>State treatment varies</strong> — Some states treat each series as a separate entity for tax purposes; others tax the master LLC as a single entity</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="practical-tax-considerations">Practical Tax Considerations</h3>
<ul>
<li>Obtain a separate EIN for each series that has its own bank account</li>
<li>Track income and expenses separately for each series</li>
<li>Consult a tax professional familiar with series LLC taxation in your state</li>
<li>Be prepared for potential state-level filing requirements</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="risks-and-limitations">Risks and Limitations</h2>
<h3 id="untested-in-many-courts">Untested in Many Courts</h3>
<p>Series LLC liability protection has limited case law. For background on the legal foundations, see Cornell Law Institute's <a href="https://www.law.cornell.edu/wex/landlord-tenant_law">landlord-tenant law overview</a>. Courts have not extensively tested whether internal liability barriers hold up, especially in states without series LLC statutes.</p>
<h3 id="financing-challenges">Financing Challenges</h3>
<ul>
<li>Many lenders are unfamiliar with series LLCs and may be reluctant to finance properties held in a series</li>
<li>Title companies may have difficulty issuing title insurance for series-held properties</li>
<li>Refinancing may require transferring the property out of the series temporarily</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="insurance-complications">Insurance Complications</h3>
<ul>
<li>Not all insurance companies understand series LLC structures</li>
<li>Ensure your insurance policy properly covers each series — consider pairing your LLC structure with <a href="/blog/umbrella-insurance-landlord">umbrella insurance for landlords</a> to fill coverage gaps</li>
<li>Verify that the named insured matches the series holding each property</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="commingling-risk">Commingling Risk</h3>
<p>If you fail to maintain separate records, accounts, and accounting for each series, a court may "pierce the series veil" and treat all assets as part of a single entity — eliminating the liability protection entirely.</p>
<h2 id="series-llc-vs-other-structures">Series LLC vs Other Structures</h2>
<h3 id="when-to-use-a-series-llc">When to Use a Series LLC</h3>
<ul>
<li>You own multiple properties in a state that recognizes series LLCs</li>
<li>You want liability isolation without the cost of forming multiple entities</li>
<li>You are comfortable with the administrative discipline of maintaining separate records</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="when-to-use-separate-llcs">When to Use Separate LLCs</h3>
<ul>
<li>You own property in states that do not recognize series LLCs</li>
<li>You want established, universally recognized liability protection</li>
<li>Your properties have different investors or ownership structures</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="when-to-use-a-trust-llc">When to Use a Trust + LLC</h3>
<ul>
<li>You prioritize estate planning and privacy alongside liability protection</li>
<li>See our <a href="/blog/trust-vs-llc-rental-property">trust vs LLC comparison</a> for details</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="key-takeaways">Key Takeaways</h2>
<p>Series LLCs offer an efficient way to isolate liability across multiple rental properties with lower cost and complexity than forming separate LLCs. However, they come with meaningful limitations: interstate recognition is uncertain, financing can be complicated, and maintaining separate records is non-negotiable. If your properties are concentrated in a state that recognizes series LLCs, the structure can be an excellent tool. For multi-state portfolios, consult an attorney to evaluate whether a series LLC, separate LLCs, or a hybrid approach is best.</p>
