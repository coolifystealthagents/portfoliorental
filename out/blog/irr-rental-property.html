Internal Rate of Return (IRR) for Rental Properties

Most landlords evaluate deals using cap rate or cash-on-cash return. These are useful tools—but they only capture part of the picture. Internal Rate of Return (IRR) is the metric that sophisticated investors and real estate private equity firms rely on to evaluate the full lifetime return of a rental property investment.

If you've ever wanted to know which of two investments truly performs better over a 5- or 10-year hold—accounting for cash flow, appreciation, loan paydown, and sale proceeds—IRR gives you the answer.

What Is IRR in Real Estate?

IRR (Internal Rate of Return) is the annualized rate of return that makes the net present value (NPV) of all cash flows from an investment equal to zero. In plain English: it's the compound annual growth rate your investment earns over its entire hold period, accounting for both the timing and magnitude of every dollar in and out.

Unlike cash-on-cash return (which only measures current-year income) or cap rate (which ignores financing), IRR captures:

  • Your initial investment (down payment + closing costs)
  • Annual cash flow from rents (after all expenses and debt service)
  • Net sale proceeds at exit (after paying off the mortgage and selling costs)
  • When each dollar is received (earlier cash flows are worth more)

This makes IRR the most comprehensive single-number measure of rental property performance.

The IRR Formula (and Why It Needs a Calculator)

IRR is technically defined as the discount rate (r) that satisfies:

0 = CF₀ + CF₁/(1+r) + CF₂/(1+r)² + CF₃/(1+r)³ + ... + CFₙ/(1+r)ⁿ

Where:

  • CF₀ = Initial investment (negative, since it's cash out)
  • CF₁ through CFₙ = Annual cash flows
  • CFₙ = Final year cash flow + net sale proceeds

There's no algebraic shortcut—IRR must be solved iteratively. Use Excel's =IRR() function, Google Sheets, or a real estate financial model. You input your cash flow series, and the tool finds the rate.

Step-by-Step IRR Calculation Example

Let's walk through a realistic example.

Property Details:

  • Purchase price: $350,000
  • Down payment (25%): $87,500
  • Closing costs: $5,500
  • Total initial investment: $93,000
  • Mortgage: $262,500 at 7.0% for 30 years → ~$1,747/month ($20,964/year)

Annual Operating Assumptions:

  • Gross rents: $36,000/year
  • Operating expenses (35% ratio): $12,600/year
  • NOI: $23,400/year
  • Annual cash flow: $23,400 - $20,964 = $2,436/year

Assumptions for 7-Year Hold:

  • Rent growth: 3% per year
  • Expense growth: 2.5% per year
  • Property appreciation: 4% per year
  • Sale at Year 7: $350,000 × (1.04)^7 = $460,700
  • Remaining mortgage balance at Year 7: ~$237,000
  • Selling costs (6%): $27,642
  • Net sale proceeds: $460,700 - $237,000 - $27,642 = $196,058

Cash Flow Stream:

Year Cash Flow
0 -$93,000
1 $2,436
2 $3,190
3 $3,972
4 $4,783
5 $5,622
6 $6,491
7 $7,390 + $196,058 = $203,448

Result: IRR = approximately 14.8%

This is your true annualized return on invested capital over the 7-year hold.

Why IRR Is Better Than Simpler Metrics

Cash-on-Cash Return Is Too Shortsighted

In Year 1 of the example above, cash-on-cash return = $2,436 ÷ $93,000 = 2.6%. That looks terrible. But the IRR of 14.8% tells a completely different story once you factor in equity growth from appreciation and loan paydown over 7 years.

Many investors would pass on this deal based on Year-1 CoC return alone—and leave strong long-term returns on the table.

Cap Rate Ignores Financing and Exit

Cap rate for this property = $23,400 ÷ $350,000 = 6.7%. That's a reasonable return metric, but it tells you nothing about:

  • How your mortgage amplifies (or diminishes) returns
  • What you'll actually net when you sell
  • The compounding effect of rent growth over time

Learn more about cap rate vs cash-on-cash return to understand when each metric applies.

IRR Accounts for the Time Value of Money

A dollar received in Year 1 is worth more than a dollar received in Year 7. IRR explicitly accounts for this by discounting future cash flows. This makes it the only metric that lets you accurately compare:

  • A high-cash-flow, low-appreciation deal vs. a low-cash-flow, high-appreciation deal
  • Short hold periods vs. long hold periods
  • Properties in different markets with different growth trajectories

What Is a Good IRR for Rental Properties?

IRR benchmarks depend on property type, market, and risk:

Investment Type Target IRR Range
Core stabilized multifamily 8–12%
Value-add single-family 12–18%
Short-term rental / Airbnb 15–25%
Ground-up development 20%+

As a rule of thumb, most buy-and-hold rental investors target 12–18% unlevered IRR on residential properties. If your IRR falls below your hurdle rate (the minimum return you require to justify the investment's risk), the deal doesn't pencil.

NCREIF data shows that institutional-grade real estate historically produces 8–11% IRR over long hold periods, providing a useful benchmark for private investors.

Factors That Most Impact IRR in Rental Properties

1. Exit Price (Appreciation)

The sale proceeds typically dominate IRR because they're a large cash flow received at the end. A property that appreciates 3% per year vs. 5% per year can shift IRR by 3–4 percentage points—more than almost any operating improvement.

This makes understanding rental property appreciation rates critical to projecting accurate IRR.

2. Hold Period

IRR is highly sensitive to the hold period:

  • Shorter holds magnify the impact of selling costs and reduce time for appreciation to compound
  • Longer holds allow compounding to work, but require more accurate long-term projections
  • Generally, 5–10 years is the sweet spot for buy-and-hold residential

3. Leverage

A higher loan-to-value ratio amplifies IRR when appreciation exceeds the mortgage rate. In a rising market, leverage can transform an 8% IRR all-cash deal into a 15%+ IRR deal with 25% down.

The flip side: leverage amplifies losses in flat or declining markets.

4. Annual Cash Flow

While appreciation dominates IRR, cash flow matters too—especially in Year 1-3 when the time-value penalty is highest. Even modest positive cash flow in early years meaningfully improves IRR compared to break-even or negative cash flow scenarios.

See our analysis of negative cash flow rental properties to understand when the math still works despite early losses.

5. Timing of Capital Improvements

Large capital expenditures (new roof, HVAC, renovation) are negative cash flows that reduce IRR. Timing these strategically—deferring them when possible or front-loading them to boost rents faster—can meaningfully move your IRR.

How to Calculate IRR in Excel or Google Sheets

  1. List your cash flows in a column (Year 0 as negative, subsequent years as positive or negative)
  2. In an empty cell, type: =IRR(A1:A8) where A1:A8 contains your cash flow series
  3. Excel/Sheets will return the IRR as a decimal—format as percentage

Pro tip: If your cash flows don't occur at exactly year-end intervals, use =XIRR() with actual dates for more precision.

IRR vs. Equity Multiple: Using Both Together

Investors often pair IRR with equity multiple (also called MOIC—multiple on invested capital):

Equity Multiple = Total Cash Received ÷ Total Cash Invested

In our example: ($2,436 + $3,190 + $3,972 + $4,783 + $5,622 + $6,491 + $203,448) ÷ $93,000 = $229,942 ÷ $93,000 = 2.47x equity multiple

Metric Our Example What It Shows
IRR 14.8% Annualized compound return
Equity Multiple 2.47x Total wealth multiplication
Cash-on-Cash (Yr 1) 2.6% Current annual yield

A high IRR over a short period might produce a low equity multiple. A long hold with modest IRR might produce an impressive equity multiple. Smart investors use both.

IRR Limitations to Understand

Reinvestment Assumption

IRR implicitly assumes that all cash flows are reinvested at the same IRR rate. If your IRR is 15% but you can only reinvest cash flows at 7%, your actual wealth accumulation will fall short of projections. Modified IRR (MIRR) corrects for this by letting you specify a reinvestment rate.

Garbage In, Garbage Out

IRR is only as accurate as your assumptions about rent growth, appreciation, expenses, and exit cap rates. Small changes in appreciation assumptions can swing IRR by 5+ percentage points. Build conservative base cases and stress-test your model.

Ignores Taxes

Pre-tax IRR doesn't account for depreciation benefits, capital gains taxes, or 1031 exchange opportunities. After-tax IRR can be significantly higher for investors in high tax brackets who use depreciation effectively. See our guide on rental property depreciation for context.

Putting IRR to Work in Your Investment Analysis

IRR works best as a comparison tool and go/no-go screen:

  1. Set a minimum IRR hurdle (e.g., 12%) based on your risk tolerance and opportunity cost
  2. Model base, bull, and bear cases with different appreciation and rent growth assumptions
  3. Compare IRR across competing deals to rank opportunities objectively
  4. Use IRR alongside cash-on-cash return to make sure you're not sacrificing too much current income for future appreciation

For a complete picture of rental property financial performance, combine IRR analysis with our rental property cash flow analysis framework.

Summary

IRR is the gold standard for evaluating rental property returns because it captures every dollar—cash flow, appreciation, and equity paydown—and weights earlier returns more heavily than later ones. While it requires a spreadsheet to calculate, the insight it provides is unmatched.

For long-term investors building wealth through real estate, understanding IRR transforms how you screen deals, compare markets, and make hold vs. sell decisions. Pair it with strategies to maximize rental income and you'll have a complete financial framework for building a high-performing portfolio.
