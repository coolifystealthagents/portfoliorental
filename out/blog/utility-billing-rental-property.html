Utility Billing Options for Rental Properties

How you handle utility billing in your rental properties has a direct and measurable impact on your net operating income. Landlords who pay utilities on behalf of tenants — without any cost recovery mechanism — routinely absorb $150 to $400 per unit per month in unnecessary expenses. Implementing the right utility billing structure can recover most or all of that cost, shift consumption incentives to tenants, and add thousands of dollars to your annual NOI.

This guide covers every major utility billing option for rental properties: individual metering, ratio utility billing systems (RUBS), submetering, flat fee inclusions, and hybrid approaches. We will help you evaluate which structure makes sense for your property type, tenant profile, and local regulations.

Why Utility Billing Structure Matters

The baseline comparison is simple:

Landlord pays all utilities:

  • Absorbs 100% of utility costs
  • No tenant incentive to conserve
  • Utility costs directly reduce NOI
  • Rent may be slightly higher but typically not enough to fully compensate

Tenant pays all utilities:

  • Landlord has zero utility expense
  • Tenants have incentive to conserve
  • Requires individual metering or billing structure
  • Rent may need to be slightly lower in some markets

In practice, many properties — especially older multifamily buildings — were built without individual metering, making direct billing impossible without capital investment. This is where RUBS and submetering come in.

Option 1: Individual Metering (Master Metering)

In properties with individual meters for each unit, utility billing is straightforward: each tenant establishes service in their own name and pays directly. The landlord has no utility expense related to that utility.

Best for: Single-family rentals, newer apartment buildings, any property built with individual meters

Advantages:

  • Zero landlord exposure to utility cost increases
  • Natural conservation incentive for tenants
  • Simplest administrative structure

Disadvantages:

  • Requires utility cooperation to transfer service between tenants (a logistical consideration at turnover)
  • Not feasible for older properties without individual meters
  • Common area and exterior utilities remain landlord-paid

For properties with existing individual meters, ensure your lease clearly states that tenants are responsible for establishing service in their name before move-in. Account for any period between tenancies when the landlord must temporarily hold service.

Option 2: RUBS — Ratio Utility Billing System

RUBS is the most common approach for multifamily properties that do not have individual meters. The landlord pays the master utility bill and then allocates costs to tenants based on a formula.

Common RUBS allocation formulas:

  • Per unit: Each unit pays an equal share (simple but ignores unit size differences)
  • By occupancy: Costs allocated by the number of occupants per unit
  • By square footage: Costs allocated proportionally by unit size
  • Hybrid: Combining occupancy and square footage factors

Example: A 12-unit building has a monthly water bill of $900. Under square footage RUBS, a 900 sq ft unit in a building with 10,800 total square feet pays $900 × (900/10,800) = $75/month.

Best for: Older multifamily properties without individual meters where submetering installation would be expensive

Advantages:

  • No capital investment required
  • Shifts some utility cost burden to tenants
  • Legal in most states (with proper notice and lease provisions)
  • Reduces landlord utility expense by 70–90% in typical applications

Disadvantages:

  • Not as accurate as submetering — tenants do not pay for their exact usage
  • Some states have specific regulations on RUBS administration and billing transparency
  • May face tenant resistance without clear communication
  • Typically landlords recover 70–90% of utility costs, not 100%, because a small percentage is retained for common areas and building systems

Legal considerations: RUBS regulations vary significantly by state. Many states require specific disclosure language, billing format requirements, and prohibit billing tenants more than the actual utility cost. Verify your state's requirements before implementing.

Option 3: Submetering

Submetering involves installing individual meters for each unit (even on a property with a single master meter). A third-party billing company reads the submeters and bills tenants directly for their measured usage, usually at the utility's rate plus a small administrative fee.

Best for: Larger multifamily properties where the cost of submeter installation is justified by the utility savings; properties with high utility costs or heavy utility usage

Advantages:

  • Most accurate billing — tenants pay exactly for what they use
  • Maximum conservation incentive
  • Can achieve near-100% cost recovery

Disadvantages:

  • Significant upfront capital investment (typically $200–$600 per unit for water submeters; more for electric)
  • Ongoing third-party billing fees
  • Regulatory requirements vary by state
  • Some states require landlords to charge only the utility's tariff rate to tenants

Typical payback period: 2–5 years on the submeter installation cost, after which utility savings are nearly all recovered.

Popular third-party submeter billing companies include Novus Utility Services and other regional providers. These companies handle meter reading, billing, collections, and compliance reporting.

Option 4: Flat Fee or Utility Allowance Inclusion

Some landlords include utilities in the rent but charge a flat monthly fee (sometimes structured as a utility fee separate from base rent, sometimes rolled into the rent price). This approach is common for single-family rentals and smaller multifamily units.

How it works: Tenant pays a fixed monthly amount (say, $75) toward utilities regardless of actual usage. The landlord pays the bill and keeps the difference if usage is below the flat fee, or absorbs the excess if usage exceeds it.

Advantages:

  • Simple for tenants and landlord
  • Predictable income contribution from tenants
  • Useful in markets where "utilities included" is a marketing advantage

Disadvantages:

  • No conservation incentive for tenants
  • Landlord bears the risk of above-average usage
  • Needs periodic recalibration as utility rates change

Option 5: Landlord Pays All Utilities

In some markets and property types, paying all utilities is standard practice and competitive necessity. Examples include furnished short-term rentals, some upscale apartments in high-demand markets, or properties where metering infrastructure simply does not exist and RUBS would be impractical.

If you pay all utilities, focus on expense reduction strategies:

  • Energy efficiency upgrades to reduce consumption
  • Water conservation measures to minimize water/sewer costs
  • Smart thermostats and other technology to control HVAC costs
  • Regular maintenance of appliances and systems to ensure efficient operation

Choosing the Right Approach for Your Property

Use this decision framework:

Scenario Recommended Approach
Single-family with individual meter Tenant pays directly
New multifamily with individual meters Tenant pays directly
Older multifamily, no individual meters RUBS or submetering
High-volume water users (laundry in units) Water submetering
Short-term rental Landlord pays (build into nightly rate)
Furnished units / student housing Flat fee inclusion

Implementing a Billing Change: Tenant Communication

Transitioning from a landlord-paid utility arrangement to RUBS or submetering requires careful tenant communication:

  1. Review your leases: You typically cannot change billing arrangements mid-lease. Plan for implementation at lease renewal.
  2. Provide advance notice: Most states require 30 days' written notice for any change in rental terms.
  3. Be transparent: Explain the allocation methodology clearly. Tenants who understand the formula are less likely to dispute bills.
  4. Adjust rent if necessary: If you are adding a utility billing component, a modest rent reduction may reduce tenant resistance and turnover risk.
  5. Ensure lease compliance: Your lease must clearly describe the utility billing arrangement and comply with state-specific requirements.

Financial Impact: Projecting Your Savings

For a 20-unit property where the landlord currently pays $400/unit/month in utilities ($96,000/year):

  • RUBS (recovering 80%): Saves $76,800/year → At a 6% cap rate, adds $1,280,000 to property value
  • Submetering (recovering 95%): Saves $91,200/year → At a 6% cap rate, adds $1,520,000 to property value
  • Minus submeter installation (20 units × $400): $(8,000) upfront

The NOI impact of proper utility billing is one of the largest single levers available in multifamily investing. Combined with other expense reduction strategies and an overall plan to maximize rental income, getting utility billing right can be transformative for your portfolio's financial performance.

Track all utility expenses carefully in your rental property expense tracking system so you have a clear baseline before and after any billing change — and can demonstrate the improvement to lenders and potential buyers.
