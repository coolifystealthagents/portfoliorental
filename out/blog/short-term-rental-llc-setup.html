Setting Up an LLC for Your Short-Term Rental

When your Airbnb or short-term rental generates significant income, you're no longer just a homeowner renting out a spare room — you're running a business. And like any business, it deserves proper legal structure. Setting up an LLC for your short-term rental is one of the most impactful steps you can take to protect your personal assets, organize your finances, and operate professionally.

This guide walks through the complete LLC formation process for STR hosts: why to form one, which state to use, the step-by-step formation process, how to maintain it properly, and common mistakes to avoid.

For the complete framework on operating a compliant STR business, see our short-term rental management guide.

Why Form an LLC for Your Short-Term Rental?

Personal Asset Protection

The SBA explains the most important reason is liability protection. As an STR host, you face significant liability exposure — guest injuries, property damage claims, neighborhood disputes. Without an LLC, you operate as a sole proprietor, meaning there is no legal boundary between you and your business. A judgment against your rental business is a judgment against you personally. Your savings, home, and retirement accounts could all be at risk.

An LLC creates a legal firewall. Properly maintained, it means creditors and lawsuit claimants can only reach the assets held inside the LLC, not your personal assets.

Tax Flexibility

LLCs are "pass-through" entities by default — income and losses pass through to your personal tax return, avoiding the double taxation that corporations face. However, LLCs can elect to be taxed as S-corporations in some circumstances, potentially reducing self-employment taxes for high-income operators.

Additionally, operating through an LLC makes it easier to deduct business expenses, track income and expenses separately, and demonstrate business intent to the IRS — which matters for claiming depreciation and other property-related deductions.

Professional Credibility

An LLC signals to guests, lenders, property managers, and vendors that you're a serious operator. Opening a business bank account, obtaining a business credit card, and contracting with service providers all become cleaner when you have a legal entity.

Estate Planning and Transferability

An LLC structure makes it easier to transfer ownership of the rental property to heirs or business partners. Rather than transferring real property (which can trigger reassessments and costs), you can transfer LLC membership interests.

Is an LLC Right for Every STR Host?

An LLC is not universally necessary for every host in every situation. Consider the tradeoffs:

When an LLC makes strong sense:

  • You own the property free and clear, with significant equity to protect
  • You have multiple properties or plan to scale
  • You have other personal assets (home, savings, investments) worth protecting
  • Your rental generates significant income
  • You're concerned about liability exposure

When an LLC may be less critical:

  • The property has a large mortgage and minimal equity
  • You rent a single room in your primary residence and have minimal liability exposure
  • You already have robust STR liability insurance that covers your exposure
  • Formation and maintenance costs would significantly impact a low-margin operation

That said, for most serious STR investors and hosts, the cost of forming and maintaining an LLC is small relative to the protection it provides. Our rental property llc benefits guide explores the full cost-benefit picture.

Step 1: Choose Where to Form Your LLC

You can form an LLC in any U.S. state, but there are important factors to consider:

Option A: Form in the State Where the Property Is Located

For most STR hosts with a single property, forming the LLC in the same state as the property is the simplest and most cost-effective approach. You avoid the complexity of registering as a foreign LLC (which you'd need to do anyway if you form in a different state but operate in another).

Option B: Delaware, Wyoming, or Nevada

These states are popular for LLC formation due to:

  • Strong asset protection laws
  • No state income tax on LLCs without in-state business (Wyoming and Nevada)
  • High privacy standards (Wyoming allows anonymous LLCs)
  • Business-friendly court systems

However, if your property is in another state, you'll still need to register as a "foreign LLC" in the property's state, pay fees in both states, and file annual reports in both. This complexity is usually worth it only for sophisticated multi-property investors.

For most single-property STR hosts, forming in your property's state is the right choice.

Step 2: Choose Your LLC Name

Your LLC name must:

  • Be unique within your state (check availability on the Secretary of State's website)
  • Include "LLC," "L.L.C.," or "Limited Liability Company" in the name
  • Not include restricted words (like "bank," "insurance," or "attorney") unless you meet specific requirements

Tips for choosing a name:

  • Keep it simple and professional — "Sunset Beachside LLC" or "Mountain View Rentals LLC"
  • Check that the domain name is available if you plan to have a website
  • Consider whether the name will still make sense as you scale

Step 3: Choose a Registered Agent

Every LLC must have a registered agent — a person or company with a physical address in the state who agrees to receive legal documents and official government correspondence on behalf of your LLC.

You can serve as your own registered agent if you have a physical address in the state (a P.O. Box is not sufficient). However, many hosts prefer to use a professional registered agent service ($50–$150/year) because:

  • They ensure you receive all legal notices promptly
  • Your personal address is kept off public records
  • They handle the administrative burden

Step 4: File Articles of Organization

The Articles of Organization is the document that officially creates your LLC. It's filed with your state's Secretary of State office. Most states allow online filing.

Typical information required:

  • LLC name
  • Principal office address
  • Registered agent name and address
  • Member names (some states require this; others allow anonymous LLCs)
  • Management structure (member-managed vs. manager-managed)
  • Duration (perpetual is standard)
  • Purpose of the LLC (real estate investment and short-term rental operations)

Filing fees range from $50 to $500 depending on the state. Processing time ranges from same-day (online) to several weeks for paper filings.

Step 5: Create an Operating Agreement

The operating agreement is the internal governance document of your LLC. Even if your state doesn't legally require one (most don't), you absolutely should have one. It:

  • Defines ownership percentages and member rights
  • Establishes how profits and losses are distributed
  • Sets out decision-making procedures
  • Describes what happens when a member wants to leave or sell their interest
  • Establishes procedures for dissolving the LLC

Without an operating agreement, disputes between members (or with creditors) are resolved by your state's default LLC laws, which may not reflect your intentions.

For a single-member LLC, an operating agreement still matters: it reinforces the legitimacy of the LLC structure and strengthens the case against personal liability if you're ever sued.

Step 6: Obtain an EIN (Employer Identification Number)

An Employer Identification Number (EIN) is your LLC's tax ID number, issued by the IRS. You need an EIN to:

  • Open a business bank account
  • File business taxes
  • Hire employees or contractors
  • Obtain business credit

Applying for an EIN is free via the IRS EIN application. You'll receive your EIN immediately upon completing the online application.

Step 7: Open a Dedicated Business Bank Account

This is one of the most important steps and one that many LLC owners skip — to their detriment. A dedicated business bank account:

  • Maintains the legal separation between personal and LLC finances
  • Makes bookkeeping and tax preparation straightforward
  • Is required for LLC banking activity (you cannot deposit LLC income into a personal account without risking piercing the corporate veil)
  • Allows you to obtain a business debit card and eventually a business credit card

Bring your Articles of Organization, operating agreement, and EIN letter to the bank. Many banks offer free business checking accounts for small businesses.

Step 8: Transfer the Property Into the LLC

If you already own the property and are forming an LLC after the fact, you'll need to transfer ownership of the property from your personal name into the LLC by recording a new deed. This is called "deeding the property" into the LLC.

Important caveats before deeding:

  1. Due-on-sale clause: Most mortgages contain a due-on-sale clause that technically allows the lender to demand full repayment if you transfer the property. In practice, lenders rarely enforce this for transfers to single-member LLCs, but you should review your mortgage terms and consider consulting a real estate attorney.

  2. Title insurance: A transfer may affect your existing title insurance policy. Consult with your title company.

  3. Transfer taxes: Some states impose transfer or recordation taxes on property transfers, even to an LLC you own. Check your state's rules.

  4. Property tax reassessment: Some states trigger a reassessment when property ownership changes. California, for example, has strict rules around this.

A real estate attorney can guide you through the deed transfer process and help you navigate these issues.

Step 9: Obtain Business Licenses and Permits in the LLC's Name

Once your LLC is formed, apply for all required STR permits, business licenses, and tax registrations in the LLC's name. This ensures all regulatory compliance runs through the entity, maintaining clean records and legal consistency. See our business license short term rental guide for what's required in your market.

Step 10: Get the Right Insurance for Your LLC

Your insurance policy must be in the LLC's name to ensure proper coverage. An STR policy purchased in your personal name may not cover the LLC entity, and vice versa. Work with a licensed insurance broker experienced in STR properties to ensure your policy is correctly structured.

For a comprehensive look at coverage options, see our short-term rental insurance guide.

Maintaining Your LLC: Ongoing Requirements

Forming an LLC is just the beginning. Ongoing maintenance is what keeps the liability protection intact.

Annual Reports and Fees

Most states require LLCs to file an annual report and pay an annual fee (typically $50–$800/year). Missing this filing can result in administrative dissolution of your LLC, which eliminates your liability protection retroactively.

Keep Finances Strictly Separate

Never use the business bank account for personal expenses or vice versa. Every transaction should be categorized and documented. This is the single most important operational discipline for maintaining LLC protection.

Document Major Decisions

Keep records of significant business decisions — accepting a new booking policy, purchasing equipment, changing insurance. These records demonstrate that you're operating as a legitimate business, not just using the LLC as a shield.

Renew Licenses and Permits in the LLC's Name

When permits, business licenses, and registrations renew, ensure they're renewed in the LLC's name, not your personal name.

Common LLC Mistakes STR Hosts Make

  1. Forming the LLC but never opening a separate bank account — commingling funds defeats the purpose
  2. Using a personal credit card for business expenses — use a business card in the LLC's name
  3. Not having an operating agreement — leaves governance up to state default rules
  4. Missing annual report filings — can result in dissolution
  5. Deeding the property without checking mortgage due-on-sale clauses
  6. Getting insurance in the wrong entity name
  7. Mixing multiple properties in one LLC — a liability claim against one property can affect others; consider separate LLCs or a Series LLC

Summary and Key Takeaways

Setting up an LLC for your short-term rental is a foundational step for serious STR investors. Here's the complete process:

  1. Decide whether an LLC is right for your situation
  2. Choose your formation state (usually where the property is located)
  3. Select and check availability of your LLC name
  4. Designate a registered agent
  5. File Articles of Organization with the Secretary of State
  6. Draft an operating agreement
  7. Obtain an EIN from the IRS
  8. Open a dedicated business bank account
  9. Transfer the property deed into the LLC (with proper legal guidance)
  10. Obtain all permits and insurance in the LLC's name
  11. Maintain the LLC with annual filings, separate finances, and proper documentation

The cost of formation is typically $200–$1,000 depending on your state and whether you use professional services. The cost of not having an LLC in the event of a serious liability claim is exponentially higher.
