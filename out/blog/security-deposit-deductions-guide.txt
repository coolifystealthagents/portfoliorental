2a:T2470,<p>Security deposit deductions are one of the most frequent sources of landlord-tenant disputes. Tenants feel they left the property in good condition; landlords see damage beyond normal wear and tear. The difference between a smooth move-out and a small claims court date often comes down to documentation, clear standards, and following your state's rules precisely.</p>
<p>This guide explains what you can deduct, how to document it, and how to handle the process correctly. For a legal overview of the landlord-tenant framework governing deposits, see the <a href="https://www.law.cornell.edu/wex/landlord-tenant_law">Cornell Law Institute</a>.</p>
<h2 id="what-you-can-deduct">What You Can Deduct</h2>
<p>Most states allow security deposit deductions for:</p>
<h3 id="unpaid-rent">Unpaid Rent</h3>
<p>Any rent owed at the time of move-out, including the final month's rent if the tenant did not pay.</p>
<h3 id="damage-beyond-normal-wear-and-tear">Damage Beyond Normal Wear and Tear</h3>
<p>This is the most common deduction category and the most disputed. The key distinction:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Normal wear and tear</strong> — Gradual deterioration from ordinary use that you cannot charge for</li>
<li><strong>Damage</strong> — Loss or destruction caused by negligence, carelessness, abuse, or misuse that you can charge for</li>
</ul>
<table>
<thead>
<tr>
<th>Normal Wear and Tear</th>
<th>Tenant Damage</th>
</tr>
</thead>
<tbody>
<tr>
<td>Faded paint from sunlight</td>
<td>Crayon marks or large holes in walls</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Worn carpet from foot traffic</td>
<td>Stains, burns, or pet damage to carpet</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Loose door handles from use</td>
<td>Broken door from forced entry</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Minor scuffs on hardwood</td>
<td>Deep gouges or water damage on floors</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Fading of countertops</td>
<td>Burns or cuts in countertops</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Dusty blinds</td>
<td>Broken or missing blinds</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>Nail holes from normal picture hanging</td>
<td>Large anchor holes or damaged drywall</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>
<h3 id="cleaning-costs">Cleaning Costs</h3>
<p>You can deduct cleaning costs if the tenant left the unit dirtier than when they moved in, beyond normal cleaning expectations. Deductions are appropriate for:</p>
<ul>
<li>Heavy grease buildup in kitchen</li>
<li>Mold or mildew from poor maintenance (the <a href="https://www.epa.gov/lead">EPA lead and toxics resources</a> cover related environmental hazards in housing)</li>
<li>Heavily soiled carpets requiring professional cleaning</li>
<li>Trash or debris left behind</li>
<li>Bathroom requiring deep cleaning beyond normal turnover</li>
</ul>
<p>You generally cannot deduct for routine turnover cleaning that you would perform between any tenants.</p>
<h3 id="lease-violation-charges">Lease Violation Charges</h3>
<p>If your lease specifies charges for certain violations and your state allows it:</p>
<ul>
<li>Unauthorized pet fees or damage</li>
<li>Early termination fees (where legally permitted)</li>
<li>Key replacement costs</li>
<li>Charges for removing unauthorized fixtures or modifications</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="utility-charges">Utility Charges</h3>
<p>Unpaid utility balances that the tenant was responsible for under the lease.</p>
<h2 id="documentation-requirements">Documentation Requirements</h2>
<h3 id="move-in-inspection">Move-In Inspection</h3>
<p>Your documentation starts at move-in, not move-out:</p>
<ul>
<li>Conduct a thorough move-in inspection with the tenant present</li>
<li>Photograph or video every room, wall, floor, appliance, and fixture</li>
<li>Note existing damage on a signed move-in checklist</li>
<li>Both parties should sign and date the inspection report</li>
<li>Provide the tenant with a copy</li>
</ul>
<p>Without a move-in inspection, proving that damage occurred during the tenancy becomes much harder.</p>
<h3 id="move-out-inspection">Move-Out Inspection</h3>
<p>At move-out:</p>
<ul>
<li>Conduct the inspection with the tenant present if possible (some states require this)</li>
<li>Photograph and video the same areas documented at move-in</li>
<li>Compare conditions side by side</li>
<li>Note any damage beyond normal wear and tear</li>
<li>Document cleaning needs</li>
<li>Record any items left behind</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="repair-documentation">Repair Documentation</h3>
<p>For each deduction, maintain:</p>
<ul>
<li>Dated photographs showing the damage</li>
<li>Contractor or vendor invoices for repairs</li>
<li>Receipts for materials if you did the work yourself</li>
<li>Comparison photos from move-in showing prior condition</li>
<li>Written description of the damage and repair</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="the-itemized-statement">The Itemized Statement</h2>
<p>Most states require landlords to provide an itemized statement of deductions when returning the security deposit. Your statement should include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Each deduction listed separately with a description</li>
<li>The amount charged for each item</li>
<li>Copies of invoices or receipts (required in some states)</li>
<li>The total amount deducted</li>
<li>The remaining deposit balance being returned</li>
<li>A check for the balance owed to the tenant</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="state-return-deadlines">State Return Deadlines</h3>
<p>Deadlines for returning the deposit and itemized statement vary by state:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>14 days</strong> — Hawaii, Montana, Washington</li>
<li><strong>21 days</strong> — California</li>
<li><strong>30 days</strong> — Common in many states (Colorado, Florida, Illinois, Texas, and others)</li>
<li><strong>45-60 days</strong> — A few states allow longer periods</li>
</ul>
<p>Missing the deadline can result in forfeiture of your right to deduct, penalty fees, or liability for the full deposit amount plus damages.</p>
<p>Check your <a href="/blog/security-deposit-laws-by-state">state's security deposit laws</a> for specific deadlines.</p>
<h2 id="common-deduction-disputes">Common Deduction Disputes</h2>
<h3 id="painting">Painting</h3>
<ul>
<li>You can charge for painting if the tenant damaged walls beyond normal wear (crayon, markers, excessive holes, unauthorized paint colors)</li>
<li>You generally cannot charge for repainting that would have been needed anyway due to age</li>
<li>Some states use a depreciation model — if the paint was already 3 years old and has a 5-year life, you can only charge for the remaining 2 years of useful life</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="carpet">Carpet</h3>
<ul>
<li>Pet stains, burns, and tears are deductible — for more on documenting and recovering costs, see our <a href="/blog/property-damage-by-tenant">property damage by tenant guide</a></li>
<li>Normal traffic patterns and fading are not</li>
<li>Apply depreciation — if carpet has a 10-year life and the tenant lived there for 7 years, you can only charge 30% of replacement cost</li>
<li>Professional cleaning costs are often deductible if the tenant left carpets excessively dirty</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="appliances">Appliances</h3>
<ul>
<li>Damage from misuse is deductible</li>
<li>Mechanical failure from age and normal use is not</li>
<li>Missing parts or accessories are deductible</li>
<li>Normal wear on surfaces is not</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="cleaning">Cleaning</h3>
<ul>
<li>Deep cleaning beyond normal turnover standards is deductible</li>
<li>Basic turnover cleaning that you would do regardless is generally not</li>
<li>Document the condition with photos to support cleaning charges</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="avoiding-disputes">Avoiding Disputes</h2>
<h3 id="set-clear-expectations">Set Clear Expectations</h3>
<ul>
<li>Provide a <a href="/blog/move-out-letter-tenant">move-out letter to tenants</a> before they leave</li>
<li>Explain the move-out inspection process in advance</li>
<li>Define your cleaning standards in the lease</li>
<li>Include specific examples of normal wear vs damage</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="use-the-pre-move-out-inspection">Use the Pre-Move-Out Inspection</h3>
<p>Some states require or allow a pre-move-out inspection where you identify issues the tenant can fix before the final move-out. This reduces disputes and helps tenants get their full deposit back.</p>
<h3 id="be-reasonable-and-fair">Be Reasonable and Fair</h3>
<p>Courts scrutinize landlords who deduct aggressively. <a href="https://www.nolo.com/legal-encyclopedia/landlord-tenant-law">Nolo's landlord-tenant guide</a> provides state-specific guidance on what courts consider reasonable. Reasonable deductions for genuine damage are upheld; excessive charges for minor issues or normal wear are not. When in doubt, err on the side of returning more of the deposit.</p>
<h2 id="key-takeaways">Key Takeaways</h2>
<p>Security deposit deductions must be documented, itemized, and returned within your state's deadline. Start documentation at move-in with thorough photos and a signed checklist. At move-out, compare conditions, photograph damage, get repair estimates, and provide a detailed itemized statement. Follow your state's specific rules precisely — procedural errors can cost you the entire deposit regardless of the damage.</p>
<p>For the complete framework, see our <a href="/blog/security-deposit-laws-by-state">security deposit laws guide</a> and <a href="/blog/security-deposit-return-timeline">security deposit return timelines</a>.</p>
