Setting Credit Score Requirements for Renters

Credit score requirements for renters are one of the most consequential — and most contested — aspects of the tenant screening process. Set your minimum too high and you eliminate otherwise qualified applicants; set it too low and you increase your risk of non-payment. Apply your standards inconsistently and you expose yourself to fair housing liability.

This guide explains what credit scores actually measure, how to set minimum thresholds that reflect your specific rental market and property risk profile, what to do with applicants who fall below your minimum, and how to read a credit report like an experienced landlord — not just a number.

What a Credit Score Actually Tells You

Credit scores are generated by three major bureaus — Experian, TransUnion, and Equifax — using proprietary scoring models. The most widely used is the FICO score. Scores range from 300 to 850.

A credit score is a snapshot of how a person has managed debt obligations. It reflects:

  • Payment history (the largest factor, roughly 35% of the FICO score)
  • Amounts owed relative to available credit (about 30%)
  • Length of credit history (about 15%)
  • Credit mix and new credit inquiries (roughly 20% combined)

What a credit score does not directly tell you is whether someone will pay rent. Rent payments are generally not reported to credit bureaus unless a landlord uses a rent reporting service. This means a tenant can have a strong credit score and a poor rent payment history, or a mediocre score and an excellent history of paying rent on time for years.

This is why the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau (CFPB) emphasizes that credit reports should be read in full — not evaluated solely by score — when making decisions that affect housing access.

Credit scores are one input in a screening process, not a substitute for one.

How to Set Minimum Credit Score Requirements

There is no universal minimum credit score for renting an apartment. Industry practice varies by market, property type, and landlord preference. Common thresholds:

  • Below 580: Typically declined without additional documentation or mitigation
  • 580–619: Often considered with conditions (higher deposit, co-signer, or prepaid rent)
  • 620–659: Acceptable to most landlords, sometimes with conditions
  • 660–699: Good; accepted by most landlords without conditions
  • 700+: Strong; accepted broadly

Factors That Should Influence Your Threshold

Property price point: Landlords renting luxury units at high price points often set higher minimums (680+) because the financial exposure from non-payment is greater. Landlords renting affordable housing often set lower minimums or use alternative criteria.

Local market conditions: In a tight rental market with high demand, you can afford to be selective. In a soft market with high vacancy, overly restrictive credit standards will extend vacancy periods and cost you more in lost rent than a slightly riskier tenant would.

Your risk tolerance and cash reserves: A landlord with a reserve fund and professional property management can absorb more screening variability. A landlord relying on a single property for income may reasonably want higher standards.

Property class: Class A properties attract tenants who expect rigorous screening. Class C properties often serve tenants with limited credit histories who are otherwise reliable.

Write your credit minimums into a formal screening criteria document. This is not optional — it is your legal protection if a denied applicant files a fair housing complaint. See our overview of tenant screening best practices for guidance on what that document should include.

How to Read a Tenant Credit Report

When you run a credit check through a tenant screening service, you receive more than a score. Here is what to look for in the full report.

Payment History

Look for late payments and their severity. A payment marked 30 days late is far less significant than one marked 90+ days late or in collections. A single late payment from three years ago on a medical bill tells a different story than a pattern of late payments across multiple accounts in the past 12 months.

Focus on recent history. A poor credit record from five or more years ago that has been replaced by clean payment history suggests the applicant has corrected their behavior. A recent pattern of delinquencies is a meaningful red flag.

Collections and Charge-Offs

Pay particular attention to any collections from landlords or utility companies. These are directly relevant to how a tenant manages housing-related financial obligations. A collection from a prior landlord or an unpaid utility balance is more predictive of rental risk than a medical collection from an unexpected hospital bill.

Public Records

Bankruptcy filings, civil judgments, and prior evictions (where reported) appear in the public records section. A prior eviction judgment is one of the most significant items on a rental credit report. However, understand its context: an eviction from ten years ago that predates a long run of stable rental history is different from an eviction within the last two years.

Credit Utilization and Account Age

High utilization (over 80% of available revolving credit used) can suggest financial stress. A thin credit file — few accounts, short history — often means the applicant is young or is an immigrant with no U.S. credit history, not that they are a bad tenant.

Checking for Errors

Credit reports contain errors more often than most people realize. If an applicant disputes an item on their report, do not immediately dismiss the application. Ask them to provide documentation and review the disputed item in context.

For more information on credit reporting standards and consumer rights, see the general resources on credit at Experian's credit education center and TransUnion's credit learning resources.

Legal Considerations When Using Credit in Tenant Screening

Using credit reports for tenant screening is governed by the Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA). Key obligations for landlords include:

  • Permissible purpose: You must have a permissible purpose (evaluating a rental application) to pull a credit report. Always obtain written consent from the applicant first.
  • Adverse action notice: If you deny an application or take adverse action (such as requiring a larger deposit) based on information in a credit report, you are required to provide an adverse action notice. This notice must identify the credit reporting agency used, inform the applicant they can get a free copy of the report, and advise them of their right to dispute.
  • Consistent application: Apply your credit standards uniformly. Requiring a credit check from some applicants but not others, or applying different standards based on protected class, is a fair housing violation.

Our guide on fair housing compliance for rental properties covers the federal and state requirements that intersect with credit screening.

Handling Applicants with Low or No Credit

A rigid minimum credit score policy eliminates applicants who may be perfectly capable of paying rent. Landlords who refuse to consider context often pass on reliable tenants while accepting less reliable ones who happen to have better-looking scores.

Here are practical ways to evaluate applicants who fall below your credit minimum:

Require Higher Income Verification

If an applicant has a 590 credit score but earns 5x the monthly rent in verifiable income, their ability to pay is clear. Consider accepting them with documentation of that income verified through pay stubs, bank statements, or tax returns.

Accept a Larger Security Deposit

Where state law permits (many states cap deposits at one or two months' rent), a larger deposit reduces your financial exposure if the tenant fails to pay.

Accept a Co-Signer or Guarantor

A creditworthy co-signer — typically a parent, relative, or employer — assumes financial responsibility if the primary tenant defaults. Verify the co-signer's credit and income as rigorously as any applicant.

Request Prepaid Rent

Some landlords accept one to three months of prepaid rent in exchange for waiving or reducing the credit requirement. Check your state law — some jurisdictions limit prepaid rent amounts.

Verify Rental History Directly

Contact prior landlords by phone (not just email — email inquiries are easy to ignore or manipulate). Ask specifically: Did this tenant pay rent on time? Would you rent to them again? A strong reference from a prior landlord can offset a mediocre credit score.

These accommodations should be documented as part of your screening policy so they are available consistently to all applicants who fall below your minimum — not offered selectively in a way that could appear discriminatory.

For specific guidance on rent collection practices that protect you financially once a tenant is in place, review our guide to rent collection best practices.

Building a Credit Screening Policy That Holds Up

A legally defensible credit screening policy has several characteristics:

  1. It is in writing: Document the minimum credit score, the score range that triggers conditional approval, and the factors you consider for applicants below the minimum.
  2. It is disclosed upfront: Provide your screening criteria to applicants before they pay an application fee. Several states require this by law, and it is good practice everywhere.
  3. It is applied uniformly: Use the same standards for every applicant. Document how each application was evaluated and why decisions were made.
  4. It accounts for the full picture: The policy acknowledges that a credit score is one factor among several, including income, rental history, references, and background.
  5. It includes adverse action procedures: Your policy should specify what happens when you deny an applicant based on credit, including the required notice.

For a broader view of managing tenants from screening through move-out, see our tenant management best practices guide.

Conclusion

Credit score requirements for renters are a useful starting point in screening, but they work best as one component of a complete evaluation process. A 700 credit score does not guarantee a great tenant, and a 590 does not guarantee a bad one.

Set your minimums based on your market, property type, and risk tolerance. Read credit reports in full rather than relying solely on scores. Build in alternative pathways for applicants who fall below your minimum but have other strong indicators. Document everything and apply your standards consistently.

The landlords who have the fewest screening problems are not necessarily those with the highest minimums — they are the ones with the most systematic and consistent processes. A well-designed credit screening policy, combined with thorough income verification and rental history checks, will produce better outcomes than any single number alone.
