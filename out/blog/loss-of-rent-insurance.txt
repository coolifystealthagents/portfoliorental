27:T22f2,<p>When a covered event — fire, storm, burst pipe — makes your rental unit uninhabitable, you lose rental income for every month the unit is vacant during repairs. Loss-of-rent insurance (also called fair rental value coverage or rental income insurance) replaces that lost income, keeping your cash flow intact while the property is restored.</p>
<p>This guide explains how the coverage works, how to file claims, and how to choose the right coverage level.</p>
<h2 id="what-is-loss-of-rent-insurance">What Is Loss-of-Rent Insurance?</h2>
<p>Loss-of-rent insurance is a component of most landlord insurance policies that reimburses you for rental income lost when a covered peril makes a unit uninhabitable. It bridges the income gap between the date the unit becomes uninhabitable and the date it is restored and re-rented.</p>
<h3 id="how-it-works">How It Works</h3>
<ol>
<li>A covered event damages the property (fire, storm, vandalism, etc.)</li>
<li>The unit becomes uninhabitable and the tenant must vacate</li>
<li>You file a claim with your insurance company</li>
<li>The insurer pays your lost rental income during the repair period</li>
<li>Coverage continues until the property is repaired and reasonably ready to rent</li>
</ol>
<h3 id="coverage-triggers">Coverage Triggers</h3>
<p>Loss-of-rent coverage typically activates when:</p>
<ul>
<li>A covered peril under your landlord policy causes the damage</li>
<li>The damage makes the unit legally or practically uninhabitable</li>
<li>The tenant vacates or cannot occupy the unit</li>
<li>Repairs are underway within a reasonable timeframe</li>
</ul>
<p>Coverage does not activate for:</p>
<ul>
<li>Voluntary vacancy (you chose not to rent)</li>
<li>Damage from excluded perils (flooding, earthquake — see <a href="/blog/flood-insurance-rental-property">flood insurance for rentals</a> for separate coverage options)</li>
<li>Tenant-caused damage that your landlord policy excludes</li>
<li>Normal wear and maintenance closures</li>
<li>Market vacancy (no tenants despite availability)</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="what-it-covers">What It Covers</h2>
<h3 id="fair-rental-value">Fair Rental Value</h3>
<p>Most policies pay the fair rental value of the unit — the rent you would have received minus any expenses you no longer incur because the unit is vacant (such as tenant-related utilities if the landlord pays them).</p>
<h3 id="duration">Duration</h3>
<p>Coverage lasts for the period reasonably necessary to repair the property, up to the policy limit. Common limits are:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>12 months</strong> of lost rent (most standard policies)</li>
<li><strong>24 months</strong> for some premium policies</li>
<li>A <strong>dollar cap</strong> instead of or in addition to a time limit</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="additional-expenses">Additional Expenses</h3>
<p>Some policies also cover reasonable additional expenses you incur because of the loss, such as:</p>
<ul>
<li>Advertising costs to find new tenants after repairs</li>
<li>Relocation assistance provided to displaced tenants</li>
<li>Temporary housing costs if required by your lease or local law (see <a href="https://www.hud.gov/topics/rental_assistance">HUD rental assistance</a> programs for tenant resources)</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="how-to-file-a-loss-of-rent-claim">How to File a Loss-of-Rent Claim</h2>
<h3 id="step-1-document-the-damage">Step 1: Document the Damage</h3>
<p>Immediately after the event:</p>
<ul>
<li>Photograph and video the damage extensively</li>
<li>Secure the property to prevent further damage</li>
<li>Contact your insurance company to report the claim</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="step-2-establish-the-rental-income">Step 2: Establish the Rental Income</h3>
<p>Provide your insurer with documentation of the rental income you are losing:</p>
<ul>
<li>Current lease agreement showing rent amount</li>
<li>Rent payment history (bank statements, ledger records)</li>
<li>If between tenants, evidence of the unit's fair rental value (market comparables)</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="step-3-cooperate-with-adjusters">Step 3: Cooperate With Adjusters</h3>
<p>Your insurer will send an adjuster to assess:</p>
<ul>
<li>The extent of damage</li>
<li>The estimated repair timeline</li>
<li>Whether the unit is truly uninhabitable</li>
<li>The fair rental value to be reimbursed</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="step-4-begin-repairs-promptly">Step 4: Begin Repairs Promptly</h3>
<p>Your duty to mitigate applies to insurance claims. You must take reasonable steps to repair the property promptly. Unnecessary delays in beginning or completing repairs can reduce your claim:</p>
<ul>
<li>Get repair estimates quickly</li>
<li>Authorize emergency repairs to prevent further damage</li>
<li>Select contractors and begin work within a reasonable timeframe</li>
<li>Document the repair timeline and any unavoidable delays</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="step-5-submit-monthly-claims">Step 5: Submit Monthly Claims</h3>
<p>Most insurers pay loss-of-rent claims on a monthly basis during the repair period. Submit:</p>
<ul>
<li>Monthly invoices for the fair rental value amount</li>
<li>Progress updates on repairs</li>
<li>Contractor timelines and any delay explanations</li>
<li>Documentation of any additional expenses</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="choosing-the-right-coverage-level">Choosing the Right Coverage Level</h2>
<h3 id="calculate-your-exposure">Calculate Your Exposure</h3>
<p>Determine how much income you could lose from your highest-risk property:</p>
<ul>
<li>Monthly rent × 12 months = maximum annual exposure per unit</li>
<li>Factor in the typical repair timeline for major damage (fire: 6-12 months, water damage: 2-6 months, storm damage: 3-6 months)</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="coverage-recommendations">Coverage Recommendations</h3>
<table>
<thead>
<tr>
<th>Portfolio Size</th>
<th>Minimum Loss-of-Rent Coverage</th>
</tr>
</thead>
<tbody>
<tr>
<td>Single unit</td>
<td>12 months of rent</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>2-5 units</td>
<td>12 months of total rent across all units</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td>5+ units</td>
<td>Consider a portfolio policy with adequate aggregate limits</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>
<h3 id="policy-limit-types">Policy Limit Types</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Stated amount</strong> — A fixed dollar amount (e.g., $30,000)</li>
<li><strong>Percentage of dwelling value</strong> — Often 10-20% of the insured dwelling value</li>
<li><strong>Time-based</strong> — 12 or 24 months of fair rental value</li>
</ul>
<p>Ensure your limit is sufficient for a worst-case scenario, not just a minor repair. Pairing this coverage with a <a href="/blog/rental-property-reserve-fund-strategies">rental property reserve fund</a> provides an additional buffer during extended vacancies.</p>
<h2 id="common-claim-pitfalls">Common Claim Pitfalls</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Insufficient documentation</strong> — Without a lease and payment history, proving your rental income is difficult</li>
<li><strong>Delayed repairs</strong> — Insurers can reduce claims if you did not begin repairs promptly</li>
<li><strong>Underinsurance</strong> — If your loss-of-rent limit is too low, you absorb the difference</li>
<li><strong>Excluded perils</strong> — Damage from flood, earthquake, or other excluded events does not trigger loss-of-rent coverage unless you have separate coverage. Consider <a href="/blog/umbrella-insurance-landlord">umbrella insurance</a> to extend your overall protection</li>
<li><strong>Failure to mitigate</strong> — You must take reasonable steps to restore the property and find new tenants</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="loss-of-rent-vs-rent-guarantee-insurance">Loss of Rent vs Rent Guarantee Insurance</h2>
<p>Loss-of-rent insurance and rent guarantee insurance cover different risks:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Loss-of-rent insurance</strong> covers income lost when the property is physically uninhabitable due to a covered peril</li>
<li><strong>Rent guarantee insurance</strong> covers income lost when a tenant stops paying rent (default/eviction)</li>
</ul>
<p>Both can be valuable, but they address different scenarios. Most landlord policies include loss-of-rent but not rent guarantee coverage.</p>
<h2 id="key-takeaways">Key Takeaways</h2>
<p>Loss-of-rent insurance is a critical component of your landlord insurance that replaces income during covered repair periods. The IRS treats reimbursed rental income as taxable; see <a href="https://www.irs.gov/publications/p527">IRS Publication 527</a> for reporting requirements. Verify your policy includes adequate coverage, document your rental income thoroughly, begin repairs promptly after a loss, and file claims on a regular schedule during the repair period. The cost of this coverage is minimal compared to the financial impact of months without rental income.</p>
<p>For the complete insurance framework, see our <a href="/blog/landlord-insurance-requirements">landlord insurance requirements guide</a> and <a href="/blog/umbrella-insurance-landlord">umbrella insurance guide</a>.</p>
