Lead Paint Disclosure Requirements for Rental Properties

If your rental property was built before 1978, federal law requires you to disclose the potential presence of lead-based paint to every tenant before they sign a lease. This is not optional, and the penalties for noncompliance are severe — up to $19,507 per violation as of 2026, plus potential criminal penalties and civil liability.

This guide explains what the law requires, how to comply, and how to protect your tenants and your business.

Why Lead Paint Matters

Lead-based paint was commonly used in residential buildings until it was banned for residential use in 1978. When lead paint deteriorates, it creates dust and chips that can be ingested or inhaled, causing serious health problems:

  • Children under 6 are most vulnerable — lead exposure causes developmental delays, learning disabilities, and behavioral problems
  • Adults can experience high blood pressure, kidney damage, and reproductive issues
  • Pregnant women face risks of miscarriage and harm to fetal development

The EPA estimates that approximately 87% of homes built before 1940 contain lead-based paint, and 24% of homes built between 1960 and 1977 contain it.

Federal Disclosure Requirements

Which Properties Are Covered

The lead paint disclosure rule applies to most housing built before 1978, including:

  • Single-family homes
  • Apartments and multi-family buildings
  • Condominiums
  • Mobile homes built before 1978

Exemptions

A few property types are exempt:

  • Housing built after January 1, 1978
  • Zero-bedroom units (studios, dormitories, SROs)
  • Housing for the elderly or disabled (unless children live there)
  • Short-term rentals of 100 days or less
  • Housing that has been certified lead-free by an accredited inspector

What You Must Disclose

Before a tenant signs a lease, you must:

  1. Provide the EPA pamphlet — "Protect Your Family From Lead in Your Home" must be given to every tenant. The current version is available from the EPA website.

  2. Disclose known lead hazards — If you know about lead paint or lead hazards in the property, you must inform the tenant. This includes:

    • Results of any lead inspections or risk assessments
    • Known locations of lead paint
    • Any lead hazard reduction activities performed
    • The condition of painted surfaces

  3. Provide the Lead Paint Disclosure Form — Both you and the tenant must sign a disclosure form that:

    • Confirms the tenant received the EPA pamphlet
    • Documents your disclosure of known lead paint information
    • Records whether lead inspections have been done
    • Acknowledges the tenant's rights

  4. Include disclosure language in the lease — Your lease must contain a lead warning statement and confirmation that disclosure requirements were met.

Tenant's Right to Test

You must allow tenants a 10-day period to conduct a lead paint inspection or risk assessment at their own expense before signing the lease. You can agree to a different timeframe, but the tenant cannot waive this right.

Compliance Steps

Step 1: Determine If Your Property Is Covered

Check when the property was built. If it was built before 1978, the disclosure requirements apply unless an exemption applies.

Step 2: Gather Known Information

Compile any information you have about lead paint in the property:

  • Previous inspection reports
  • Abatement or remediation records
  • Contractor reports noting lead paint
  • Disclosure forms from previous owners

You are not required to conduct a lead inspection. You are required to disclose what you know.

Step 3: Prepare Disclosure Documents

For each new tenant or lease renewal, prepare:

  • A copy of the EPA pamphlet
  • The lead paint disclosure form (EPA provides standard forms)
  • Any available lead inspection reports
  • Lease language containing the required warning

Step 4: Complete Disclosure Before Lease Signing

Present all documents to the tenant before they commit to the lease. The tenant should have time to review the information and exercise their right to test.

Step 5: Collect Signatures and Retain Records

Both parties sign the disclosure form. Retain signed copies for at least three years. This three-year retention requirement applies even after the tenant moves out.

Renovation and Repair Obligations

The RRP Rule

If you renovate, repair, or paint a pre-1978 property, the EPA's Renovation, Repair, and Painting (RRP) Rule requires that work be performed by an EPA-certified renovator using lead-safe work practices. This applies to:

  • Any renovation disturbing more than 6 square feet of interior painted surface per room
  • Any renovation disturbing more than 20 square feet of exterior painted surface
  • Window replacement in pre-1978 buildings

What This Means for Landlords

  • Hire only EPA-certified contractors for renovation work in pre-1978 properties
  • Ensure contractors use lead-safe work practices (containment, HEPA vacuuming, wet methods)
  • Obtain written confirmation of lead-safe practices from contractors
  • Provide tenants with the EPA pre-renovation disclosure form before work begins

State and Local Requirements

Many states have additional lead paint requirements beyond federal law:

  • Testing requirements — Some states require lead testing before renting pre-1978 housing (Massachusetts, Rhode Island, and others)
  • Abatement requirements — Some jurisdictions require landlords to remove or stabilize lead paint, not just disclose it
  • Reporting requirements — Some states require reporting elevated blood lead levels found in tenants or their children
  • Stricter penalties — State penalties may exceed federal penalties

Check your state and local regulations in addition to federal requirements. See our landlord-tenant law overview for state-specific resources.

Penalties for Noncompliance

Civil Penalties

  • Up to $19,507 per violation for failure to disclose (adjusted periodically for inflation)
  • Each lease or renewal without proper disclosure is a separate violation

Criminal Penalties

  • Knowing violations can result in criminal prosecution
  • Penalties include fines and potential imprisonment

Civil Liability

  • Tenants can sue for damages caused by lead exposure
  • Failure to disclose known lead hazards strengthens tenant claims
  • Damages can include medical costs, remediation costs, relocation expenses, and pain and suffering — robust landlord liability protection strategies should account for this exposure in pre-1978 properties

Triple Damages

Under the Residential Lead-Based Paint Hazard Reduction Act, tenants may recover up to three times their actual damages for landlord violations.

Managing Lead Paint Risk

Property Condition Monitoring

For pre-1978 properties, regularly inspect painted surfaces for deterioration as part of your overall property inspection requirements:

  • Peeling, chipping, or flaking paint
  • Deteriorating window sills and frames
  • Damaged door frames and trim
  • Friction surfaces (windows, doors) where paint wears
  • Impact surfaces (walls near doors, baseboards)

Address deteriorating paint promptly using lead-safe practices.

Documentation Best Practices

  • Keep all disclosure forms for a minimum of three years
  • Maintain a property file with all lead-related inspection and remediation records
  • Document the condition of painted surfaces during routine inspections
  • Photograph areas of concern during inspections

Key Takeaways

Lead paint disclosure is a mandatory federal requirement for all pre-1978 rental properties. The process is straightforward: provide the EPA pamphlet, disclose what you know, sign the form, and keep records for three years. The penalties for skipping this step are steep, and the liability for lead exposure in a unit where you failed to disclose can be devastating. Make lead disclosure a standard part of your leasing process and address deteriorating paint promptly.
