Handling Water Damage in a Rental Property

Water damage is one of the most common and costly emergencies a rental property owner faces. Whether it comes from a burst pipe, a roof leak during a storm, a washing machine supply line failure, or a sewage backup, water damage requires immediate, decisive action. Every hour of delay increases the damage, the remediation cost, and the risk of mold growth.

This guide walks landlords through the complete water damage response process — from the first emergency call to insurance claim filing, tenant communication, and prevention of future incidents.

Common Sources of Water Damage in Rental Properties

Understanding where water damage originates helps in both response and prevention:

  • Burst or leaking pipes — Especially common in winter (frozen pipes) or in older properties with aging supply lines
  • Roof leaks — From damaged shingles, failed flashing, or ice dams
  • Appliance failures — Washing machine supply hoses, dishwasher drain hoses, water heater failures
  • HVAC condensate overflow — Clogged condensate drain lines cause air handlers to overflow
  • Sewage backups — Blocked main drains or municipal sewer backups can cause raw sewage to enter the unit
  • Exterior flooding — Heavy rain, storm surge, or poor drainage directing water into basements
  • Sprinkler system malfunctions — Fire suppression systems that activate accidentally

Immediate Emergency Response (First 24 Hours)

The first 24 hours after water damage are the most critical for minimizing total damage and mold risk.

Step 1: Stop the Water Source

As quickly as possible:

  • Shut off the water supply if the source is a plumbing failure (main shut-off or individual fixture shut-off)
  • Call a roofing contractor for emergency roof tarping if the source is a roof failure
  • Contact your HVAC technician if the source is HVAC-related
  • Contact your plumber immediately for sewage backups — do not attempt to clean up sewage without professional guidance

Step 2: Assess Safety

Before entering a flooded area:

  • Do not enter if electrical outlets, panels, or equipment are in the flooded area — turn off circuit breakers serving the affected area first
  • Do not enter if there is any possibility of structural compromise
  • Use flashlights rather than turning on lights if electrical safety is uncertain
  • For sewage backups, wear rubber gloves, boots, and eye protection — the EPA water safety guidelines classify raw sewage as a serious health hazard

Step 3: Document Before Cleanup

Before moving anything or starting cleanup:

  • Photograph and video the entire affected area
  • Document the water level, all affected materials (flooring, walls, furniture, appliances)
  • Note what items are damaged and their approximate condition

This documentation is essential for insurance claims.

Step 4: Begin Water Extraction

Time is critical. The longer water sits, the deeper it penetrates and the higher the mold risk:

  • Use a wet/dry vacuum to remove standing water from hard surfaces
  • Call a professional water damage restoration company (SERVPRO, ServiceMaster, or local equivalent) for significant flooding
  • Begin moving portable fans and dehumidifiers into the affected area to accelerate drying

Professional restoration companies have industrial extraction equipment and moisture meters that are far more effective than consumer equipment for significant water events. Many operate 24/7 for emergency response.

Step 5: Remove Wet Materials

  • Remove wet carpet and padding — these cannot be dried in place and must be removed within 24-48 hours to prevent mold
  • Remove wet insulation from within walls if water has penetrated wall cavities
  • Furniture should be moved out of the wet area and assessed separately
  • Do not place wet furniture on hardwood floors — the moisture will transfer

Tenant Communication During Water Damage

Water damage directly affects your tenant's living situation. Communicate proactively:

Immediate Communication

Contact the tenant (by phone and in writing) to:

  • Explain the situation and what you are doing to address it
  • Provide any safety instructions (do not use certain areas, do not touch electrical, etc.)
  • Give an initial timeline estimate for restoration
  • Advise them to document any personal property damage for their renter's insurance claim

Relocation

If the unit is uninhabitable during remediation:

  • Determine whether your property falls under a policy that provides alternative housing coverage
  • Provide a rent abatement or credit for any period the unit is uninhabitable
  • Some states require landlords to provide temporary housing; others allow rent withholding by the tenant until habitability is restored
  • Communicate the expected restoration timeline in writing

Written Record

Every communication about water damage should be documented in writing. Create a file for the incident including dates, times, and content of all conversations and messages.

Structural Drying and Remediation

Professional restoration contractors use a science-based approach to structural drying:

Moisture Mapping

Industrial moisture meters measure moisture content in walls, floors, and ceilings. This allows technicians to:

  • Identify all affected areas (not just visible damage)
  • Set drying goals and monitor progress daily
  • Determine when materials have reached acceptable moisture levels

Equipment-Based Drying

Commercial drying typically involves:

  • Air movers — High-velocity fans that accelerate evaporation from surfaces
  • Dehumidifiers — Commercial dehumidifiers remove moisture from the air as it evaporates
  • Drying mats and injection drying systems — Allow drying of flooring and wall cavities without removing materials

Drying Duration

Most residential water damage requires 3-5 days of active drying equipment to reach acceptable moisture levels. This varies based on the extent of damage, materials affected, and ambient conditions.

Mold Prevention During Water Damage Response

Mold can begin growing within 24-48 hours in wet conditions. Preventive steps during water damage response:

  • Start drying equipment immediately — every hour counts
  • Apply antimicrobial treatment to all affected surfaces after water extraction
  • Remove wet porous materials (carpet, insulation, drywall) within 24-48 hours
  • Keep HVAC running to maintain air circulation during drying (unless HVAC system itself was affected)
  • Test for mold approximately 1-2 weeks after drying is complete; the CDC mold cleanup guide covers safe remediation practices

For detailed mold response guidance, see our article on mold in rental properties.

Scope of Repairs After Water Damage

Once structural drying is complete, repairs typically include:

  • Flooring replacement — Wet carpet, padding, hardwood, and laminate typically require replacement
  • Drywall replacement — Water-damaged drywall that was wet more than 24-48 hours usually needs replacement
  • Insulation replacement — Wet insulation must be replaced; it loses R-value permanently when wet
  • Painting — All affected areas require repainting after repairs
  • Cabinetry and millwork — Assess on a case-by-case basis; solid wood may dry successfully; particleboard typically swells and must be replaced
  • Appliances — Any appliances exposed to flooding should be inspected by a qualified technician before being returned to use

Filing a Landlord Insurance Claim

Most landlord insurance policies (also called dwelling fire policies or landlord property insurance) cover sudden and accidental water damage. Common exclusions include:

  • Flooding from external water — Requires separate NFIP flood insurance or private policy
  • Gradual leaks — Damage from slow, ongoing leaks that the insured knew or should have known about
  • Sewer backup — Often excluded from standard policies; sewer backup coverage is typically an endorsement

Filing Process

  1. Call your insurance agent immediately after securing the property and documenting damage
  2. Request an adjuster visit — Do not complete significant repairs before the adjuster has assessed the damage
  3. Provide your documentation — Photos, videos, moisture reports, contractor estimates
  4. Get multiple contractor bids — While most policies allow you to use any licensed contractor, competitive bids support your claim and ensure fair pricing
  5. Maintain a repair log — Document all expenses, including emergency services, equipment rental, and temporary housing costs

Deductibles and Coverage Limits

Know your deductible and coverage limits before you need to file. A $2,500 deductible on a $4,000 damage claim means you are paying most of it out of pocket — the claim may not be worth filing depending on the long-term premium impact.

Assessing Liability

Depending on the cause of the water damage, different parties may be responsible:

Cause Likely Responsibility
Burst pipe from freezing (landlord failed to winterize) Landlord liable
Burst pipe from freezing (tenant failed to maintain heat per lease) Tenant may be liable
Appliance failure (landlord-provided appliance) Landlord liable
Tenant-caused leak (overflowing bathtub, etc.) Tenant liable
Neighbor's leak in multi-family building Neighboring tenant/owner
Roof failure (aging, neglected roof) Landlord liable
Sewage backup (blocked main line) Landlord typically liable

Document the cause carefully. The cause determines insurance coverage, liability, and who pays.

Prevention: Reducing Water Damage Risk

Proactive steps that significantly reduce water damage risk:

  • Install water leak detection devices — Smart water sensors near water heaters, washing machines, and under sinks alert you (and tenant) immediately when water is detected. See our guide on water leak detection systems.
  • Replace rubber washing machine hoses with braided stainless steel
  • Maintain HVAC condensate drains — Clear annually
  • Inspect and maintain the roof regularly — See our roof maintenance guide
  • Maintain gutters — Clogged gutters cause ice dams and overflow that leads to water damage
  • Install sump pump with battery backup in flood-prone basements
  • Require renter's insurance — Tenant's belongings are covered by their renter's insurance, not yours. Always require tenants to carry renter's insurance to avoid disputes about personal property damage.

Conclusion

Water damage response is time-critical. Every hour of delay after a water event increases total damage costs, mold risk, and tenant disruption. The best-prepared landlords have a water damage response plan — including contractor contacts, insurance information, and tenant emergency communication protocols — before any event occurs. Combined with preventive maintenance, this preparation transforms a potential catastrophe into a manageable repair event.
