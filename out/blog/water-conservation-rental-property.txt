1f:T2772,<p>Water and sewer costs are among the fastest-growing utility expenses for landlords, particularly in multifamily properties where the owner pays the master utility bill. In many markets, combined water and sewer rates have increased 40–80% over the past decade — and the trend continues upward as aging infrastructure demands capital reinvestment.</p>
<p>For landlords who pay water utilities, conservation is not an environmental preference — it is a direct cost reduction strategy. This guide covers the most effective water conservation measures for rental properties, their costs and payback periods, and how to structure tenant education programs that encourage conservation even when you do not control behavior directly.</p>
<h2 id="the-financial-case-for-water-conservation">The Financial Case for Water Conservation</h2>
<p>Consider a 10-unit multifamily property where the landlord pays water and sewer. Average water consumption in a typical apartment is 50–70 gallons per day per person. With 1.5 occupants per unit on average:</p>
<ul>
<li>Daily consumption: 10 units × 1.5 persons × 60 gallons = <strong>900 gallons/day</strong></li>
<li>Monthly consumption: ~27,000 gallons</li>
<li>Annual consumption: ~324,000 gallons</li>
</ul>
<p>At a combined water/sewer rate of $0.008/gallon (typical in many markets), annual cost: <strong>$2,592/year</strong>.</p>
<p>A 30% reduction in consumption through low-flow fixtures, leak repair, and landscaping improvements saves $778/year. At a 6% cap rate, that adds <strong>$12,967 to property value</strong> — far more than the cost of the improvements.</p>
<h2 id="high-impact-water-conservation-upgrades">High-Impact Water Conservation Upgrades</h2>
<h3 id="1-low-flow-showerheads">1. Low-Flow Showerheads</h3>
<p>Cost: $20–$80 per fixture | Annual savings: $50–$150 per unit</p>
<p>Standard showerheads use 2.0–2.5 gallons per minute (GPM). <a href="https://www.epa.gov/watersense">EPA WaterSense</a>-certified low-flow models use 1.5–1.8 GPM — a 25–40% reduction. Modern low-flow showerheads have improved dramatically in quality; today's best models deliver strong pressure and are indistinguishable from conventional showerheads in most users' experience.</p>
<p>For 10 units, replacement cost of $400–$800 total pays back in 6–18 months through water savings alone.</p>
<h3 id="2-faucet-aerators">2. Faucet Aerators</h3>
<p>Cost: $5–$15 per faucet | Annual savings: $20–$60 per unit</p>
<p>Faucet aerators screw onto the tip of the faucet and reduce flow from 2.2 GPM (standard) to 1.0–1.5 GPM. They are the single cheapest water conservation upgrade available — typically costing under $10 per faucet and installable without tools in minutes.</p>
<p>Standard aerators are also commonly dislodged or removed by tenants; inspect and replace at every turnover.</p>
<h3 id="3-high-efficiency-toilets">3. High-Efficiency Toilets</h3>
<p>Cost: $150–$400 per toilet | Annual savings: $40–$120 per unit</p>
<p>Older toilets use 3.5–6 gallons per flush (GPF). Standard <a href="https://www.epa.gov/watersense">WaterSense</a> toilets use 1.28 GPF or less — a 60–75% reduction. Dual-flush models allow 0.8 GPF for liquid waste and 1.28 GPF for solid waste.</p>
<p>Toilets are typically replaced only when they fail. However, units with pre-1994 toilets (6 GPF) represent such significant savings potential that proactive replacement can have a payback period of 3–5 years purely on water savings.</p>
<p>Dual-flush conversion kits ($20–$50) can retrofit existing toilets to dual-flush operation, providing most of the savings at a fraction of the replacement cost.</p>
<h3 id="4-leak-detection-and-repair">4. Leak Detection and Repair</h3>
<p>Cost: $0–$200 to detect and repair minor leaks | Annual savings: $100–$500+ per unit</p>
<p>A running toilet or dripping faucet wastes an extraordinary amount of water. A toilet that runs continuously can waste 200 gallons per day — $600/year at typical rates. A dripping faucet at 10 drops per minute wastes 3,000 gallons per year.</p>
<p>Implement a proactive leak audit:</p>
<ul>
<li>Inspect all toilet tank internals (flapper, fill valve) at every annual inspection</li>
<li>Listen and observe during move-in and move-out inspections</li>
<li>Educate tenants to report running toilets and dripping faucets immediately</li>
<li>Install leak detection sensors near water heaters, under sinks, and in mechanical rooms</li>
</ul>
<p>Leak detection sensors cost $15–$40 each and can alert you (via smartphone) to water presence before significant damage occurs. This protects against both water waste and property damage.</p>
<h3 id="5-efficient-irrigation-and-landscaping">5. Efficient Irrigation and Landscaping</h3>
<p>Cost: $200–$2,000 depending on system | Annual savings: $200–$800</p>
<p>Outdoor water use accounts for 25–40% of total water consumption in many single-family and garden-style multifamily properties. Strategies include:</p>
<p><strong>Smart irrigation controllers</strong>: Replace traditional timer-based systems with weather-sensing smart controllers (like Rachio or Hunter Hydrawise) that adjust watering based on rainfall and evapotranspiration data. These systems reduce outdoor water use by 30–50% with no reduction in turf or plant health. Cost: $80–$200; typical payback under 2 years.</p>
<p><strong>Drip irrigation for beds</strong>: Convert sprinkler-irrigated planting beds to drip irrigation, which delivers water directly to plant roots with minimal evaporation. Up to 50% more efficient than overhead sprinklers.</p>
<p><strong>Drought-tolerant landscaping</strong>: Replace water-intensive turf with drought-tolerant native plants, mulched beds, or hardscape elements. Higher upfront cost but dramatically reduces or eliminates irrigation needs. Particularly impactful in arid markets (Southwest, Mountain West).</p>
<p><strong>Mulching</strong>: A 2–3 inch layer of mulch in planting beds retains moisture and reduces watering frequency. Cost-effective and reduces both water use and landscaping time.</p>
<h3 id="6-common-area-upgrades">6. Common Area Upgrades</h3>
<p>For multifamily properties with shared laundry facilities:</p>
<p><strong>ENERGY STAR washing machines</strong>: Commercial-grade ENERGY STAR washers use 14–25 gallons per load vs. 35–45 for standard top-load machines — a 40–50% reduction. Payback period on replacement depends on laundry volume but is typically 4–8 years on water savings plus energy savings.</p>
<p><strong>Coin/app-operated laundry pricing</strong>: If you offer coin laundry, pricing at market rates (rather than below) does not reduce conservation but ensures you recover costs.</p>
<h2 id="submetering-for-water-conservation">Submetering for Water Conservation</h2>
<p>If you pay a master water bill, you have no direct mechanism to hold individual tenants accountable for their consumption — which reduces their incentive to conserve. Water submetering (installing individual meters per unit) provides a billing mechanism that shifts cost to the user and dramatically improves conservation behavior.</p>
<p>Studies show that submetered tenants reduce water consumption by 20–40% compared to master-metered buildings, simply because they receive a bill proportional to their usage. For a 10-unit building, that reduction could save $500–$1,000/year on top of the fixture-based savings.</p>
<p>For more detail on how to implement submetering and RUBS billing, see our <a href="/blog/utility-billing-rental-property">utility billing for rental properties guide</a>.</p>
<h2 id="tenant-education-encouraging-conservation">Tenant Education: Encouraging Conservation</h2>
<p>Even when tenants pay their own utilities, conservation matters — because water restrictions, drought conditions, and building-level issues can affect you. And when you pay utilities, educating tenants is essential.</p>
<p>Effective tenant communication includes:</p>
<p><strong>Move-in welcome materials</strong>: Include a simple one-page guide on water conservation in your welcome packet. Note that reporting leaks quickly protects the building and helps keep operating costs (and future rent increases) lower.</p>
<p><strong>Posting in laundry rooms</strong>: A small sign reminding residents to report leaks and run full loads of laundry is surprisingly effective.</p>
<p><strong>Annual reminders</strong>: A seasonal newsletter or email noting "if you notice a running toilet or dripping faucet, please submit a maintenance request" keeps this top of mind.</p>
<p><strong>Incentive programs</strong>: For properties where water costs are high and you want to drive behavioral change, some landlords use modest rebates or credits for tenants who reduce consumption — measurable if you have submetering.</p>
<h2 id="measuring-your-water-conservation-savings">Measuring Your Water Conservation Savings</h2>
<p>Track monthly water consumption in your <a href="/blog/rental-property-expense-tracking">rental property expense tracking</a> system with:</p>
<ul>
<li>Total gallons consumed per month</li>
<li>Cost per gallon (water + sewer combined)</li>
<li>Cost per unit per month</li>
<li>Year-over-year comparison</li>
</ul>
<p>Document improvements before and after each conservation upgrade so you can quantify ROI and share results with future partners or lenders.</p>
<p>Water conservation upgrades also improve your <a href="/blog/energy-efficiency-upgrades-rental">energy efficiency profile</a>, contribute to <a href="/blog/reducing-rental-operating-expenses">reducing rental operating expenses</a>, and support the goal of <a href="/blog/maximize-rental-income-strategies">maximizing rental income</a> through systematic cost control. The <a href="https://www.energy.gov/energysaver/thermostats">Energy.gov energy saver program</a> provides additional guidance on reducing utility costs across your rental portfolio. In markets with high or rising water rates, these investments are among the highest-return improvements available to landlords.</p>
