24:T25f9,<p>Reasonable accommodation requests are a core obligation under the Fair Housing Act. When a tenant with a disability asks you to make an exception to a rule, policy, or practice so they can use and enjoy their home, you must take that request seriously. Getting it wrong exposes you to discrimination complaints, lawsuits, and significant financial penalties.</p>
<p>This guide explains what reasonable accommodations are, how to evaluate requests, and how to build a compliant process for your rental properties.</p>
<h2 id="what-is-a-reasonable-accommodation">What Is a Reasonable Accommodation?</h2>
<p>A reasonable accommodation is a change, exception, or adjustment to a rule, policy, practice, or service that allows a person with a disability to have equal opportunity to use and enjoy their housing.</p>
<p>Accommodations differ from modifications:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Accommodations</strong> change rules or policies (no physical changes to the property)</li>
<li><strong>Modifications</strong> change the physical structure of the unit or common areas</li>
</ul>
<p>Both are required under the Fair Housing Act, but they follow different rules. This guide focuses on accommodations. For physical modifications, see our <a href="/blog/disability-accommodation-rental">disability accommodations guide</a>.</p>
<h3 id="common-accommodation-examples">Common Accommodation Examples</h3>
<ul>
<li>Allowing an emotional support animal despite a no-pet policy</li>
<li>Assigning a specific parking space closer to the unit entrance</li>
<li>Allowing a live-in aide who is not on the lease</li>
<li>Permitting early lease termination due to disability-related relocation needs</li>
<li>Adjusting rent payment dates to align with disability benefit deposit schedules</li>
<li>Allowing a tenant to receive packages at the office if they cannot access the mailroom</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="legal-framework">Legal Framework</h2>
<h3 id="federal-requirements">Federal Requirements</h3>
<p>The <a href="https://www.justice.gov/crt/fair-housing-act-1">Fair Housing Act</a> requires landlords to provide reasonable accommodations to tenants and applicants with disabilities. This applies to virtually all residential properties, including single-family homes rented through a broker or agent.</p>
<p>The law defines disability broadly, consistent with the <a href="https://www.ada.gov">ADA standards</a>. It covers physical and mental impairments that substantially limit one or more major life activities, people with a record of such impairment, and people regarded as having an impairment.</p>
<h3 id="state-and-local-laws">State and Local Laws</h3>
<p>Many states and cities extend reasonable accommodation protections beyond federal requirements. Some jurisdictions:</p>
<ul>
<li>Define disability more broadly</li>
<li>Impose shorter response timelines</li>
<li>Require landlords to engage in an interactive process</li>
<li>Provide additional remedies for violations</li>
</ul>
<p>Always check your <a href="/blog/landlord-tenant-law-by-state">local landlord-tenant laws</a> in addition to federal requirements.</p>
<h2 id="how-to-evaluate-a-reasonable-accommodation-request">How to Evaluate a Reasonable Accommodation Request</h2>
<h3 id="step-1-recognize-the-request">Step 1: Recognize the Request</h3>
<p>Tenants do not need to use legal terminology. Any communication indicating that a person with a disability needs a change to a rule or policy is a reasonable accommodation request. It can be verbal, written, or made by a third party on behalf of the tenant.</p>
<p>Train your staff to recognize requests even when tenants do not use the words "reasonable accommodation."</p>
<h3 id="step-2-determine-if-the-person-has-a-disability">Step 2: Determine If the Person Has a Disability</h3>
<p>If the disability is obvious or already known, do not request documentation. Only ask for verification when the disability and the need for accommodation are not apparent.</p>
<p>When verification is appropriate, you may request documentation confirming:</p>
<ul>
<li>The person has a disability as defined by law</li>
<li>There is a disability-related need for the requested accommodation</li>
</ul>
<p>You may not ask for medical records, a specific diagnosis, or details about the severity of the condition.</p>
<h3 id="step-3-assess-whether-the-accommodation-is-reasonable">Step 3: Assess Whether the Accommodation Is Reasonable</h3>
<p>An accommodation is unreasonable if it would:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Impose an undue financial or administrative burden</strong> on the landlord</li>
<li><strong>Fundamentally alter the nature</strong> of the housing provider's operations</li>
<li><strong>Pose a direct threat</strong> to the health or safety of others that cannot be mitigated</li>
</ul>
<p>The bar for "unreasonable" is high. Most common accommodation requests are considered reasonable. Courts and <a href="https://www.hud.gov/program_offices/fair_housing_equal_opp">HUD's fair housing office</a> have consistently ruled that waiving no-pet policies, adjusting parking assignments, and modifying payment schedules impose minimal burden on landlords.</p>
<h3 id="step-4-engage-in-an-interactive-process">Step 4: Engage in an Interactive Process</h3>
<p>If the specific request is not feasible, you must explore alternatives with the tenant. For example, if a tenant requests a reserved parking spot but none are available, discuss whether a closer general spot or a different arrangement would meet their needs.</p>
<p>The interactive process demonstrates good faith and protects you legally even if you cannot grant the exact request.</p>
<h3 id="step-5-respond-in-writing">Step 5: Respond in Writing</h3>
<p>Document your decision and communicate it to the tenant in writing. Include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Whether the accommodation is approved, denied, or if you are proposing an alternative</li>
<li>The reasoning behind your decision</li>
<li>Next steps or additional information needed</li>
</ul>
<p>Respond within a reasonable timeframe. While no specific federal deadline exists, 10 to 14 days is widely considered reasonable. Some state laws impose shorter deadlines.</p>
<h2 id="accommodations-you-should-almost-always-grant">Accommodations You Should Almost Always Grant</h2>
<p>These requests are well-established in fair housing law and rarely justify denial:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Assistance animals</strong> including emotional support animals, regardless of pet policies. See our <a href="/blog/emotional-support-animal-landlord-guidelines">ESA landlord guidelines</a> for details.</li>
<li><strong>Reserved accessible parking</strong> when a disability requires closer proximity to the unit</li>
<li><strong>Permission for a live-in aide</strong> even if the lease limits occupancy</li>
<li><strong>Transferring to a more accessible unit</strong> when one is available</li>
<li><strong>Modified communication methods</strong> such as larger-print notices for visually impaired tenants</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="accommodations-that-require-more-analysis">Accommodations That Require More Analysis</h2>
<p>These requests may be reasonable but require case-by-case evaluation:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Early lease termination</strong> without penalty due to disability-related circumstances</li>
<li><strong>Extended time to cure</strong> lease violations when disability contributed to the violation</li>
<li><strong>Changes to common area usage rules</strong> that affect other tenants</li>
<li><strong>Permission for structural modifications</strong> at the tenant's expense</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="documentation-and-record-keeping">Documentation and Record-Keeping</h2>
<p>Maintain detailed records of every accommodation request and your response. Your file should include:</p>
<ol>
<li>The initial request (dated)</li>
<li>Any supporting documentation received</li>
<li>Your verification requests and the tenant's responses</li>
<li>Notes from any interactive process discussions</li>
<li>Your written decision with reasoning</li>
<li>Follow-up communications</li>
</ol>
<p>Store accommodation records separately from general tenant files to protect disability-related information. Only individuals involved in the decision should have access.</p>
<p>Good record-keeping practices align with your broader <a href="/blog/rental-property-record-keeping">rental property record-keeping requirements</a>.</p>
<h2 id="common-mistakes-to-avoid">Common Mistakes to Avoid</h2>
<ul>
<li><strong>Ignoring verbal requests</strong> — They count, even without a formal written submission</li>
<li><strong>Applying blanket policies</strong> — Every accommodation request must be evaluated individually</li>
<li><strong>Requiring excessive documentation</strong> — Asking for medical records or diagnoses violates fair housing law</li>
<li><strong>Delaying responses</strong> — Unreasonable delays are treated as constructive denials</li>
<li><strong>Charging for accommodations</strong> — You cannot charge extra fees for reasonable accommodations</li>
<li><strong>Retaliating</strong> — Any negative action against a tenant who made an accommodation request is illegal</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="key-takeaways">Key Takeaways</h2>
<p>Reasonable accommodation requests are a legal obligation, not a favor. Build a clear process, train your staff, respond promptly, and document everything. When in doubt, err on the side of granting the accommodation or engage a fair housing attorney. The cost of a single discrimination complaint far exceeds the cost of most accommodations.</p>
<p>For the full picture of your fair housing obligations, review our <a href="/blog/fair-housing-compliance-rental">fair housing compliance guide</a>.</p>
