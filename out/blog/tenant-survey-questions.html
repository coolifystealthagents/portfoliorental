Tenant Satisfaction Survey Questions to Ask

Most landlords have a general sense of how their tenants feel — but a general sense is not data. Tenant satisfaction surveys give you something more valuable: specific, actionable feedback that tells you exactly what is working, what is not, and what would convince a good tenant to stay for another year.

This guide walks through how to design effective tenant surveys, the best questions to include across key categories, the right timing for surveys, the tools available, and — critically — what to do with the results.

Why Tenant Surveys Are Worth Your Time

A survey is a structured listening exercise. Done well, it surfaces issues before they become crises, identifies your best opportunities to improve the tenant experience, and communicates to tenants that their opinion matters.

The data is also valuable at scale. If you manage multiple units and discover that maintenance response time is rated poorly across properties, that tells you something concrete about your process. If one building scores consistently lower on cleanliness of common areas, you know where to direct attention.

Surveys also function as a retention signal. A tenant who fills out a satisfaction survey is engaging with you. Tenants who are about to leave often disengage first — they stop communicating, stop reporting issues, and quietly begin apartment shopping. A survey can re-engage these tenants and give you an early warning before they decide not to renew.

For landlords who want to build a more systematic approach to tenant relationships, satisfaction surveys are a natural complement to tenant retention strategies and consistent tenant communication.

Survey Design Principles

Before writing specific questions, establish some design principles:

Keep it short. Five to ten questions is ideal. Twenty-question surveys have low completion rates. If you want deeper feedback, send follow-up surveys on specific topics rather than one giant annual survey.

Mix question types. Combine rating scales (1-5 or 1-10) with multiple choice and open-ended questions. Scales are easy to complete and easy to analyze. Open-ended questions reveal the specific details and nuance that scales cannot capture.

Be specific. "How satisfied are you with management?" is too vague to be actionable. "How satisfied are you with the speed of maintenance response?" gives you something to work with.

Avoid leading questions. "Would you agree that we handle maintenance requests well?" pushes respondents toward a positive answer. "How would you rate our maintenance response time?" is neutral.

Promise to act on responses. If tenants believe you will read their feedback and do nothing, they will not bother filling out the survey. Be explicit that you review all responses and use them to improve.

Question Categories and Sample Questions

Maintenance and Property Condition

Maintenance is the number one driver of tenant satisfaction and dissatisfaction. These questions should appear in every survey:

  1. How would you rate the speed of our response to maintenance requests? (1 = Very slow, 5 = Very fast)

  2. How would you rate the quality of repairs and maintenance work completed in your unit? (1 = Poor quality, 5 = Excellent quality)

  3. In the past six months, have any maintenance issues gone unresolved? (Yes / No / Not applicable)

    • If yes, please describe:

  4. How would you rate the overall condition of common areas in the building (hallways, lobby, laundry room, etc.)? (1 = Poor, 5 = Excellent) (For single-family rentals: How would you rate the condition of the property's exterior and landscaping?)

  5. Is there any maintenance or repair issue in your unit or the building you would like us to address? (Open text)

These questions identify both process problems (slow response) and property problems (deferred maintenance). According to the National Apartment Association, maintenance quality is consistently ranked as the top factor in tenant renewal decisions.

Communication and Management Responsiveness

Tenants who feel heard and respected are far more likely to stay and refer others. These questions assess your communication quality:

  1. How easy is it to reach us when you have a question or concern? (1 = Very difficult, 5 = Very easy)

  2. When you contact us, how satisfied are you with the time it takes to receive a response? (1 = Very dissatisfied, 5 = Very satisfied)

  3. How would you rate the clarity of communication from our office (emails, notices, updates)? (1 = Very unclear, 5 = Very clear)

  4. Do you feel that your concerns are taken seriously and addressed? (Always / Usually / Sometimes / Rarely / Never)

Amenities and Building Features

This category varies by property type but helps you understand which amenities tenants actually value:

  1. Which of the following amenities do you use regularly? (Checklist: parking, laundry, gym, pool, outdoor space, package lockers, storage, etc.)

  2. How satisfied are you with the amenities available at the property? (1 = Very dissatisfied, 5 = Very satisfied)

  3. Is there an amenity or feature that is not currently available that would meaningfully improve your experience? (Open text)

The answers to question 12 can be illuminating. Sometimes tenants want small things — a package locker, a bike storage area, better outdoor lighting — that cost relatively little to provide but significantly improve the living experience.

Lease Renewal and Likelihood to Stay

These questions are the most directly tied to your retention goals:

  1. How likely are you to renew your lease when it comes up for renewal? (Definitely will renew / Probably will renew / Unsure / Probably will not renew / Definitely will not renew)

  2. What is the primary factor that would influence your decision to renew? (Multiple choice: Rent price / Property condition / Management responsiveness / Location / Personal circumstances / Other)

  3. Is there anything we could do differently that would make you more likely to stay long-term? (Open text)

The answers to these questions give you direct intelligence about where you stand with each tenant before the renewal conversation happens.

Overall Satisfaction and Net Promoter Score

  1. Overall, how satisfied are you with your living experience at [property name]? (1 = Very dissatisfied, 10 = Very satisfied)

  2. How likely are you to recommend this property to a friend or colleague looking for a rental? (0 = Not at all likely, 10 = Extremely likely)

Question 17 is a standard Net Promoter Score (NPS) question. Respondents who answer 9-10 are "promoters," 7-8 are "passives," and 0-6 are "detractors." NPS is a useful benchmark for tracking satisfaction trends over time and comparing across properties. For more on using NPS in property management, the Institute of Real Estate Management has published research on tenant experience benchmarks.

Survey Timing: When to Ask

Move-in survey (30 days after move-in): Captures first impressions while they are fresh. Ask about the move-in process, welcome communication, and initial condition of the unit.

Mid-lease survey (6 months in): Identifies any building issues or communication problems before they fester into non-renewal drivers.

Pre-renewal survey (90-120 days before lease expiration): Your most strategically important survey. Gives you time to address concerns before the tenant makes a renewal decision.

Move-out survey: Even if a tenant is leaving, their feedback is valuable. Ask what drove the decision, what you could have done differently, and whether they would consider renting from you again in the future.

Post-maintenance survey: A brief 2-question survey after each maintenance completion — "Was the issue resolved?" and "How would you rate this service?" — gives real-time feedback on your maintenance process.

Tools for Sending Tenant Surveys

Property management software: Most modern property management platforms (such as AppFolio, Buildium, or Rent Manager) include survey or messaging tools. This is the most seamless option because it keeps responses tied to a tenant record.

Google Forms: Free, easy to set up, and results feed automatically into a spreadsheet. Good for landlords who are not using a comprehensive property management platform.

SurveyMonkey or Typeform: More polished survey tools with better analytics. Typeform in particular has excellent mobile UX, which matters since most tenants will complete surveys on their phone.

Email with embedded questions: For very short surveys (one or two questions), embedding the question directly in an email can boost completion rates by removing the friction of clicking to a separate page.

For landlords managing a portfolio, integrating surveys into a property management platform creates a complete record of tenant feedback alongside lease documents, maintenance history, and payment records. See our guide to property management services for an overview of platform options.

Acting on Survey Results: The Critical Step

Surveys only work if you act on them — and if tenants know you acted on them.

Step 1: Review every response individually. Even if trends are consistent, individual open-ended answers often contain specific, actionable information that aggregates hide.

Step 2: Categorize feedback. Sort responses into themes: maintenance, communication, amenities, pricing, noise, cleanliness, etc. This tells you where problems concentrate.

Step 3: Prioritize by impact and frequency. A complaint that appears in 80% of responses is a different priority than one mentioned by a single tenant. But a complaint that is easy to fix and mentioned by multiple tenants should also be addressed quickly.

Step 4: Respond to tenants who provided contact information. If a tenant identified themselves in the survey and raised a specific concern, follow up with them directly. "I read your feedback about the parking lot lighting — I wanted to let you know we are getting quotes this week" demonstrates responsiveness.

Step 5: Close the loop building-wide. For issues that affect all tenants (a common area complaint, a policy concern), send a follow-up note to all tenants summarizing what you heard and what you are doing about it. This is one of the highest-impact things you can do to build trust.

Step 6: Track trends over time. Save every survey response and compare scores period over period. A maintenance satisfaction score that drops from 4.1 to 3.2 over six months is an early warning signal that warrants attention.

Common Survey Mistakes to Avoid

Surveying and doing nothing. If tenants fill out a survey and nothing ever changes, they will not fill out the next one — and they will lose trust in your responsiveness.

Surveying too frequently. Monthly surveys create survey fatigue. Quarterly is typically the maximum frequency for comprehensive surveys. Post-maintenance micro-surveys can be more frequent because they are brief and immediately relevant.

Making surveys mandatory. Optional surveys get more honest responses than mandatory ones. If tenants feel obligated to respond, they may give safe, neutral answers rather than genuine feedback.

Ignoring negative feedback. It is human nature to focus on positive responses. Force yourself to pay equal attention to critical feedback — it is where the most valuable improvement opportunities live.

Combining Surveys with Other Feedback Channels

Surveys are one of several feedback channels. The full picture comes from combining them with:

  • In-person conversations: Annual walk-through visits or informal check-ins reveal things tenants are reluctant to put in writing.
  • Maintenance request analysis: Patterns in what tenants report tell you about recurring property issues.
  • Renewal decision data: Track why tenants leave. If multiple tenants in the same building cite the same reason, it is a systemic issue.
  • Online reviews: Google, Yelp, and apartment listing sites sometimes contain feedback that tenants did not share with you directly.

See our guide to tenant management best practices for more on building comprehensive feedback systems.

Summary

Effective tenant survey questions span four key areas: maintenance and property condition, communication and responsiveness, amenities and building features, and renewal likelihood. Send surveys at move-in, mid-lease, pre-renewal, and move-out. Keep surveys short (five to ten questions), mix rating scales with open-ended questions, and use tools that fit your management style — from property management software to Google Forms.

The most important part of the process is what you do after: act on the feedback, follow up with individual tenants who raised specific concerns, and close the loop by communicating what you heard and what you are doing about it. Landlords who take this approach consistently report higher renewal rates and more cooperative tenant relationships.
