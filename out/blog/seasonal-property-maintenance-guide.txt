39:T50b5,<p>Rental properties need attention all year long. Each season brings different challenges, from frozen pipes in winter to overgrown landscaping in summer. A seasonal property maintenance guide helps you stay ahead of problems, protect your investment, and keep your tenants happy.</p>
<p>The cost of preventive maintenance is always lower than the cost of emergency repairs. A $150 HVAC tune-up in the fall prevents a $3,000 furnace replacement in January. Cleaning gutters for $100 in the spring prevents $5,000 in water damage by summer. The math is clear: seasonal maintenance saves you money.</p>
<p>This guide gives you a complete checklist for every season, plus tips on HVAC prep, landscaping, pest control, and tenant communication throughout the year.</p>
<h2 id="spring-maintenance-tasks-march-through-may">Spring Maintenance Tasks (March through May)</h2>
<p>Spring is the season for recovery and preparation. Winter takes a toll on buildings, and spring is your chance to assess the damage and get everything ready for the warmer months.</p>
<h3 id="exterior-inspection">Exterior Inspection</h3>
<p>Start with a full walk-around of the property:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Roof</strong>: Look for missing, cracked, or curled shingles. Check flashing around chimneys, vents, and skylights. Note any areas where ice dams may have caused damage.</li>
<li><strong>Gutters and downspouts</strong>: Clean out leaves, debris, and ice damage. Make sure downspouts direct water at least 4 feet away from the foundation.</li>
<li><strong>Siding</strong>: Check for cracks, holes, or loose panels. Look for signs of moisture damage or pest entry points.</li>
<li><strong>Foundation</strong>: Walk the perimeter and look for new cracks or shifting. Check that the ground slopes away from the foundation for proper drainage.</li>
<li><strong>Windows and doors</strong>: Inspect caulking and weatherstripping. Replace anything that is cracked, peeling, or missing.</li>
<li><strong>Walkways and driveways</strong>: Repair cracks caused by freeze-thaw cycles. Fill potholes. Reseal asphalt if needed.</li>
<li><strong>Decks and porches</strong>: Check for loose boards, rusted nails, and structural issues. Clean and reseal wood surfaces.</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="landscaping-kickoff">Landscaping Kickoff</h3>
<p>Spring is when your landscaping comes back to life:</p>
<ul>
<li>Clean up fallen branches and winter debris</li>
<li>Prune trees and shrubs before new growth starts</li>
<li>Edge sidewalks and garden beds</li>
<li>Apply pre-emergent weed control</li>
<li>Reseed bare patches in the lawn</li>
<li>Mulch garden beds (2 to 3 inches deep)</li>
<li>Start regular mowing once grass begins growing</li>
<li>Check irrigation systems and repair any winter damage</li>
<li>Trim tree branches that hang over the roof or touch the building</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="spring-hvac-prep">Spring HVAC Prep</h3>
<p>Get the cooling system ready before the first hot day:</p>
<ul>
<li>Schedule a professional AC tune-up ($75 to $150)</li>
<li>Replace or clean all HVAC filters</li>
<li>Clean AC condenser coils and clear debris from around outdoor units</li>
<li>Test the AC system and check refrigerant levels</li>
<li>Clean or replace humidifier pads if applicable</li>
<li>Open and clean supply vents throughout the unit</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="plumbing-checks">Plumbing Checks</h3>
<ul>
<li>Inspect outdoor faucets for freeze damage (turn them on and check for leaks)</li>
<li>Check hose bibs and exterior pipes</li>
<li>Test sump pumps (pour water in the pit to trigger the pump)</li>
<li>Inspect water heater for signs of corrosion or leaks</li>
<li>Check all interior faucets, toilets, and drains for slow leaks</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="pest-control">Pest Control</h3>
<p>Spring is when pests wake up and start looking for homes:</p>
<ul>
<li>Inspect the foundation for entry points and seal them</li>
<li>Check for signs of carpenter ants, termites, or other wood-destroying insects</li>
<li>Clear standing water to prevent mosquitoes</li>
<li>Move woodpiles and mulch away from the foundation</li>
<li>Schedule a professional pest inspection if needed</li>
<li>Set out ant baits and traps in common areas</li>
</ul>
<p>Refer to your <a href="/blog/rental-property-maintenance-checklist">rental property maintenance checklist</a> to make sure nothing gets missed.</p>
<h2 id="summer-maintenance-tasks-june-through-august">Summer Maintenance Tasks (June through August)</h2>
<p>Summer is about keeping things running smoothly and tackling outdoor projects while the weather is good.</p>
<h3 id="exterior-maintenance">Exterior Maintenance</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Paint and stain</strong>: Summer is the best time for exterior painting and staining. Tackle any peeling or fading surfaces.</li>
<li><strong>Power washing</strong>: Clean siding, walkways, patios, and driveways. This is a low-cost task that makes a big visual impact.</li>
<li><strong>Fence repair</strong>: Fix leaning posts, broken boards, and rusted hardware.</li>
<li><strong>Parking lot maintenance</strong>: Seal cracks, repaint lines, and repair potholes.</li>
<li><strong>Window cleaning</strong>: Clean exterior windows for better curb appeal and natural light.</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="landscaping-upkeep">Landscaping Upkeep</h3>
<p>Summer landscaping requires consistent attention:</p>
<ul>
<li>Mow lawns weekly (never cut more than one-third of the grass height at once)</li>
<li>Water deeply but infrequently (1 inch per week for most lawns)</li>
<li>Weed regularly to prevent spreading</li>
<li>Trim hedges and shrubs as needed</li>
<li>Deadhead flowers to keep them blooming</li>
<li>Monitor irrigation systems for leaks or broken heads</li>
<li>Watch for brown patches that may indicate grub damage or disease</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="summer-hvac-management">Summer HVAC Management</h3>
<p>Your AC system works hardest in summer:</p>
<ul>
<li>Check filters monthly and replace as needed</li>
<li>Keep outdoor units clear of debris, plants, and obstructions (2 feet of clearance minimum)</li>
<li>Monitor energy bills for sudden spikes (which may indicate a problem)</li>
<li>Make sure all vents are open and unblocked inside the unit</li>
<li>Address any tenant complaints about cooling quickly, as small problems become big ones fast in summer heat</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="safety-checks">Safety Checks</h3>
<ul>
<li>Test all smoke detectors and carbon monoxide detectors</li>
<li>Check fire extinguishers (replace if expired)</li>
<li>Inspect outdoor lighting for burned-out bulbs and damaged fixtures</li>
<li>Ensure all railings and stairs are sturdy and safe</li>
<li>Check that address numbers are visible from the street (important for emergency responders)</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="pest-control">Pest Control</h3>
<p>Summer brings its own pest challenges:</p>
<ul>
<li>Monitor for wasps, hornets, and yellow jackets (especially around eaves and decks)</li>
<li>Check for ant trails and treat as needed</li>
<li>Look for signs of rodents around trash areas and entry points</li>
<li>Keep grass short and bushes trimmed to reduce hiding spots for pests</li>
<li>Ensure trash cans have tight-fitting lids</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="fall-maintenance-tasks-september-through-november">Fall Maintenance Tasks (September through November)</h2>
<p>Fall is all about preparing for winter. The work you do now prevents expensive problems when temperatures drop.</p>
<h3 id="winterization-prep">Winterization Prep</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Drain and shut off exterior water lines</strong>: Disconnect garden hoses. Shut off and drain outdoor faucet supply lines. Blow out irrigation systems.</li>
<li><strong>Insulate pipes</strong>: Add insulation to pipes in unheated areas like crawl spaces, attics, and garages.</li>
<li><strong>Check weatherstripping</strong>: Replace worn weatherstripping on all doors and windows. The <a href="https://www.energy.gov/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">U.S. Department of Energy's weatherization guidelines</a> recommend sealing all air leaks before adding insulation for maximum energy efficiency.</li>
<li><strong>Caulk gaps</strong>: Seal any gaps around windows, doors, pipes, and wires entering the building.</li>
<li><strong>Inspect insulation</strong>: Check attic insulation levels. Add more if needed (R-38 to R-60 for most climates).</li>
<li><strong>Storm windows</strong>: Install storm windows if your property has them. Make sure they seal properly.</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="gutter-cleaning">Gutter Cleaning</h3>
<p>Fall gutter cleaning is one of the most important seasonal tasks:</p>
<ul>
<li>Clean all gutters and downspouts after the leaves have fallen</li>
<li>Check for proper pitch (gutters should slope toward downspouts)</li>
<li>Repair any leaking joints or damaged sections</li>
<li>Make sure downspouts direct water well away from the foundation</li>
<li>Consider installing gutter guards to reduce future cleaning</li>
</ul>
<p>Clogged gutters cause ice dams, foundation damage, and roof leaks. This $100 to $200 cleaning can save you thousands in repairs.</p>
<h3 id="fall-hvac-prep">Fall HVAC Prep</h3>
<p>Get the heating system ready before cold weather arrives:</p>
<ul>
<li>Schedule a professional furnace or boiler tune-up ($80 to $150)</li>
<li>Replace all HVAC filters</li>
<li>Bleed radiators if you have a hot water heating system</li>
<li>Test the thermostat to make sure it switches to heating mode</li>
<li>Clean and inspect the chimney and fireplace if applicable</li>
<li>Check carbon monoxide detectors near all fuel-burning appliances</li>
<li>Stock up on furnace filters for the winter months</li>
</ul>
<p>Set up a <a href="/blog/preventive-maintenance-schedule-rental">preventive maintenance schedule</a> so these tasks happen automatically each year.</p>
<h3 id="roof-and-exterior">Roof and Exterior</h3>
<ul>
<li>Inspect the roof one more time before winter</li>
<li>Clear all debris from the roof surface</li>
<li>Check flashing and sealant around penetrations</li>
<li>Trim any tree branches hanging over the roof</li>
<li>Inspect and repair mortar joints on brick buildings</li>
<li>Check and repair any exterior cracks that could let water in and freeze</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="landscaping-wind-down">Landscaping Wind-Down</h3>
<ul>
<li>Rake and remove fallen leaves from lawns and garden beds</li>
<li>Apply fall fertilizer to lawns</li>
<li>Plant spring-blooming bulbs</li>
<li>Cut back perennials after first frost</li>
<li>Protect tender plants with burlap or mulch</li>
<li>Drain and store garden hoses</li>
<li>Winterize lawn mowers and outdoor equipment</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="pest-control">Pest Control</h3>
<p>Fall is when mice and other rodents look for warm winter shelter:</p>
<ul>
<li>Seal all entry points larger than a quarter inch (mice can squeeze through surprisingly small gaps)</li>
<li>Set traps in basements, attics, and crawl spaces</li>
<li>Remove food sources (bird feeders near buildings, fallen fruit, accessible trash)</li>
<li>Trim vegetation away from the building</li>
<li>Inspect for stink bugs and ladybeetles, which seek indoor shelter in fall</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="winter-maintenance-tasks-december-through-february">Winter Maintenance Tasks (December through February)</h2>
<p>Winter maintenance focuses on keeping systems running, preventing weather damage, and responding quickly to cold-weather emergencies.</p>
<h3 id="snow-and-ice-management">Snow and Ice Management</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Snow removal</strong>: Keep walkways, driveways, and parking areas clear. Know your local ordinances for snow removal timelines.</li>
<li><strong>Ice treatment</strong>: Apply salt or ice melt to prevent slips and falls. Use sand for traction on steep areas.</li>
<li><strong>Roof snow</strong>: Monitor snow accumulation on roofs. Remove if it exceeds safe load limits (typically 20 to 25 pounds per square foot).</li>
<li><strong>Ice dams</strong>: Watch for ice dams along roof edges. Address them quickly to prevent water intrusion.</li>
<li><strong>Liability</strong>: Document your snow and ice removal efforts in case of slip-and-fall claims.</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="freeze-prevention">Freeze Prevention</h3>
<p>Frozen pipes are one of the most common and expensive winter emergencies:</p>
<ul>
<li>Keep building heat at a minimum of 55 degrees Fahrenheit, even in vacant units</li>
<li>Open cabinet doors under sinks on exterior walls during extreme cold</li>
<li>Let faucets drip during severe freezes (a slow drip prevents freezing)</li>
<li>Monitor weather forecasts and take extra precautions when temperatures drop below 20 degrees</li>
<li>Know where the main water shutoff is in every property</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="winter-hvac-monitoring">Winter HVAC Monitoring</h3>
<ul>
<li>Check filters monthly (heating systems push more air, so filters clog faster)</li>
<li>Monitor heating bills for unexpected increases</li>
<li>Respond immediately to any heating complaints from tenants</li>
<li>Keep a backup plan (space heaters, portable radiators) in case the primary system fails</li>
<li>Schedule emergency heating repair contacts before you need them</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="interior-checks">Interior Checks</h3>
<ul>
<li>Check for drafts around windows and doors</li>
<li>Look for condensation on windows (a sign of humidity problems)</li>
<li>Inspect attics for ice dam-related leaks</li>
<li>Monitor for signs of frozen pipes (reduced water flow, frost on pipes)</li>
<li>Test all safety devices (smoke detectors, CO detectors)</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="winter-landscaping">Winter Landscaping</h3>
<p>Even though growth has stopped, there are still tasks to handle:</p>
<ul>
<li>Protect exposed plants and shrubs from heavy snow</li>
<li>Prune dead branches that could fall under snow or ice weight</li>
<li>Plan spring landscaping projects during the slow season</li>
<li>Order materials and schedule vendors for spring work</li>
</ul>
<p>For detailed cost planning across all seasons, check our guide on <a href="/blog/property-maintenance-cost-budgeting">property maintenance cost budgeting</a>.</p>
<h2 id="hvac-seasonal-prep-a-deeper-dive">HVAC Seasonal Prep: A Deeper Dive</h2>
<p>HVAC systems are your biggest maintenance expense and your tenants' top comfort concern. Here is a more detailed look at seasonal HVAC care.</p>
<h3 id="spring-and-summer-cooling">Spring and Summer (Cooling)</h3>
<ol>
<li>Replace air filters (do this monthly during heavy use)</li>
<li>Clean evaporator and condenser coils</li>
<li>Check refrigerant levels</li>
<li>Inspect and clean condensate drain lines</li>
<li>Test thermostat accuracy</li>
<li>Lubricate moving parts</li>
<li>Check electrical connections and voltage</li>
<li>Inspect ductwork for leaks</li>
</ol>
<h3 id="fall-and-winter-heating">Fall and Winter (Heating)</h3>
<ol>
<li>Replace air filters</li>
<li>Clean or replace the furnace igniter</li>
<li>Inspect the heat exchanger for cracks (critical safety issue)</li>
<li>Check the gas valve and connections</li>
<li>Test safety controls</li>
<li>Clean the blower assembly</li>
<li>Inspect exhaust flue for blockages</li>
<li>Test carbon monoxide levels</li>
</ol>
<h3 id="hvac-maintenance-costs">HVAC Maintenance Costs</h3>
<p>Budget for these annual HVAC maintenance costs:</p>
<ul>
<li>Spring AC tune-up: $75 to $150</li>
<li>Fall furnace tune-up: $80 to $150</li>
<li>Filters (replaced 4 to 6 times per year): $40 to $120</li>
<li>Minor repairs and adjustments: $100 to $300</li>
</ul>
<p>Total annual HVAC maintenance: $295 to $720 per unit. This is far less than the $3,000 to $8,000 cost of replacing a system that failed due to neglect.</p>
<h2 id="tenant-communication-throughout-the-year">Tenant Communication Throughout the Year</h2>
<p>Good seasonal maintenance requires cooperation from your tenants. Keep them informed and involved.</p>
<h3 id="seasonal-newsletters-or-emails">Seasonal Newsletters or Emails</h3>
<p>Send tenants a brief note at the start of each season covering:</p>
<ul>
<li>What maintenance you will be doing (and when)</li>
<li>What you need from them (access for inspections, moving items away from vents)</li>
<li>Seasonal tips for their unit (how to report ice, when to change their own filters)</li>
<li>Contact information for maintenance requests</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="give-advance-notice">Give Advance Notice</h3>
<p>For any maintenance that requires property access, give tenants proper notice (usually 24 to 48 hours, depending on your state). Include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Date and time window</li>
<li>What work will be done</li>
<li>How long it will take</li>
<li>Whether they need to be home</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="empower-tenants-to-help">Empower Tenants to Help</h3>
<p>Teach tenants simple tasks that prevent big problems:</p>
<ul>
<li>How to change HVAC filters (if this is their responsibility per the lease)</li>
<li>How to shut off the main water valve in an emergency</li>
<li>When and how to report maintenance issues</li>
<li>How to prevent frozen pipes during cold snaps</li>
<li>When to clean dryer vents</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="document-everything">Document Everything</h3>
<p>Keep records of all seasonal maintenance:</p>
<ul>
<li>What was done and when</li>
<li>Who performed the work</li>
<li>What it cost</li>
<li>Any issues found and follow-up needed</li>
<li>Photos of completed work</li>
</ul>
<p>This documentation protects you in liability cases, helps with insurance claims, and makes <a href="/blog/property-maintenance-cost-budgeting">budgeting for maintenance</a> more accurate over time.</p>
<h2 id="creating-your-seasonal-maintenance-calendar">Creating Your Seasonal Maintenance Calendar</h2>
<p>Put it all together in a simple calendar. Here is a month-by-month overview:</p>
<p>Use resources like <a href="https://www.weather.gov/" rel="noopener" target="_blank">the National Weather Service</a> to track seasonal weather patterns and time your maintenance tasks accordingly.</p>
<p><strong>January</strong>: Winter inspection, check heating systems, monitor for ice dams
<strong>February</strong>: Plan spring projects, order materials, schedule vendors
<strong>March</strong>: Begin spring exterior inspection, clean gutters, test sump pumps
<strong>April</strong>: HVAC spring tune-up, landscaping kickoff, pest inspection
<strong>May</strong>: Exterior repairs, painting prep, irrigation system startup
<strong>June</strong>: Exterior painting and staining, power washing, safety checks
<strong>July</strong>: Mid-summer landscaping check, HVAC filter replacement, pest monitoring
<strong>August</strong>: Pre-fall planning, schedule fall HVAC tune-up, roof inspection
<strong>September</strong>: Begin winterization, gutter cleaning, seal gaps and cracks
<strong>October</strong>: Furnace tune-up, final landscaping tasks, drain exterior water lines
<strong>November</strong>: Final winterization, storm window installation, freeze prevention
<strong>December</strong>: Winter monitoring, snow and ice management, interior checks</p>
