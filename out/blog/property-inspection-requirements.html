Regular property inspections protect your investment and keep your tenants safe. But inspections come with rules. You cannot just walk into a rental unit whenever you want. There are laws about when, why, and how you can inspect your property.

This guide covers everything you need to know about property inspection requirements. We will walk through different types of inspections, notice requirements, documentation, and tenant rights. By the end, you will have a clear plan for inspecting your rentals the right way.

Why Property Inspections Matter

Inspections catch problems early. A small leak turns into a mold problem if you do not find it. A broken smoke detector puts lives at risk if nobody checks it. Routine inspections help you spot these issues before they become expensive disasters.

Inspections also protect you legally. When you document the condition of a property at move-in and move-out, you have proof of any damage a tenant caused. This makes security deposit disputes much easier to resolve.

Finally, inspections show tenants that you care about the property. When tenants see that you maintain the property well, they are more likely to take care of it too.

Types of Property Inspections

There are several types of inspections landlords should perform. Each one serves a different purpose and has its own set of rules.

Move-In Inspections

A move-in inspection happens before the tenant takes possession of the unit. This is one of the most important inspections you will ever do.

During a move-in inspection, you and the tenant walk through the entire property together. You note the condition of every room, every appliance, and every fixture. You document any existing damage such as scratches on hardwood floors, stains on carpet, nail holes in walls, or chips in countertops.

Both you and the tenant should sign the inspection report. This signed document becomes your baseline. When the tenant moves out, you compare the property's condition to this report. Any new damage is the tenant's responsibility.

For a detailed checklist of what to cover, see our tenant move-in checklist.

What to Include in a Move-In Inspection

Go room by room and check:

  • Walls and ceilings: Cracks, holes, stains, paint condition
  • Floors: Scratches, stains, loose tiles, carpet condition
  • Windows: Cracks, broken locks, screen condition, seals
  • Doors: Locks working, hinges tight, weather stripping intact
  • Kitchen: Appliance condition, cabinet doors, countertops, faucet
  • Bathroom: Toilet function, shower/tub condition, grout, caulking, ventilation
  • Electrical: Outlets working, light fixtures, switches
  • HVAC: Heating and cooling function, filter condition, thermostat
  • Smoke and CO detectors: Present, working, batteries fresh
  • Exterior: Porch, deck, yard, fencing, gutters, driveway

Move-Out Inspections

A move-out inspection happens after the tenant vacates the unit. You compare the current condition to the move-in inspection report. Any damage beyond normal wear and tear can be deducted from the security deposit. Understanding security deposit laws is essential to handling these deductions properly.

Normal wear and tear includes things like:

  • Small nail holes from hanging pictures
  • Minor scuffs on walls
  • Slightly worn carpet in high-traffic areas
  • Faded paint from sunlight

Damage beyond normal wear and tear includes:

  • Large holes in walls
  • Broken windows
  • Stained or burned carpet
  • Missing fixtures or appliances
  • Excessive dirt requiring professional cleaning

Some states require you to give the tenant a chance to attend the move-out inspection. Others require you to provide a written list of damages before you make deductions. Check your state's specific laws in our guide on landlord-tenant law by state.

Routine Inspections

Routine inspections happen during the tenancy. They let you check the overall condition of the property and catch maintenance issues early.

Most landlords do routine inspections once or twice a year. Some do them quarterly, especially for properties with a history of problems.

During a routine inspection, look for:

  • Water damage or leaks (keep a rental property maintenance checklist handy for tracking issues)
  • Mold or mildew growth
  • Pest infestations
  • Unauthorized occupants or pets
  • Lease violations like smoking indoors
  • Safety hazards
  • HVAC filter condition
  • Smoke detector function

Drive-By Inspections

A drive-by inspection is an informal check of the property's exterior. You do not need to enter the unit or notify the tenant for a drive-by.

Look at the yard, the exterior walls, the roof, and any common areas. Check for:

  • Overgrown landscaping
  • Trash or debris in the yard
  • Visible damage to the exterior
  • Unauthorized vehicles or structures
  • Signs of hoarding near windows

Drive-by inspections are quick and easy. They help you stay aware of your property's condition between formal inspections.

Seasonal Inspections

Seasonal inspections focus on weather-related issues. Different seasons bring different risks.

Spring Inspections

  • Check gutters and downspouts for debris
  • Inspect the roof for winter damage
  • Test air conditioning systems
  • Look for water damage from snowmelt
  • Check landscaping and drainage

Fall Inspections

  • Test heating systems before winter
  • Check weatherstripping and insulation
  • Inspect the roof and chimney
  • Clean gutters before leaf fall
  • Check pipes that could freeze

Notice Requirements for Inspections

You cannot enter a rental property without proper notice. Every state has laws about how much notice you must give and what reasons justify entry.

General Notice Rules

Most states require 24 to 48 hours of written notice before entering a rental unit. Some states require more. Here is a general breakdown:

  • 24 hours notice: Most common requirement (California, Florida, Colorado, and many others)
  • 48 hours notice: Some states like Hawaii and Delaware
  • Reasonable notice: Some states use this vague standard instead of a specific number
  • No specific requirement: A few states do not have a set time, but you should still give at least 24 hours

Valid Reasons for Entry

Even with proper notice, you need a legitimate reason to enter. Valid reasons typically include:

  • Performing repairs or maintenance
  • Showing the property to prospective tenants or buyers
  • Conducting routine inspections agreed to in the lease
  • Emergency situations (no notice required)
  • Court-ordered access

Emergency Entry

In an emergency, you can enter without notice. Emergencies include:

  • Fire
  • Flooding or burst pipes
  • Gas leaks
  • Suspected criminal activity inside the unit
  • Situations that threaten life or property

Document any emergency entry carefully. Write down what happened, when you entered, and what you did. Take photos.

How to Give Notice

Always give notice in writing. Include:

  • The date and time of the planned inspection
  • The reason for the inspection
  • How long the inspection will take
  • Your contact information in case the tenant has questions

Send the notice through the method specified in your lease. This could be email, posted notice on the door, or certified mail. Keep a copy for your records.

Documentation: Photos, Videos, and Written Records

Good documentation can save you thousands of dollars. It is your proof of the property's condition at any point in time.

Photo Documentation

Take photos of every room during every inspection. Use these tips:

  • Take wide-angle shots of each room to show overall condition
  • Take close-up shots of any damage, no matter how small
  • Photograph all appliances including serial numbers and model numbers
  • Include timestamps in your photos or use an app that adds them automatically
  • Store photos in the cloud so they are backed up and easy to access

Video Documentation

Video walkthroughs add another layer of proof. They show things photos might miss, like running water, squeaky doors, or HVAC noise.

When recording video:

  • Narrate as you go, calling out the room and any issues you see
  • Move slowly so the camera captures details
  • Record the date and time at the start of the video
  • Save videos with a clear naming convention like "123MainSt_MoveIn_2026-03-31"

Written Inspection Reports

Every inspection should produce a written report. The report should include:

  • Property address
  • Date and time of inspection
  • Names of everyone present
  • Condition of each room and feature
  • List of any damage or maintenance needs
  • Tenant's comments or concerns
  • Signatures of both landlord and tenant

Use a standardized checklist so you cover the same items every time. This consistency makes it easy to compare conditions over time.

Inspection Checklists

A good inspection checklist makes sure you do not miss anything. Here is a basic template you can adapt for your properties.

Kitchen Checklist

  • Refrigerator: clean, working, no leaks
  • Stove/oven: burners work, clean, no damage
  • Dishwasher: runs properly, no leaks
  • Sink and faucet: no leaks, drains properly
  • Garbage disposal: works, no clogs
  • Cabinets: doors close, no damage
  • Countertops: no chips, cracks, or stains
  • Floor: clean, no damage
  • Walls: no holes, stains, or grease buildup

Bathroom Checklist

  • Toilet: flushes, no leaks, no cracks
  • Shower/tub: drains, no leaks, caulking intact
  • Sink and faucet: no leaks, drains properly
  • Mirror: no cracks
  • Ventilation fan: works properly
  • Tile and grout: no cracks, no mold
  • Floor: no water damage

Living Areas Checklist

  • Walls: no holes, marks, or damage
  • Ceiling: no water stains, cracks, or peeling
  • Windows: open and close, locks work, screens intact
  • Doors: open and close, locks work
  • Light fixtures: all working
  • Outlets: all working
  • Floor: no damage or excessive wear
  • Smoke detector: present and working
  • CO detector: present and working (if required)

Tenant Rights During Inspections

Tenants have rights during inspections. Respecting these rights protects you from complaints and legal trouble.

Right to Notice

As discussed above, tenants have a right to advance notice before any inspection. If you fail to give proper notice, the tenant can refuse entry. Repeated violations could be considered harassment. The HUD housing standards page outlines tenant protections that apply to inspections.

Right to Be Present

Tenants have the right to be present during any inspection. You cannot force them to leave while you inspect. If a tenant wants to be there, schedule the inspection at a time that works for both of you.

Right to Privacy

You cannot rummage through a tenant's belongings during an inspection. Stick to the structure of the property: walls, floors, ceilings, fixtures, and appliances. Do not open dresser drawers, closets full of personal items, or personal storage containers.

Right to Quiet Enjoyment

Every tenant has a right to quiet enjoyment of their home. This means you cannot inspect so frequently that it disrupts their life. Inspecting once every three to six months is reasonable. Showing up every week is not.

What to Do If a Tenant Refuses Inspection

If a tenant refuses to allow a lawfully scheduled inspection, do not force entry. Instead:

  1. Document the refusal in writing
  2. Send a formal notice citing the lease clause that allows inspections
  3. Explain the legal requirements and your rights as the landlord
  4. If the tenant continues to refuse, consult a lawyer

Never change locks, shut off utilities, or threaten a tenant to gain access. These actions are illegal in every state.

For a full breakdown of what tenants can and cannot do, read our guide on tenant rights and responsibilities.

Tips for Smooth Inspections

Here are some practical tips to make your inspections go smoothly:

  1. Be friendly and professional. Inspections should not feel confrontational.
  2. Bring your checklist. Do not rely on memory.
  3. Take photos of everything. Even if things look fine, document them.
  4. Listen to the tenant. They may know about issues you cannot see.
  5. Follow up on problems. If you find a maintenance issue, fix it promptly.
  6. Keep records. File every inspection report where you can find it easily.
  7. Be consistent. Inspect all your properties on the same schedule.
  8. Respect the tenant's time. Keep inspections under 30 minutes when possible.

How to Use Inspection Results

Inspection data is only useful if you act on it. After each inspection:

  • Create a maintenance work order for any issues found
  • Update your property condition records with photos and notes
  • Compare to previous inspections to spot trends
  • Address lease violations with a written notice to the tenant
  • Budget for upcoming repairs based on what you see

Over time, your inspection records will show patterns. Maybe one property always has plumbing issues. Maybe another needs a new roof soon. These patterns help you plan ahead and avoid surprises.

Final Thoughts

Property inspections are not optional. They are a core part of being a responsible landlord. Regular inspections protect your investment, keep your tenants safe, and give you the documentation you need to handle disputes.

Follow your state's notice requirements. Document everything with photos, videos, and written reports. Respect your tenants' rights. And always act on what you find.

A well-inspected property is a well-maintained property. For additional legal guidance on notice requirements and tenant privacy, Nolo's landlord right of entry guides provide state-by-state details. And a well-maintained property keeps good tenants for years to come.
