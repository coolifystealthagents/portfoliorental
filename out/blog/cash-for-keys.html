Cash for Keys: A Faster Alternative to Eviction

Evictions are expensive, slow, and unpredictable. Filing fees, attorney costs, court dates, and the risk of property damage can push the total bill to several thousand dollars, and months of lost rent on top of that.

Cash for keys offers a faster path: you pay a problem tenant to leave voluntarily, skip the courthouse, and get your property back in a fraction of the time.

This guide covers everything landlords need to know about using cash for keys effectively, from how the arrangement works, to typical payment amounts, to the tax implications you may not have considered.

What Is Cash for Keys?

Cash for keys (also written "cash-for-keys") is a voluntary agreement between a landlord and a tenant in which the landlord offers a lump-sum payment in exchange for the tenant vacating the property by a specific date, leaving it in acceptable condition, and surrendering all keys and access devices.

The arrangement is entirely informal in the sense that no court is involved. Both parties negotiate terms directly, sign a written agreement, and the tenant walks away with cash while the landlord regains possession without the delays and costs of a formal eviction process.

Cash for keys is not a new concept. Lenders have used it for decades to clear properties after foreclosure.

Landlords have adopted it as a practical tool for dealing with nonpaying tenants, tenants holding over after lease expiration, or situations where difficult tenants are better managed with an exit incentive than a court battle.

Is Cash for Keys Legal?

Yes, cash for keys is legal in all 50 states. It is a private contract between two parties.

Neither party is required to enter the agreement, and no court approval is needed. The only legal requirement is that both parties enter the agreement voluntarily, without coercion or fraud.

Some jurisdictions with strong tenant protections, particularly in California, New York, and Oregon, have specific rules about how cash for keys offers can be made and whether tenants must be notified of their right to consult an attorney. Always check your state's landlord-tenant law before proceeding.

How Cash for Keys Works

The process follows a straightforward sequence:

  1. Identify the situation. Decide whether cash for keys makes sense given the tenant's circumstances, the likely cost of eviction, and the condition of the property.
  2. Make an initial offer. Approach the tenant respectfully. Frame the conversation as a mutual benefit: they receive immediate cash, you avoid court.
  3. Negotiate terms. Agree on the payment amount, the vacate date, and the condition the property must be left in.
  4. Draft and sign a written agreement. The agreement protects both parties and confirms the tenant's voluntary departure.
  5. Conduct a walkthrough. Inspect the property with the tenant before releasing the payment.
  6. Exchange keys for cash. Hand over the agreed payment in exchange for keys, garage openers, mailbox keys, and any other access items.
  7. Re-rent the property. Begin turnover and re-leasing immediately.

The entire process can take as little as a few days if the tenant is cooperative.

Typical Cash for Keys Amounts

There is no fixed formula, but most successful cash for keys agreements fall in a predictable range based on several factors:

  • Local rental market rates. In high-cost cities like San Francisco or New York, payments of $5,000, $15,000 or more are not unusual because courts are slow and tenant protections are strong. In lower-cost markets, $500, $2,000 is often sufficient.
  • Cost of eviction in your jurisdiction. If eviction will cost you $3,000 in fees and four months of lost rent, offering $1,500 to leave in two weeks is a rational financial decision.
  • Condition concerns. If you suspect the tenant may damage the property during an eviction, paying for a clean exit has additional value.
  • How much the tenant owes. In exchange for the cash payment, most agreements include waiving or reducing the tenant's outstanding balance. Factor this into your calculation.

A practical starting point: calculate your all-in eviction cost (court fees, attorney fees, lost rent, turnover costs), then offer the tenant roughly 30, 50% of that figure. You keep the savings; they get a clean break.

What to Include in the Offer

Beyond the cash payment, consider offering:

  • Waiver of unpaid rent (clearly stated in the agreement)
  • A neutral rental reference
  • Flexibility on the vacate date (within reason)
  • Help connecting with housing resources if appropriate

The Cash for Keys Agreement Template

A written agreement is non-negotiable. An oral promise is nearly impossible to enforce if the tenant later refuses to leave.

Your agreement should include at minimum:

Required Elements:

  • Full names of all parties (all adults on the lease)
  • Property address
  • Payment amount
  • Vacate date and time
  • Condition requirements (broom-clean, no damage beyond normal wear)
  • Itemized list of items to be returned (keys, fobs, openers)
  • Waiver language (what claims, if any, each party is releasing)
  • Signatures of all parties
  • Date of signing

Optional but Recommended Elements:

  • Language confirming the tenant is entering the agreement voluntarily
  • A statement that the tenant has had the opportunity to consult an attorney
  • A provision that the payment is contingent on meeting all conditions (property condition, vacate date, key return)
  • Photographs documenting the property's condition on the vacate date

You can download a basic template from your state's landlord association or have a local real estate attorney draft one for your specific jurisdiction. The HUD website provides general guidance on landlord-tenant rights that can inform your drafting process.

When to Use Cash for Keys vs. Formal Eviction

Cash for keys is not always the right tool. Here is how to decide:

Use Cash for Keys When:

  • The tenant has a history of cooperation and is simply in financial distress
  • The cost of formal eviction in your market is high (attorney-dependent jurisdictions)
  • You are concerned about property damage during a drawn-out eviction
  • You need the unit back quickly for renovation, sale, or a waiting tenant
  • The lease has expired and the tenant is holding over
  • You want to avoid a public eviction record that can make future tenant screening harder

Use Formal Eviction When:

  • The tenant is involved in illegal activity (cash for keys may appear to reward or enable this)
  • There is active property damage or dangerous conditions
  • The tenant has already violated a previous cash for keys agreement
  • Multiple unsuccessful negotiation attempts have occurred
  • The situation involves domestic violence or complex legal circumstances requiring court oversight

Handling Unpaid Rent in the Agreement

One of the most valuable aspects of cash for keys is the ability to resolve unpaid rent cleanly. Rather than pursuing a judgment (which takes time and may be uncollectible), many landlords choose to waive the outstanding balance as part of the agreement.

If you waive the debt, say so explicitly in the agreement: "Landlord waives all claims for unpaid rent through [date] in exchange for Tenant's timely compliance with all terms of this agreement."

If you want to preserve your right to pursue the balance, keep that language out of the agreement, but understand that tenants are less likely to sign without some debt relief. Find a balance that makes financial sense.

Tax Implications of Cash for Keys

This is the part most landlords overlook. Cash for keys payments have tax consequences for both parties.

For the Landlord

The payment is a deductible business expense. You are paying to recover possession of a business asset (your rental property), which the IRS treats as an ordinary and necessary business expense.

Keep records of the payment, a canceled check or bank transfer is ideal, not cash.

If you waive unpaid rent as part of the deal, the waived amount is simply income you never received. You do not need to report it as a deduction unless you had previously recognized it as income on an accrual basis.

For the Tenant

Here is where it gets complicated. The IRS has ruled in some circumstances that cash for keys payments received by tenants are taxable income.

Whether this applies depends on the nature of the payment (relocation assistance vs. consideration for releasing a lease right vs. compensation for agreeing to vacate early).

You may be required to issue a 1099-MISC to tenants who receive $600 or more. Consult a tax professional before completing the transaction, and include language in your agreement specifying the nature of the payment to support proper classification.

The IRS website has guidance on rental income and expenses that can help clarify the landlord side of this equation.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Not getting everything in writing. A verbal agreement is worthless if the tenant claims they never agreed to leave.

Paying before the tenant vacates. Always make payment contingent on the tenant meeting all conditions, full vacancy, property condition, key return. Conduct the walkthrough first.

Forgetting to address all occupants. If there are multiple adults in the unit, all of them must sign. An unsigned occupant has no obligation to leave.

Leaving the waiver language ambiguous. Be specific about what you are and are not releasing. Vague language creates disputes later.

Skipping the property inspection. Document the condition at time of vacating with photos and a written checklist signed by both parties.

Ignoring local legal requirements. Some cities require landlords to provide tenant relocation assistance under certain conditions, and paying cash for keys may or may not satisfy that requirement. Check your local ordinances.

What Happens if the Tenant Takes the Money and Doesn't Leave?

If a tenant accepts payment and then refuses to vacate by the agreed date, you have a breach of contract. You can pursue the matter in small claims court to recover the payment, and you can simultaneously proceed with a formal eviction since the lease has typically been endd by the agreement.

The written agreement is your evidence. This is precisely why skipping the paperwork is so costly.

Summary

Cash for keys is one of the most practical tools in a landlord's toolkit for recovering possession of a rental unit without the cost, delay, and uncertainty of formal eviction. When structured correctly, with a clear written agreement, a reasonable payment, and a thorough walkthrough, it benefits both parties and gets your property back on the market faster.

Key takeaways:

  • Cash for keys is legal in all states and requires no court involvement
  • Calculate your total eviction cost to determine a rational offer amount
  • Always use a written agreement signed by all adults in the unit
  • Make payment contingent on meeting all conditions, including property condition and key return
  • Consult a tax professional regarding 1099 requirements
  • Know when formal eviction is the better path, especially for illegal activity situations
