Rental Property Tax Filing Guide: Schedule E Explained

Filing taxes as a landlord involves more than a standard individual return. Rental income, expenses, depreciation, passive loss rules, and multiple properties all add complexity — and all carry meaningful opportunities to reduce your tax bill if handled correctly. This rental property tax filing guide covers everything you need to know to complete your return accurately, capture every available deduction, and stay out of IRS trouble.

The Foundation: Where Rental Income Is Reported

Most residential rental property income and expenses are reported on Schedule E (Supplemental Income and Loss), which is filed as part of your Form 1040. IRS Publication 527 is the primary reference for rental property taxation rules. Schedule E is designed for passive rental activities — long-term rentals where your average rental period exceeds 7 days and you do not provide substantial hotel-like services to tenants.

Exceptions — when you file on Schedule C instead:

  • Short-term rentals where you provide substantial services (daily cleaning, meals, concierge) similar to a hotel or bed-and-breakfast
  • Some short-term rental scenarios with average rental periods of 7 days or less where you materially participate

For the vast majority of landlords with traditional rental properties, Schedule E is the right form. For those with Airbnb or VRBO properties, the analysis is more nuanced — see our short-term rental tax rules guide for specifics.

What You Need Before Filing

Gather these documents before starting your return or handing materials to your CPA:

Income documentation:

  • Bank statements showing all rent deposits
  • Rent ledger or property management statements
  • 1099-MISC or 1099-NEC forms (if a property manager paid you $600 or more)

Expense documentation:

  • Form 1098 (mortgage interest statement from your lender)
  • Property tax payment records
  • Insurance premium statements
  • Utility bills (if you pay any utilities)
  • Receipts and invoices for repairs and maintenance
  • Property management fee statements
  • Professional service invoices (CPA, attorney, contractor)
  • HOA fee records
  • Advertising and listing fee receipts
  • Mileage log for property-related travel

Depreciation records:

  • Prior year depreciation schedule from your CPA
  • Settlement statement from property purchase (to verify cost basis)
  • Records of capital improvements with dates and costs

Other:

  • Security deposit account balance (generally not income, but track it)
  • Any refunded deposits applied to damage or unpaid rent (these become income)

Clean rental property bookkeeping throughout the year makes this document collection trivial rather than stressful.

Completing Schedule E: Line by Line

Schedule E Part I is for rental real estate. You can report up to three properties per page (using the property address and type as identifiers), with additional pages for more properties.

Income Lines

Line 3: Rents received Report the total gross rent collected for the year. This includes:

  • Monthly rent payments
  • Late fees
  • Pet fees
  • Parking fees paid separately

Do not include security deposits (unless forfeited or applied to damages/rent), which are handled differently.

Expense Lines (Lines 5–19)

Report each expense in the appropriate category:

  • Line 5 – Advertising: Listing fees, photography, signage
  • Line 6 – Auto and travel: Deductible mileage and travel expenses for property-related trips. Use the IRS standard mileage rate or actual vehicle expenses
  • Line 7 – Cleaning and maintenance: Cleaning between tenants, lawn care, snow removal, routine upkeep
  • Line 8 – Commissions: Leasing commissions paid to agents
  • Line 9 – Insurance: Landlord insurance premiums (not renters' insurance)
  • Line 10 – Legal and professional fees: CPA fees, attorney fees, filing costs
  • Line 11 – Management fees: Property management company fees
  • Line 12 – Mortgage interest: From your Form 1098. This is usually your largest expense deduction
  • Line 13 – Other interest: Non-mortgage interest related to the property
  • Line 14 – Repairs: Costs to maintain the property in working condition (not improvements)
  • Line 15 – Supplies: Cleaning supplies, light bulbs, small maintenance items purchased for property use
  • Line 16 – Taxes: Property taxes paid during the year
  • Line 17 – Utilities: Any utilities you pay (water, gas, electric, trash)
  • Line 18 – Depreciation: From Form 4562 — this is one of your most important deductions

For the full list of what qualifies as a deductible expense, see our rental property tax deductions guide.

Depreciation: Form 4562 and Schedule E Line 18

Depreciation is the deduction that makes rental property investment so tax-efficient. It is also the most complex line on Schedule E.

Basic depreciation calculation:

  1. Determine the depreciable basis: Purchase price + closing costs + capital improvements, minus the land value
  2. Divide by 27.5 years (residential) or 39 years (commercial) for straight-line depreciation per IRS Publication 946
  3. Report the annual amount on Form 4562, which flows to Schedule E Line 18

Your first year of ownership uses a mid-month convention — you get a partial year of depreciation based on when the property was placed in service.

Form 4562 also reports:

  • Section 179 deductions
  • Bonus depreciation on qualifying assets
  • Depreciation on vehicles used for the rental business

For a complete explanation of how depreciation works and how to optimize it, review our rental property depreciation guide.

Calculating Net Income or Loss

After entering all income and expenses (including depreciation), Schedule E calculates your net rental income or loss for each property. These amounts are summed and carried to Schedule E Part I, Line 26, which flows to your Form 1040.

If you have a net loss, the passive activity loss rules determine how much (if any) you can deduct currently. This is where Form 8582 comes into play.

Form 8582: Passive Activity Loss Limitations

If your Schedule E shows a rental loss, you must complete Form 8582 to determine how much is deductible. The form:

  1. Aggregates passive income and losses across all your rental activities
  2. Applies the $25,000 special allowance (if your MAGI is under $150,000 and you actively participate)
  3. Carries forward any suspended losses that cannot be deducted this year

Understanding the full mechanics of Form 8582 requires familiarity with the passive activity loss rules for rental property. If you have suspended losses from prior years that are now being released (due to a sale, for example), Form 8582 also handles the release calculation.

Real Estate Professional Status on Your Return

If you qualify as a real estate professional, attach a statement to your return explaining that you qualify under IRC Section 469(c)(7), documenting your hours and the basis for your claim. Without this disclosure, the IRS may treat your losses as passive by default.

Your CPA should include this statement and check the box on Schedule E indicating that you are a real estate professional if applicable.

Security Deposits: Handling Them Correctly

Security deposits are not income when received — they are liabilities because you may need to return them. Do not report them as rent on Schedule E.

Security deposits become income when:

  • You apply them to unpaid rent (in the year applied)
  • You apply them to damages and do not return the balance (in the year applied)
  • The tenant forfeits the deposit

Track your security deposits in a separate account (required by law in many states) and reconcile this account carefully each year.

Selling a Rental Property: Additional Tax Considerations

The year you sell a rental property, your tax return becomes more complex:

  • Form 4797 (Sale of Business Property): Reports the gain or loss on sale of the rental property
  • Depreciation recapture: All depreciation taken (or allowable) is recaptured. Section 1250 unrecaptured gain is taxed at a maximum 25% rate
  • Capital gain: Any remaining gain above recapture is long-term capital gain (if held over one year), taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on your income
  • Release of suspended passive losses: All passive losses accumulated on the property are released and can offset any income in the sale year

If you are using a 1031 exchange, the sale is not a taxable event — instead, basis and suspended losses carry to the replacement property.

State Tax Filing for Rental Properties

Do not forget state income taxes. Key considerations:

  • Most states require you to report rental income and allow similar expense deductions
  • Some states do not conform to federal bonus depreciation rules — you may need to add back bonus depreciation on your state return
  • States where your property is located may require you to file a nonresident return (relevant for out-of-state investors)
  • Property taxes are typically handled separately as they are paid locally, not through your income tax return

Your CPA should identify all state filing obligations for your properties.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Mixing personal and rental expenses: Only expenses attributable to the rental property are deductible. If you use part of a property personally, you must allocate expenses.

Failing to depreciate: The IRS requires you to reduce your basis by allowable depreciation even if you fail to take it. When you sell, you are taxed as if you took the deduction. Always claim your depreciation.

Misclassifying improvements as repairs: Review our guide on capital improvements vs repairs to ensure expenses are properly categorized.

Omitting income: All rent received must be reported, including cash payments. The IRS can cross-reference bank deposits against reported income.

Forgetting loan origination fees: Points paid on a rental property mortgage are typically deducted over the loan term, not all at once. Track these separately.

Missing mileage deductions: Property-related driving is deductible. Keep a contemporaneous mileage log.

A Year-End Tax Planning Checklist for Landlords

Before December 31, review these strategies with your CPA:

  • Accelerate deductible expenses into the current year if beneficial (pay December insurance in December, not January)
  • Identify capital improvement projects to complete before year-end
  • Review whether any properties were placed in service this year and verify depreciation start dates
  • Model estimated tax liability and adjust quarterly payments if needed
  • Confirm cost segregation study status for any recent acquisitions
  • Review passive loss carryforward balances
  • Assess whether a property sale in the current year would benefit from releasing suspended losses

Tax filing is the culmination of a year of financial activity. The better your records, the more accurate and advantageous your return will be. Combined with strategic planning and specialized professional guidance, getting this right is a core part of any effort to maximize rental income and after-tax returns.
