Owning rental property comes with risks. A fire can destroy your building.

A tenant can slip on an icy walkway and sue you. A storm can leave your property uninhabitable for months.

Without the right insurance, any of these events could wipe out your investment.

Landlord insurance is different from regular homeowner's insurance. It covers risks specific to renting out property.

Insurance requirements can vary based on landlord-tenant law by state, so check your local regulations. This guide explains every type of coverage you need, what it costs, and how to make sure you are properly protected.

Why Regular Homeowner's Insurance Is Not Enough

If you rent out your property and only have a standard homeowner's policy, you may have no coverage at all. Most homeowner's policies exclude properties that are not owner-occupied.

Did You Know?

Real estate has created more millionaires than any other investment type. About 90% of millionaires have invested in real estate at some point.

If you file a claim and the insurance company discovers you are renting the property out, they can deny the claim entirely.

Landlord insurance, also called a dwelling fire policy or rental property insurance, is designed specifically for properties you rent to others. It covers the unique risks landlords face.

Types of Landlord Insurance Coverage

There are several types of coverage that make up a complete landlord insurance policy. Let us break down each one.

Dwelling Coverage

Dwelling coverage protects the physical structure of your rental property. This includes the building itself, attached structures (like a garage), and built-in appliances.

What it covers:

  • Fire and smoke damage
  • Wind and hail damage
  • Lightning strikes
  • Vandalism
  • Certain types of water damage (like burst pipes)
  • Falling trees or debris

What it does not cover:

  • Flood damage (requires separate flood insurance)
  • Earthquake damage (requires separate earthquake insurance)
  • Normal wear and tear
  • Damage caused by the tenant's negligence (though you can pursue the tenant)

How much do you need? Your dwelling coverage should equal the cost to rebuild the property, not the market value. The cost to rebuild might be higher or lower than what you paid for the property. Get a replacement cost estimate from your insurer or a local contractor.

Liability Coverage

Liability coverage protects you when someone gets injured on your rental property and holds you responsible. This is one of the most important types of coverage for landlords.

What it covers:

  • Medical expenses for someone injured on your property
  • Legal defense costs if you are sued
  • Settlements or judgments against you
  • Property damage you cause to others

Common scenarios:

  • A tenant slips on a wet stairway and breaks their wrist
  • A visitor trips on a cracked sidewalk
  • A faulty railing gives way and someone falls
  • A dog bite occurs on the property (coverage varies)

How much do you need? Most landlord policies offer $100,000 to $500,000 in liability coverage as a starting point. For many landlords, $300,000 to $500,000 is a reasonable minimum. If you own multiple properties or have significant assets to protect, you should consider higher limits or an umbrella policy.

For more on protecting yourself from liability, read our guide on landlord liability protection.

Loss of Rental Income Coverage

Also called "fair rental value" coverage, this pays you when your property cannot be rented because of a covered loss.

How it works: If a fire makes your rental unit uninhabitable, this coverage pays you the rent you would have collected while the property is being repaired. It bridges the gap so you do not lose income during repairs.

What triggers it: The loss of income must result from a covered event. If the property is vacant because you cannot find a tenant, this coverage does not apply. But if a covered disaster forces the tenant out, you are protected.

How long does it last? Most policies cover lost rent for 12 months or until the property is repaired, whichever comes first. Some policies offer shorter or longer periods.

Example: A kitchen fire makes your rental unit unlivable. Repairs take 4 months. Your rent is $1,800 per month. Loss of income coverage pays you $7,200 to replace the rent you missed.

Other Structures Coverage

This covers structures on your property that are not attached to the main building.

Examples:

  • Detached garages
  • Storage sheds
  • Fences
  • Detached carports

Most policies set this coverage at 10% of your dwelling coverage amount. So if your dwelling coverage is $200,000, you would have $20,000 for other structures.

You can usually increase this if needed.

Personal Property Coverage (Landlord's Contents)

This covers items you own that are kept at the rental property for use in the rental business.

Examples:

  • Appliances you provide (refrigerator, washer, dryer, dishwasher)
  • Lawn mowers and maintenance equipment
  • Furniture in furnished rentals
  • Tools stored at the property

What it does not cover: The tenant's personal belongings. That is what renters insurance is for (more on that below).

Umbrella Insurance for Landlords

An umbrella policy provides extra liability coverage beyond what your landlord policy offers. It kicks in when your standard liability coverage is exhausted.

Why you need it: Lawsuits can be expensive. If someone is seriously injured on your property, medical bills and legal damages can exceed $500,000 quickly. A $1 million umbrella policy gives you an extra layer of protection.

What it covers:

  • Liability claims that exceed your landlord policy limits
  • Some claims that your standard policy excludes
  • Legal defense costs

How much does it cost? Umbrella policies are surprisingly affordable. A $1 million umbrella policy might cost $200 to $400 per year. Each additional million typically costs $75 to $150 more.

Who needs it? Any landlord with significant assets or multiple properties should consider an umbrella policy. If you own three or more rental units, it is almost essential. Holding your properties in an LLC adds another layer of protection; see our guide on rental property LLC benefits.

Flood and Earthquake Insurance

Standard landlord policies do not cover flood or earthquake damage. If your property is in a risk zone for either, you need separate coverage.

Flood Insurance

Who needs it: If your property is in a FEMA-designated flood zone, your lender probably requires flood insurance. Even if you are not in a high-risk zone, flooding can happen anywhere. About 25% of flood claims come from outside high-risk areas.

Where to get it: You can buy flood insurance through the National Flood Insurance Program (NFIP) or through private insurers. NFIP policies cap at $250,000 for the building and $100,000 for contents. Private insurers may offer higher limits.

Cost: Flood insurance costs vary widely based on location, elevation, and the property's flood history. Expect to pay $500 to $3,000 per year or more in high-risk areas.

Earthquake Insurance

Who needs it: If your property is in a seismically active area, earthquake insurance is important. California, Washington, Oregon, and Alaska have the highest risk, but earthquakes can happen in other states too.

Cost: Earthquake insurance is expensive, especially in high-risk areas. Policies often have high deductibles (10% to 20% of the coverage amount). Annual premiums can range from $800 to several thousand dollars. If you rent your property on platforms like Airbnb or Vrbo, you may also need short-term rental insurance to cover the additional risks.

Requiring Tenants to Carry Renters Insurance

Smart landlords require tenants to carry renters insurance. This protects both you and the tenant.

Why You Should Require It

Protects the tenant's belongings. Your landlord policy does not cover the tenant's personal property. If there is a fire, the tenant loses everything unless they have renters insurance.

Reduces your liability. If a tenant's guest is injured, the tenant's liability coverage can help cover the claim before your insurance gets involved.

Reduces frivolous claims. Tenants with insurance are less likely to pursue claims against you for losses that their own policy would cover.

It is affordable. Renters insurance typically costs $15 to $30 per month. Most tenants can easily afford it.

How to Require It

  • Add a clause in your lease requiring the tenant to maintain renters insurance
  • Specify a minimum coverage amount (typically $100,000 in liability)
  • Require the tenant to name you as an "additional interest" on their policy so you are notified if the policy is canceled
  • Ask for proof of insurance before move-in and at lease renewal

Can You Require It?

In most states, yes. Landlords can require renters insurance as a condition of the lease.

A few states have restrictions, so check your local laws.

How to Compare Landlord Insurance Policies

Not all landlord insurance policies are the same. Here is what to look at when comparing options.

Replacement Cost vs. Actual Cash Value

  • Replacement cost pays to rebuild or repair using current prices, without deducting for depreciation. This gives you more money when you file a claim.
  • Actual cash value (ACV) pays the depreciated value of the damaged item. A 10-year-old roof replaced under ACV would pay far less than the cost of a new roof.

Always choose replacement cost if you can afford it. The difference in premium is usually worth it. NerdWallet's landlord insurance comparison can help you evaluate policies side by side.

Deductibles

The deductible is the amount you pay out of pocket before insurance kicks in. Higher deductibles mean lower premiums, but more out-of-pocket cost when you file a claim.

Common deductibles range from $500 to $2,500. Consider how much you can comfortably pay in an emergency when choosing your deductible.

Exclusions

Read the exclusions section carefully. Every policy has events it does not cover.

Common exclusions include:

  • Flood and earthquake (as discussed above)
  • Mold damage (unless added as an endorsement)
  • Bed bug infestations
  • Acts of war or terrorism
  • Intentional damage by the policyholder
  • Government actions (like eminent domain)

Endorsements and Riders

Endorsements (also called riders) add coverage to your base policy. Common endorsements for landlords include:

  • Guaranteed replacement cost - Pays to rebuild even if costs exceed your coverage limit
  • Ordinance or law coverage - Pays the extra cost of rebuilding to meet current building codes
  • Water backup coverage - Covers damage from sewer or drain backups
  • Equipment breakdown - Covers HVAC, boiler, or other equipment failures

What Affects the Cost of Landlord Insurance

Several factors determine how much you pay for landlord insurance.

Property Factors

  • Location - Properties in areas prone to natural disasters, crime, or severe weather cost more to insure
  • Age of the property - Older properties generally cost more because they are more likely to have problems
  • Construction type - Brick and concrete are cheaper to insure than wood frame
  • Condition - Well-maintained properties cost less
  • Property value - Higher-value properties need more coverage, which costs more

Coverage Factors

  • Coverage amounts - Higher limits mean higher premiums
  • Deductible - Higher deductibles lower your premium
  • Endorsements - Each add-on increases cost
  • Claims history - Past claims can raise your rates

Landlord Factors

  • Number of properties - Insuring multiple properties with one carrier often gets you discounts
  • Experience - Some insurers offer discounts to experienced landlords
  • Credit score - Better credit can mean lower premiums in most states
  • Claims history - Filing many claims raises your rates

Average Costs

Landlord insurance typically costs 15% to 25% more than a standard homeowner's policy for the same property. As a rough guide:

  • Single-family rental - $1,000 to $2,500 per year
  • Multi-family (2-4 units) - $1,500 to $4,000 per year
  • Larger properties - Commercial policies vary widely

Remember, insurance is a tax-deductible expense for rental properties. For more on deductions, see our guide to rental property tax deductions.

The Claims Process

When damage occurs, knowing how to file a claim properly makes a big difference.

Step 1: Document the Damage

As soon as it is safe, take photos and videos of all damage. Document everything before you start cleaning up or making repairs.

Step 2: Prevent Further Damage

You have a duty to prevent additional damage. This might mean covering a broken window with plastic or turning off water to a burst pipe.

Keep receipts for any emergency expenses. Your policy usually covers these costs.

Step 3: Contact Your Insurance Company

Call your insurer as soon as possible. Most policies require you to report claims promptly.

Have your policy number ready and be prepared to describe what happened.

Step 4: Work With the Adjuster

Your insurance company will send an adjuster to inspect the damage. Be present during the inspection if possible.

Share your photos, receipts, and any other documentation.

Step 5: Get Repair Estimates

Get at least two or three repair estimates from licensed contractors. Your insurer may have preferred contractors, but you are usually not required to use them.

Step 6: Review the Settlement

Your insurer will send you a settlement offer. Review it carefully.

If you disagree with the amount, you can negotiate or hire a public adjuster to advocate for you.

Step 7: Make Repairs

Once you accept the settlement, make the repairs promptly. Your loss of income coverage is usually tied to how long repairs take, so there is an incentive to move quickly.

How Landlord Insurance Connects to Property Management

If you use a property management company, they can help with insurance in several ways.

Risk reduction: Good property managers maintain the property, handle repairs quickly, and enforce lease terms. This reduces the chance of claims.

Documentation: Property managers keep detailed records of property condition, maintenance, and tenant interactions. This documentation is valuable if you need to file a claim.

Claims support: When damage occurs, a property manager can document the damage, get repair estimates, and oversee the repair process.

Common Insurance Mistakes Landlords Make

Mistake 1: Using a Homeowner's Policy

As we discussed earlier, a homeowner's policy does not cover rental properties. If you rent out your home and keep your homeowner's policy, you may have zero coverage.

Mistake 2: Underinsuring the Property

If your dwelling coverage is too low, you will not have enough money to rebuild after a major loss. Review your coverage amounts every few years to keep up with construction costs.

Mistake 3: Skipping Liability Coverage

Some landlords skimp on liability coverage to save money. This is risky.

One serious injury can result in a lawsuit that exceeds your coverage.

Mistake 4: Not Requiring Renters Insurance

When tenants do not have renters insurance, they are more likely to come after you for losses that their own policy would have covered.

Mistake 5: Not Reviewing the Policy Annually

Your insurance needs change over time. Property values increase, you add improvements, or laws change.

Review your policy each year to make sure it still fits.

Bottom Line

Landlord insurance is not optional. It is a critical part of protecting your rental property investment.

The right policy covers your building, your liability, your lost income, and more.

Start by making sure you have the basics: dwelling coverage, liability, and loss of income. Then consider add-ons like umbrella insurance, flood coverage, and equipment breakdown.

Require your tenants to carry renters insurance. And review your policy every year.

The cost of insurance is small compared to the cost of being uninsured when disaster strikes. For legal context on what coverage your state may require, Nolo's landlord insurance guides are a helpful starting point.

Protect your investment so one bad event does not undo years of hard work.
