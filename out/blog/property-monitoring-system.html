One of the most significant challenges for rental property owners — particularly those managing properties across multiple locations — is maintaining awareness of what is happening at each property between visits. A pipe leaks for three days before anyone notices. A heating system fails during a cold snap. A vacant unit gets broken into. These scenarios share a common thread: if the landlord had known sooner, the outcome would have been far less costly.

Remote property monitoring systems address this gap by deploying sensors and smart devices that continuously monitor property conditions and send alerts when something needs attention — without requiring the landlord to be physically present.

This guide covers the components of a comprehensive remote monitoring system, recommended technology, and how to build a monitoring infrastructure that scales across a rental portfolio.

What Remote Property Monitoring Can Detect

A well-designed monitoring system can alert you to:

  • Water leaks — At water heaters, supply lines, under sinks, from HVAC drain pans
  • Freeze conditions — Temperature drops that put pipes at risk
  • HVAC failure — Loss of heat or cooling, suggesting a system malfunction
  • Power outages — Loss of power to the property
  • Unauthorized access — Motion detection or access alerts in vacant properties
  • Smoke or fire — Smoke detector alerts per CPSC fire safety guidelines
  • Carbon monoxide — CO detector alerts with remote notification
  • Basement flooding — Water level detection in basement areas
  • High humidity — Conditions that could lead to mold growth
  • Energy usage anomalies — Unusually high consumption suggesting HVAC issues or unauthorized occupancy

Building Blocks of a Remote Monitoring System

1. Smart Thermostats (Temperature Monitoring)

A smart thermostat provides continuous temperature monitoring — alerting you when the unit temperature drops below a set threshold (important for freeze protection) or when the HVAC system is unable to maintain the set temperature (suggesting failure).

Best options: Google Nest, ecobee, Honeywell Home T6

A smart thermostat is typically the first monitoring device to install at any rental property. See our detailed guide on smart thermostats for rental units.

2. Water Leak Sensors

Water sensors placed near high-risk appliances detect leaks before they cause significant damage. They are inexpensive, easy to install, and provide alerts within seconds of detecting moisture.

Best options: Moen Flo sensors, Govee WiFi sensors, SmartThings Water sensors

For detailed guidance on water sensor selection and placement, see our guide on water leak detection for landlords.

3. Smoke and CO Detectors with Remote Alerts

Traditional smoke and CO detectors alarm audibly but do not notify the landlord. Smart connected detectors (or adapters for existing detectors) can send push notifications to your phone:

  • Nest Protect — Combination smoke/CO detector with mobile alerts; excellent for rental properties
  • First Alert OneLink Safe & Sound — Combination smoke/CO with Alexa integration and mobile alerts
  • Roost Smart Battery — A smart battery that fits many existing smoke detectors and adds Wi-Fi notification capability without replacing the detector

4. Smart Electrical Monitoring

Smart electrical monitors track power usage at the circuit or whole-home level:

  • Sense Energy Monitor — Installs in the electrical panel; tracks whole-home energy usage, identifies individual appliance signatures, and alerts you to power anomalies
  • Emporia Energy Monitor — Monitors individual circuits; useful for tracking HVAC usage, identifying failing appliances, and detecting unusual power draw

Use cases for landlords:

  • Detect HVAC systems running continuously (can indicate thermostat failure or refrigerant issue)
  • Monitor vacant properties for unauthorized occupancy (power usage without known tenants)
  • Track utility expenses for utility-paid properties

5. Motion Sensors (Vacant Properties)

For vacant units, motion sensors alert you to unauthorized entry or occupancy:

  • SimpliSafe sensors — Wireless, easy to deploy temporarily during vacancy
  • Wyze Motion Sensor — Inexpensive, Wi-Fi connected, good for basic intrusion detection
  • Ring Alarm Motion Detector — Integrates with Ring ecosystem and Amazon monitoring

Configure motion sensors to send alerts only during periods when the unit should be vacant. Remove or reconfigure before new tenant move-in.

6. Door and Window Sensors

Contact sensors on entry doors and accessible windows alert you when they open:

  • Useful for vacant properties to detect unauthorized entry
  • Can be used to confirm contractor entry at a specific time
  • Work in conjunction with smart locks for complete entry monitoring

7. Wi-Fi Connected Cameras

For vacant properties or common areas, cameras provide visual verification when other sensors trigger:

  • Arlo Pro 4 — Outdoor-rated, solar-powered option available
  • Ring Stick Up Cam — Easy installation, battery or wired options
  • Wyze Cam Outdoor — Budget-friendly

Note: Camera placement must comply with tenant privacy requirements. See our guide on security camera rules for rental properties.

Hub-Based vs Cloud-Based Systems

Cloud-Based (Wi-Fi Direct)

Many modern smart devices connect directly to Wi-Fi and communicate via the manufacturer's cloud service. Pros:

  • Simple setup — no hub required
  • Each device has its own app
  • Widely available

Cons:

  • Requires a reliable Wi-Fi signal at each device location
  • Multiple apps for different devices
  • Cloud service dependency (if manufacturer discontinues service, device may become non-functional)

Hub-Based (Z-Wave or Zigbee)

A smart home hub (SmartThings, Hubitat) acts as a central controller for Z-Wave and Zigbee devices. Pros:

  • Works without internet (local processing)
  • Single platform for all devices regardless of brand
  • More reliable for critical applications like leak detection

Cons:

  • Requires hub installation at each property
  • More complex initial setup

For landlords with multiple properties: A hub-based system offers more reliable and consistent monitoring. Hubitat is particularly popular with landlords because it processes automation locally without cloud dependency.

Monitoring Platforms for Multi-Property Landlords

Several platforms are designed specifically for landlords managing monitoring across multiple properties:

SmartRent

SmartRent is a proptech platform designed for institutional and professional landlords:

  • Smart thermostat, smart lock, water sensor, and leak detection integration
  • Single dashboard for all properties
  • Designed for multi-family buildings (20+ units)
  • Integration with major property management software (Yardi, RealPage, AppFolio)
  • White-label resident app

Dwelo

Similar to SmartRent; comprehensive smart home platform designed for multi-family rentals with centralized monitoring and management.

Alertify (formerly Party Squasher)

Alertify is designed for short-term rental operators:

  • Noise monitoring
  • Occupancy detection
  • Smoking detection (cigarette smoke sensors)
  • Integration with Airbnb and VRBO platforms

Self-Managed Dashboard

For smaller portfolios (2-20 properties), a self-managed approach works well:

  • Choose a smart home platform (SmartThings, Apple Home, or Google Home)
  • Add compatible sensors from major manufacturers
  • Configure automated alerts for all critical conditions
  • Monitor from a single app

Telecommunications Infrastructure

For monitoring systems to work, each property needs:

Reliable Wi-Fi

At minimum, a reliable broadband connection and router at each monitored property. Consider:

  • Eero or Google Nest WiFi — Mesh Wi-Fi systems that provide whole-property coverage reliably
  • Dedicated router (separate from tenant's service) for landlord monitoring devices

Cellular Backup

For critical monitoring (vacant properties, high-risk units), cellular backup ensures monitoring continues during internet outages. Devices like the SimpliSafe cellular communicator or LTE backup modems provide this capability.

Power Backup

Smart home hubs and routers lose function during power outages unless backed up. A small UPS (uninterruptible power supply) for your router and hub ensures monitoring continues during short power outages.

Privacy and Tenant Communication

Disclosure

Disclose all monitoring devices in your lease. Specifically describe:

  • What devices are installed (thermostats, smoke/CO detectors, water sensors, motion sensors)
  • What each monitors
  • How alerts are handled
  • That cameras (if any) are located only in exterior/common areas

Tenant Consent

Include a technology addendum per FTC privacy guidelines that tenants sign. This protects you legally and ensures tenants are not surprised.

During Tenancy

Remove or disable motion sensors and cameras in occupied units (they are only appropriate for vacant properties). Thermostats, water sensors, and smoke/CO detectors can operate throughout the tenancy as they monitor property conditions, not tenant behavior.

Building a Monitoring Rollout Plan

For landlords with existing portfolios who want to add monitoring:

Phase 1 (Immediate protection — $100-$200 per unit):

  • Smart thermostat at each unit
  • Water sensor at water heater and washing machine location

Phase 2 (Expanded coverage — $100-$200 per unit additional):

  • Water sensors at all remaining high-risk locations
  • Connected smoke/CO detector

Phase 3 (Full monitoring — $200-$400 per unit additional):

  • Smart electrical monitoring
  • Hub-based system for reliability
  • Motion sensors for vacant unit periods

Conclusion

Remote property monitoring systems are no longer futuristic technology — they are practical, affordable, and increasingly essential for landlords who want to protect their assets between visits. Starting with the highest-value sensors (smart thermostat and water leak sensors) provides immediate protection at modest cost. Building out to a comprehensive monitoring platform over time creates a robust early-warning system that dramatically reduces emergency repairs, tenant disruption, and insurance claims.
