Premises Liability for Landlords: Reducing Risk

Premises liability holds property owners responsible for injuries that occur on their property due to unsafe conditions. For landlords, this means you can be sued when a tenant, guest, or visitor is injured because of a hazard you knew about or should have known about. Premises liability claims are among the most common and costly lawsuits landlords face.

This guide explains your duty of care, the most common claims, and practical strategies to reduce your exposure.

What Is Premises Liability?

Premises liability is a legal theory under landlord-tenant law that holds property owners and managers responsible for injuries caused by dangerous conditions on their property. To win a premises liability claim against a landlord, the injured party typically must prove:

  1. You owned or controlled the property where the injury occurred
  2. A dangerous condition existed on the property
  3. You knew or should have known about the dangerous condition
  4. You failed to fix the condition or warn about it within a reasonable time
  5. The condition caused the injury

The Duty of Care

Your duty of care depends on who is on the property:

  • Tenants (invitees) — You owe the highest duty of care. You must regularly inspect, maintain, and repair the property to keep it reasonably safe.
  • Tenant guests (invitees) — Same high duty of care as tenants
  • Trespassers — Lowest duty of care, but you still cannot create intentional hazards (except limited "attractive nuisance" obligations toward children in some states)

Most Common Premises Liability Claims

Slip, Trip, and Fall

The most frequent claim type. Common causes include:

  • Ice and snow on walkways and stairs
  • Wet floors in lobbies and laundry rooms
  • Uneven sidewalks, parking lots, or steps
  • Poor lighting in hallways, stairwells, and parking areas
  • Loose handrails or missing stair treads
  • Torn carpet or uneven flooring
  • Debris in common areas

Inadequate Security

Claims that the landlord failed to provide reasonable security, leading to criminal activity:

  • Broken locks on exterior doors or gates
  • Non-functioning security cameras or lighting
  • Failure to address known criminal activity
  • Broken or missing locks on individual units
  • Unrestricted access to common areas

Structural Defects

Claims arising from building defects:

  • Collapsing decks, balconies, or stairways
  • Falling ceiling tiles or fixtures
  • Broken windows or doors
  • Defective elevators
  • Collapsing retaining walls

Environmental Hazards

  • Lead paint exposure (especially dangerous for children)
  • Mold from unaddressed water intrusion
  • Carbon monoxide from faulty heating systems
  • Asbestos exposure during renovations
  • Pest infestations causing health issues

Defective Conditions in Common Areas

  • Playground equipment failures
  • Swimming pool hazards (missing fences, broken drains)
  • Laundry room equipment malfunctions
  • Fitness room equipment failures
  • Mailroom or entry area hazards

High-Risk Areas on Rental Properties

Focus your maintenance and inspection efforts on these areas:

Stairways and Walkways

  • Inspect weekly during winter for ice
  • Repair uneven surfaces promptly
  • Ensure handrails are secure and code-compliant
  • Maintain adequate lighting

Parking Lots

  • Fill potholes and repair cracked pavement
  • Maintain lighting to prevent dark areas
  • Mark speed bumps and curbs clearly
  • Remove snow and ice promptly

Common Area Entries

  • Ensure doors close and lock properly
  • Keep floors clean and dry
  • Maintain working lighting
  • Remove tripping hazards

Outdoor Amenities

  • Fence swimming pools to code
  • Inspect playground equipment regularly
  • Maintain BBQ and patio areas
  • Trim trees and address dead branches

Reducing Your Liability Exposure

Proactive Maintenance

The single most effective strategy. Most premises liability claims arise from conditions that existed for long enough that you should have discovered and fixed them.

  • Follow a rigorous preventive maintenance schedule
  • Inspect common areas weekly
  • Address reported hazards within 24 hours
  • Document all inspections and repairs

Regular Inspections

Create a documented inspection program:

  • Weekly common area walkthroughs
  • Monthly safety system checks (lighting, locks, fire equipment)
  • Seasonal hazard inspections (ice, drainage, HVAC)
  • Annual comprehensive property inspections

See our property inspection requirements guide for detailed checklists.

Prompt Hazard Response

When you become aware of a hazard:

  1. Address it immediately if possible (remove the debris, salt the ice)
  2. If immediate repair is not possible, warn people (post signs, block access)
  3. Schedule the repair as quickly as possible
  4. Document the hazard, the warning measures, and the repair

Insurance Protection

  • Maintain adequate landlord insurance with at least $500,000 liability coverage
  • Carry umbrella insurance for catastrophic claims
  • Require renters insurance from tenants
  • Verify contractor insurance before any work begins

Documentation

Document everything related to property safety:

  • Inspection dates and findings
  • Maintenance requests and completion dates
  • Hazard reports and response actions
  • Safety improvements and upgrades
  • Incident reports for any injuries on the property

This documentation is your defense if a claim is filed. It demonstrates that you met your duty of care.

When You Are Sued

If a premises liability claim is filed:

  1. Notify your insurance company immediately — Most policies require prompt notification
  2. Preserve all evidence — Do not destroy or alter anything related to the claim
  3. Do not admit fault — Do not apologize or accept responsibility (your insurer handles this)
  4. Cooperate with your insurer's investigation — Provide documentation and access
  5. Consult with your attorney — Especially for claims exceeding your insurance limits

Key Takeaways

Premises liability is an inherent risk of owning rental property. You cannot eliminate it, but you can significantly reduce it through proactive maintenance, regular inspections, prompt hazard response, and thorough documentation. When combined with proper insurance coverage, these practices protect both your tenants and your financial assets.
