23:T3093,<p>The question every landlord faces when preparing a unit for market: should you furnish it or rent it empty? The answer depends heavily on your market, target tenant, hold strategy, and willingness to manage additional complexity. Get it right, and furnished rentals can earn 15–50% more than their unfurnished equivalents. Get it wrong, and you'll spend more on furniture and turnover than you'll ever recover in premium rent.</p>
<p>This guide gives you a clear framework to make the right choice for your specific property.</p>
<h2 id="the-income-premium-how-much-more-can-furnished-rentals-earn">The Income Premium: How Much More Can Furnished Rentals Earn?</h2>

<p>The premium is highest in markets with strong demand from:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Corporate relocatees:</strong> Employees on temporary assignments (3–12 months)</li>
<li><strong>Travel nurses and medical professionals:</strong> On 13-week hospital contracts</li>
<li><strong>Graduate students and visiting academics:</strong> Semester or year-long placements</li>
<li><strong>Executives and consultants:</strong> On project-based deployments</li>
</ul>
<p>In standard suburban markets catering to long-term family renters, the furnished premium shrinks to 5–15%—often not worth the investment.</p>
<h2 id="furnished-rental-cost-analysis">Furnished Rental Cost Analysis</h2>
<p>Before calculating your profit, understand the true cost of furnishing a unit:</p>
<h3 id="one-time-furniture-investment">One-Time Furniture Investment</h3>
<p><strong>Studio (400 sq ft):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Bed frame, mattress, nightstand: $600–$1,200</li>
<li>Sofa or loveseat: $400–$900</li>
<li>Dining table and chairs: $300–$600</li>
<li>Desk and chair: $200–$400</li>
<li>Kitchen essentials (cookware, dishes, utensils): $300–$600</li>
<li>Bedding, towels, window treatments: $400–$700</li>
<li><strong>Total studio: $2,200–$4,400</strong></li>
</ul>
<p><strong>1-Bedroom (600–750 sq ft):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>All above plus additional bedroom furniture: $3,500–$6,500</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>2-Bedroom (900–1,100 sq ft):</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Full furnishing: $5,500–$10,000</li>
</ul>
<p>These are budget-to-mid-range estimates. Luxury furnished units in premium markets may invest $15,000–$30,000 in high-end furniture and staging.</p>
<h3 id="ongoing-furnished-rental-costs">Ongoing Furnished Rental Costs</h3>
<p>Beyond the initial investment, furnished rentals incur higher operating costs:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Replacement/repair:</strong> Expect 15–25% of furniture value annually as turnover damages and wears items</li>
<li><strong>Cleaning between tenancies:</strong> $150–$400 per turnover (more intensive than unfurnished)</li>
<li><strong>Storage (if tenant wants to bring own furniture):</strong> Rarely practical; most furnished tenants want everything included</li>
<li><strong>Inventory management:</strong> Time to track, replace, and maintain furnishings</li>
</ul>

<h2 id="tenant-profile-differences">Tenant Profile Differences</h2>
<p>The type of tenant you attract changes significantly with furnished vs. unfurnished:</p>
<h3 id="unfurnished-tenants">Unfurnished Tenants</h3>
<ul>
<li>Typical lease term: 12+ months (often longer)</li>
<li>Profile: Families, established professionals, long-term residents</li>
<li>Turnover frequency: Every 2–4 years on average</li>
<li>Vacancy risk: Lower</li>
<li>Damage risk: Moderate (typical wear and tear)</li>
<li>Management intensity: Low</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="furnished-tenants">Furnished Tenants</h3>
<ul>
<li>Typical lease term: 1–6 months (short-to-medium term)</li>
<li>Profile: Relocating employees, travel nurses, students, temporary workers</li>
<li>Turnover frequency: Every 1–4 months for short-term; every 6–12 months for medium-term</li>
<li>Vacancy risk: Higher (more frequent re-leasing required)</li>
<li>Damage risk: Higher (more tenants, more wear on furnishings)</li>
<li>Management intensity: Moderate to high</li>
</ul>
<p>The shorter lease cycles of furnished rentals mean more time and cost marketing and screening tenants. Factor this into your true cost analysis.</p>
<h2 id="market-demand-check-is-there-a-market-for-furnished-rentals">Market Demand Check: Is There a Market for Furnished Rentals?</h2>
<p>Before furnishing, validate demand in your specific market:</p>
<p><strong>Strong indicators of furnished rental demand:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Major hospitals or medical centers nearby (travel nurse market)</li>
<li>Large corporate campuses or office parks (relocation market)</li>
<li>University or college nearby (faculty, grad students, visiting researchers)</li>
<li>Military base or government installation (PCS move market)</li>
<li>Tourism district or vacation destination (short-term rental market)</li>
<li>High employee turnover industries (consulting, tech, finance)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>How to check:</strong> Search furnished rentals on Furnished Finder, Airbnb (monthly stays), Zillow (furnished filter), and corporate housing platforms. If there are many listings but many available (high supply, low demand), the market may be saturated.</p>

<h2 id="partial-furnishing-a-middle-ground">Partial Furnishing: A Middle Ground</h2>
<p>Some landlords compromise with partial furnishing:</p>
<ul>
<li>Provide kitchen appliances (refrigerator, dishwasher, microwave, stove) as standard—this is expected by most tenants anyway</li>
<li>Include window treatments and lighting fixtures</li>
<li>Leave the rest unfurnished</li>
</ul>
<p>This approach adds minimal cost, reduces friction for tenants who already have furniture, and doesn't create the turnover/replacement cost burden of full furnishing. The income premium is modest (0–10%) but so is the cost.</p>
<h2 id="when-unfurnished-is-almost-always-better">When Unfurnished Is Almost Always Better</h2>
<p>For most residential landlords in standard markets, unfurnished is the right default:</p>
<ul>
<li>Long-term family and professional tenants don't need furnishings</li>
<li>The premium is too small to justify the cost and management overhead</li>
<li>Long lease stability has real financial value (predictable income, lower vacancy cost)</li>
<li>The risk of furnished-unit damage is higher per tenancy</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Unfurnished wins when:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>You want stable, long-term tenants</li>
<li>Your market doesn't have strong furnished rental demand</li>
<li>You want to minimize management complexity</li>
<li>Your <a href="/blog/rental-pricing-strategy">rental pricing strategy</a> is built around maximum lease stability</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="when-furnished-is-a-strong-strategy">When Furnished Is a Strong Strategy</h2>
<p><strong>Furnished wins when:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li>Your market has clear demand from corporate, medical, or academic tenants</li>
<li>You're near an Airbnb/STR-friendly destination with strong nightly demand</li>
<li>The income premium clearly exceeds your furniture + management costs</li>
<li>You want maximum flexibility (furnished properties are easier to sell owner-occupied if you decide to exit)</li>
<li>You're using the BRRRR or value-add strategy and need to maximize income during a stabilization period</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="tax-considerations-for-furnished-rentals">Tax Considerations for Furnished Rentals</h2>
<p>Furniture purchased for rental properties is depreciable. Under Section 179 and bonus depreciation rules (consult a tax professional for current rules), you may be able to deduct the full cost of furniture in the year purchased rather than depreciating it over 5–7 years.</p>
<p>This accelerated deduction can significantly improve the after-tax return on your furnishing investment. Our <a href="/blog/rental-property-depreciation-guide">rental property depreciation guide</a> covers how to structure these deductions effectively.</p>
<h2 id="summary-making-the-decision">Summary: Making the Decision</h2>
<p>Use this decision framework:</p>
<p><strong>Choose furnished if:</strong> You have documented local demand from corporate/medical/academic tenants, the income premium clearly exceeds 15% above market unfurnished rent, and you're prepared for higher turnover and management.</p>
<p><strong>Choose unfurnished if:</strong> You're in a standard residential market targeting families and long-term professionals, or if the premium is below 10% and doesn't justify the investment.</p>
<p><strong>Choose medium-term furnished if:</strong> You want higher income than unfurnished but less management than daily STR, and you're near hospitals, corporate campuses, or universities.</p>
<p>Run the <a href="/blog/rental-property-cash-flow-analysis">rental property cash flow analysis</a> for both scenarios with realistic vacancy assumptions before deciding. The math will make the right choice clear—and the right choice is always the one that maximizes your net income after all costs, not just your headline rent.</p>
