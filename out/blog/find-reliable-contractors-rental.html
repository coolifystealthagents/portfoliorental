How to Find Reliable Contractors for Your Rentals

Finding contractors who show up on time, do quality work, and charge fair prices is one of the biggest operational challenges for rental property owners. A bad contractor can turn a routine repair into a costly disaster, while a great one becomes one of your most valuable business relationships.

This guide covers where to find contractors, how to vet them, and how to build a reliable network that scales with your portfolio.

Where to Find Contractors

Referrals From Other Landlords

The most reliable source is other property owners and managers. Landlord associations, real estate investor meetups, and online forums like BiggerPockets have active communities where members share contractor recommendations.

Ask specifically about:

  • Responsiveness and communication
  • Quality of work on rental-grade projects (not luxury renovations)
  • Pricing consistency
  • Reliability over multiple jobs

Supply House Recommendations

Local plumbing, electrical, and building supply houses interact with contractors daily. Staff can tell you which contractors buy supplies regularly (indicating steady work and established businesses) and which ones have good reputations.

Online Platforms

Platforms like Angi, HomeAdvisor, and Thumbtack provide contractor listings with reviews. Use these as a starting point but verify independently:

  • Check reviews for rental property work specifically
  • Look for contractors who serve multiple landlords
  • Filter for licenses and insurance verification

Property Management Companies

If you use or know property managers in your market, ask who they use. Management companies that handle hundreds of units have already vetted contractors through volume and experience.

Trade Associations

Local chapters of trade associations like the National Association of the Remodeling Industry (NARI) or the Associated Builders and Contractors (ABC) maintain member directories of licensed, insured professionals.

How to Vet Contractors

Verify Licensing

Check your state and local licensing requirements for the type of work you need. Most jurisdictions require licenses for:

  • Electrical work
  • Plumbing
  • HVAC
  • General contracting above a certain dollar threshold
  • Roofing

Verify licenses through your state's contractor licensing board. A valid license confirms minimum competency and provides recourse if something goes wrong.

The SBA's business guide also covers licensing basics for small businesses.

Confirm Insurance

Every contractor working on your property should carry:

  • General liability insurance, Covers damage to your property during work
  • Workers' compensation, Covers injuries to the contractor's employees on your property (see OSHA worker protections for related requirements)
  • Auto insurance, If they drive company vehicles to your properties

Request certificates of insurance directly from the contractor's insurance company, not from the contractor. Certificates from contractors can be outdated or fabricated.

For more on this process, see our contractor insurance verification guide.

Check References

Ask for references from other landlords or property managers, not homeowners. Rental property work has different expectations than residential renovation:

  • Speed matters more than perfection
  • Durability matters more than aesthetics
  • Cost efficiency is critical at scale
  • Tenant coordination skills are important

Call at least three references and ask about the contractor's reliability, communication, and pricing over time.

Review Work History

If possible, inspect completed work at another property. Look for:

  • Clean, professional workmanship
  • Proper materials used
  • Code compliance
  • Attention to detail in less visible areas (behind walls, under sinks)

Start With a Small Job

Before trusting a new contractor with a major project, test them on a smaller job. Evaluate:

  • Did they show up when they said they would?
  • Did they communicate proactively about timing and costs?
  • Was the work quality acceptable?
  • Did the final invoice match the estimate?
  • Did they clean up after themselves?

Red Flags to Watch For

  • No written estimates, Professional contractors provide written quotes
  • Demanding large upfront payments, Standard practice is a small deposit (10-30%) with the balance due on completion
  • No license or insurance, Walking liability risk for your property
  • Cannot provide references, Either new or has a poor track record
  • Significantly below-market pricing, Often indicates cut corners, unlicensed workers, or bait-and-switch billing
  • Poor communication, If they are hard to reach during the hiring process, it only gets worse during the job
  • Pressure to decide immediately, Reliable contractors do not need high-pressure sales tactics
  • No written contract, Every job should have a written scope, timeline, and cost agreement

Building Long-Term Contractor Relationships

Pay Promptly

Contractors who get paid on time focus on your jobs. Pay invoices within the agreed terms, typically net-15 or net-30.

If cash flow is tight, communicate proactively rather than delaying payments.

Provide Consistent Volume

Contractors value steady work. If you can provide regular business, you become a priority client.

Bundle non-urgent work to create efficient schedules for your contractors.

Communicate Clearly

Provide clear scope descriptions, property access instructions, and tenant contact information. The less time a contractor spends figuring out logistics, the better your pricing and turnaround.

Give Honest Feedback

If work does not meet expectations, address it directly and promptly. Good contractors want to know about issues so they can correct them.

Bad contractors will not fix problems regardless of feedback, which tells you everything you need to know.

For more on managing your contractor relationships at scale, see our vendor management best practices guide.

Scaling Your Contractor Network

As your portfolio grows, you need multiple contractors in each trade to avoid single points of failure.

Primary and Backup Contractors

Maintain at least two contractors for each critical trade:

  • Plumbing, Emergencies cannot wait for one plumber's schedule
  • Electrical, Safety issues require immediate response
  • HVAC, Seasonal demand means your primary may be booked
  • General handyman, Turnover work has tight timelines

Geographic Coverage

If your properties span multiple areas, build separate contractor networks for each zone. A contractor two hours away will not respond to emergencies efficiently.

Specialty Contractors

Beyond general trades, identify specialists for:

  • Appliance repair
  • Locksmith services
  • Pest control
  • Tree service
  • Cleaning crews for turnovers

Key Takeaways

Finding reliable contractors takes upfront effort but pays dividends across your entire portfolio. Source candidates through referrals, verify credentials independently, test with small jobs before major projects, and invest in long-term relationships with your best performers.

A strong contractor network is a competitive advantage that reduces costs, speeds turnovers, and keeps your tenants satisfied.
