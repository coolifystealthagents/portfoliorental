Running Google Ads for Rental Properties

When a prospective tenant types "two-bedroom apartment in Denver" into Google, the first results they see are often paid ads. If you are not advertising there, a competitor is. Google Ads — Google's pay-per-click advertising platform — gives landlords and property managers the ability to appear at the very top of search results for exactly the queries their ideal tenants are typing.

Unlike organic search, which can take months to build through SEO, Google Ads deliver results immediately. You set a budget, define your keywords, write your ads, and your listing appears within hours. You pay only when someone clicks.

This guide walks through the practical realities of using Google Ads for rental properties — including whether it makes financial sense for your situation, how to set up campaigns that do not waste money, and how to measure whether your ads are actually working.

Is Google Ads Right for Your Rental Business?

Google Ads is not the right tool for every landlord. Before spending money on paid search, consider:

Your vacancy cost. Bankrate covers rental investment costs including vacancy impact. If your property rents for $1,800/month and sits vacant for 30 days, that is $1,800 in lost revenue. If a $300 ad spend fills that unit a week faster, the math works clearly in your favor. If your monthly rent is $650, the calculus is tighter.

Your market competition. In high-demand markets where rentals fill in days, ads may be overkill. In competitive markets with high inventory, ads can be the difference between 7 days vacant and 45.

Your property type. Google Ads works best for landlords and property managers with multiple units or a recurring need to attract tenants. Single-unit landlords who rent infrequently may find the learning curve not worth the investment.

Your alternatives. If free channels like Facebook Marketplace and your Google Business Profile are filling your units reliably, paid search may not be necessary. If you are not getting enough qualified leads, it is worth testing.

As part of your overall rental property marketing strategies, Google Ads works best as a performance accelerator rather than a primary strategy.

Understanding How Google Ads Works

Google Ads operates on an auction system. When a user searches for a term you are bidding on, Google runs an auction among advertisers bidding on that same term. The winner is determined not just by bid amount, but by "Quality Score" — a combination of:

  • Expected click-through rate: How likely someone is to click your ad
  • Ad relevance: How closely your ad matches the search intent
  • Landing page experience: How useful and relevant your landing page is

This matters for landlords because it means a well-crafted, highly relevant ad can outrank a competitor who is bidding more. Quality beats pure spend.

Cost-per-click (CPC) for rental keywords varies significantly by market and keyword. Broad terms like "apartments for rent" in major cities can cost $3-8 per click or more. Longer, more specific phrases — "pet-friendly 2-bedroom apartments Austin with garage" — cost less per click and convert better because they attract more qualified searchers.

Setting Up Your First Campaign: Step by Step

Step 1: Define Your Goal

In Google Ads, choose the right campaign objective. For rental marketing, the most relevant options are:

  • Website traffic: Drives clicks to your property listing or website
  • Leads: Optimizes for form submissions (contact forms, inquiry forms)

Leads campaigns are often more valuable for landlords because you are paying for action, not just visits.

Step 2: Choose Your Campaign Type

For rental properties, Search campaigns are the foundation. These show text ads alongside Google search results when users type relevant queries.

Consider also:

  • Display campaigns: Show banner ads on websites across the Google Display Network. Useful for brand awareness, but lower intent than search.
  • Local campaigns: Specifically designed to drive calls, visits, and inquiries from local users. A strong fit for property management businesses with a physical presence.

Step 3: Set Your Budget

Google Ads budgets are set daily. Start conservatively — $15-30 per day is enough to gather meaningful data — and scale up once you know what is working.

Budget considerations:

  • $15/day: Good for testing and data collection in most markets
  • $30-50/day: Active lead generation for a single property or small portfolio
  • $100+/day: Aggressive campaigning for property management companies with multiple vacancies

You can pause campaigns at any time and only run them when you have a vacancy.

Step 4: Define Your Geographic Targeting

This is critical. You only want your ads to show to people searching for rentals in your specific market. Set your geographic targeting to:

  • Your city and immediate surrounding areas
  • Specific ZIP codes if your property is in a desirable sub-market
  • Commuter zones if your property is near public transit (target the area people commute from)

Exclude irrelevant geographies. If your property is in Austin, there is no reason to show ads to people in Los Angeles.

Step 5: Build Your Keyword List

Keyword selection is where most beginners make costly mistakes. Here is a practical approach:

Core keyword categories for rental properties:

Location + property type:

  • "apartments for rent in [city]"
  • "[neighborhood] apartments"
  • "houses for rent [city]"
  • "condos for rent [city]"

Feature-specific:

  • "pet-friendly apartments [city]"
  • "2 bedroom apartments [city]"
  • "apartments with in-unit laundry [city]"
  • "furnished apartments [city]"

Intent-specific:

  • "rent an apartment in [city]"
  • "affordable apartments [city]"
  • "move-in ready apartments [city]"

Match types: Use a mix of phrase match and exact match keywords. Avoid broad match when starting out — it can cause your ad to show for irrelevant searches and waste budget.

Negative keywords: These are searches you want to exclude. Critical negative keywords for landlords:

  • "for sale"
  • "buy"
  • "purchase"
  • "mortgage"
  • "vacation rental"
  • "sublease" (unless you allow it)
  • "roommate" (unless relevant)

Negative keywords prevent budget waste and improve your Quality Score.

Step 6: Write Your Ads

Each ad group should have 2-3 ads with different copy. Google rotates them and optimizes toward top performers.

Google Responsive Search Ads (RSAs) allow you to provide up to 15 headlines and 4 descriptions. Google mixes and matches these automatically. Write specific, benefit-driven headlines:

Strong headline examples:

  • "2BR Apartments in [City] From $1,450"
  • "Pet-Friendly Units Available Now"
  • "Move In This Month — Limited Availability"
  • "In-Unit Washer/Dryer Included"
  • "Schedule a Showing Today"
  • "[Neighborhood] Apartments — Apply Online"

Description examples:

"Newly renovated apartments in [City]. Updated kitchens, private parking, and responsive maintenance. Apply online in minutes." "Spacious floor plans starting at $1,450/mo. Pet-friendly. Walk to dining and transit. Limited units available — tour today."

Include your key differentiators: Price, location, amenities, and a clear call to action.

Step 7: Build a Strong Landing Page

Your ad is only as good as the page it leads to. A strong rental property landing page includes:

  • Photos of the unit and property
  • Clear rent price and availability date
  • Key amenities and features
  • An easy-to-complete inquiry or application form
  • Your contact information
  • A simple, uncluttered layout

If you send ad traffic to a generic homepage with no clear path to inquire, you will waste money. The landing page should match the ad's promise exactly.

Advanced Campaign Strategies

Ad Scheduling

Run your ads during times when prospective tenants are most active. Typically, rental search peaks:

  • Weekday evenings (after work, 5-9pm)
  • Weekend mornings (9am-1pm)

You can reduce bids or pause ads during low-activity periods (late night, early morning on weekdays) to stretch your budget.

Device Targeting

Mobile devices account for the majority of rental-related searches. Ensure your landing page is fully mobile-responsive. Consider bidding higher on mobile if your analytics show strong mobile conversion rates.

Retargeting

Google's Display Network allows you to show ads to people who have previously visited your website. If someone browsed your property listing but did not inquire, retargeting keeps your property top of mind as they continue their search. Retargeting campaigns typically have much lower CPCs than search campaigns.

Call-Only Campaigns

If your goal is phone calls rather than website visits, call-only campaigns display a phone number prominently and charge per call rather than per click. This works well for landlords who prefer to speak with prospective tenants directly.

Measuring ROI on Google Ads

The most important question: is your ad spend generating positive returns?

Key metrics to track:

Metric What It Tells You
Impressions How many times your ad was shown
Click-through rate (CTR) % of impressions that resulted in a click; aim for 3-8%
Cost per click (CPC) What you pay per visitor
Conversions Inquiries, form submissions, or calls generated
Cost per conversion Total spend ÷ conversions
Conversion rate % of clicks that became inquiries

Calculate your acceptable cost per lead. If you can rent a unit for $2,000/month and it typically takes 10 leads to find a tenant, you can afford up to $200 per lead before paid advertising becomes unprofitable. Most well-run Google Ads campaigns for rental properties generate leads for $15-60 each, well within profitable range for most markets.

Set up conversion tracking in Google Ads to automatically track form submissions and phone calls from your ads. Forbes explains PPC advertising metrics and ROI measurement. Without tracking, you are flying blind.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  1. Not using negative keywords — Your ads show for irrelevant searches and you pay for worthless clicks.
  2. Sending traffic to a poor landing page — Low conversion rates mean high cost per lead.
  3. Too broad geographic targeting — You attract out-of-area leads who are not ready to rent.
  4. No conversion tracking — You cannot measure what is working.
  5. Setting it and forgetting it — Google Ads requires ongoing monitoring and optimization.
  6. Running ads when fully occupied — Pause campaigns the moment your unit is filled.

Combining Google Ads with Organic Strategies

Paid search is most powerful when it supplements, rather than replaces, free marketing channels. While your ads drive immediate traffic, build your property management website SEO simultaneously so you develop long-term organic visibility that costs nothing per click.

Google Ads official help center is comprehensive and free for learning campaign setup.

Conclusion

Google Ads gives landlords and property managers an immediate, measurable way to attract qualified tenants in competitive markets. When set up correctly — with tight geographic targeting, relevant keywords, compelling ad copy, and a strong landing page — it consistently delivers a positive return on investment.

The key is discipline: start with a modest budget, track everything, optimize based on data, and pause campaigns when your units are rented. Do not overpay for broad keywords, and do not neglect the fundamentals of your listing and landing page.

For landlords looking to reduce vacancy time and establish a consistent tenant pipeline, Google Ads deserves a serious look as part of your broader rental property marketing strategies.
