How to Write a Pet Addendum for Your Lease

Pets are present in roughly 70% of American households, which means that if you exclude pet owners from your tenant pool entirely, you're significantly narrowing your market. At the same time, pets introduce real risks to rental properties — carpet damage, odor, scratches, and noise complaints are all legitimate concerns that a poorly managed pet policy fails to address.

The right solution is a well-drafted pet addendum to your lease. This document — signed separately from the main lease but incorporated by reference — defines exactly what is permitted, what is prohibited, and who bears responsibility for any damage or liability. When done well, it lets you open your property to responsible pet owners while protecting your investment.

What Is a Pet Addendum?

A pet addendum is a supplemental document attached to your standard lease agreement that sets out the specific terms and conditions under which a tenant may keep a pet in your rental unit. It becomes part of the binding lease and is enforceable just like any other lease provision.

A well-structured pet addendum addresses:

  • Which pets are approved (by species, breed, weight, and number)
  • Financial terms (pet deposit, pet rent, or both)
  • Tenant responsibilities for pet-related damage and behavior
  • Rules governing pets in common areas
  • Consequences for unauthorized pets or violations
  • The distinction between pets, service animals, and emotional support animals

Pet Deposit vs. Pet Rent: Understanding the Difference

These two fee structures serve different purposes, and many landlords use both.

Pet Deposit

A pet deposit is a one-time, refundable sum collected at move-in to cover potential pet-related damage. At move-out, you can apply it against documented damage beyond normal wear and tear. If no pet damage occurs, you must return it — the same way you handle a standard security deposit.

Key considerations:

  • Many states cap total security deposits, and some states specify whether a pet deposit counts toward that cap or is separate
  • You must follow your state's deposit return rules — the same timelines and itemization requirements that govern security deposits typically apply
  • If your state caps security deposits at two months' rent, and you're already collecting that amount, you may not be able to add a separate pet deposit without running afoul of the limit

For a full breakdown of deposit rules and state-by-state limits, see our security deposit laws guide.

Pet Rent

Pet rent is a non-refundable monthly surcharge — typically $25 to $100 per pet per month — added to the base rent in exchange for allowing a pet. It is not applied to damages; it is pure additional income that compensates you for the increased wear on the unit over time.

Pet rent has become increasingly popular because:

  • It generates ongoing income rather than sitting in an escrow account
  • It is legally simpler — no deposit return requirements or itemization obligations
  • It reflects the ongoing incremental wear pets cause, not just potential damage

Best practice: Use both. Charge pet rent as ongoing income compensation and collect a modest pet deposit as a damage buffer. Make clear in your addendum that the pet deposit covers damage and the pet rent is a non-refundable service fee.

Breed and Weight Restrictions

Many landlords restrict certain dog breeds based on perceived liability risk. Common restricted breeds include Pit Bulls, Rottweilers, Dobermans, and German Shepherds — largely because some insurance carriers exclude coverage for incidents involving these breeds.

Before setting breed restrictions, check:

  1. Your homeowner's or landlord's insurance policy. If your policy excludes liability for certain breeds, that is a legitimate business reason for restricting them.
  2. Your state and local laws. Some jurisdictions have banned breed discrimination in rental housing, or have preempted municipal breed-specific legislation. Consult your landlord-tenant law by state resources for current rules.

Weight limits are another common approach — many landlords set a 25 to 50-pound limit, which effectively limits large breeds without explicitly naming them. This can be simpler to enforce and harder to dispute than a breed list, though a tenant with a well-behaved 60-pound dog may push back.

Whatever restrictions you use, apply them consistently across all applicants to avoid fair housing issues.

Service Animals vs. Emotional Support Animals: Your Legal Obligations

This is one of the most frequently misunderstood areas in pet policy. The law treats service animals, emotional support animals, and pets very differently, and your pet addendum does not apply to the first two categories.

Service Animals

Under the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), service animals are dogs (and in limited cases, miniature horses) individually trained to perform specific tasks for a person with a disability. Examples include guide dogs for the visually impaired, seizure alert dogs, and mobility assistance dogs.

Landlord obligations:

  • You must allow service animals even if you have a no-pets policy
  • You cannot charge a pet deposit or pet rent for a service animal
  • You can ask two questions: (1) Is this a service animal required because of a disability? (2) What task has the animal been trained to perform? You cannot require documentation or certification.

Emotional Support Animals (ESAs)

ESAs are a separate category governed by the Fair Housing Act (FHA), not the ADA. An ESA provides emotional support to a person with a documented mental or emotional disability but does not need to be trained to perform specific tasks.

Landlord obligations under the FHA:

  • You must provide reasonable accommodation for an ESA if the tenant has a disability-related need
  • You cannot charge a pet deposit or pet rent for an ESA
  • You can request documentation — specifically, a letter from a licensed mental health professional confirming the disability-related need
  • You can deny an ESA request if the animal poses a direct threat to the health or safety of others, or if accommodating the animal would cause an undue hardship

The practical implication: Your pet addendum's financial terms, breed restrictions, and weight limits cannot be applied to service animals or properly documented ESAs. These animals must be addressed separately, and your refusal or accommodation decisions must be carefully documented to avoid fair housing liability.

The U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD) provides detailed guidance on assistance animals in housing.

Damage Liability Clauses

Your pet addendum should explicitly assign liability for pet-related damage to the tenant. This matters both for enforcing the lease and for any potential small claims or court proceedings.

Recommended language (adapt with your attorney):

"Tenant accepts full financial responsibility for all damages caused by the approved pet(s), including but not limited to: carpet staining or odor, scratching of floors or trim, damage to screens or doors, and landscaping damage. Pet-related damage shall be assessed at move-out and charged against the pet deposit; if damages exceed the deposit, Tenant shall be liable for the remaining balance."

In addition to the damage clause, include provisions covering:

  • Flea treatment: Require professional flea treatment documentation from a licensed exterminator at move-out if a pet was present
  • Carpet cleaning: Require professional carpet cleaning at move-out, regardless of visible damage
  • Odor remediation: Reserve the right to charge for ozone treatment or other odor remediation if pet odors are present at move-out
  • Noise and nuisance: State that tenants are responsible for ensuring pets do not create a nuisance for neighbors, and that repeated complaints can be grounds for lease violation proceedings

Rules for Common Areas and Outdoor Spaces

If your rental property includes common areas — hallways, laundry rooms, yards, parking lots — spell out the rules clearly:

  • Pets must be leashed in all common areas at all times
  • Tenants must immediately clean up waste on the property and in surrounding areas
  • Pets may not be left unattended in common areas or on balconies/patios
  • Aggressive behavior by any pet toward residents or guests is grounds for immediate revocation of pet privileges

Unauthorized Pet Provisions

Your addendum should specify what happens if a tenant acquires a pet without approval. This is a common issue, and the consequences need to be predetermined and documented.

Typical provisions include:

  • Retroactive application of pet deposit and pet rent from the date the unauthorized pet was acquired
  • Issuance of a lease violation notice with a cure period (typically 10 to 30 days to remove the unauthorized pet or complete the approval process)
  • Grounds for lease termination if the violation is not cured

See our guide on lease agreement templates for more on drafting enforceable lease provisions, and review property management contract templates if you use a property manager who handles these enforcement actions on your behalf.

Sample Pet Addendum Clauses

The following are sample clauses for reference only. Always have an attorney review your lease documents before use, as requirements vary significantly by state and locality.

Approved Pets: "Tenant is approved to keep the following pet(s) on the premises: [Species], [Breed], [Name], [Weight]. No additional pets are permitted without prior written approval from Landlord."

Pet Deposit: "Tenant shall pay a pet deposit of $[amount] upon execution of this Addendum. This deposit is refundable, subject to deduction for pet-related damage as described herein, and shall be returned in accordance with the security deposit return requirements of [State] law."

Pet Rent: "In addition to monthly base rent, Tenant shall pay a non-refundable pet fee of $[amount] per month for each approved pet. This fee is not a deposit and is not applied toward any damages."

Damage Liability: "Tenant is solely responsible for all damages caused by approved or unapproved pets. Damages include, but are not limited to, carpet damage, flooring damage, odor remediation, and pest extermination. Tenant authorizes deduction of such costs from the pet deposit and agrees to pay any remaining balance within 30 days of written notice."

Service Animal Notice: "Nothing in this Addendum shall be construed to limit the rights of persons with disabilities to use service animals or emotional support animals as provided by applicable federal and state law."

Practical Tips for Managing a Pet-Friendly Policy

  • Take move-in photos and video. Document the condition of all flooring, trim, and other surfaces at move-in with the tenant present. This is your baseline for move-out comparisons.
  • Conduct pet-specific inspections. Include pet condition checks in your regular property inspections — look for signs of damage, odor, or unauthorized animals.
  • Require pet owner's liability insurance. Some landlords require tenants with pets to carry renter's insurance with liability coverage. This adds a financial backstop beyond the deposit.
  • Limit to two pets maximum. This is a common threshold that balances accommodation with manageability.

Summary

A well-drafted pet addendum is one of the more important documents in a landlord's toolkit. It expands your potential tenant pool by accommodating pet owners while creating clear, enforceable terms that protect your property.

Key elements to include:

  • Specific pet approval (species, breed, weight, name)
  • Both a refundable pet deposit and non-refundable pet rent
  • Explicit damage liability assigned to the tenant
  • Rules for common areas and outdoor spaces
  • Unauthorized pet enforcement provisions
  • A clear statement about service animal and ESA exemptions under federal law

Get the document reviewed by a real estate attorney in your state before using it. The cost of a legal review is minimal compared to the liability exposure of an unenforceable or legally deficient addendum.
