Key Deductions Every Rental Owner Should Know

Rental property owners enjoy a broad array of deductions that are unavailable to other investors. Mortgage interest, property taxes, insurance premiums, repairs and maintenance, professional fees (accounting, legal, property management), travel to inspect properties, and home office expenses (for those who manage their properties from home) are all potentially deductible against rental income.

The distinction between repairs and improvements is one of the most consequential—and frequently misunderstood—tax concepts for rental property owners. Repairs restore a property to its existing condition and are deductible in the year incurred. Improvements add value or extend useful life and must be capitalized and depreciated over time. The IRS provides guidance through its tangible property regulations, and a qualified CPA can help you make this determination correctly.

Passive activity loss rules govern how rental losses can be used to offset other income. Most investors are subject to these rules, meaning rental losses can only offset rental and other passive income. However, the real estate professional exception allows those who spend more than 750 hours per year in real estate activities—and more time in real estate than in any other profession—to deduct rental losses against ordinary income, a potentially enormous tax benefit.

Section 179 and bonus depreciation provisions allow qualifying personal property within a rental—appliances, carpeting, certain fixtures—to be expensed immediately rather than depreciated over time. Cost segregation studies accelerate the depreciation of components within residential and commercial properties, front-loading deductions that improve current-year cash flow significantly for investors in higher tax brackets.

Depreciation and Cost Segregation Basics

Depreciation is the single most powerful tax benefit available to rental property owners. The IRS allows residential rental properties to be depreciated over 27.5 years on a straight-line basis, meaning a property with a depreciable basis of $275,000 generates $10,000 of paper loss annually—a non-cash deduction that reduces taxable income without any out-of-pocket expense.

The depreciable basis of a rental property is not simply the purchase price. It is the purchase price plus acquisition costs, less the value allocated to land (which is not depreciable) and less any portions expensed in the year of purchase. Getting this calculation right from day one—ideally with the help of a CPA—ensures you capture the full depreciation benefit you are entitled to.

Cost segregation is an engineering-based tax strategy that reclassifies components of a property from the standard 27.5-year schedule to 5, 7, or 15-year schedules, dramatically accelerating early-year deductions. A cost segregation study typically pays for itself many times over in tax savings, particularly for properties acquired at significant cost or undergoing substantial renovation.

Bonus depreciation allows qualifying personal property and certain improvements to be expensed at 100% in the year placed in service, though the bonus depreciation percentage phases down over time under current tax law. Investors who pair cost segregation with bonus depreciation can generate substantial first-year deductions that significantly reduce or eliminate taxable income from rental activities.

Entity Structures and Tax Efficiency

Choosing the right entity structure for rental property ownership is a decision with significant tax, legal, and operational implications. The most common options are sole proprietorship (default for individual ownership), LLCs (popular for liability protection with pass-through taxation), S corporations, and C corporations, each with distinct advantages and drawbacks in the real estate context.

LLCs are the most widely used entity structure for rental property because they provide a liability shield between the property and the owner's personal assets while allowing income and losses to flow through directly to the owner's tax return without entity-level taxation. In many states, LLCs also offer favorable charging order protections that make it more difficult for creditors to reach the underlying assets.

S corporations can be beneficial for investors who also earn active income from real estate activities, as they allow a portion of income to be taken as a distribution rather than a salary, potentially reducing self-employment tax exposure. However, S corps have restrictions—including limits on the number and type of shareholders—that make them less flexible than LLCs for certain investment structures.

Series LLCs, available in some states, allow a single entity to hold multiple properties in separate 'series,' each with its own liability isolation. This can simplify administration compared to creating a separate LLC for each property while still providing the liability segregation that risk-conscious investors seek. As always, entity strategy should be developed in consultation with both a real estate attorney and a CPA.

Working with Tax Professionals

A qualified CPA with specific experience in real estate taxation is one of the most valuable members of any rental investor's advisory team. The complexity of rental income, depreciation, passive activity rules, entity structuring, and 1031 exchange requirements makes DIY tax preparation a false economy that often results in missed deductions or costly errors.

The first conversation with a real estate CPA should happen before you acquire your first property, not after. Pre-acquisition planning determines entity structure, establishes cost basis tracking procedures, and identifies strategies—like cost segregation or short-term rental elections—that must be put in place proactively to be effective.

Real estate attorneys complement CPAs by handling the legal dimensions of investment structure, lease documentation, entity formation, and dispute resolution. Investors who skip legal counsel to save fees routinely pay far more in legal fees later when preventable problems escalate into formal disputes or litigation.

Building ongoing relationships with your advisory team—rather than engaging them reactively at tax time or when a crisis hits—produces better outcomes. Professionals who understand your portfolio, your goals, and your financial picture can provide proactive guidance that anticipates issues before they arise and identifies opportunities that reactive, transactional engagement would miss.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

One of the most common mistakes new landlords make is underestimating the true cost of ownership. When you factor in vacancy, maintenance, capital expenditures, property management fees, insurance, and property taxes, the actual expense ratio is often 40–50% of gross rents—sometimes more. Investors who fail to account for this in their upfront analysis routinely end up with negative cash flow on properties they believed would be profitable.

Over-leveraging is another pitfall that derails otherwise promising portfolios. When investors stretch to acquire as many properties as possible using maximum available debt, they leave themselves with no margin for error. A single job loss, a major repair, or a brief vacancy can trigger a liquidity crisis that forces a distressed sale at exactly the wrong moment in the market cycle. Conservative leverage—even when lenders are willing to offer more—is a hallmark of portfolio longevity.

Neglecting due diligence on a property's physical condition is a mistake that can cost tens of thousands of dollars within the first year of ownership. A thorough pre-purchase inspection by a qualified inspector, combined with contractor walk-throughs for any major systems of concern, is the minimum standard before closing on an investment property. The cost of an inspection is trivially small relative to the potential surprises it prevents.

Many landlords make the mistake of treating their rental properties as a side project rather than a business. This mindset leads to inconsistent systems, reactive rather than proactive management, poor financial tracking, and missed optimization opportunities. Investors who adopt a business-owner mindset—setting performance targets, reviewing financials regularly, and making decisions based on data—consistently outperform those who manage their properties informally.

Frequently Asked Questions

How does depreciation work for rental properties?

Residential rental properties are depreciated over 27.5 years on a straight-line basis. Each year, you can deduct 1/27.5 of the depreciable basis (purchase price plus improvements, minus land value) as a paper loss against rental income, even if the property is appreciating in value. This non-cash deduction is one of the most valuable tax benefits in real estate.

What happens to depreciation when I sell a rental property?

When you sell a rental property, the IRS recaptures the depreciation you have taken over the years and taxes it at a maximum rate of 25% (depreciation recapture tax), separate from and in addition to capital gains tax on any appreciation. A 1031 exchange can defer both depreciation recapture and capital gains taxes by rolling proceeds into a like-kind replacement property.

Do I need an LLC to own rental property?

An LLC is not legally required to own rental property, but many investors choose this structure for the liability protection it offers. An LLC creates a legal separation between the property's liabilities and the owner's personal assets. The decision should be made in consultation with a real estate attorney and CPA, as there are tax, financing, and administrative implications to consider.
