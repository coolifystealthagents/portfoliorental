Reasonable Accommodation Requests in Rental Housing

Reasonable accommodation requests are a core obligation under the Fair Housing Act. When a tenant with a disability asks you to make an exception to a rule, policy, or practice so they can use and enjoy their home, you must take that request seriously. Getting it wrong exposes you to discrimination complaints, lawsuits, and significant financial penalties.

This guide explains what reasonable accommodations are, how to evaluate requests, and how to build a compliant process for your rental properties.

What Is a Reasonable Accommodation?

A reasonable accommodation is a change, exception, or adjustment to a rule, policy, practice, or service that allows a person with a disability to have equal opportunity to use and enjoy their housing.

Accommodations differ from modifications:

  • Accommodations change rules or policies (no physical changes to the property)
  • Modifications change the physical structure of the unit or common areas

Both are required under the Fair Housing Act, but they follow different rules. This guide focuses on accommodations. For physical modifications, see our disability accommodations guide.

Common Accommodation Examples

  • Allowing an emotional support animal despite a no-pet policy
  • Assigning a specific parking space closer to the unit entrance
  • Allowing a live-in aide who is not on the lease
  • Permitting early lease termination due to disability-related relocation needs
  • Adjusting rent payment dates to align with disability benefit deposit schedules
  • Allowing a tenant to receive packages at the office if they cannot access the mailroom

Legal Framework

Federal Requirements

The Fair Housing Act requires landlords to provide reasonable accommodations to tenants and applicants with disabilities. This applies to virtually all residential properties, including single-family homes rented through a broker or agent.

The law defines disability broadly, consistent with the ADA standards. It covers physical and mental impairments that substantially limit one or more major life activities, people with a record of such impairment, and people regarded as having an impairment.

State and Local Laws

Many states and cities extend reasonable accommodation protections beyond federal requirements. Some jurisdictions:

  • Define disability more broadly
  • Impose shorter response timelines
  • Require landlords to engage in an interactive process
  • Provide additional remedies for violations

Always check your local landlord-tenant laws in addition to federal requirements.

How to Evaluate a Reasonable Accommodation Request

Step 1: Recognize the Request

Tenants do not need to use legal terminology. Any communication indicating that a person with a disability needs a change to a rule or policy is a reasonable accommodation request. It can be verbal, written, or made by a third party on behalf of the tenant.

Train your staff to recognize requests even when tenants do not use the words "reasonable accommodation."

Step 2: Determine If the Person Has a Disability

If the disability is obvious or already known, do not request documentation. Only ask for verification when the disability and the need for accommodation are not apparent.

When verification is appropriate, you may request documentation confirming:

  • The person has a disability as defined by law
  • There is a disability-related need for the requested accommodation

You may not ask for medical records, a specific diagnosis, or details about the severity of the condition.

Step 3: Assess Whether the Accommodation Is Reasonable

An accommodation is unreasonable if it would:

  • Impose an undue financial or administrative burden on the landlord
  • Fundamentally alter the nature of the housing provider's operations
  • Pose a direct threat to the health or safety of others that cannot be mitigated

The bar for "unreasonable" is high. Most common accommodation requests are considered reasonable. Courts and HUD's fair housing office have consistently ruled that waiving no-pet policies, adjusting parking assignments, and modifying payment schedules impose minimal burden on landlords.

Step 4: Engage in an Interactive Process

If the specific request is not feasible, you must explore alternatives with the tenant. For example, if a tenant requests a reserved parking spot but none are available, discuss whether a closer general spot or a different arrangement would meet their needs.

The interactive process demonstrates good faith and protects you legally even if you cannot grant the exact request.

Step 5: Respond in Writing

Document your decision and communicate it to the tenant in writing. Include:

  • Whether the accommodation is approved, denied, or if you are proposing an alternative
  • The reasoning behind your decision
  • Next steps or additional information needed

Respond within a reasonable timeframe. While no specific federal deadline exists, 10 to 14 days is widely considered reasonable. Some state laws impose shorter deadlines.

Accommodations You Should Almost Always Grant

These requests are well-established in fair housing law and rarely justify denial:

  • Assistance animals including emotional support animals, regardless of pet policies. See our ESA landlord guidelines for details.
  • Reserved accessible parking when a disability requires closer proximity to the unit
  • Permission for a live-in aide even if the lease limits occupancy
  • Transferring to a more accessible unit when one is available
  • Modified communication methods such as larger-print notices for visually impaired tenants

Accommodations That Require More Analysis

These requests may be reasonable but require case-by-case evaluation:

  • Early lease termination without penalty due to disability-related circumstances
  • Extended time to cure lease violations when disability contributed to the violation
  • Changes to common area usage rules that affect other tenants
  • Permission for structural modifications at the tenant's expense

Documentation and Record-Keeping

Maintain detailed records of every accommodation request and your response. Your file should include:

  1. The initial request (dated)
  2. Any supporting documentation received
  3. Your verification requests and the tenant's responses
  4. Notes from any interactive process discussions
  5. Your written decision with reasoning
  6. Follow-up communications

Store accommodation records separately from general tenant files to protect disability-related information. Only individuals involved in the decision should have access.

Good record-keeping practices align with your broader rental property record-keeping requirements.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Ignoring verbal requests — They count, even without a formal written submission
  • Applying blanket policies — Every accommodation request must be evaluated individually
  • Requiring excessive documentation — Asking for medical records or diagnoses violates fair housing law
  • Delaying responses — Unreasonable delays are treated as constructive denials
  • Charging for accommodations — You cannot charge extra fees for reasonable accommodations
  • Retaliating — Any negative action against a tenant who made an accommodation request is illegal

Key Takeaways

Reasonable accommodation requests are a legal obligation, not a favor. Build a clear process, train your staff, respond promptly, and document everything. When in doubt, err on the side of granting the accommodation or engage a fair housing attorney. The cost of a single discrimination complaint far exceeds the cost of most accommodations.
