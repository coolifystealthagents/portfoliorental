Co-Living Security Deposit Rules: State-by-State Guide for Landlords

Security deposits in co-living properties are more complex than in traditional single-family rentals. With multiple individual tenants on separate leases sharing common spaces, questions arise that standard deposit law doesn't explicitly address: Who is responsible for damage in shared areas? How do you handle deposits when one tenant leaves but others remain? What happens when shared space damage can't be traced to a specific resident? This guide addresses the unique deposit challenges of co-living and provides a state-by-state overview of the key deposit rules landlords must follow.

Security Deposit Fundamentals in Co-Living

In a co-living property where each tenant has an individual lease, each tenant has their own security deposit. Unlike a single family household where one deposit covers the entire unit, co-living operators collect separate deposits from each resident—typically equal to one month's rent per tenant, though state law may allow more.

This individual deposit structure has important practical implications. When one resident moves out, you process their deposit return independently of the other residents who remain. When damage occurs in a shared space, determining which deposit to charge requires investigation and documentation that doesn't exist in traditional rental contexts. These complications make thorough documentation—room-by-room condition records, clear assignment of responsibility in the lease and community agreement, and detailed move-out inspections—more important in co-living than in any other rental model.

The shared space damage attribution problem is real. If your kitchen countertop is damaged between move-in and a specific resident's move-out, but four other residents also have access to that kitchen, which deposit do you charge? The safest approach is to: (1) document the condition of all shared spaces at every individual move-in and move-out; (2) charge the departing resident's deposit only for damage that can be specifically attributed to them or occurred during their tenancy; and (3) spread genuinely unattributable shared space damage across all residents pro-rata if your lease authorizes this. Make sure your lease explicitly addresses shared space deposit liability before this situation arises.

Maximum Deposit Limits by State

State law imposes maximum security deposit limits that apply regardless of whether you're renting a single-family home or a co-living room. These limits are typically expressed as a multiple of monthly rent. Here are the key limits for major co-living markets as of 2026:

California: Maximum two months' rent for unfurnished units; three months' for furnished units. With California's high rents and furnished co-living rooms, landlords can collect meaningful deposits—but must follow strict holding and return requirements.

New York: No statutory maximum for non-stabilized units in most of the state. However, New York City's Housing Stability and Tenant Protection Act of 2019 generally limits deposits to one month's rent for most residential tenancies. Verify NYC-specific rules carefully.

Texas: No statutory maximum security deposit. However, deposits must be returned within 30 days of lease termination with an itemized list of deductions, or 60 days if the landlord is providing an itemized list of damages the tenant disputes.

Florida: No statutory maximum. Deposits must be returned within 15-30 days depending on whether deductions are claimed, with specific notice requirements.

Illinois: No statutory maximum. Chicago has specific local requirements that may differ from state law—always verify local ordinances.

Washington: Maximum two months' rent as of 2023 legislation. Must be held in a trust account separate from operating funds.

Colorado: No statutory maximum for most rental properties. Return deadline is 30 days after lease termination or 60 days if damages are claimed.

Georgia: No statutory maximum. Return deadline is 30 days.

Arizona: No statutory maximum. Return deadline is 14 days.

Massachusetts: Maximum one month's rent security deposit. Strict handling requirements including placing deposit in a separate interest-bearing account, providing a written receipt, and conducting a move-in inspection within 10 days. Massachusetts has some of the most demanding deposit requirements in the country.

For states not listed above, check your state's landlord-tenant statute directly. Nolo's state security deposit laws page provides an up-to-date summary of all 50 states' requirements.

Deposit Holding Requirements

How you hold security deposits matters as much as how much you collect. Most states require that security deposits be held in a dedicated account separate from your operating funds. Co-mingling deposits with operating money—even temporarily—is a violation in most jurisdictions and can result in losing your right to make any deductions from the deposit.

States with the strictest holding requirements (California, Massachusetts, New York, and several others) require that deposits be held in interest-bearing accounts with the interest paid to tenants annually or at move-out. Check your state's specific requirements. According to Investopedia, landlords who fail to comply with deposit holding requirements face statutory penalties including forfeiture of all deposit rights and mandatory return of the full deposit plus damages.

Provide written receipts for all deposits collected. Include in the receipt: the amount collected, the date collected, the bank account where it's held (in states that require this disclosure), and the purpose of the deposit. Keep a copy in the tenant's file. Some states require you to provide the tenant with written notice of the financial institution and account number where the deposit is held within a specified period after collection.

In co-living properties, track each tenant's deposit separately in your accounting system from day one. Using property management software that maintains separate ledgers for each tenant's deposit makes compliance dramatically easier and provides the documentation trail you need if a dispute arises.

Conducting Move-Out Inspections and Making Deductions

The move-out inspection is your primary tool for documenting any damages that warrant security deposit deductions. In co-living properties, you need to conduct two levels of inspection when a tenant leaves: their private room and any shared spaces.

For the private room, compare current condition to the documented move-in condition using your move-in inspection report and photos. Normal wear and tear—scuffs on walls, minor carpet wear in traffic patterns, small nail holes from pictures—cannot be charged against the deposit. Damage beyond normal wear and tear—holes in walls, stains, broken fixtures, missing items—can be charged at the cost of repair.

For shared spaces, photograph the current condition and compare to your baseline documentation. If you can specifically attribute damage to the departing tenant (a witness saw them cause it, it appeared during their tenancy and no other residents had an opportunity to cause it), you can charge their deposit. If you cannot attribute shared space damage, consult your lease language—if you included a shared liability clause, you may be able to charge all remaining and departing residents proportionately.

Return the deposit (with any itemized deductions) within the deadline required by your state. Most states require 14-30 days from lease termination or the tenant vacating. Missing the return deadline often results in forfeiture of your right to make any deductions—you must return the full deposit regardless of actual damages. Set a calendar reminder for the deposit return deadline the moment a tenant gives notice.

Disputing Deposit Deductions: What Landlords Need to Know

Despite careful documentation, some tenants will dispute your deposit deductions—claiming damage was pre-existing, that charges are excessive, or that you missed the return deadline. Understanding how to handle these disputes protects both your finances and your time.

When a tenant disputes a deduction, respond in writing within a few days. Provide your documentation: move-in photos, move-out photos, vendor receipts or repair estimates, and the specific clause in your lease authorizing the deduction. Most deposit disputes resolve at this stage when landlords can demonstrate clear before-and-after documentation. Tenants who see their own signature on a move-in condition report documenting the absence of the damage they're now claiming typically drop the dispute.

For unresolved disputes, the venue depends on the amount involved. Small claims court—which handles most tenant deposit disputes—has jurisdiction limits that vary by state, typically $5,000-$10,000. The process is relatively simple: file a claim, serve the tenant, attend the hearing, and present your documentation. You don't need an attorney for small claims court, though it helps to have organized documentation and a clear timeline.

Some states impose penalties on landlords who improperly withhold deposits, including mandatory return of two or three times the withheld amount. This is why procedural compliance—correct deposit accounting, timely return, proper itemization—matters as much as the substantive merits of your deduction claims. A landlord who correctly identifies legitimate damage but misses the return deadline may owe more in penalties than the damage was worth.

Build dispute prevention into your move-out process. Offer residents a pre-move-out walkthrough two weeks before their departure date. Walk through their room and any shared spaces they're responsible for together. Document what, if anything, needs to be addressed before they leave. Residents who have this walkthrough almost always address the issues before move-out, eliminating deposit disputes entirely.

Key Takeaways

  • Co-living requires separate security deposits for each tenant on an individual lease; document shared space condition at every individual move-in and move-out to support any damage attribution.
  • Maximum deposit limits range from one month's rent (New York City, Massachusetts) to no statutory maximum (Texas, Florida, Georgia)—always verify your state's current limit.
  • Hold deposits in a dedicated separate account, provide written receipts, and return deposits with itemized deductions within your state's required deadline—missing the deadline typically results in forfeiture of all deduction rights.
