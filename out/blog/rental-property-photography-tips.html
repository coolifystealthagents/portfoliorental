Great photos are the number one thing that gets renters to click on your listing. Studies show that listings with high-quality photos get two to three times more views than those with dark, blurry, or poorly framed images. Yet many landlords rush through photos or skip them altogether.

You do not need expensive equipment or professional training to take great rental property photos. With the right techniques and a little preparation, you can create listing photos that attract quality tenants and help you fill vacancies faster.

This guide covers everything you need to know about rental property photography, from equipment and lighting to staging and editing.

Why Great Photos Matter

Before we get into the how-to, let us talk about why photos deserve your attention.

Renters scroll through dozens of listings at a time. They spend about two seconds on each listing before deciding to click or keep scrolling. Your photos are what make them stop.

Great listing photos help you:

  • Get more clicks on your online listing
  • Attract serious renters who already like what they see
  • Reduce wasted showings with people who would not like the property in person
  • Justify your asking rent by showing the property at its best
  • Fill vacancies faster by creating demand

These photos become part of your broader rental property marketing strategy and work alongside your listing description to sell the property.

Phone vs. DSLR: Which Should You Use?

The first question most landlords ask is whether they need a fancy camera. Here is the honest answer: modern smartphones take excellent photos for rental listings.

Use your smartphone if:

  • You are on a tight budget
  • You manage just a few properties
  • You are comfortable with basic photo editing
  • Your phone is from the last three to four years

Consider a DSLR or mirrorless camera if:

  • You manage many properties and take photos regularly
  • You want the absolute best quality
  • You are willing to learn manual camera settings
  • Your properties are high-end and compete with professional listings

Smartphone tips for better photos:

  • Use the wide-angle lens (usually the default on newer phones)
  • Turn on the grid overlay to keep your lines straight
  • Tap the screen to set focus, then adjust brightness
  • Use HDR mode for rooms with bright windows and dark corners
  • Hold the phone horizontally for every shot (landscape orientation)

If you go with a DSLR:

  • Use a wide-angle lens (10-22mm on crop sensor or 16-35mm on full frame)
  • Shoot in aperture priority mode at f/8 to f/11 for sharp photos
  • Use a tripod for steady, consistent shots
  • Shoot in RAW format for better editing flexibility

Either way, the techniques below will dramatically improve your results.

Lighting Techniques That Make Rooms Shine

Lighting is the single biggest factor in photo quality. Here is how to get it right every time.

Use Natural Light

Natural light makes rooms look warm, inviting, and spacious. Follow these rules:

  • Shoot during the day between 10 AM and 2 PM when the sun is high
  • Open all blinds and curtains to let in as much light as possible
  • Turn on every light in the property including lamps, overhead lights, and under-cabinet lighting
  • Open interior doors to let light flow between rooms

Avoid Common Lighting Mistakes

  • Never use flash. On-camera flash creates harsh shadows and makes rooms look cold and flat.
  • Watch out for mixed lighting. If a room has warm lamps and cool overhead lights, it can look odd in photos. Stick with one type when possible.
  • Be careful with windows. Bright windows can make the rest of the room look dark. Use HDR mode to balance the exposure.

Cloudy Days Are Your Friend

Overcast skies act like a giant softbox, spreading light evenly without harsh shadows. Many professional photographers prefer cloudy days for interior shots. If you can, schedule your photo session on a lightly overcast day.

Staging Your Property for Photos

Staging means arranging the property to look its best in photos. You do not need to hire a professional stager or buy expensive furniture. Simple staging goes a long way.

The Basics of Staging

  1. Deep clean everything. Clean floors, counters, windows, and fixtures. A dirty property photographs poorly no matter what else you do.
  2. Declutter every surface. Remove personal items, excess furniture, and anything that makes rooms look small or messy.
  3. Make beds with fresh linens. A neatly made bed with matching pillows makes a bedroom look like a hotel.
  4. Add small touches. A bowl of fruit on the kitchen counter, fresh towels in the bathroom, or a plant on the coffee table adds warmth.
  5. Hide cords and cables. Loose wires make rooms look messy. Tuck them behind furniture.
  6. Remove trash cans, toilet brushes, and cleaning supplies. These everyday items look terrible in photos.

Staging an Empty Property

Empty rooms are harder to photograph because they lack scale and feel cold. Here are some ways to handle empty units:

  • Add a few key pieces. A simple dining table with chairs, a bed with bedding, or a couch with throw pillows can transform an empty room.
  • Use virtual staging. Software can add furniture to photos of empty rooms. This is cheaper than physical staging and looks great in listings. We will cover this more below.
  • Shoot from corners. Empty rooms look bigger when photographed from a corner, showing two walls and the floor.

For more detailed staging advice, see our guide on rental property staging tips.

Room-by-Room Photography Guide

Every room has its own challenges. Here is how to photograph each one for the best results.

Living Room

  • Shoot from the corner that shows the most space
  • Include windows to show natural light
  • If furnished, arrange seating to face the camera
  • Show the view from the living room into adjacent rooms to emphasize flow

Kitchen

  • Clean the counters completely. No dishes, no appliances, no clutter
  • Wipe down all appliances so they do not show fingerprints
  • Turn on under-cabinet lighting if available
  • Shoot from the doorway looking in and from the corners
  • Open one or two upper cabinets to show storage space

Bedrooms

  • Make the bed with clean, neutral bedding
  • Remove personal photos and clutter from nightstands
  • Open closet doors to show closet size (make sure the closet is organized)
  • Shoot from the doorway and from the corner opposite the bed

Bathrooms

  • Close the toilet lid
  • Hang fresh, matching towels
  • Remove all personal products from the counter and shower
  • Clean the mirror until it sparkles (dirty mirrors are very obvious in photos)
  • Shoot from the doorway since bathrooms are small and hard to photograph from inside

Laundry Area

  • Close washer and dryer doors
  • Remove detergent bottles and laundry baskets
  • Wipe down surfaces
  • Show shelving or storage if available

Exterior Shots

Exterior photos are just as important as interior ones. The outside of your property is the first thing renters see, and it sets expectations for what is inside.

Front of the Property

  • Shoot on a clear day with blue sky if possible
  • Stand across the street to capture the full facade
  • Mow the lawn, trim bushes, and clean up the yard before shooting
  • Remove cars from the driveway and trash cans from the curb
  • Shoot at a slight angle rather than straight on for a more dynamic look

Backyard and Outdoor Spaces

  • Photograph patios, decks, pools, and outdoor seating areas
  • Stage outdoor furniture with cushions and a few accessories
  • Clean the grill and outdoor kitchen if applicable
  • Show fencing for renters with pets or children

Parking and Common Areas

  • Include photos of the garage, parking spaces, or carport
  • Show common areas like lobbies, gyms, pools, and laundry rooms
  • Photograph mailboxes, storage units, and other amenities

Editing Your Photos

Even great photos benefit from a little editing. You do not need to be a Photoshop expert. Simple adjustments make a big difference.

Free and Easy Editing Tools

  • Google Photos has built-in editing with auto-adjust
  • Snapseed is a free mobile app with powerful editing tools
  • Lightroom Mobile offers professional-level editing for free
  • Canva lets you add text overlays and create collages

Basic Edits to Make on Every Photo

  1. Straighten the image. Make sure walls and door frames are perfectly vertical.
  2. Adjust brightness. Increase exposure slightly if the photo looks dark.
  3. Increase contrast. A small contrast boost makes the image pop.
  4. Correct white balance. Make sure whites look white, not yellow or blue.
  5. Crop for composition. Remove distracting edges and center the subject.

Edits to Avoid

  • Do not over-edit. Heavy filters make photos look fake and turn renters off.
  • Do not misrepresent the property. Editing should enhance, not deceive. If you make a small room look huge through editing tricks, renters will be disappointed in person.
  • Do not use black and white or sepia. Renters want to see accurate colors.

Virtual Staging

Virtual staging uses software to add furniture and decor to photos of empty rooms. It is a cost-effective way to help renters visualize the space.

Benefits of virtual staging:

  • Costs 25 to 75 dollars per photo versus 1,000+ dollars for physical staging
  • Quick turnaround, often within 24 to 48 hours
  • Multiple style options for different target demographics
  • Easy to update or change

Tips for virtual staging:

  • Use realistic furniture that fits the room proportions
  • Match the style to your target renter (modern for young professionals, traditional for families)
  • Label virtually staged photos clearly in your listing to avoid misleading renters
  • Use it for the main living areas and bedrooms but not for kitchens and bathrooms

Popular virtual staging services include BoxBrownie, VirtualStagingSolutions, and Stuccco. For an even more immersive experience, consider adding virtual tours to your rental property listings alongside your photos.

Drone Photography

Drone photos give a unique perspective that sets your listing apart. They are especially useful for:

  • Properties with large lots or attractive surroundings
  • Multi-family buildings where you want to show the full property
  • Homes near parks, water, or scenic views
  • Properties where the neighborhood is a major selling point

Important notes on drone photography:

  • You need an FAA Part 107 license to fly drones commercially in the US
  • Hiring a licensed drone photographer costs 100 to 300 dollars
  • Check local regulations since some areas restrict drone flights
  • Do not fly over neighboring properties without permission

Photo Order in Your Listing

The order you place photos in your listing matters more than you might think. Renters often stop scrolling after the first five to seven photos. Put your best content first.

Recommended photo order:

  1. Hero shot - The best exterior photo of the property
  2. Living room - The main gathering space
  3. Kitchen - Often the most important room for renters
  4. Primary bedroom - Shows the main sleeping space
  5. Primary bathroom - Especially if it has been updated
  6. Additional bedrooms - One photo each
  7. Additional bathrooms - One photo each
  8. Special features - Laundry, garage, office, bonus rooms
  9. Outdoor spaces - Backyard, patio, pool
  10. Neighborhood - Parks, shops, views nearby

How Many Photos to Include

More photos generally means more interest, but there is a point of diminishing returns.

  • Minimum: 10 photos for a studio or one-bedroom
  • Ideal: 15 to 25 photos for a two- to three-bedroom
  • Maximum: 30 to 35 photos for a large home

Do not include multiple photos of the same angle or room unless they show something different. Every photo should add new information for the renter.

Creating a Shot List

Before you start shooting, make a list of every photo you need. This keeps you organized and ensures you do not miss anything.

Sample shot list for a two-bedroom apartment:

  • Building exterior (2 angles)
  • Front entrance or lobby
  • Living room (2 to 3 angles)
  • Kitchen (2 to 3 angles)
  • Dining area
  • Primary bedroom (2 angles)
  • Primary bedroom closet
  • Second bedroom (2 angles)
  • Primary bathroom
  • Second bathroom
  • Laundry area
  • Balcony or patio
  • Parking area
  • Building amenities (pool, gym, etc.)
  • Neighborhood highlights

Walk through the property with this list and check off each shot as you take it.

Common Photography Mistakes

Avoid these errors that make otherwise good properties look bad:

  1. Shooting in portrait mode. Always hold your camera or phone horizontally.
  2. Including yourself in reflections. Watch for mirrors, windows, and shiny surfaces.
  3. Leaving toilet seats up. Always close the lid before photographing a bathroom.
  4. Forgetting to remove personal items. Family photos, medications, and valuables should be put away.
  5. Shooting at night. Even with lights on, nighttime photos look dark and uninviting.
  6. Tilting the camera up or down. Keep it level at about chest height for natural-looking perspectives.

Putting It All Together

Great rental property photography is a skill you can learn and improve over time. Start with these basics: clean the property, use natural light, shoot from corners, and make simple edits. As you get more comfortable, you can add techniques like virtual staging and drone photography.

Your photos work together with your listing description to create a complete picture of your property. When both are done well, you attract better tenants and fill vacancies faster.

Take the time to do your photos right. The effort pays off in shorter vacancy periods, higher-quality applicants, and better rental income over time. For more marketing resources, the National Association of Realtors offers guidance on real estate photography standards, and Canva provides free templates and design tools for creating listing graphics and photo collages.
