Fair housing law affects every decision you make as a landlord. From the words you use in a listing to how you screen applicants, fair housing rules shape your entire rental process.

Breaking these rules can cost you tens of thousands of dollars in fines, legal fees, and damages.

The good news is that fair housing compliance is straightforward once you understand the basics. This guide covers everything you need to know to stay compliant and treat every applicant and tenant fairly.

What Is the Fair Housing Act?

The Fair Housing Act is a federal law passed in 1968. It makes it illegal to discriminate in housing based on certain characteristics.

Did You Know?

Real estate has created more millionaires than any other investment type. About 90% of millionaires have invested in real estate at some point.

The law covers selling, renting, and financing homes and apartments.

For landlords, the Fair Housing Act applies to:

  • Advertising your rental property
  • Screening applicants
  • Setting rental terms and conditions
  • Providing services and maintenance
  • Enforcing lease terms
  • Ending a tenancy

The law is enforced by the U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD).

Tenants can also file lawsuits in federal or state court.

Protected Classes Under Federal Law

The Fair Housing Act protects seven classes of people. You cannot discriminate against anyone based on these characteristics:

  1. Race - This includes all races and ethnicities
  2. Color - Skin color, which can differ within racial groups
  3. Religion - All religions and lack of religion
  4. Sex - Includes gender identity and sexual orientation (as clarified by recent court rulings and HUD guidance)
  5. National origin - Country of birth or ancestry
  6. Familial status - Families with children under 18, pregnant women, and people getting custody of children
  7. Disability - Physical or mental disabilities

State and Local Protected Classes

Many states and cities add extra protected classes. Common additions include:

  • Age - Protecting older or younger adults
  • Marital status - Single, married, divorced
  • Source of income - Such as Section 8 vouchers or other housing assistance
  • Sexual orientation and gender identity - In states where federal protections are not yet fully applied
  • Veteran or military status
  • Student status
  • Citizenship or immigration status

You must follow both federal and state or local fair housing laws. When laws overlap, follow the stricter rule.

Advertising Rules and Compliance

The words and images you use in your rental listing can create fair housing problems. Here is how to advertise the right way.

What You Cannot Say

You cannot use language that shows a preference for or against a protected class. Here are examples of what to avoid:

  • "Perfect for young professionals" - This could discriminate based on age or familial status
  • "Great for single person" - This suggests a preference against families
  • "Near churches" - This could show a religious preference
  • "English speakers only" - This could discriminate based on national origin
  • "No children" - This violates familial status protections (unless you qualify as senior housing)
  • "Must be able to climb stairs" - This could discriminate against people with disabilities

What You Can Say

Focus on the property, not the people. Describe the unit, the amenities, and the neighborhood features.

  • "Two-bedroom apartment with updated kitchen" - Good
  • "Close to parks and shopping" - Good
  • "Ground-floor unit with wide doorways" - Good (describes the property)
  • "Quiet building" - Acceptable (describes the environment)

Images and Photos

Be careful with photos too. If your marketing materials only show people of one race or age group, that could suggest a preference.

Use photos of the property itself rather than people.

Online Listings

Fair housing rules apply to online listings the same way they apply to print ads. Social media posts, Craigslist ads, and your website must all comply.

Be especially careful with social media ad targeting. Do not target your ads by race, religion, or other protected classes.

Major platforms have changed their ad tools to prevent this, but you should still be aware. For compliant advertising strategies, see our guide on rental property marketing strategies.

Tenant Screening Compliance

Screening applicants is where many fair housing violations happen. You must apply the same standards to every applicant without exception.

Set Written Screening Criteria

Before you start taking applications, write down your screening criteria. Apply these same standards to every applicant.

Your criteria might include:

  • Minimum credit score
  • Income requirement (such as income must be 3 times the rent)
  • Rental history (such as no evictions in the past 5 years)
  • Criminal background check parameters
  • Employment verification

For more on building a fair screening process, read our tenant screening best practices guide.

Apply Criteria Equally

The most important rule in screening is consistency. If you require a credit score of 650 for one applicant, you must require it for all applicants.

You cannot bend the rules for some people and not others.

Document every decision. Keep records of why you approved or denied each applicant.

This protects you if someone files a complaint.

Criminal Background Checks

Criminal background checks are legal in most places, but you must use them carefully. HUD has issued guidance saying that blanket bans on anyone with a criminal record can have a disparate impact on certain racial groups.

Best practices for criminal background checks:

  • Do not use blanket bans. Instead, consider the type of crime, how long ago it happened, and whether it is relevant to being a good tenant.
  • Do not ask about arrests. Only consider convictions. An arrest without a conviction should not affect your decision.
  • Be consistent. Apply the same criminal background standards to every applicant.
  • Check local laws. Some cities have "ban the box" laws that limit when you can ask about criminal history.

Income Verification

Requiring proof of income is standard and legal. But be careful about source of income discrimination.

In many states and cities, you cannot reject an applicant because they use a housing voucher (like Section 8) to pay rent.

Check your local laws to see if source of income is a protected class in your area.

Reasonable Accommodations and Modifications

The Fair Housing Act requires landlords to make reasonable accommodations and allow reasonable modifications for tenants with disabilities. Understanding the difference between these two is important.

Reasonable Accommodations

A reasonable accommodation is a change to a rule, policy, or practice. It does not cost the landlord money (in most cases).

Examples include:

  • Allowing a service animal in a no-pets building
  • Providing a reserved parking space closer to the unit for a tenant with mobility issues
  • Allowing a tenant to pay rent on a different date if their disability check arrives at a specific time
  • Permitting a live-in aide even if the lease limits occupants

You must grant a reasonable accommodation unless it would cause an "undue hardship" on you or a "fundamental alteration" of your business. In practice, most accommodations are simple and cost nothing.

Reasonable Modifications

A reasonable modification is a physical change to the unit or common area. Examples include:

  • Installing grab bars in the bathroom
  • Widening doorways for wheelchair access
  • Adding a ramp to the entrance
  • Lowering kitchen counters

For private landlords, the tenant usually pays for the modification. The landlord must allow it but does not have to pay for it.

However, for properties that receive federal funding, the landlord may be required to pay.

You can require the tenant to restore the unit to its original condition when they move out, as long as this is reasonable. You can read the full text of the Fair Housing Act at Cornell Law for the statutory details on reasonable modifications.

How to Handle Accommodation Requests

When a tenant or applicant requests an accommodation or modification:

  1. Do not ignore the request. Respond promptly.
  2. You can ask for documentation if the disability is not obvious. A letter from a doctor or therapist confirming the disability and the need for the accommodation is enough. You cannot ask for detailed medical records.
  3. Engage in a good faith discussion. If the specific request does not work, suggest an alternative that meets the tenant's needs.
  4. Document everything. Keep records of the request, your response, and any communication.

Service Animals and Assistance Animals

Service and assistance animals are one of the most common fair housing issues landlords face. Here is what you need to know.

Service Animals vs. Emotional Support Animals

  • Service animals are trained to perform specific tasks for a person with a disability. They are usually dogs.
  • Emotional support animals (ESAs) provide comfort by their presence. They do not need special training. They can be any type of animal.

Both service animals and ESAs are protected under fair housing law. You must allow them even if your property has a no-pets policy.

You cannot charge a pet deposit or pet rent for either type.

What You Can and Cannot Ask

  • You can ask if the animal is required because of a disability
  • You can ask for documentation from a medical professional for ESAs (if the disability is not obvious)
  • You cannot ask about the nature of the disability
  • You cannot require special certifications, registrations, or ID cards
  • You cannot restrict the breed, size, or type of assistance animal based on pet policies

When You Can Deny an Assistance Animal

You can deny an assistance animal only if:

  • The animal poses a direct threat to others (based on actual behavior, not breed)
  • The animal would cause significant property damage that cannot be reduced by a reasonable accommodation
  • The request is not legitimate (for example, the documentation is from an online-only ESA letter mill, which HUD has flagged as potentially unreliable)

Fair Housing Testing and Enforcement

Fair housing organizations regularly test landlords for discrimination. Here is how testing works and what happens if violations are found.

How Testing Works

Fair housing organizations send "testers" who pose as potential renters. They send testers from different protected classes to see if they are treated differently.

For example, a testing organization might send a white tester and a Black tester to ask about the same apartment. If the landlord tells one tester the unit is available and tells the other it is taken, that is evidence of discrimination.

Testers may also call about listings that contain potentially discriminatory language, or they may apply online to test screening criteria.

What Happens If You Fail a Test

If a fair housing test reveals discrimination, the organization can file a complaint with HUD or take you to court.

Filing a Complaint

Anyone who believes they experienced housing discrimination can file a complaint. There are two main paths:

  1. HUD complaint. The person files with HUD, which investigates for free. If HUD finds evidence of discrimination, it can hold an administrative hearing or refer the case to the Department of Justice.
  2. Federal lawsuit. The person can file a lawsuit in federal court within two years of the alleged discrimination.

Many states and cities also have their own fair housing agencies that handle complaints.

Penalties for Fair Housing Violations

Fair housing penalties are serious. Here is what you could face:

  • Civil penalties up to $21,039 for a first violation and up to $105,194 for repeat violations (these amounts are adjusted for inflation)
  • Actual damages to the victim, including emotional distress
  • Punitive damages if the court finds intentional discrimination
  • Attorney's fees for the winning party
  • Injunctive relief - a court order requiring you to change your practices

Beyond legal penalties, a fair housing violation damages your reputation. It can make it harder to attract good tenants and can affect your relationships with property management companies and real estate professionals.

How to Build a Fair Housing Compliant Rental Business

Follow these steps to make sure your rental business complies with fair housing law at every level.

Step 1: Educate Yourself and Your Team

Everyone involved in your rental business needs fair housing training. This includes property managers, leasing agents, maintenance staff, and anyone who interacts with tenants or applicants.

Many state and local landlord associations offer free or low-cost fair housing training. HUD also provides resources on its website.

Step 2: Create Written Policies

Put your screening criteria, accommodation procedures, and advertising guidelines in writing. Written policies help you stay consistent and give you something to point to if a complaint is filed.

Step 3: Document Everything

Keep records of every application, every decision, and every interaction with tenants. If a tenant requests an accommodation, document the request and your response.

If you deny an applicant, document the reason.

Good records are your best defense against a fair housing complaint.

Step 4: Audit Your Practices

Review your advertising, screening, and management practices at least once a year. Look for anything that could be seen as discriminatory.

Ask yourself:

  • Do my ads describe the property, not the ideal tenant?
  • Do I apply screening criteria equally to everyone?
  • Have I denied any accommodation requests? If so, was it justified?
  • Are there patterns in who I accept and reject?

Step 5: Respond Quickly to Complaints

If someone raises a fair housing concern, take it seriously. Investigate the issue, correct any problems, and document your response.

Quick action can sometimes prevent a formal complaint.

Common Fair Housing Myths

Myth: "I can choose my tenant based on gut feeling." Fact: You must base decisions on objective, written criteria. A "gut feeling" can lead to unconscious bias.

Myth: "Fair housing only applies to big apartment complexes." Fact: Fair housing applies to most rental properties. The main exceptions are owner-occupied buildings with four or fewer units and single-family homes rented without a broker (and even these exceptions do not apply to advertising).

Myth: "I can set different rules for families with children." Fact: You cannot charge higher deposits, restrict children to certain units, or set different rules for families. The exception is legitimate senior housing communities.

Myth: "I do not need to allow emotional support animals." Fact: ESAs are protected under fair housing law. You must allow them in no-pet properties without charging pet fees.

Bottom Line

Fair housing compliance is not just about avoiding lawsuits. It is about running a professional rental business that treats everyone with respect.

The rules are clear: apply the same standards to everyone, describe the property instead of the ideal tenant, accommodate disabilities, and document your decisions.

When you follow fair housing law, you protect your business, attract a wider pool of qualified tenants, and build a reputation as a fair and professional landlord. For a full overview of what tenants can expect from you, review our guide to tenant rights and responsibilities.
