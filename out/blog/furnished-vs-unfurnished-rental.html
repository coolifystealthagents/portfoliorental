Furnished vs Unfurnished Rental: Which Earns More?

The question every landlord faces when preparing a unit for market: should you furnish it or rent it empty? The answer depends heavily on your market, target tenant, hold strategy, and willingness to manage additional complexity. Get it right, and furnished rentals can earn 15–50% more than their unfurnished equivalents. Get it wrong, and you'll spend more on furniture and turnover than you'll ever recover in premium rent.

This guide gives you a clear framework to make the right choice for your specific property.

The Income Premium: How Much More Can Furnished Rentals Earn?

In the right market, furnished rentals command significant premiums:

Market Type Unfurnished Monthly Rent Furnished Premium Furnished Rent
Major corporate market (NYC, SF, Chicago) $3,000 30–50% $3,900–$4,500
Secondary market near hospital/university $1,500 20–35% $1,800–$2,025
Vacation/tourist destination $1,800 (annual) 50–100%+ (seasonal STR) $2,700–$3,600
Average suburban market $1,400 5–15% $1,470–$1,610

The premium is highest in markets with strong demand from:

  • Corporate relocatees: Employees on temporary assignments (3–12 months)
  • Travel nurses and medical professionals: On 13-week hospital contracts
  • Graduate students and visiting academics: Semester or year-long placements
  • Executives and consultants: On project-based deployments

In standard suburban markets catering to long-term family renters, the furnished premium shrinks to 5–15%—often not worth the investment.

Furnished Rental Cost Analysis

Before calculating your profit, understand the true cost of furnishing a unit:

One-Time Furniture Investment

Studio (400 sq ft):

  • Bed frame, mattress, nightstand: $600–$1,200
  • Sofa or loveseat: $400–$900
  • Dining table and chairs: $300–$600
  • Desk and chair: $200–$400
  • Kitchen essentials (cookware, dishes, utensils): $300–$600
  • Bedding, towels, window treatments: $400–$700
  • Total studio: $2,200–$4,400

1-Bedroom (600–750 sq ft):

  • All above plus additional bedroom furniture: $3,500–$6,500

2-Bedroom (900–1,100 sq ft):

  • Full furnishing: $5,500–$10,000

These are budget-to-mid-range estimates. Luxury furnished units in premium markets may invest $15,000–$30,000 in high-end furniture and staging.

Ongoing Furnished Rental Costs

Beyond the initial investment, furnished rentals incur higher operating costs:

  • Replacement/repair: Expect 15–25% of furniture value annually as turnover damages and wears items
  • Cleaning between tenancies: $150–$400 per turnover (more intensive than unfurnished)
  • Storage (if tenant wants to bring own furniture): Rarely practical; most furnished tenants want everything included
  • Inventory management: Time to track, replace, and maintain furnishings

Furnished vs. Unfurnished: Direct Financial Comparison

Example: 1-bedroom unit in a corporate-heavy market

Factor Unfurnished Furnished
Monthly rent $1,800 $2,300
Annual gross rent $21,600 $27,600
Vacancy (furnished: higher turnover) 5% ($1,080) 12% ($3,312)
Furniture replacement/maintenance $0 $900
Additional cleaning costs $0 $600
Net annual income $20,520 $22,788

In this scenario, furnished earns $2,268 more per year—before accounting for the initial $5,000 furniture investment. Payback period: approximately 2.2 years.

Does it make sense? At 2.2 years payback and $2,268 annual ongoing premium, yes—if you're confident in the market demand for furnished rentals. If vacancy is actually 20%+ (common for furnished units in weak markets), the math reverses.

Tenant Profile Differences

The type of tenant you attract changes significantly with furnished vs. unfurnished:

Unfurnished Tenants

  • Typical lease term: 12+ months (often longer)
  • Profile: Families, established professionals, long-term residents
  • Turnover frequency: Every 2–4 years on average
  • Vacancy risk: Lower
  • Damage risk: Moderate (typical wear and tear)
  • Management intensity: Low

Furnished Tenants

  • Typical lease term: 1–6 months (short-to-medium term)
  • Profile: Relocating employees, travel nurses, students, temporary workers
  • Turnover frequency: Every 1–4 months for short-term; every 6–12 months for medium-term
  • Vacancy risk: Higher (more frequent re-leasing required)
  • Damage risk: Higher (more tenants, more wear on furnishings)
  • Management intensity: Moderate to high

The shorter lease cycles of furnished rentals mean more time and cost marketing and screening tenants. Factor this into your true cost analysis.

Market Demand Check: Is There a Market for Furnished Rentals?

Before furnishing, validate demand in your specific market:

Strong indicators of furnished rental demand:

  • Major hospitals or medical centers nearby (travel nurse market)
  • Large corporate campuses or office parks (relocation market)
  • University or college nearby (faculty, grad students, visiting researchers)
  • Military base or government installation (PCS move market)
  • Tourism district or vacation destination (short-term rental market)
  • High employee turnover industries (consulting, tech, finance)

How to check: Search furnished rentals on Furnished Finder, Airbnb (monthly stays), Zillow (furnished filter), and corporate housing platforms. If there are many listings but many available (high supply, low demand), the market may be saturated.

Furnished Rentals vs. Short-Term Rentals: The Spectrum

Furnished rentals exist on a spectrum from long-term to short-term:

Strategy Lease Length Platform Management Level Income Potential
Unfurnished long-term 12+ months MLS, Zillow Low Baseline
Furnished medium-term 1–6 months Furnished Finder, Zillow Moderate +15–35%
Short-term rental (STR) Days to weeks Airbnb, VRBO High +50–150% in STR markets

Medium-term furnished rentals (30+ days) are the sweet spot for many investors: higher income than unfurnished, without the daily management burden of STR platforms. Platforms like Furnished Finder specifically serve the travel nurse and corporate relocation market.

Partial Furnishing: A Middle Ground

Some landlords compromise with partial furnishing:

  • Provide kitchen appliances (refrigerator, dishwasher, microwave, stove) as standard—this is expected by most tenants anyway
  • Include window treatments and lighting fixtures
  • Leave the rest unfurnished

This approach adds minimal cost, reduces friction for tenants who already have furniture, and doesn't create the turnover/replacement cost burden of full furnishing. The income premium is modest (0–10%) but so is the cost.

When Unfurnished Is Almost Always Better

For most residential landlords in standard markets, unfurnished is the right default:

  • Long-term family and professional tenants don't need furnishings
  • The premium is too small to justify the cost and management overhead
  • Long lease stability has real financial value (predictable income, lower vacancy cost)
  • The risk of furnished-unit damage is higher per tenancy

Unfurnished wins when:

  • You want stable, long-term tenants
  • Your market doesn't have strong furnished rental demand
  • You want to minimize management complexity
  • Your rental pricing strategy is built around maximum lease stability

When Furnished Is a Strong Strategy

Furnished wins when:

  • Your market has clear demand from corporate, medical, or academic tenants
  • You're near an Airbnb/STR-friendly destination with strong nightly demand
  • The income premium clearly exceeds your furniture + management costs
  • You want maximum flexibility (furnished properties are easier to sell owner-occupied if you decide to exit)
  • You're using the BRRRR or value-add strategy and need to maximize income during a stabilization period

Tax Considerations for Furnished Rentals

Furniture purchased for rental properties is depreciable. Under Section 179 and bonus depreciation rules (consult a tax professional for current rules), you may be able to deduct the full cost of furniture in the year purchased rather than depreciating it over 5–7 years.

This accelerated deduction can significantly improve the after-tax return on your furnishing investment. Our rental property depreciation guide covers how to structure these deductions effectively.

Summary: Making the Decision

Use this decision framework:

Choose furnished if: You have documented local demand from corporate/medical/academic tenants, the income premium clearly exceeds 15% above market unfurnished rent, and you're prepared for higher turnover and management.

Choose unfurnished if: You're in a standard residential market targeting families and long-term professionals, or if the premium is below 10% and doesn't justify the investment.

Choose medium-term furnished if: You want higher income than unfurnished but less management than daily STR, and you're near hospitals, corporate campuses, or universities.

Run the rental property cash flow analysis for both scenarios with realistic vacancy assumptions before deciding. The math will make the right choice clear—and the right choice is always the one that maximizes your net income after all costs, not just your headline rent.
