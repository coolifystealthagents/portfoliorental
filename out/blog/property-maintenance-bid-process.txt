28:T2597,<p>Getting multiple bids on maintenance projects is fundamental to controlling costs and finding the right contractor for the job. The <a href="https://www.sba.gov/business-guide/launch-your-business">SBA business guide</a> recommends competitive bidding as a best practice for managing business expenses. But a poorly run bid process wastes time for everyone. Vague scope descriptions produce incomparable bids. Too few bidders limit competition. Too many bidders discourage quality contractors from participating.</p>
<p>This guide shows you how to run a structured bid process that delivers competitive pricing and reliable results for your rental properties.</p>
<h2 id="when-to-use-a-formal-bid-process">When to Use a Formal Bid Process</h2>
<p>Not every maintenance task needs competitive bidding. Use a bid process when:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Project cost exceeds $2,000 to $5,000</strong> — Below this threshold, the time spent managing bids often exceeds potential savings</li>
<li><strong>The scope is clearly definable</strong> — If you cannot describe the work precisely, bidding produces unreliable numbers</li>
<li><strong>Multiple qualified contractors are available</strong> — Bidding works when there is genuine competition</li>
<li><strong>You are not in an emergency</strong> — Emergency repairs require immediate response, not a bid cycle</li>
</ul>
<p>For routine maintenance and minor repairs, build relationships with preferred contractors and negotiate standing rates. See our <a href="/blog/vendor-management-property-maintenance">vendor management best practices</a> for that approach.</p>
<h2 id="creating-a-bid-package">Creating a Bid Package</h2>
<p>A strong bid package ensures every contractor prices the same scope of work, making their proposals directly comparable.</p>
<h3 id="project-description">Project Description</h3>
<p>Write a clear overview including:</p>
<ul>
<li>Property address and unit information</li>
<li>Current condition and the problem being addressed</li>
<li>Desired outcome or finished state</li>
<li>Any constraints (tenant occupied, access hours, noise restrictions)</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="detailed-scope-of-work">Detailed Scope of Work</h3>
<p>Break the project into specific tasks:</p>
<ul>
<li>Demolition and removal requirements</li>
<li>Materials and specifications (be as specific as possible)</li>
<li>Installation or construction requirements</li>
<li>Cleanup and disposal requirements</li>
<li>Permit and inspection responsibilities</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="site-visit-requirements">Site Visit Requirements</h3>
<p>For projects above $5,000, require a site visit before bidding. Contractors who bid without seeing the property frequently submit change orders once they discover site conditions.</p>
<p>Coordinate site visits with tenants in advance. Provide access schedules and any relevant property documentation.</p>
<h3 id="bid-submission-requirements">Bid Submission Requirements</h3>
<p>Specify:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Deadline</strong> for bid submission</li>
<li><strong>Format</strong> — Itemized breakdown, not just a lump sum</li>
<li><strong>Required documentation</strong> — License number, insurance certificates, references</li>
<li><strong>Questions deadline</strong> — A date by which contractors must submit questions about the scope</li>
<li><strong>Selection timeline</strong> — When you will make a decision</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="terms-and-conditions">Terms and Conditions</h3>
<p>Include your standard requirements:</p>
<ul>
<li>Payment terms and schedule</li>
<li>Insurance minimums and additional insured requirements</li>
<li>Warranty expectations</li>
<li>Lien waiver requirements</li>
<li>Your <a href="/blog/contractor-service-agreement-template">contractor service agreement</a> or a summary of key contract terms</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="how-many-bids-to-solicit">How Many Bids to Solicit</h2>
<h3 id="the-sweet-spot-three-to-five">The Sweet Spot: Three to Five</h3>
<p>Three bids is the minimum for meaningful comparison. Five is the practical maximum before the process becomes unwieldy. More than five bidders reduces each contractor's probability of winning, discouraging top-tier contractors from investing time in detailed proposals.</p>
<h3 id="selecting-bidders">Selecting Bidders</h3>
<p>Invite contractors you have reason to believe are qualified:</p>
<ul>
<li>Contractors recommended by other landlords or property managers</li>
<li>Contractors with verified licenses and insurance (verify through your state's licensing board and confirm <a href="https://www.osha.gov/workers">OSHA compliance</a>)</li>
<li>Contractors with experience on similar rental property projects</li>
</ul>
<p>Avoid posting open bid requests on general platforms where unqualified contractors inflate your evaluation workload.</p>
<h2 id="evaluating-bids">Evaluating Bids</h2>
<h3 id="create-a-scoring-matrix">Create a Scoring Matrix</h3>
<p>Evaluate each bid on consistent criteria:</p>
<table>
<thead>
<tr>
<th>Criteria</th>
<th>Weight</th>
<th>Description</th>
</tr>
</thead>
<tbody>
<tr>
<td><strong>Price</strong></td>
<td>30-40%</td>
<td>Total cost and line-item reasonableness</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td><strong>Qualifications</strong></td>
<td>20-25%</td>
<td>License, insurance, relevant experience</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td><strong>Timeline</strong></td>
<td>15-20%</td>
<td>Proposed schedule and availability</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td><strong>References</strong></td>
<td>10-15%</td>
<td>Feedback from other landlords</td>
</tr>
<tr>
<td><strong>Materials quality</strong></td>
<td>10-15%</td>
<td>Specifications of proposed materials</td>
</tr>
</tbody>
</table>
<p>Adjust weights based on project priorities. An emergency roof repair might weight timeline at 40%, while a scheduled renovation might weight price at 40%.</p>
<h3 id="comparing-apples-to-apples">Comparing Apples to Apples</h3>
<p>Before scoring price, verify that all bids cover the same scope:</p>
<ul>
<li>Are the same materials specified?</li>
<li>Are permits and inspections included?</li>
<li>Is cleanup and disposal included?</li>
<li>Are the same access hours assumed?</li>
<li>What is excluded (and what might those exclusions cost later)?</li>
</ul>
<p>The lowest bid is often missing scope items that other bidders included. Normalize bids by adding estimated costs for excluded items before comparing.</p>
<h3 id="red-flags-in-bids">Red Flags in Bids</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Significantly lower than others</strong> — The contractor may have missed scope items, plans to use inferior materials, or will submit change orders</li>
<li><strong>No itemized breakdown</strong> — Makes it impossible to identify where cost differences arise</li>
<li><strong>Vague timeline</strong> — "A few weeks" instead of specific milestone dates</li>
<li><strong>No questions asked</strong> — A contractor who did not ask any questions about the scope may not have read it carefully</li>
<li><strong>Missing insurance or license documentation</strong> — Non-negotiable requirements</li>
</ul>
<h2 id="negotiation-after-selection">Negotiation After Selection</h2>
<p>Once you select your preferred contractor, you may negotiate:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Price adjustments</strong> on specific line items (not across-the-board cuts that reduce quality)</li>
<li><strong>Payment milestones</strong> aligned with project stages</li>
<li><strong>Timeline commitments</strong> with specific start and completion dates</li>
<li><strong>Warranty terms</strong> that exceed the standard offer</li>
</ul>
<p>Frame negotiations as collaborative problem-solving, not adversarial price-beating. Contractors who feel squeezed on price often cut corners to maintain margins.</p>
<h2 id="managing-the-process-at-scale">Managing the Process at Scale</h2>
<p>For landlords managing larger portfolios, standardize your bid process:</p>
<h3 id="template-bid-packages">Template Bid Packages</h3>
<p>Create templates for common project types:</p>
<ul>
<li>Unit turnover renovation</li>
<li>Roof replacement</li>
<li>Parking lot resurfacing</li>
<li>Exterior painting</li>
<li>HVAC system replacement</li>
</ul>
<p>Templates save time and ensure consistency across projects. Customize the scope details for each specific property.</p>
<h3 id="preferred-contractor-lists">Preferred Contractor Lists</h3>
<p>Maintain a pre-qualified list of contractors by trade who have already provided license and insurance documentation. When a project comes up, you can skip the qualification step and move straight to pricing.</p>
<h3 id="bid-management-tools">Bid Management Tools</h3>
<p>For high-volume operations, consider property management software or project management tools that centralize bid collection, comparison, and contractor communications. See our <a href="/blog/property-management-software-comparison">property management software comparison</a> for platforms that include vendor management features.</p>
<h2 id="key-takeaways">Key Takeaways</h2>
<p>A structured bid process saves money and reduces risk on significant maintenance projects. Create clear bid packages, invite three to five qualified contractors, evaluate proposals on consistent criteria, and negotiate fairly with your selected contractor. For smaller and routine work, build standing relationships with preferred vendors instead. The goal is matching the right process to the right project size.</p>
<p>For more on building your contractor network, see our guides on <a href="/blog/find-reliable-contractors-rental">finding reliable contractors</a> and <a href="/blog/negotiating-contractor-rates">negotiating contractor rates</a>.</p>
