Verifying Contractor Insurance Before Hiring

Hiring an uninsured or underinsured contractor is one of the most expensive mistakes a landlord can make. If a worker is injured on your property or a contractor damages your building, you may be personally liable for medical bills, property repair, and legal costs. Verifying insurance takes 15 minutes and can save you hundreds of thousands of dollars.

This guide covers which insurance coverages to require, how to verify them, and what to do when documentation raises concerns.

Why Contractor Insurance Matters for Landlords

When a contractor works on your rental property, several risks emerge:

  • Worker injury — If a contractor or their employee is hurt on your property and lacks workers' compensation, you could be held liable
  • Property damage — A plumbing mistake that floods a unit or an electrical error that causes a fire creates massive repair costs
  • Tenant injury — Shoddy work that later injures a tenant exposes you to negligence claims
  • Third-party damage — Work that damages a neighbor's property or common areas
  • Vehicle incidents — A contractor's vehicle damaging your parking lot or another tenant's car

Without proper insurance, these costs come out of your pocket. Your own landlord insurance may deny claims if you hired an uninsured contractor, arguing that you failed to exercise due diligence.

Required Insurance Coverages

General Liability Insurance

What it covers: Bodily injury and property damage caused by the contractor's work at your property.

Minimum recommended coverage: $1 million per occurrence, $2 million aggregate.

Why you need it: This is the most critical coverage. If a contractor accidentally starts a fire, floods a unit, or causes structural damage, their general liability policy covers the repair costs.

Workers' Compensation Insurance

What it covers: Medical expenses and lost wages for the contractor's employees who are injured on the job.

When required: Most states require workers' comp for contractors with employees. Some states exempt sole proprietors. OSHA workplace safety requirements apply regardless of insurance status.

Why you need it: Without workers' comp, an injured worker on your property may file a claim against you as the property owner. Even if you prevail legally, defense costs are substantial.

Commercial Auto Insurance

What it covers: Accidents involving the contractor's work vehicles on your property.

When to require: When contractors drive vehicles onto your property regularly, such as landscaping companies or contractors who park in tenant areas.

Umbrella or Excess Liability

What it covers: Additional coverage above the limits of the primary general liability and auto policies.

When to require: For major renovation projects or high-risk work like roofing, demolition, or multi-story exterior work.

How to Verify Insurance

Step 1: Request a Certificate of Insurance

Ask the contractor to provide a Certificate of Insurance (COI) before any work begins. The COI is a standard form (ACORD 25 is most common) that summarizes the contractor's active policies.

Step 2: Read the Certificate Carefully

Key fields to review:

  • Named insured — Must match the contractor's legal business name exactly
  • Policy numbers — Listed for each coverage type
  • Effective and expiration dates — Ensure all policies are current and will cover the duration of your project
  • Coverage limits — Verify they meet your minimums
  • Certificate holder — Should list your name or entity

Step 3: Verify Directly With the Insurance Company

Do not rely solely on the COI from the contractor. Certificates can be forged or outdated. Contact the insurance company listed on the certificate to confirm:

  • The policy is active and in good standing
  • Coverage limits match the certificate
  • The named insured is correct
  • No cancellations or lapses are pending

Most insurance companies have online verification portals or phone lines for certificate holders.

Step 4: Request Additional Insured Status

Ask the contractor to add you as an additional insured on their general liability policy. This means:

  • You receive direct notification if the policy is cancelled or modified
  • You can file claims directly under their policy if needed
  • You have defense coverage under their policy for claims arising from their work

The contractor's insurance agent can add an additional insured endorsement, usually at no extra cost or for a small fee.

Step 5: Collect Updated Certificates Annually

Insurance policies renew annually. For contractors you use regularly, request updated COIs each year. Set calendar reminders 30 days before expiration dates.

Red Flags in Insurance Documentation

Watch for these warning signs:

  • Certificate issued by the contractor instead of their insurance agent or company
  • Policy expiration within the project timeline without confirmation of renewal
  • Coverage limits below your requirements with no explanation
  • Missing workers' compensation for a contractor who clearly has employees
  • General liability only, no umbrella for high-risk work
  • Hand-altered certificates — Any manual changes to a printed COI should be verified
  • Insurance company you cannot verify — Look up the company through your state's insurance department or the NAIC consumer resources

What to Do When a Contractor Is Underinsured

Negotiate Higher Coverage

For valuable contractors who are underinsured, consider:

  • Asking them to increase their coverage limits (they may not realize it is affordable)
  • Requesting a project-specific insurance policy for larger jobs
  • Offering to pay the premium difference in exchange for proper coverage

Require a Waiver

In some states, sole proprietors can sign waivers acknowledging they are not covered by workers' comp and accept personal responsibility for injuries. However, these waivers have limited legal protection and are not available in all states.

Walk Away

For contractors without any insurance, the answer is simple: do not hire them. The risk far outweighs any cost savings. For tips on finding insured, reliable contractors, see our guide to finding contractors.

Insurance Requirements by Project Type

Project Type General Liability Workers' Comp Auto Umbrella
Minor repair $1M / $2M If has employees No No
Turnover renovation $1M / $2M Required If driving on-site Optional
Major renovation $2M / $4M Required Required Recommended
Roofing $2M / $4M Required Required Required
Demolition $2M / $4M Required Required Required

Building Insurance Verification Into Your Process

Make insurance verification a standard step, not an afterthought:

  1. Include insurance requirements in your contractor service agreement
  2. Do not issue a deposit or authorize work until you have a verified COI
  3. Maintain a file of current COIs for all active contractors
  4. Set renewal reminders for long-term contractor relationships
  5. Brief your property managers on insurance requirements if they hire contractors independently

For more on managing contractors across your portfolio, see our vendor management best practices guide.

Key Takeaways

Verifying contractor insurance is a non-negotiable step in your hiring process. The SBA business guide recommends verifying all vendor credentials before engagement. Request certificates, verify them directly with insurance companies, and require additional insured status. The 15 minutes this takes per contractor is nothing compared to the liability exposure of hiring an uninsured or underinsured worker. Make it a standard part of your operations and enforce it consistently.
