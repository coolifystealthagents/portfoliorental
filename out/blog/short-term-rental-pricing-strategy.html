Setting the right price for your short-term rental is one of the most important decisions you will make as a host. Price too high and your calendar sits empty. Price too low and you leave money on the table. A solid short-term rental pricing strategy helps you find the sweet spot that keeps bookings flowing and revenue growing.

In this guide, we walk through everything you need to know about pricing your short-term rental. From dynamic pricing basics to the best tools on the market, you will learn how to earn more from every night your property is available.

Why Pricing Strategy Matters for Short-Term Rentals

Unlike long-term rentals where rent stays the same for months, short-term rental prices can change every single night. This is both a challenge and an opportunity. The hosts who treat pricing as a set-it-and-forget-it task often underperform. Those who build a real strategy around pricing tend to earn 20% to 40% more per year.

Your pricing strategy affects more than just revenue. It influences your occupancy rate, guest quality, review scores, and even your listing's search ranking on platforms like Airbnb and VRBO. A well-priced listing attracts more views, gets more bookings, and earns better reviews because guests feel they got fair value.

If you are new to short-term rental management, check out our short-term rental management guide for a broader overview of running a successful rental business.

Dynamic Pricing Basics

Dynamic pricing means adjusting your nightly rate based on real-time supply and demand. Hotels have done this for decades. Now short-term rental hosts can do the same thing.

The core idea is simple. When demand is high, you raise your price. When demand is low, you lower it. But the details matter a lot.

How Dynamic Pricing Works

Dynamic pricing tools look at several data points to suggest the right price for each night. These include:

  • Local demand signals such as hotel occupancy rates, flight searches, and event calendars
  • Supply levels showing how many similar listings are available on key dates
  • Historical booking patterns that reveal when guests tend to book and at what price
  • Day-of-week trends since weekends usually command higher rates than weekdays
  • Lead time or how far in advance the booking date is from today

When you use dynamic pricing, your rates might change daily. A Tuesday night in January might be $95. A Saturday during a local festival could be $275. This flexibility is what makes short-term rentals so profitable when managed well.

Setting Your Base Price

Your base price is the starting point for all dynamic adjustments. To find the right base price, consider these factors:

  1. Your costs. Add up your mortgage, utilities, cleaning fees, supplies, insurance, and platform fees. Your base price should cover these costs and leave room for profit.
  2. Comparable listings. Search for similar properties in your area on Airbnb and VRBO. Note their nightly rates, amenities, and review scores.
  3. Your property's strengths. A hot tub, great view, or walkable location can justify a higher base price.
  4. Your occupancy goals. If you want 75% occupancy, you may need a lower base price than if you are happy with 50%.

A good rule of thumb is to start with the average rate of your top five competitors and adjust based on your property's unique features. For more on finding the right price point, see our rental pricing strategy guide.

Seasonal Adjustments

Almost every short-term rental market has seasons. Beach towns peak in summer. Ski resorts boom in winter. City apartments may see spikes during convention season or holiday weekends.

Building a Seasonal Calendar

Create a calendar that maps out your market's high, shoulder, and low seasons. For each season, set a pricing multiplier:

  • Peak season: 1.3x to 2.0x your base price
  • Shoulder season: 1.0x to 1.3x your base price
  • Low season: 0.7x to 1.0x your base price

These multipliers give you a framework. You will fine-tune them over time as you gather data on what works in your market.

Month-by-Month Planning

Go through each month and identify the demand drivers. Ask yourself:

  • Are there school breaks that bring families?
  • Do any holidays create travel surges?
  • Is there a weather pattern that affects demand?
  • Are there annual events or festivals?

Document all of this in a pricing calendar. Update it each year as you learn more about your market's patterns.

Event-Based Pricing

Special events can be the most profitable nights on your calendar. Concerts, sports championships, conferences, marathons, and festivals all drive huge demand spikes.

How to Capitalize on Events

Start by building a list of every major event in your area. Check convention center schedules, sports team calendars, festival listings, and local tourism websites. For each event, note:

  • The dates and expected attendance
  • How far guests typically travel for the event
  • Whether hotels tend to sell out
  • What you charged last year (if applicable)

During major events, you can often charge two to four times your normal rate. Some hosts report earning a full month's revenue in a single weekend during events like the Super Bowl or major music festivals.

Timing Your Event Pricing

Raise your prices for event dates well in advance. Many guests book event travel months ahead. If you wait too long to adjust, you may have already accepted bookings at regular rates.

Set minimum stays during high-demand events too. A three-night minimum during a big weekend prevents someone from booking just the cheapest night and blocking your calendar.

Minimum Stay Requirements

Minimum stays are a powerful pricing lever. They reduce your turnover costs and help you attract better guests.

When to Use Minimum Stays

  • Peak weekends: Require two or three-night minimums to avoid single-night bookings on your most profitable dates.
  • Holiday periods: Set five to seven-night minimums during Christmas, Thanksgiving, or summer peak weeks.
  • Low season: Drop to one-night minimums to maximize occupancy when demand is soft.

The Math Behind Minimum Stays

Consider this example. Your cleaning cost is $150 per turnover. If you accept a one-night booking at $200, you net $50 before other expenses. But a two-night booking at $180 per night gives you $360 minus $150 in cleaning, netting $210. The lower nightly rate actually earns you more money.

Pricing Tools: PriceLabs, Beyond, and Wheelhouse

Manual pricing can work when you have one or two listings. But as your portfolio grows, pricing tools become essential. Here are the three most popular options.

PriceLabs

PriceLabs is one of the most widely used dynamic pricing tools in the short-term rental industry. It connects to Airbnb, VRBO, Booking.com, and most major channel managers.

Key features include:

  • Market data from millions of listings
  • Customizable pricing rules and minimums
  • Portfolio-level analytics
  • Neighborhood-level demand data

PriceLabs charges per listing per month, making it affordable for hosts with small portfolios.

Beyond (formerly Beyond Pricing)

Beyond focuses on simplicity. It pulls market data and automatically adjusts your prices with minimal setup. Many hosts like Beyond because it requires less hands-on management than other tools.

Beyond uses a revenue-based pricing model, charging a percentage of your bookings rather than a flat monthly fee.

Wheelhouse

Wheelhouse gives you more control over your pricing strategy. It lets you choose between fully automated pricing and a recommendation mode where you approve each price change.

Wheelhouse stands out for its transparent market data and scenario modeling. You can see exactly how different pricing strategies would affect your projected revenue.

Choosing the Right Tool

The best tool depends on your management style. If you want a hands-off approach, Beyond is a strong choice. If you like to dig into data and customize rules, PriceLabs or Wheelhouse may be better fits. Most tools offer free trials, so test a few before committing. Many of these pricing tools integrate directly with Airbnb automation tools for seamless rate management across your listings.

Competitor Monitoring

Knowing what your competitors charge is essential for smart pricing. But competitor monitoring goes beyond just checking nightly rates.

What to Track

  • Nightly rates for similar properties on the same dates
  • Occupancy levels by watching how quickly their calendars fill
  • New listings entering your market that could increase supply
  • Amenity changes like new hot tubs or renovations that shift competitive positioning
  • Review scores and recent feedback that might affect their demand

How to Monitor Effectively

Create a shortlist of five to ten comparable listings. Check their calendars and pricing weekly. Many pricing tools include competitor tracking features that automate this process.

Pay attention to patterns. If a competitor drops prices sharply, it could signal a market slowdown. If they raise prices and still book up, it might mean you are underpricing your listing.

Last-Minute Discounts

Empty nights earn zero revenue. A discounted booking is almost always better than no booking at all.

How to Structure Last-Minute Discounts

Set up automatic price reductions as the check-in date approaches. A common approach is:

  • 14 days out: Drop price by 10%
  • 7 days out: Drop price by 15% to 20%
  • 3 days out: Drop price by 25% to 30%

Most pricing tools can automate these reductions. You can also use Airbnb's built-in last-minute discount feature for a simpler approach.

Setting a Floor Price

Always set a minimum price below which you will not go. This floor should cover your variable costs at a minimum, including cleaning, supplies, and platform fees. Accepting a booking below your costs is worse than leaving the night empty.

Length-of-Stay Discounts

Offering discounts for longer stays reduces turnover costs and provides more predictable income. Most platforms let you set weekly and monthly discounts directly in your listing settings.

Recommended Discount Ranges

  • Weekly discount (7+ nights): 10% to 15% off the nightly rate
  • Monthly discount (28+ nights): 20% to 35% off the nightly rate

These discounts make sense because longer stays mean fewer turnovers, less wear and tear, and lower platform fees per dollar earned.

When to Offer Bigger Discounts

During low season or if your market has weak weekday demand, larger length-of-stay discounts can help fill your calendar. A property earning $100 per night at 50% occupancy makes less than one earning $75 per night at 80% occupancy, once you factor in turnover costs.

Occupancy Targets and Rate Optimization

Many hosts focus on either occupancy or nightly rate. The best hosts focus on revenue per available night, often called RevPAN. This metric balances both factors.

Finding Your Optimal Occupancy

For most short-term rentals, the sweet spot is between 65% and 80% occupancy. Here is why:

  • Below 65%: Your rate may be too high, or your listing needs improvement.
  • 65% to 80%: You are likely priced well and capturing good revenue.
  • Above 80%: You may be underpricing. You could raise rates, accept fewer bookings, and still earn the same or more.

Tracking and Adjusting

Review your occupancy and revenue numbers monthly. Compare them to the previous year and to your market averages. Adjust your base price and seasonal multipliers based on what the data tells you.

If your occupancy consistently exceeds 85%, raise your base price by 5% to 10%. If it drops below 60%, consider lowering your base price or offering more aggressive discounts.

Putting It All Together

A winning short-term rental pricing strategy combines all of these elements. Here is a simple framework to follow:

  1. Set your base price using cost analysis and competitor research.
  2. Apply seasonal multipliers based on your market's demand calendar.
  3. Add event-based adjustments for major local events.
  4. Configure minimum stays to protect high-demand dates.
  5. Set up a pricing tool to automate daily adjustments.
  6. Enable last-minute and length-of-stay discounts to fill gaps.
  7. Monitor competitors weekly and adjust as needed.
  8. Review your RevPAN monthly and refine your strategy over time.

Pricing is not something you set once. It is a process you improve month after month, season after season. The hosts who commit to this process consistently outperform those who guess at their rates.

Start with the basics, use the tools available, and let the data guide your decisions. Your short-term rental pricing strategy will get stronger with every booking.
