The people you surround yourself with shape your investing trajectory more than almost any other factor. Real estate investment clubs — also called REIAs (Real Estate Investor Associations) or investor meetups — are where experienced investors and beginners come together to share knowledge, deals, and connections.

Joining the right investment club can compress years of learning into months, surface deals you'd never find on your own, and connect you with lenders, contractors, property managers, and mentors who can accelerate your portfolio growth. This guide explains what to look for, where to find them, and how to extract maximum value once you're in the room.

What Are Real Estate Investment Clubs?

Real estate investment clubs are organized groups of investors who meet regularly — typically monthly — to learn, network, and discuss local market conditions. They range from formal organizations with dues, structured education programs, and hundreds of members to informal monthly meetups at a coffee shop with a dozen local investors.

Most clubs feature some combination of:

  • Educational presentations: Guest speakers covering topics like financing, tax strategy, market analysis, or legal issues
  • Deal sharing: Members presenting properties for review, feedback, or partnership
  • Networking time: Informal connections with fellow investors and vendors
  • Subgroups or mastermind sessions: Smaller groups focused on specific strategies or experience levels

REIA vs. Informal Meetup

The largest and most established clubs operate as REIAs — official nonprofit or member associations with formal structures, regular meeting schedules, and educational programs. The National Real Estate Investors Association (National REIA) is the largest umbrella organization, with affiliated chapters in most major markets.

Informal meetups are often organized through Meetup.com, BiggerPockets, or local Facebook groups. These tend to be less structured but sometimes more intimate and action-oriented.

Both formats have value. Many serious investors participate in multiple groups simultaneously.

Why Real Estate Investment Clubs Are Worth Your Time

Accelerated Learning

In a good club, you'll learn more in a year than you'd learn in several years of independent study. You're exposed to real deals, real mistakes, and real-world experience from investors who've been operating in your target market for years. No book or podcast can replicate that.

Deal Flow

Club members refer deals to each other constantly. Wholesalers present off-market opportunities. Investors looking to sell notify their network before listing publicly. Hard money lenders and private money sources meet the investors who need them. For many active investors, their investment club is their primary source of off-market deal flow.

Vendor Relationships

Property managers, contractors, insurance agents, attorneys, CPAs, and lenders all attend investment clubs, both to contribute and to find clients. Building relationships with quality vendors is one of the most practical benefits of club membership. When you need a recommendation for a reliable plumber or a CPA who understands rental property tax deductions, a fellow club member's referral is worth its weight in gold.

Accountability and Motivation

Real estate investing is often a solitary pursuit. Club membership provides community, accountability, and motivation to keep moving when progress feels slow. Surrounding yourself with people actively closing deals maintains your momentum.

Finding Partners and Private Money

Many real estate investment partnerships and joint ventures are formed within investment clubs. Investors with capital but no time connect with operators with time but limited capital. This is also where you'll find private money lenders — experienced investors willing to lend to newer investors at market rates.

How to Find Real Estate Investment Clubs

National REIA Directory

The National REIA maintains a searchable directory of affiliated local chapters. Start here for established, structured clubs with regular programming.

BiggerPockets Events and Groups

BiggerPockets lists local investor meetups and has location-based forums where you can find active groups in your target market. The BiggerPockets community is also an excellent supplement to in-person groups.

Meetup.com

Search "real estate investing," "real estate investors," or "landlord" in your target city on Meetup.com. Many active informal groups operate exclusively through this platform.

Facebook Groups

Local real estate investor Facebook groups are often active deal-sharing and networking communities. Search "[Your City] Real Estate Investors" or "[Your City] Landlords."

LinkedIn

Professional investors and real estate groups on LinkedIn can surface both local clubs and virtual communities organized around specific investment strategies.

Local Real Estate Offices

Boutique commercial real estate firms and investment-focused realty offices often know which clubs are active and worth attending. Ask a commercial broker or investment-property agent for recommendations.

How to Evaluate a Real Estate Investment Club

Not all clubs are created equal. Some are excellent educational and networking resources. Others are primarily vendor marketing events thinly disguised as investment education. Before committing significant time or money, evaluate:

Quality of Leadership

Who runs the club? Are they active investors with real track records, or primarily sales people? The best clubs are led by practicing investors who share genuine knowledge.

Composition of Members

What's the ratio of beginners to experienced investors? Clubs dominated by beginners offer limited experienced perspective. Clubs with a mix — plenty of veterans willing to engage with newcomers — create the best learning environment.

Educational Content

Are the presentations substantive and educational, or are they primarily sales pitches for courses, software, or deals? Some clubs have a culture of vendors using presentations as extended commercials. This isn't necessarily disqualifying, but it should be balanced with genuinely educational content.

Openness to New Members

The best clubs are welcoming to newcomers and actively mentor new investors. If veterans treat beginners dismissively or the culture is cliquish, find a different group.

Meeting Frequency and Format

Monthly meetings with consistent programming are a good baseline. Clubs with subgroups, workshops, or smaller mastermind sessions often provide deeper value than a single monthly event.

How to Get the Most from Investment Club Membership

Showing up to meetings isn't enough. Active participation is what delivers real value.

Come Prepared

Before each meeting:

  • Review your current portfolio and any deals you're evaluating
  • Prepare one or two specific questions you want answered
  • Identify two or three people you want to connect with based on the membership directory or attendee list

Be a Contributor, Not Just a Consumer

The members who get the most referrals, deal flow, and connections are the ones who give generously — sharing knowledge, making introductions, and contributing to discussions. Even as a beginner, you can add value by sharing your specific market research, your recent due diligence learnings, or introducing people who should know each other.

Follow Up After Every Meeting

Collect business cards or contact information, and follow up within 24-48 hours with a personal note to every meaningful connection. Reference something specific from your conversation. Relationships are built in the follow-up, not the meeting.

Join a Subgroup or Mastermind

If the club has subgroups focused on specific strategies — multifamily, short-term rentals, value-add, beginners — join the one most relevant to your goals. Smaller groups build deeper relationships and more targeted knowledge.

Track Referrals and Reciprocate

When someone refers you to a deal, a contractor, or a lender, acknowledge it and reciprocate when opportunities arise. Your reputation within a club is a long-term asset.

Online Communities as Supplements to In-Person Clubs

While in-person clubs have unique value, online communities extend your network beyond your geographic area. This matters especially if you're investing in markets other than where you live — a common approach for investors seeking better cash-flow markets. Our guide on real estate market analysis covers the fundamentals of evaluating out-of-state markets.

Online communities to consider:

  • BiggerPockets Forums: The largest online real estate investor community, organized by topic and location
  • Facebook investor groups: Many market-specific groups with active discussions and deal sharing
  • Discord servers: Increasingly popular for more real-time investor discussions
  • Substack and newsletter communities: Several real estate investor newsletters have active subscriber communities

Building Your Advisory Network Through Clubs

Beyond deal flow and education, investment clubs are where you'll build the advisory team that supports your growth:

  • The attorney who structures your LLCs and reviews your leases
  • The CPA who specializes in real estate and knows every relevant deduction
  • The lender who has financed 200 investment properties and can move fast
  • The property manager who operates in your target market and takes good care of owners

These relationships are foundational to everything from your rental property business plan to your long-term portfolio management. You won't find them all at one meeting, but over a year of consistent participation, your professional network will grow in ways that compound your investing results significantly.

For investors serious about building a rental property portfolio, investment club participation isn't optional — it's an essential component of the growth strategy.
