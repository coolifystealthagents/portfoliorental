21:T3399,<p>If you own rental property near a military installation — or anywhere that active-duty service members might rent — you need to understand the Servicemembers Civil Relief Act (SCRA) and what it means for your lease obligations. The SCRA is federal law, and it overrides any conflicting lease provision. You cannot contract around it.</p>
<p>A military clause in your lease doesn't give service members rights they wouldn't already have under SCRA. What it does is demonstrate that you understand the law, set clear expectations from the start, and reduce the likelihood of disputes when a service member needs to exercise their termination rights.</p>
<p>This guide explains the SCRA's key lease provisions, what a compliant military clause looks like, and what happens to landlords who don't follow the rules.</p>
<h2 id="what-is-the-servicemembers-civil-relief-act">What Is the Servicemembers Civil Relief Act?</h2>
<p>The <a href="https://www.justice.gov/servicemembers/servicemembers-civil-relief-act">Servicemembers Civil Relief Act (SCRA)</a>, 50 U.S.C. §§ 3901-4043, is a federal law that provides legal protections to active-duty military personnel and their dependents in civil matters, including residential leases. Originally enacted as the Soldiers' and Sailors' Civil Relief Act in 1940, it was substantially updated and renamed in 2003.</p>
<p>The SCRA's protections are automatic for qualifying service members. They do not depend on what the lease says, and a lease provision that purports to waive SCRA rights is generally unenforceable.</p>
<h2 id="who-is-protected-under-the-scra">Who Is Protected Under the SCRA?</h2>
<p>SCRA lease protections apply to:</p>
<ul>
<li>Active-duty members of the Army, Navy, Marine Corps, Air Force, Space Force, and Coast Guard</li>
<li>National Guard and Reserve members called to active duty under federal orders for more than 30 days</li>
<li>Commissioned officers of the Public Health Service or National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration on active service</li>
<li>Dependents of service members in some circumstances (see below)</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Dependents:</strong> The lease termination rights under SCRA may also be exercised by dependents when the service member has been deployed or is on orders. The definition of dependent under SCRA includes spouses and children, as well as any person for whom the service member provides more than half of their support.</p>
<h2 id="scra-lease-termination-rights-the-core-provisions">SCRA Lease Termination Rights: The Core Provisions</h2>
<h3 id="termination-after-entering-military-service">Termination After Entering Military Service</h3>
<p>If a tenant who is not in the military at the time of signing later enters active duty service, they can terminate the lease upon receipt of orders, provided those orders require relocation for at least 90 days.</p>
<p>This situation — a tenant who enlists or is called to service after signing — is governed by 50 U.S.C. § 3955(a)(1)(A).</p>
<h3 id="termination-based-on-pcs-or-deployment-orders">Termination Based on PCS or Deployment Orders</h3>
<p>This is the more common scenario. A service member who is already on active duty when they sign a lease can terminate it under SCRA if they receive:</p>
<p><strong>Permanent Change of Station (PCS) orders</strong> requiring relocation to a duty station at least 35 miles away from the rental unit.</p>
<p><strong>Deployment orders</strong> for a period of at least 90 days.</p>
<p><strong>Orders to move into government quarters</strong> (on-base housing).</p>
<p>The 35-mile rule for PCS orders is an important threshold. If a service member receives orders to a duty station that is, say, 20 miles from their rental, SCRA termination rights may not apply, though your state may have additional protections. Always consult the specific law.</p>
<h2 id="notice-requirements-and-effective-date">Notice Requirements and Effective Date</h2>
<p>To exercise SCRA termination rights, the service member must provide written notice of termination along with a copy of the applicable military orders (or a written certification from a commanding officer when orders are not available). Verbal notice is not sufficient.</p>
<p><strong>Effective date of termination under SCRA:</strong></p>
<p>The effective date depends on the type of lease:</p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Month-to-month leases:</strong> Termination is effective 30 days after the first date on which the next rent payment is due following delivery of the notice.</li>
<li><strong>Fixed-term leases:</strong> Termination is effective on the last day of the month following the month in which proper notice is delivered.</li>
</ul>
<p><strong>Practical example — fixed-term lease:</strong></p>
<p>A service member delivers their PCS orders and written termination notice on April 14. Termination is effective on May 31 — the last day of the month following April (the month in which notice was delivered). Rent for May is still owed.</p>
<p><strong>Practical example — month-to-month lease:</strong></p>
<p>A service member delivers notice on April 14. Their rent is due on the 1st of each month. The next rent due date after notice delivery is May 1. Termination is effective 30 days after May 1 — which is May 31.</p>
<p>In both examples, the service member owes rent through the effective date of termination. They do not owe any rent after that date, regardless of what the lease says.</p>
<h2 id="what-landlords-cannot-do-under-scra">What Landlords Cannot Do Under SCRA</h2>
<p>The SCRA imposes affirmative obligations on landlords and prohibits specific actions:</p>
<p><strong>You cannot charge an early termination fee.</strong> The SCRA explicitly prohibits landlords from charging any penalty, fee, or damages for early termination exercised pursuant to SCRA rights. If your lease has an early termination clause with a fee, that clause cannot be applied against a service member exercising SCRA rights.</p>
<p><strong>You cannot withhold the security deposit</strong> for costs attributable solely to the early termination. You can still apply the deposit to legitimate damage claims or unpaid rent through the effective termination date. See our <a href="/blog/security-deposit-laws-guide">security deposit laws guide</a> for deposit handling requirements.</p>
<p><strong>You cannot attempt to hold the service member liable</strong> for rent beyond the effective termination date. Even if months remain on the lease, SCRA severs the obligation as of the effective date.</p>
<p><strong>You cannot retaliate</strong> against a service member for exercising SCRA rights. This includes issuing violation notices, refusing to return the deposit within required timeframes, or any other adverse action.</p>
<h2 id="penalties-for-non-compliance">Penalties for Non-Compliance</h2>
<p>Landlords who violate SCRA provisions face serious consequences. The U.S. Department of Justice actively enforces SCRA violations and has brought enforcement actions against landlords and property management companies.</p>
<p><strong>Potential consequences include:</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Civil liability</strong> to the service member for actual damages, including any wrongfully withheld deposits, unlawfully collected fees, and consequential damages</li>
<li><strong>Punitive damages</strong> in cases of willful non-compliance</li>
<li><strong>Attorney's fees</strong> awarded to the service member in successful SCRA claims</li>
<li><strong>DOJ enforcement actions</strong> — the DOJ can bring pattern-or-practice cases against landlords who systematically violate SCRA rights</li>
<li><strong>State attorney general actions</strong> — many states have incorporated SCRA provisions into state law with additional enforcement mechanisms</li>
</ul>
<p>There have been multi-million dollar settlements in SCRA cases involving large property management companies. For individual landlords, even a single violation can result in damages far exceeding any rent you might have collected.</p>
<h2 id="what-a-military-clause-in-your-lease-should-say">What a Military Clause in Your Lease Should Say</h2>
<p>Including an explicit military clause in your lease accomplishes two things: it shows good faith and familiarity with the law, and it gives the service member a clear framework for exercising their rights rather than navigating the issue from scratch.</p>
<p><strong>Sample military clause (not legal advice — consult your attorney):</strong></p>
<p>"Military Clause: In accordance with the Servicemembers Civil Relief Act, 50 U.S.C. §§ 3901 et seq., a Tenant who is an active-duty member of the Armed Forces of the United States may terminate this Lease upon receipt of (a) permanent change of station orders requiring relocation 35 or more miles from the premises; (b) deployment orders for a period of 90 days or more; or (c) orders to occupy government quarters. To exercise this right, Tenant must provide written notice of termination together with a copy of the applicable orders (or a commanding officer certification if orders are unavailable). Termination shall be effective on the last day of the month following the month in which such notice is delivered. Rent shall be owed through the effective date of termination only. No early termination fee shall apply to termination pursuant to this section."</p>
<p>This clause is a summary of SCRA rights, not an expansion or modification. The rights themselves are set by federal law.</p>
<p>For additional guidance on full lease drafting, see our <a href="/blog/lease-agreement-template-guide">lease agreement template guide</a>.</p>
<h2 id="documentation-best-practices-for-scra-terminations">Documentation Best Practices for SCRA Terminations</h2>
<p>When a service member delivers SCRA termination notice:</p>
<ol>
<li>
<p><strong>Acknowledge receipt in writing.</strong> Send a written confirmation that you received the notice and orders, state the effective termination date you've calculated, and confirm the rent owed through that date.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Keep copies of all orders and correspondence.</strong> Store the original termination notice, the military orders, your acknowledgment letter, and any subsequent communications.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Process the move-out like any standard move-out.</strong> Conduct a walk-through inspection, document the condition of the unit, and handle the security deposit according to your state's laws and SCRA requirements.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Return the deposit promptly.</strong> Apply it only to legitimate damage claims and unpaid rent through the effective termination date. Any withheld amounts must be itemized in writing.</p>
</li>
<li>
<p><strong>Do not attempt to pursue the service member for rent beyond the termination date.</strong> This is a common and costly mistake.</p>
</li>
</ol>
<h2 id="state-law-protections-beyond-scra">State Law Protections Beyond SCRA</h2>
<p>Many states have enacted laws that provide service members with protections that go beyond SCRA. These may include:</p>
<ul>
<li>Shorter notice periods before termination is effective</li>
<li>Protection for National Guard members on state active-duty orders (not covered by federal SCRA)</li>
<li>Extended protections for family members</li>
<li>Lower deployment duration thresholds</li>
</ul>
<p>A military-friendly lease clause should account for applicable state law in addition to SCRA. See our <a href="/blog/landlord-tenant-law-by-state">landlord-tenant law by state</a> guide, and consult a local attorney for state-specific requirements.</p>

For a comprehensive overview of all grounds, fees, and procedures related to tenants leaving before their lease ends, see our guide on <a href="/blog/early-lease-termination">early lease termination</a>.</p>
<p>And for a full overview of tenant rights and responsibilities in the landlord-tenant relationship, see our <a href="/blog/tenant-rights-responsibilities">tenant rights and responsibilities guide</a>.</p>
<h2 id="summary">Summary</h2>
<p>SCRA compliance is not optional, and it is not difficult when you understand the rules. The key principles are:</p>
<ul>
<li>Service members on active duty can terminate their lease for PCS orders (35+ miles away), deployment of 90+ days, or orders to government quarters</li>
<li>They must provide written notice plus a copy of orders; the effective termination date is defined by statute, not by the lease</li>
<li>You cannot charge an early termination fee for SCRA exits</li>
<li>You cannot pursue rent beyond the effective termination date</li>
<li>Non-compliance exposes you to civil liability, punitive damages, and potential DOJ enforcement</li>
</ul>
<p>Including an explicit military clause in your lease, handling SCRA termination requests promptly and correctly, and training any property managers who work on your behalf are the straightforward steps that keep you on the right side of this federal law.</p>
