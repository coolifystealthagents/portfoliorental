Handling Storm and Tree Damage at Rental Properties

Storms and falling trees cause some of the most dramatic and costly damage to rental properties. A large tree falling on a roof can cause $20,000 to $100,000+ in damage, and even a significant limb strike can require emergency tarping, structural repair, and extensive interior remediation. Beyond the financial impact, storm and tree damage raises important questions about landlord liability, tenant displacement, and insurance coverage.

This guide covers how to handle storm and tree damage at rental properties — from emergency response to insurance claims, tenant communication, liability, and proactive tree maintenance.

Common Types of Storm Damage to Rental Properties

Storm events cause a range of damage types:

  • Direct tree or limb falls — The tree or limb directly strikes the roof, walls, or vehicles
  • Wind damage to roofing — Lost shingles, lifted flashing, or ridge cap damage from high winds
  • Ice storm damage — Ice loading causes limb breakage and can damage gutters, roofing, and utilities
  • Hail damage — Impacts on roofing, siding, windows, and HVAC equipment
  • Flooding from storms — See our guide on water damage in rental properties
  • Power line contact — Trees that contact overhead power lines create fire and electrocution risks requiring immediate utility company response

Emergency Response After Storm Damage

Step 1: Prioritize Safety

Do not approach fallen trees, downed limbs, or damaged structures until you have confirmed:

  • No downed or energized power lines are involved — treat any downed line as live and keep everyone at least 30 feet away; call the utility company
  • The structure is structurally stable — if the tree is still partially supported by the roof, the roof structure may be compromised
  • There are no active gas leaks (smell, sound of hissing)

If any of these hazards exist, call 911 and the utility company immediately and keep tenants evacuated.

Step 2: Protect the Interior

After safety is confirmed, the priority is preventing additional water damage from the open structure:

  • Call an emergency maintenance contractor for immediate emergency tarping
  • Cover any openings in the roof or walls with heavy polyethylene sheeting
  • Move tenant belongings away from exposed areas if possible
  • Document all damage with photographs and video before any cleanup begins

Step 3: Document Everything

Before removing debris or starting any repairs:

  • Photograph and video the exterior damage, interior damage, and the tree or limb
  • Note the size, species, and condition of the tree (was it alive or dead/diseased?)
  • Photograph any visible damage to vehicles if applicable
  • Contact your insurance carrier immediately

Step 4: Remove the Tree or Limb

Do not attempt to remove large trees or significant limbs yourself. Hire a licensed and insured tree service:

  • Insurance adjuster should assess damage before significant debris removal where possible
  • Emergency removal to protect life and prevent further building damage is appropriate before the adjuster visit
  • Document what was removed and retain receipts

Insurance Coverage for Storm and Tree Damage

Your Landlord Insurance Policy

Most landlord insurance (dwelling fire) policies cover damage to the building from:

  • Wind and hail (windstorm coverage)
  • Falling objects, including trees (whether the tree was on your property or a neighbor's)
  • Weight of ice and snow

Important: Most policies cover the damage to your building from the tree — not the cost of removing the tree from your yard if it did not damage a structure. If a tree falls in the yard but causes no structural damage, removal may not be covered.

Coverage for Damage to Tenant Property

Your landlord insurance does not cover tenants' personal property. Tenants' belongings damaged by storm or tree damage are the tenant's renter's insurance responsibility. This is one of the primary reasons to require renter's insurance in your lease.

Coverage for Tenant's Vehicle

A tenant's vehicle damaged by a falling tree from your property is typically handled through the tenant's comprehensive auto insurance, not your landlord policy. This may seem counterintuitive — see the liability section below for when you could have additional exposure.

Rental Income Coverage

If the unit is uninhabitable after storm damage, your landlord policy may include fair rental value or rental income coverage that pays you for lost rent during repairs. Verify your coverage limits — typically this covers 12 months of rental income during a covered loss.

Tenant Communication After Storm Damage

Communicate with tenants immediately after any storm damage:

Immediate Communication

  • Confirm the tenant's safety
  • Explain the extent of the damage and what you are doing to address it
  • Provide any safety instructions (stay out of damaged areas, etc.)
  • Give an initial timeline for emergency repairs
  • Remind them to document and file a renter's insurance claim for any personal property damage

Relocation

If the unit is uninhabitable:

  • Provide temporary housing or a rent abatement for the period the unit is uninhabitable
  • Some landlord policies cover temporary housing costs for tenants displaced by a covered loss
  • Communicate the estimated repair timeline in writing

Liability: When Is the Landlord Responsible?

The question of liability when a tree damages a tenant's property, vehicle, or injures a person is complex and depends heavily on whether the landlord knew or should have known the tree posed a hazard.

Healthy Tree, Storm Event

When a healthy, well-maintained tree falls during an unusual storm (high winds, ice storm, tornado), most courts find this to be an act of nature. The landlord is generally not liable for damage to tenant property or vehicles from a healthy tree.

Dead, Diseased, or Structurally Compromised Tree

If a tree was visibly dead, diseased, or leaning dangerously, and the landlord failed to remove it despite:

  • Knowing about the condition
  • Having received warnings from a tree service or arborist
  • Having received complaints from tenants about the tree

...then the landlord may be liable for resulting damage. Courts look at whether the landlord had notice of the hazard and failed to act.

Neighbor's Tree

If a neighbor's tree falls on your rental property:

  • Your insurance covers damage to the building
  • You may have a claim against the neighbor if you can establish they were negligent (had a dead or obviously dangerous tree they failed to remove)
  • Neighbor claims are complex and often not pursued for modest damage amounts

Proactive Tree Management

The most effective way to reduce storm and tree damage risk — and minimize liability exposure — is a proactive tree management program:

Annual Tree Inspection

Each spring, walk the property and inspect all trees for:

  • Dead or dying branches — Look for absence of leaves or buds in season, brittle or cracked bark, fungal growth at base or on trunk
  • Structural defects — Included bark (where two major limbs grow in a V that invites splitting), heavy side weight, trunk damage, cavities
  • Proximity to the building — Branches overhanging the roof or within striking distance of structures
  • Leaning — Newly leaning trees or those that have progressively leaned over time
  • Root zone disturbance — Construction, soil compaction, or grade changes near tree roots

Professional Arborist Assessment

For any tree that shows significant defects or is large enough to cause major damage if it falls:

  • Hire a certified arborist (International Society of Arboriculture, ISA certified) for an assessment
  • Get a written report — this documents that you sought professional advice and acted on it
  • Have the arborist recommend any needed pruning, cabling, or removal

An ISA-certified arborist can be found through the ISA's website (treesaregood.org).

Documentation

Maintain records of:

  • Annual tree inspection dates
  • Any professional arborist reports
  • All tree work performed (pruning, removal, cabling), including contractor invoices
  • Any tenant complaints about trees with your written responses

This documentation demonstrates a reasonable standard of care if a tree-related incident leads to a legal claim.

After the Storm: Repair Timeline

Major storm damage repairs typically require:

  1. Emergency tarping and securing — Within hours of the event
  2. Debris removal — Within 1-7 days
  3. Insurance adjuster assessment — Within days to 1-2 weeks
  4. Structural repair estimate — Contractor visits and estimates take 1-2 weeks
  5. Roof replacement or major structural repair — 2-8 weeks depending on contractor availability and material supply
  6. Interior repairs — Drywall, painting, flooring — an additional 2-4 weeks following exterior structural repairs

Communicate realistic timelines to tenants. If the unit will be uninhabitable for an extended period, coordinate with your insurance company for rental income coverage and tenant relocation support.

Documenting Storm Damage for Insurance Claims

A strong insurance claim requires:

  • Photographs and video taken immediately after the event
  • A complete inventory of all damaged components
  • Professional repair estimates from licensed contractors
  • Any arborist reports about the tree's condition before the event
  • Your maintenance records showing ongoing tree care

Conclusion

Storm and tree damage is an emergency that requires immediate safety assessment, rapid protective measures to prevent additional water damage, and clear communication with tenants. Proactive tree maintenance — annual inspections, professional arborist assessments for large or problematic trees, and prompt removal of dead or dangerous trees — is your most effective tool for reducing both the frequency of tree damage events and your liability exposure when they do occur.
