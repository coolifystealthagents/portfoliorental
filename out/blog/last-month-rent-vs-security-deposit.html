Last Month's Rent vs Security Deposit Explained

When tenants move into a rental property, landlords commonly collect money upfront beyond the first month's rent. Two of the most common upfront payments are the security deposit and last month's rent. While both are collected at the beginning of a tenancy, they serve entirely different legal and financial purposes — and treating them the same way can create significant legal liability.

Understanding exactly what each payment is, how it must be handled, and what you can legally use it for is critical for every landlord. General landlord-tenant law principles govern both instruments, but the details vary by state.

What Is a Security Deposit?

A security deposit is a refundable amount held by the landlord as a financial guarantee against:

  • Unpaid rent
  • Physical damage to the unit beyond normal wear and tear
  • Unpaid utilities the landlord is responsible for
  • Other costs specified in the lease and permitted by state law

The key word is refundable. At the end of the tenancy, the landlord must return the security deposit (minus any permissible deductions) to the tenant within the state-mandated timeframe — typically 14 to 45 days depending on the state. See the security deposit return timeline guide for state-by-state requirements.

A security deposit is not income to the landlord. It is held in trust for the tenant and cannot be spent or commingled with the landlord's operating funds in many states.

What Is Last Month's Rent?

Last month's rent (LMR) is prepaid rent — the tenant pays the final month's rent in advance at the start of the tenancy. Unlike a security deposit, last month's rent is rent, not a deposit. It is payment for occupancy during the final month of the tenancy.

Because it is rent, last month's rent:

  • Is income to the landlord (usually reportable in the year received)
  • Cannot be used for damage repairs or cleaning costs — it is strictly applied to the rent obligation
  • Does not need to be "returned" at the end of the tenancy — it is applied to rent
  • Is not subject to the same deposit return notice requirements as a security deposit

However, because it involves holding the tenant's money for a potentially long period, several states regulate last month's rent collection similarly to security deposits — including requiring interest payments and limiting the total amount collected.

How States Treat Last Month's Rent

Not all states treat last month's rent the same way. There are three general approaches:

States That Count LMR Toward the Deposit Cap

In some states, last month's rent counts against the maximum deposit amount the landlord can collect. For example:

  • Massachusetts — The maximum "security deposit" is one month's rent. Additionally, a landlord may collect first month's rent, last month's rent, and a security deposit — but the security deposit itself cannot exceed one month's rent. However, the LMR collected must be held separately and earn interest.
  • New York — As of the 2019 HSTPA, the total security deposit is capped at one month's rent. Last month's rent is permitted but must be disclosed properly.

States That Regulate LMR Holding Requirements

Some states require that last month's rent be held in a separate account and/or earn interest, similar to a security deposit:

  • Massachusetts requires landlords to hold LMR in a separate, interest-bearing account and pay annual interest to the tenant.
  • Connecticut requires disclosure of any bank account holding tenant prepaid funds.

States With No Specific LMR Rules

Many states do not specifically regulate last month's rent beyond general landlord-tenant principles. In these states, the LMR is treated as prepaid rent and is governed primarily by contract law and basic fair dealing principles.

The Tax Implications of Last Month's Rent

Because last month's rent is rent — not a deposit — it may be taxable income in the year you receive it, not the year it is applied. The IRS rental income guidelines and most state tax authorities treat prepaid rent as income in the year received.

Example: If you collect last month's rent in January 2026 and the tenant's final month is December 2027, you may need to report that income on your 2026 tax return.

Consult your tax advisor about the proper treatment of prepaid rent in your specific situation. This is one reason some landlords prefer to collect a larger security deposit rather than last month's rent — deposits are not income until deductions are made. For a full overview of allowable deductions, see the security deposit deductions guide.

Using Last Month's Rent Correctly at Move-Out

When the tenant gives notice to vacate, the last month's rent is applied to the final month's rent obligation. The process:

  1. Confirm the tenant's final month of occupancy
  2. Apply the prepaid last month's rent to that month's rent — the tenant owes nothing further for rent that month
  3. If the tenant vacates early, the LMR may cover the last partial month pro-rated in some cases
  4. Conduct a separate accounting for the security deposit — any unpaid rent, damages, or permitted costs are deducted from the security deposit, not the LMR

What If the Tenant Skips Out?

If a tenant abandons the property before their final month, the last month's rent may be applied to the final occupied month or to unpaid rent depending on your lease terms and state law. It cannot be used for property damage — that comes from the security deposit.

Should You Collect Last Month's Rent?

There are arguments both for and against collecting last month's rent.

Reasons to Collect LMR

  • Protects against rent default at the very end of a tenancy, when some tenants stop paying because they know they are leaving
  • Provides additional financial security beyond the standard security deposit
  • Useful in markets where deposits are capped at one month's rent and you want additional protection

Reasons Not to Collect LMR

  • Creates an immediate tax liability (income in the year received)
  • May require separate holding accounts and interest in some states
  • Can reduce your tenant pool — asking for first, last, and security deposit upfront is a significant financial burden for many renters
  • Adds administrative complexity at move-out
  • In some rent-controlled markets, collecting LMR may interact with rent increase rules in complex ways

Practical Scenarios

Scenario 1: Tenant Leaves Without Paying Last Month's Rent

A tenant who has already paid last month's rent as a prepayment cannot simply stop paying rent during their final month. However, some tenants may try to informally "use" the LMR for the last month even if you collected it as part of a combined deposit arrangement. Your lease should clearly address this — many landlords include language stating that the last month's rent is not to be applied to rent by the tenant and is held separately.

Scenario 2: Damages Exceed the Security Deposit

If repair costs at move-out exceed the security deposit, you must pursue the tenant in small claims court for the excess. You cannot use the last month's rent for damages — it is rent, and applying it to non-rent costs would be improper.

Scenario 3: Lease Breaks Before the Final Month

If the tenant breaks their lease six months early, the last month's rent may be applied to any unpaid rent obligations depending on your state's law and your lease terms. Consult your lease provisions and applicable law for the proper treatment in this scenario.

Best Practices for Managing Upfront Funds

  • Open separate bank accounts — Hold the security deposit in a dedicated trust/escrow account and, where required, the last month's rent in a separate account. Never commingle with operating funds.
  • Clearly identify each payment in the lease — Your lease should separately identify the security deposit amount and the last month's rent amount with distinct sections. Consult the lease agreement template guide for proper clause language.
  • Provide a receipt — In many states, you are required to provide written receipts for all deposits and prepaid funds collected.
  • Track interest obligations — If your state requires interest on held deposits or LMR, track this carefully and pay it at the required intervals.
  • Consult a tax professional — Get guidance on how to properly report LMR on your tax return in the year received. The IRS provides additional detail on rental expense deductions.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I require both last month's rent and a security deposit?

Yes, in most states — though some states count the LMR toward the deposit cap. Always verify your state's specific limits.

Can I use last month's rent to cover unpaid utilities?

No. Last month's rent is a rent payment and can only be applied to rent. Utilities and damages come from the security deposit.

What if the tenant asks for the last month's rent back when they move out?

If the last month's rent was collected as prepaid rent, it is applied to the final month's rent — there is nothing to return. If the tenant argues they should receive it back, refer to the lease terms that clearly identify it as prepaid rent.

Does last month's rent earn interest?

In some states (Massachusetts, for example), yes — LMR must be held in an interest-bearing account and interest must be paid to the tenant. Check your state's specific requirements.

Conclusion

Last month's rent and a security deposit look similar on the surface — both are collected upfront at the start of a tenancy — but they serve completely different legal and financial purposes. A security deposit is a refundable guarantee held in trust. Last month's rent is prepaid income applied to the final month of occupancy. Mixing up these purposes — or failing to hold them as required by your state — can result in financial penalties and legal disputes.

The simplest approach is to document each payment clearly in the lease, hold them in separate accounts as required by state law, and apply each only for its intended purpose.
