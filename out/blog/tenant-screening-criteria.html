Setting Fair and Legal Tenant Screening Criteria

Setting tenant screening criteria sounds straightforward — decide what you want in a tenant, write it down, apply it consistently. In practice, the process is more nuanced. Criteria that seem reasonable on the surface can cross fair housing lines. Standards applied inconsistently create legal exposure. And criteria that are too rigid can cost you good tenants while criteria that are too loose can lead to costly placements.

This guide walks through how to build tenant screening criteria that are fair, legally defensible, and actually predictive of tenancy success. Whether you are screening your first tenant or standardizing policies across a growing portfolio, having written criteria is the single most important step you can take.

For a broader look at the full screening workflow, see tenant screening best practices.

Why Written Screening Criteria Are Non-Negotiable

Landlords who make screening decisions based on intuition or informal standards face two problems: inconsistency and legal risk.

Inconsistency means you may accept a tenant with a 620 credit score in one case and reject one with a 650 in another, based on factors you were not consciously tracking. If those factors correlate with a protected class — race, national origin, familial status — you may be in violation of the Fair Housing Act even without discriminatory intent.

Legal risk compounds the inconsistency problem. Under fair housing law, disparate impact — policies that disproportionately exclude a protected class, even without intent — can constitute a violation. Documented, consistently applied criteria are your primary defense.

Written criteria serve multiple purposes:

  • They create a paper trail showing you applied the same standards to all applicants
  • They help you make faster decisions because the framework is already built
  • They can be shared with applicants upfront, reducing time wasted on unqualified leads
  • They reduce the emotional pressure of individual decisions

Fair Housing Laws and How They Affect Screening Criteria

The Fair Housing Act prohibits discrimination based on seven protected classes: race, color, national origin, religion, sex, disability, and familial status. Many states and municipalities have expanded that list to include source of income, sexual orientation, gender identity, marital status, age, and other categories.

Before setting any screening criteria, confirm which protected classes apply in your state and city. The U.S. Department of Housing and Urban Development maintains comprehensive fair housing resources that cover both federal requirements and guidance on how criteria should be applied.

Key rules to keep in mind:

  • You cannot ask about or consider national origin, religion, or family status in any screening decision
  • You cannot have a blanket policy against renting to people with disabilities without engaging in the reasonable accommodation process
  • You cannot charge higher security deposits or apply different income requirements based on a protected class
  • You cannot refuse to accept housing vouchers (Section 8) in jurisdictions where source of income is a protected class

The safest approach: apply the same written criteria to every applicant, document your decisions, and ensure your standards are not designed in a way that would disproportionately screen out a protected group.

For a deeper dive into fair housing compliance, see fair housing compliance for rental properties.

Core Components of a Tenant Screening Criteria Policy

Income Requirements

Income criteria are among the most common screening standards and among the most litigation-prone if applied incorrectly.

Standard income threshold: Most landlords require gross monthly income of 2.5x to 3x the monthly rent. A 3x threshold means a tenant applying for a $1,500/month unit should earn at least $4,500/month gross. This is widely accepted and defensible.

Important nuances:

  • Count all verifiable income sources, not just employment income. Social Security, disability payments, housing vouchers (where source of income is protected), child support, and alimony may all qualify
  • Do not require that income come from a specific type of employment. Requiring a W-2 job while excluding self-employed applicants may not be defensible in all markets
  • Document how you calculated income for each applicant

Income-to-rent ratio: Some landlords use a 40x monthly rent rule for annual income (e.g., rent of $1,500 requires annual income of $60,000). This is equivalent to the 3x monthly standard. Pick one approach and apply it consistently.

Credit Score Minimums

Credit scores are a legitimate predictor of payment reliability, but minimum thresholds need to be applied uniformly.

Common credit score standards:

  • Excellent applicant: 720+
  • Acceptable applicant: 650–719
  • Conditional applicant: 580–649 (may require higher deposit or co-signer)
  • Automatic decline: Below 580

These are examples, not universal standards. Set your own thresholds based on your market and risk tolerance, then stick to them.

Contextual factors to consider:

  • Recent derogatory marks (collections, charge-offs within 12–24 months) are more predictive of risk than old negative history
  • Medical collections are often excluded from newer scoring models but may still appear on reports
  • A high score with a thin file (few accounts, short history) may warrant closer review of other factors

What to avoid: Do not decline based on the score alone without reviewing the underlying report. Two applicants with 640 scores may have very different credit profiles.

For guidance on how credit fits into tenant rights disclosures, see tenant rights and responsibilities.

Rental History Standards

Rental history is one of the strongest predictors of tenancy success. An applicant who paid rent on time, maintained the property, and left on good terms with past landlords is likely to do the same for you.

Minimum rental history standards to consider:

  • At least one verifiable prior tenancy (or adequate explanation if first-time renter)
  • No evictions within the past 5–7 years
  • No pattern of early lease terminations
  • No outstanding debt owed to previous landlords

Exceptions and context:

  • First-time renters (recent college graduates, people who previously owned homes) may not have rental history. In these cases, weight income, credit, and references more heavily
  • A single eviction from several years ago may be acceptable if the applicant demonstrates stability since then
  • Applicants who were evicted due to COVID-era hardship may deserve additional consideration depending on the circumstances

Criminal History Policies

Criminal history screening is one of the most legally sensitive areas in tenant selection. HUD has issued guidance warning that blanket bans on renting to people with criminal records may constitute discriminatory housing practices under the Fair Housing Act because of racial disparities in the criminal justice system.

Best practices for criminal history screening:

  • Avoid categorical bans. Instead, conduct individualized assessments
  • Consider the nature of the offense (violent vs. non-violent, drug-related vs. financial)
  • Consider how long ago the offense occurred
  • Consider evidence of rehabilitation or changed circumstances
  • Focus on offenses that pose a demonstrable risk to property or the safety of other residents

Offenses that are generally reasonable to weigh:

  • Manufacturing controlled substances (especially where the property could be used)
  • Violent felony convictions within the past 5–7 years
  • Registered sex offender status

Offenses that should not automatically disqualify an applicant:

  • Arrests without convictions
  • Misdemeanor convictions, especially older ones
  • Drug possession offenses that do not indicate property risk

Document your reasoning for any decision influenced by criminal history. The individualized assessment approach — reviewing the specific offense, its age, and its relevance to the tenancy — is the most defensible standard.

Employment and Stability Factors

Beyond income level, employment stability is a useful indicator of lease compliance. Consider:

  • Length of time at current employer (less than 6 months at a new job may be higher risk)
  • Self-employed applicants with 2+ years of verifiable income history
  • Employment gaps and whether the applicant can explain them credibly

Do not require a specific type of employment or exclude applicants based on income source in protected-class jurisdictions.

How to Document and Communicate Your Criteria

Create a Written Screening Policy

Put your criteria in writing before you advertise a vacancy. The document should include:

  • Income threshold (e.g., gross monthly income of 3x monthly rent)
  • Credit score minimum and how you handle borderline scores
  • Rental history requirements and disqualifying factors
  • Criminal history policy (individualized assessment vs. specific offense categories)
  • Employment and stability standards
  • Application fee and what it covers

Share Criteria with Applicants Upfront

Post your criteria on your rental listing or share them when an applicant first contacts you. This saves time for both parties and demonstrates you apply the same standards to everyone.

Use a Consistent Application Process

Every applicant should complete the same application form and authorize the same screening checks. Do not run a credit check on one applicant and skip it for another. Document the date and outcome of every screening decision.

Apply the Same Standards to Every Applicant

This is the most important rule. If you make an exception for one applicant — accepting a lower credit score, overlooking a prior eviction — document your reasoning and be prepared to make the same exception for future applicants in similar circumstances. Inconsistent exceptions are a liability.

When to Use Conditional Approval

Conditional approval allows you to accept applicants who fall slightly below your standard criteria with additional requirements. Common conditions include:

  • Higher security deposit: Appropriate for applicants with borderline credit or limited rental history (subject to state security deposit limits)
  • Co-signer or guarantor: A creditworthy co-signer takes on legal responsibility for rent and damages
  • Prepaid rent: Some landlords require first and last month's rent upfront for higher-risk applicants (check state laws)
  • Shorter initial lease term: A 6-month lease for a new applicant, with renewal based on payment history

If you offer conditional approval, apply it consistently. Do not offer a co-signer option to one applicant and deny it to another with a similar profile.

Red Flags That Warrant Closer Review (Not Automatic Denial)

These factors alone should not disqualify an applicant, but they warrant additional documentation or discussion:

  • Credit report with recent collections or delinquencies
  • Multiple address changes in a short period
  • Gaps in rental history with no explanation
  • References that are personal contacts rather than prior landlords
  • Self-reported income that does not match bank statement deposits

In each case, give the applicant an opportunity to explain before making a decision.

Credit Reporting and Adverse Action Requirements

Under the Fair Credit Reporting Act (FCRA), if you deny an applicant or take adverse action based on information in a consumer report, you must:

  1. Provide a pre-adverse action notice before the final decision, including a copy of the report
  2. Give the applicant time to dispute any errors
  3. Issue a final adverse action notice with the name of the consumer reporting agency

The Consumer Financial Protection Bureau provides resources on credit reporting rights that landlords should be aware of, particularly when communicating adverse action decisions to applicants.

Building Criteria That Work With Your Full Screening Process

Screening criteria do not exist in isolation. They are most effective when integrated into a broader tenant management system that includes structured reference checks, income verification, and consistent lease documentation.

For guidance on how to connect your criteria to your day-to-day management approach, see tenant management best practices and the detailed walkthrough in tenant screening best practices.

Conclusion

Fair and legal tenant screening criteria are not about finding reasons to reject applicants — they are about identifying residents who can reliably meet their lease obligations. Written criteria protect you legally, make your decisions defensible, and ultimately lead to better placements.

Start with the core pillars: income-to-rent ratio, credit score threshold, rental history standards, and a documented criminal history policy. Apply them consistently, document every decision, and review your criteria annually to make sure they reflect current legal standards and your actual risk experience.

The investment in building a solid screening policy pays off every time you avoid a difficult tenancy before it starts.
