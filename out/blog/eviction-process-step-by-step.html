Eviction is the hardest part of being a landlord. Nobody wants to go through it. But sometimes it is the only option left.

Whether a tenant has stopped paying rent, violated the lease, or created an unsafe environment, you need to know how the eviction process works. Doing it wrong can delay the process by months, cost you thousands of dollars, and even result in the case being thrown out of court.

Eviction falls under the broader scope of tenant management best practices. This guide walks you through the eviction process step by step. You will learn about grounds for eviction, notice requirements, court procedures, costs, and alternatives. By the end, you will have a clear understanding of what to expect and how to protect yourself.

When Is Eviction the Right Choice?

Eviction should be your last resort, not your first reaction. Before you begin, make sure you have exhausted other options.

Ask yourself:

  • Have you communicated the problem clearly to the tenant?
  • Have you given the tenant a chance to fix the issue?
  • Have you documented every incident and conversation?
  • Have you tried mediation or a payment plan?

If you have done all of this and the problem continues, eviction may be necessary. Our guide on how to handle difficult tenants covers the steps you should take before reaching this point.

Grounds for Eviction

You cannot evict a tenant without a legal reason. The grounds for eviction vary by state, but the most common include:

Non-Payment of Rent

This is the most common reason for eviction. If a tenant stops paying rent, you have grounds to evict after the grace period and proper notice have passed.

Lease Violations

Any violation of the lease terms can be grounds for eviction. Common violations include:

  • Unauthorized pets
  • Unauthorized occupants
  • Excessive noise after warnings
  • Smoking in a non-smoking unit
  • Subletting without permission
  • Running a business in a residential unit

Property Damage

If a tenant causes significant damage beyond normal wear and tear, you may have grounds for eviction. Document the damage thoroughly with photos and repair estimates.

Illegal Activity

Drug manufacturing, drug dealing, assault, or other illegal activities on the property are grounds for eviction in every state. In many states, you can use an expedited eviction process for illegal activity.

Holdover Tenancy

When a lease expires and the tenant refuses to leave after proper notice, they become a holdover tenant. You can evict them even if they continue to pay rent.

Health and Safety Violations

Hoarding, unsanitary conditions, or other behaviors that create health hazards can be grounds for eviction.

Important: You cannot evict a tenant for exercising their legal rights. This includes filing complaints with code enforcement, joining a tenants' union, or reporting you to a housing authority. Retaliatory eviction is illegal.

Step 1: Send the Proper Notice

Every eviction starts with a written notice. The type of notice depends on the reason for eviction and your state's laws.

Pay or Quit Notice

Used for non-payment of rent. This notice tells the tenant to pay the overdue rent within a specific number of days or move out.

  • Timeline: 3 to 14 days, depending on the state
  • Content: Amount owed, deadline to pay, consequences of not paying

Cure or Quit Notice

Used for lease violations that the tenant can fix. This notice tells the tenant to correct the violation within a specific number of days or move out.

  • Timeline: 7 to 30 days, depending on the state
  • Content: The specific violation, the lease clause it breaks, what the tenant must do to fix it, deadline

Unconditional Quit Notice

Used for serious violations where the tenant has no option to fix the problem. The tenant must leave within the specified number of days.

  • Timeline: 3 to 30 days, depending on the state and reason
  • Used for: Repeated lease violations, illegal activity, serious property damage

Notice of Non-Renewal

Used when the lease is expiring and you do not want to renew. This is not technically an eviction notice, but it starts the process if the tenant refuses to leave.

  • Timeline: 30 to 90 days before the lease expires, depending on the state

Critical rules for notices:

  • Use the correct notice form for your state
  • Serve the notice properly (in person, posted on the door, or by certified mail, depending on state law)
  • Keep a copy of the notice and proof of service
  • Do not accept partial rent after serving a pay or quit notice (this can restart the notice period in some states)

Consult your state's landlord-tenant laws by state or an attorney to make sure your notice meets every requirement. You can also reference Nolo's eviction notice guides for state-specific forms and timelines. One small error can invalidate the entire eviction.

Step 2: File the Eviction Lawsuit

If the tenant does not comply with the notice by the deadline, you can file an eviction lawsuit. This is sometimes called an "unlawful detainer" action.

Here is what the filing process involves:

  1. Go to your local courthouse. Evictions are handled in small claims court or a specific housing court, depending on your jurisdiction.
  2. Complete the complaint form. This form states who you are, who the tenant is, the property address, the reason for eviction, and the relief you are seeking (possession of the property and unpaid rent).
  3. Attach supporting documents. Include a copy of the lease, the notice you served, proof of service, and any evidence of the violation (photos, payment records, communication logs). The Legal Information Institute at Cornell Law provides the statutory text underlying eviction procedures in each state.
  4. Pay the filing fee. This ranges from $30 to $400, depending on your location.
  5. Serve the tenant. The court will issue a summons that must be served on the tenant. This is usually done by a process server, sheriff, or certified mail.

After the tenant is served, they have a set number of days to respond. This is usually 5 to 30 days, depending on the state.

Step 3: Prepare for the Hearing

The court will schedule a hearing where both parties can present their case. Preparation is the key to a successful outcome.

Gather your evidence:

  • Signed lease agreement
  • Copy of the notice you served, with proof of delivery
  • Rent payment records showing missed or late payments
  • Photos or videos of property damage
  • Written communications (emails, texts, letters)
  • Witness statements from neighbors or other tenants
  • Repair invoices or estimates for damage
  • Any other relevant documentation

Organize your timeline:

Create a clear, chronological timeline of events. Start from when the problem began and include every notice, communication, and incident. Courts like clear, organized presentations.

Dress professionally and be respectful. Address the judge as "Your Honor." Stick to the facts. Do not get emotional or argue with the tenant in court.

Consider hiring an attorney. While you can represent yourself, an attorney improves your chances of success, especially if the tenant has legal representation.

Step 4: Understand Common Tenant Defenses

Tenants have the right to defend themselves in court. Knowing the common defenses helps you prepare.

Improper Notice

The tenant claims the notice was not served correctly, did not include the right information, or did not give enough time. This is the most common defense, and it works if you made a mistake.

Retaliation

The tenant claims you are evicting them because they reported a code violation, requested repairs, or exercised a legal right. If the eviction was filed shortly after the tenant's complaint, the court may see a pattern.

Discrimination

The tenant claims the eviction is based on their race, religion, sex, disability, familial status, or other protected class. Fair housing violations are taken very seriously by courts.

Habitability Issues

The tenant claims the property was not livable. If there are serious maintenance issues you failed to address (no heat, no running water, mold, pest infestations), the tenant may argue they had the right to withhold rent.

Acceptance of Rent

If you accepted rent after serving the eviction notice, the tenant may argue that you waived your right to evict. This is why it is critical not to accept any payment after filing.

Improper Procedure

Any deviation from your state's required eviction procedure can be used as a defense. This includes wrong forms, wrong court, wrong timeline, or wrong service method.

Understanding tenant rights and responsibilities helps you anticipate these defenses and build a stronger case.

Step 5: Obtain the Judgment

After the hearing, the judge will issue a ruling. There are three possible outcomes:

Judgment in Your Favor

The court orders the tenant to vacate by a specific date. If the tenant does not leave voluntarily, you can request a writ of possession, which authorizes the sheriff or marshal to remove the tenant.

Judgment in the Tenant's Favor

The court rules against you. The tenant stays, and you may be ordered to pay the tenant's legal fees. This usually happens when there is a procedural error, a valid defense, or insufficient evidence.

Continuance

The court delays the hearing to a later date. This can happen if either party needs more time to prepare, or if the court calendar is full.

If the judgment is in your favor, do not celebrate yet. You still need to enforce it.

Step 6: Enforce the Judgment

If the tenant does not leave by the date in the court order, you must go through the legal enforcement process.

  1. Request a writ of possession. This is a court order that authorizes law enforcement to physically remove the tenant.
  2. Schedule the lockout. The sheriff or marshal will post a final notice (usually 24 to 72 hours) and then return to remove the tenant and their belongings.
  3. Change the locks. Once the tenant is removed, change the locks immediately.
  4. Handle abandoned property. Most states have rules about how to handle belongings left behind. You may need to store them for a set period before disposing of them.

Never perform a self-help eviction. Do not change the locks, shut off utilities, remove doors or windows, or throw out the tenant's belongings on your own. Self-help evictions are illegal everywhere and can result in lawsuits, fines, and criminal charges against you.

Eviction Costs and Timeline

Eviction is expensive and slow. Here is what you can expect:

Typical Costs

Expense Estimated Cost
Court filing fee $30 - $400
Process server $30 - $100
Attorney fees $500 - $5,000
Lost rent (during process) $1,000 - $5,000+
Property repairs after move-out $500 - $3,000+
Sheriff/lockout fee $50 - $400
Total $2,110 - $13,900+

Typical Timeline

Step Estimated Time
Notice period 3 - 30 days
Filing and service 1 - 2 weeks
Waiting for hearing 2 - 6 weeks
Hearing and judgment 1 day - 2 weeks
Writ of possession 1 - 2 weeks
Total 3 weeks - 4 months

In some states and cities with tenant-friendly laws, the process can take 6 months or longer. Factor this into your planning.

Alternatives to Eviction

Before you file, consider whether an alternative approach might resolve the situation faster and cheaper.

Cash for Keys

Offer the tenant money to leave voluntarily. This sounds counterintuitive, but it is often the fastest and cheapest option. A typical offer is one to two months' rent. The tenant moves out, you avoid court, and you can re-rent the unit weeks or months sooner.

Payment Plan

If the issue is unpaid rent and the tenant wants to stay, offer a structured payment plan. Put it in writing with clear deadlines and consequences for missed payments.

Mediation

A neutral mediator helps both parties reach an agreement. This works well for disputes that are not about money, such as noise, behavior, or lease interpretation issues.

Lease Modification

Sometimes the problem is the lease itself. If a tenant is violating a rule they did not understand, consider modifying the lease to address the issue. This works when the tenant is otherwise a good renter.

Non-Renewal

If the lease is ending soon, simply choose not to renew. Give the required notice and let the lease expire naturally. This avoids the eviction process entirely.

How to Prevent Future Evictions

The best eviction is the one that never happens. Here are ways to reduce your risk:

  • Screen tenants carefully. A thorough screening process catches most problems before they move in.
  • Use a strong lease. Clear terms and expectations prevent misunderstandings.
  • Communicate early. Address issues as soon as they come up. Small problems become big ones when ignored.
  • Build relationships. Tenants who respect their landlord are less likely to cause problems.
  • Stay on top of maintenance. A well-maintained property attracts better tenants and gives them fewer reasons to complain.
  • Know the law. Understanding your rights and obligations helps you act quickly and correctly when problems arise.

After the Eviction: Next Steps

Once the eviction is complete and the tenant has moved out, take these steps:

  1. Inspect the property. Document the condition with photos and video.
  2. Process the security deposit. Deduct for unpaid rent and damage beyond normal wear and tear. Send the itemized statement and any remaining balance within your state's deadline.
  3. Make repairs. Fix any damage and prepare the unit for the next tenant.
  4. Pursue unpaid rent. If the tenant owes money beyond the security deposit, you can pursue a judgment in small claims court.
  5. Update your process. Review what went wrong and improve your screening, lease, or management procedures to prevent it from happening again.

Final Thoughts

Eviction is stressful, expensive, and time-consuming. But sometimes it is the only way to protect your property and your business.

If you must evict, follow these principles:

  • Know the legal grounds for eviction in your state
  • Serve the correct notice in the correct way
  • Document everything from the beginning
  • Follow your state's procedure exactly
  • Prepare thoroughly for the hearing
  • Never take matters into your own hands
  • Consider alternatives before filing

The eviction process is designed to be fair to both parties. When you follow it correctly, the law is on your side. Take it step by step, stay professional, and protect your investment.
