2b:T2988,<p>Choosing between a trust and an LLC for holding your rental properties is one of the most common structuring decisions landlords face. Both offer distinct advantages, and many experienced investors use both for different purposes. The right choice depends on your goals: liability protection, privacy, estate planning, financing flexibility, or some combination.</p>
<p>This guide compares the two structures across the factors that matter most to rental property owners.</p>
<h2 id="llc-overview-for-rental-properties">LLC Overview for Rental Properties</h2>
<p>A limited liability company (LLC) is a business entity that separates your personal assets from your rental property liabilities.</p>
<h3 id="how-it-works">How It Works</h3>
<ul>
<li>You form the LLC with your state's secretary of state</li>
<li>Transfer property ownership to the LLC (see <a href="https://www.sba.gov/business-guide/launch-your-business">SBA business formation guide</a> for LLC setup steps)</li>
<li>The LLC owns and operates the rental property</li>
<li>Rental income flows through the LLC to your personal tax return (for single-member LLCs)</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="key-advantages">Key Advantages</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Liability protection</strong> — Claims against the property (tenant injuries, contractor disputes, environmental issues) are limited to the LLC's assets. Your personal savings, home, and investments are generally protected.</li>
<li><strong>Operational flexibility</strong> — LLCs can have multiple members, assign management roles, and create operating agreements that define decision-making authority</li>
<li><strong>Tax flexibility</strong> — LLCs can be taxed as sole proprietorships, partnerships, S-corps, or C-corps</li>
<li><strong>Credibility</strong> — Tenants, contractors, and lenders deal with a business entity rather than an individual</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="key-disadvantages">Key Disadvantages</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Transfer triggers</strong> — Moving property into an LLC can trigger due-on-sale clauses in mortgages (though the Garn-St. Germain Act provides some protection for transfers to trusts)</li>
<li><strong>Financing challenges</strong> — Some lenders do not offer residential mortgages to LLCs, or charge higher rates for commercial loans</li>
<li><strong>Ongoing costs</strong> — Annual state filing fees, registered agent fees, and potentially separate tax returns</li>
<li><strong>Public record</strong> — LLC ownership and registered agent information is generally public</li>
</ul>
<p>For a deeper look at LLC benefits, see our <a href="/blog/rental-property-llc-benefits">rental property LLC benefits guide</a>.</p>
<h2 id="trust-overview-for-rental-properties">Trust Overview for Rental Properties</h2>
<p>A trust is a legal arrangement where a trustee holds property for the benefit of beneficiaries. Several types of trusts are relevant to rental property owners.</p>
<h3 id="revocable-living-trust">Revocable Living Trust</h3>
<ul>
<li>You create the trust and typically serve as both trustee and beneficiary during your lifetime</li>
<li>Property transfers into the trust and is managed according to trust terms</li>
<li>You retain full control during your lifetime</li>
<li>At death, the property passes to successor beneficiaries without probate</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="land-trust">Land Trust</h3>
<ul>
<li>A specialized trust designed specifically for real estate</li>
<li>The property is held in the trust's name</li>
<li>A trustee (often a title company or attorney) holds legal title</li>
<li>The beneficial owner (you) retains control and economic benefits</li>
<li>Provides privacy because public records show the trust name, not your personal name</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="irrevocable-trust">Irrevocable Trust</h3>
<ul>
<li>Once created, you give up control of the property</li>
<li>Provides stronger asset protection than revocable trusts</li>
<li>May offer estate tax benefits for large estates</li>
<li>Less common for active rental property management due to loss of control</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="key-advantages">Key Advantages</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Privacy</strong> — Land trusts keep your name off public records, reducing exposure to lawsuits and solicitation</li>
<li><strong>Estate planning</strong> — Revocable trusts avoid probate, ensuring smooth property transfer at death</li>
<li><strong>Financing</strong> — Transferring property into a revocable trust typically does not trigger due-on-sale clauses under the Garn-St. Germain Act</li>
<li><strong>No annual state fees</strong> — Trusts generally do not require annual state filings or fees</li>
<li><strong>Simplicity</strong> — Less administrative overhead than an LLC for a single property</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="key-disadvantages">Key Disadvantages</h3>
<ul>
<li><strong>Limited liability protection</strong> — Revocable trusts provide little to no liability protection. If someone sues over a property in a revocable trust, your personal assets are still at risk.</li>
<li><strong>No operational structure</strong> — Trusts are not designed for business operations the way LLCs are</li>
<li><strong>Complexity for multiple properties</strong> — Managing multiple trusts can be administratively burdensome</li>
<li><strong>Limited creditor protection</strong> — Revocable trust assets are generally reachable by your creditors</li>
</ul>

<h2 id="the-combined-approach-trust-llc">The Combined Approach: Trust + LLC</h2>
<p>Many experienced investors combine both structures:</p>
<h3 id="land-trust-holding-llc-interest">Land Trust Holding LLC Interest</h3>
<ul>
<li>Create an LLC to own and operate the rental property</li>
<li>Transfer your LLC membership interest into a land trust</li>
<li>The LLC provides liability protection</li>
<li>The land trust provides privacy (your name is not connected to the LLC in public records)</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="llc-as-trust-beneficiary">LLC as Trust Beneficiary</h3>
<ul>
<li>Create a revocable trust for estate planning</li>
<li>The trust owns the LLC membership interests</li>
<li>The LLC owns and operates the properties</li>
<li>At your death, LLC interests pass through the trust without probate</li>
<li>The LLC continues to provide liability protection</li>
</ul>
<p>This layered approach provides liability protection, privacy, and estate planning in a single structure.</p>
<h2 id="choosing-the-right-structure">Choosing the Right Structure</h2>
<h3 id="best-for-single-property-simple-situation">Best for Single Property, Simple Situation</h3>
<p><strong>Land trust</strong> — Provides privacy and probate avoidance without the ongoing cost of an LLC. Pair with strong <a href="/blog/landlord-insurance-requirements">landlord insurance</a> and an <a href="/blog/umbrella-insurance-landlord">umbrella policy</a> for liability protection.</p>
<h3 id="best-for-multiple-properties-active-management">Best for Multiple Properties, Active Management</h3>
<p><strong>LLC (or multiple LLCs)</strong> — Provides liability protection and operational structure. Some investors use a separate LLC for each property or group of properties to further isolate liability.</p>
<h3 id="best-for-estate-planning-focus">Best for Estate Planning Focus</h3>
<p><strong>Revocable living trust</strong> — Ensures property passes to heirs without probate. Combine with an LLC for liability protection.</p>
<h3 id="best-for-maximum-protection">Best for Maximum Protection</h3>
<p><strong>LLC + land trust</strong> — The LLC provides liability protection and operational structure. The land trust provides privacy and estate planning benefits.</p>
<h2 id="tax-considerations">Tax Considerations</h2>
<h3 id="single-member-llc">Single-Member LLC</h3>
<ul>
<li>Treated as a disregarded entity by the IRS</li>
<li>Income and expenses reported on your personal Schedule E (see <a href="https://www.irs.gov/publications/p527">IRS Publication 527</a> for rental property tax rules)</li>
<li>No separate tax return required (though some states require LLC-level filings)</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="multi-member-llc">Multi-Member LLC</h3>
<ul>
<li>Treated as a partnership by the IRS</li>
<li>Requires a separate partnership tax return (Form 1065)</li>
<li>Income and expenses pass through to members' personal returns</li>
</ul>
<h3 id="trust">Trust</h3>
<ul>
<li>Revocable trusts use your Social Security number for tax purposes</li>
<li>Income is reported on your personal return</li>
<li>Irrevocable trusts file their own tax returns and may have different tax rates</li>
</ul>
<p>Consult a tax professional for your specific situation. Entity structure decisions have tax consequences that should be evaluated alongside liability and estate planning goals. The <a href="https://www.irs.gov/publications/p946">IRS depreciation guide</a> covers how property depreciation works across different entity types.</p>
<h2 id="key-takeaways">Key Takeaways</h2>
<p>Trusts and LLCs serve different primary purposes. LLCs excel at liability protection and business operations. Trusts excel at privacy and estate planning. The best approach for many landlords is a combination of both. Start with the protection that addresses your most pressing need, and build additional layers as your portfolio and assets grow.</p>
