Adding Laundry Room Revenue to Your Rental

Shared laundry facilities are one of the most consistently profitable amenity investments a multifamily landlord can make. Tenants will pay for the convenience of on-site laundry—and unlike most property improvements, a laundry room generates income from the first week of operation with minimal ongoing management.

This guide covers everything from estimating your revenue potential to choosing the right equipment model, so you can decide whether adding laundry room revenue makes sense for your property.

Why Shared Laundry Still Makes Sense

With in-unit laundry increasingly common, you might wonder if shared laundry is worth the investment. Here's why it often is:

  • In-unit laundry isn't always feasible: Older buildings, small units, and buildings without sufficient plumbing or electrical capacity often can't support in-unit hookups
  • Cost savings vs. in-unit: Shared laundry avoids the cost and maintenance of individual washers/dryers in every unit
  • Revenue generation: Unlike in-unit laundry (which tenants use at no extra charge), shared laundry generates ongoing income
  • Tenant amenity value: Properties with on-site laundry command higher rents than those without—you get the premium AND the laundry revenue

How Much Laundry Room Revenue Can You Earn?

Laundry revenue depends on the number of units, equipment cycles, and pricing:

Revenue estimation formula:

Monthly Revenue = (# of machines) × (average cycles/day) × (days in month) × (price per cycle)

Example: 12-unit apartment building

  • 2 washers, 2 dryers
  • Average utilization: 3 cycles/machine/day (conservative)
  • Price: $2.50/wash, $2.00/dry
  • Washer revenue: 2 × 3 × 30 × $2.50 = $450/month
  • Dryer revenue: 2 × 3 × 30 × $2.00 = $360/month
  • Gross monthly laundry revenue: $810
  • Annual gross: $9,720

Industry benchmarks suggest $15–$25/unit/month in laundry revenue for buildings with well-maintained equipment and competitive pricing. For a 12-unit building: $180–$300/month, or $2,160–$3,600/year.

Larger buildings with more tenants per machine yield lower per-machine utilization due to queuing constraints; smaller buildings with high occupancy often exceed the per-unit benchmarks.

Three Laundry Room Business Models

Model 1: Coin-Operated (Traditional)

The classic approach: coin-operated washers and dryers where tenants pay per use.

Pros:

  • Simple, reliable technology
  • No subscription or app required for tenants
  • Easy to manage

Cons:

  • Cash handling and theft risk
  • Regular coin collection required
  • Increasingly uncommon (tenants may not carry coins)

Best for: Older buildings with existing coin infrastructure; markets where tenants prefer cash payments

Model 2: Smart Card or Token System

Proprietary card or token systems where tenants add value to a card and swipe to pay.

Pros:

  • Eliminates coin handling
  • Tracks usage data
  • Reduces theft

Cons:

  • Tenants must maintain card balance
  • Higher equipment cost than basic coin
  • Card management overhead

Best for: Mid-size multifamily (20–100 units) where a semi-centralized payment system makes sense

Model 3: App-Based Payment (Modern Standard)

Machines equipped with QR codes or NFC readers connected to a mobile app (e.g., LaundryConnect, Laundrylux, Speed Queen Connect). Tenants pay via credit card or digital wallet.

Pros:

  • No cash handling
  • Real-time usage monitoring and alerts
  • Revenue reporting and machine diagnostics remotely
  • Most appealing to modern renters
  • Can implement dynamic pricing (lower prices during off-peak hours)

Cons:

  • Higher equipment cost
  • Technology-dependent (requires good WiFi in laundry room)
  • Less accessible for tenants without smartphones

Best for: New installations in Class B/A properties; tech-forward markets

Model 4: Laundry Service Partnership

Partner with a commercial laundry company that installs and maintains machines at no cost to you in exchange for a revenue share (typically 50–70% to the landlord).

Pros:

  • Zero capital investment
  • Zero equipment maintenance responsibility
  • Immediate income

Cons:

  • Lower revenue share vs. owning equipment outright
  • Less control over pricing and equipment quality
  • Typical contract terms: 5–10 years

Best for: Landlords who want laundry income without capital outlay; larger buildings (20+ units) that are attractive to laundry service companies

Equipment Costs and ROI Calculation

If you're purchasing equipment outright:

Equipment Cost

  • Commercial washer (front-load): $1,200–$2,500 (new), $400–$1,200 (refurbished)
  • Commercial dryer: $1,000–$2,000 (new), $350–$1,000 (refurbished)
  • App payment hardware/software: $300–$800 per machine
  • Plumbing and electrical installation: $2,000–$6,000 (varies by existing infrastructure)
  • Laundry room construction/renovation (if needed): $5,000–$20,000

Total typical investment (2 washers + 2 dryers, new equipment, existing space): $8,000–$15,000

ROI Example

Using the 12-unit example above:

  • Total investment: $12,000
  • Annual revenue: $9,720
  • Annual maintenance/utilities (water, electricity, maintenance): $2,400
  • Net annual laundry income: $7,320
  • Cash-on-cash return: 61%
  • Payback period: 1.6 years

Even at more conservative utilization (50% of the example):

  • Net annual income: $3,060
  • Cash-on-cash return: 25.5%
  • Payback period: 3.9 years

This is among the highest-returning capital improvements available in multifamily real estate.

Property value impact: At a 6.5% cap rate, $7,320 in annual NOI from laundry adds $112,615 in property value—from a $12,000 investment.

For context on how this fits into your overall NOI strategy, see our guide on net operating income for rental properties.

Setting Laundry Pricing

Pricing has a significant impact on both revenue and tenant satisfaction:

Current market rates (2026):

  • Washer: $2.25–$4.00/cycle (varies by market and machine size)
  • Dryer: $1.75–$3.50/cycle (or $0.25–$0.50 per 10 minutes)
  • Premium (large capacity): Add $0.50–$1.00 per cycle

Pricing strategy tips:

  • Research local laundromats to understand what the market supports
  • Price slightly below the nearest laundromat to emphasize on-site convenience
  • Consider a lower introductory rate to establish usage habits
  • Review and adjust pricing annually—most tenants accept modest increases

Laundry Revenue for Single-Family Rentals

Can single-family landlords benefit from laundry revenue? Sometimes, yes:

  • If the house has laundry hookups, offering the machines themselves (as included appliances) allows you to charge higher rent vs. requiring tenants to bring their own
  • Some landlords include coin-op laundry in single-family rentals, but this rarely pencils out—the revenue rarely justifies the equipment cost for one household
  • The better play for single-family: use washer/dryer inclusion as a rent justification rather than a coin-op income stream

Maintaining Laundry Equipment for Maximum Revenue

Equipment downtime is your biggest enemy. A broken washer for 2 weeks = lost revenue + frustrated tenants. Maintenance best practices:

  • Service contract: Commercial equipment companies offer annual service contracts ($200–$600/year) that cover regular maintenance and emergency repairs
  • Regular lint trap cleaning: Weekly or bi-weekly to prevent fire hazards and maintain efficiency
  • Ventilation maintenance: Clogged dryer vents are a fire risk; inspect quarterly
  • Prompt repairs: Keep a relationship with a local appliance repair company for fast response
  • Machine monitoring: App-based systems alert you to malfunctions in real time

Budget 2–3% of equipment replacement cost annually for maintenance. Well-maintained commercial laundry equipment lasts 10–15 years.

Legal and Tax Considerations

Lease clauses: If your lease charges separately for laundry, ensure your lease or building rules clearly describe the laundry policy—availability, pricing, rules, and landlord's right to change pricing.

Utilities: Who pays the water and electricity for the laundry room? This is typically the landlord. Track these separately to calculate your true net income.

Tax deductions: Laundry equipment is depreciable personal property (typically 5-year MACRS schedule). Under Section 179 and bonus depreciation provisions, you may be able to deduct the full equipment cost in Year 1. Consult our rental property depreciation guide for details.

Revenue reporting: Laundry income is rental income for tax purposes. Report it on Schedule E as part of your rental property income.

Laundry Room Revenue as Part of Your Ancillary Income Strategy

Laundry revenue is one component of a broader ancillary income strategy that can add 5–15% to your annual rental income. Other high-ROI ancillary income streams include:

  • Garage and parking rental income
  • Storage unit rental income
  • Pet rent and fees
  • Vending machines (snacks, beverages) in common areas
  • Package lockers (growing demand post-COVID)

Combined, these streams can meaningfully increase your net operating income and property value without acquiring additional properties.

The Coin Laundry Association's industry research provides detailed data on laundry revenue benchmarks and equipment ROI for various building sizes.

Summary

Adding shared laundry to your rental property is one of the highest-ROI improvements available in multifamily real estate. The combination of immediate income generation, strong payback periods (often under 4 years), and meaningful property value increase makes it compelling for most buildings lacking existing laundry facilities.

For maximum return, choose modern app-based payment systems, price competitively with local laundromats, maintain equipment proactively, and track your laundry revenue separately to monitor performance.
