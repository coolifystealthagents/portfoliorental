Full service property management means handing over every operational aspect of your rental to a professional team. From finding tenants to filing taxes, a full service manager handles it all.

You collect your net income and make high-level decisions. They handle everything else.

But full service does not mean the same thing at every company. Services, quality, and pricing vary widely.

This guide explains exactly what full service property management should include, what it costs, and how to decide if it is the right choice for your portfolio.

What Full Service Property Management Means

Full service property management covers the entire lifecycle of rental property operations. It starts before a tenant moves in and continues through their entire tenancy, including move-out, turnover, and re-leasing.

Think of it this way: a full service manager operates your rental as if it were their own business, adhering to professional standards defined by organizations like the National Association of Residential Property Managers. They handle the day-to-day work, follow established systems, and report results to you on a regular schedule.

This is different from a la carte property management, where you hire a company to handle only specific tasks like tenant placement or rent collection. For a complete overview of all management approaches, see our property management services complete guide.

The Complete Services Breakdown

Here is everything a true full service property management company should provide.

Tenant Placement and Leasing

Filling vacancies quickly with qualified tenants is the most important job a property manager does. Every empty day costs you money.

A full service manager handles:

Market analysis and pricing. They research comparable rentals in your area to set the right asking rent. Pricing too high creates long vacancies. Pricing too low leaves money on the table. A skilled manager finds the sweet spot that attracts quality tenants fast.

Property preparation. Before listing your rental, the manager inspects it, recommends any improvements that will increase its appeal, and coordinates cleaning and minor repairs. They know which upgrades deliver a return and which are a waste of money.

Marketing and advertising. Your listing goes on the MLS, Zillow, Apartments.com, Realtor.com, Facebook Marketplace, and other platforms. Professional photos, detailed descriptions, and virtual tours are standard at quality companies. Good marketing cuts vacancy time significantly.

Showings and open houses. The manager schedules and conducts showings, answers prospect questions, and follows up with interested applicants. This is time-intensive work that many landlords underestimate.

Tenant screening. Every applicant goes through a thorough screening process: credit check, criminal background check, eviction history, income verification, employment verification, and landlord references. This step protects your property and your income. The screening criteria must comply with Fair Housing laws.

Lease preparation and execution. The manager prepares a legally compliant lease that protects your interests. They review all terms with the tenant, collect security deposits, and handle the signing process electronically or in person.

Move-in inspection. A detailed, documented move-in inspection records the property's condition with photos and written descriptions. This protects you during the eventual move-out process.

Rent Collection and Financial Management

Money management is the core of property management. A full service company handles:

Rent collection. Tenants pay through an online portal, ensuring funds are tracked and deposited into your account on schedule. Most managers distribute owner payments between the 10th and 15th of each month.

Late payment enforcement. When rent is late, the manager sends notices, assesses late fees per the lease, and initiates collection procedures. They follow a consistent process that complies with your state's landlord-tenant laws.

Security deposit management. Security deposits are held in a separate trust account as required by law. At move-out, the manager conducts inspections, documents any damage, calculates deductions, and returns the balance within the legal timeframe.

Monthly financial statements. You receive a detailed statement showing income, expenses, and net proceeds. Good managers provide this through an online owner portal where you can access reports anytime.

Annual reporting. At year-end, you receive a full financial summary and 1099 tax forms. Some managers also provide profit and loss statements formatted for your accountant. For more on how management fees fit into your financial picture, see our property management fees breakdown.

Bill payment. The manager pays property-related bills on your behalf, including mortgage payments, insurance premiums, HOA dues, and utility bills if applicable.

Maintenance and Repairs

Maintenance management separates good property managers from great ones. A full service company provides:

24/7 emergency response. Tenants can report emergencies any time. The manager dispatches qualified vendors immediately for issues like water leaks, heating failures, or security concerns.

Routine maintenance coordination. Non-emergency repairs are triaged, focus ond, and assigned to vetted vendors. The manager gets competitive bids for larger jobs and supervises the work to ensure quality.

Preventive maintenance programs. Smart managers schedule regular maintenance to prevent costly repairs. This includes HVAC servicing, gutter cleaning, pest treatment, and seasonal property inspections.

Vendor management. A full service manager maintains relationships with licensed, insured contractors across all trades: plumbing, electrical, HVAC, roofing, painting, landscaping, and general handyman services. Their volume buying power often results in lower costs than what you would pay as an individual landlord.

Spending authorization. You set a dollar limit for repairs. The manager handles anything below that threshold without calling you. For larger expenses, they get your approval first and present multiple bids. Common authorization limits range from $300 to $500 per repair.

Property inspections. Regular inspections, typically quarterly or semi-annually, catch maintenance issues early, verify tenant compliance with lease terms, and document the property's condition. These inspections protect your investment and can help you maximize rental income by keeping the property in top shape.

Legal Compliance and Risk Management

Landlord-tenant law is complex and varies by jurisdiction. A full service manager protects you by:

Ensuring lease compliance. The manager enforces lease terms consistently and documents every interaction. When a tenant violates their lease, the manager follows proper notice and cure procedures.

Handling evictions. If eviction becomes necessary, the manager handles the process from notice to court filing to lockout. They work with local eviction attorneys and follow every procedural step to avoid delays or dismissed cases.

Staying current on regulations. Fair housing laws, rent control ordinances, habitability requirements, security deposit rules, and eviction moratoriums change regularly. A full service manager monitors these changes and adjusts procedures accordingly.

Maintaining proper documentation. Every communication, inspection, maintenance request, and financial transaction is documented. This paper trail is your best protection in any legal dispute.

Insurance coordination. The manager verifies that your landlord insurance is adequate and helps you file claims when needed. They also require tenants to carry renter's insurance.

Tenant Relations and Retention

Keeping good tenants is cheaper than finding new ones. A full service manager invests in tenant relationships through:

Responsive communication. Tenants have a professional point of contact for all questions and concerns. Problems are addressed promptly and respectfully.

Lease renewal management. Before a lease expires, the manager evaluates market conditions, recommends a renewal rate, and negotiates with the tenant. They handle all paperwork and documentation.

Conflict resolution. Noise complaints, neighbor disputes, and other tenant-to-tenant conflicts are managed professionally, keeping your property peaceful and your tenants satisfied.

Move-out coordination. When a tenant leaves, the manager conducts a thorough move-out inspection, processes the security deposit, and immediately begins the turnover and re-leasing process to minimize vacancy.

What Full Service Property Management Costs

Full service management typically costs between 8% and 12% of monthly collected rent, plus additional fees for specific services. Here is a realistic cost breakdown for a property renting at $2,000 per month.

Fee Amount Frequency
Monthly management fee (10%) $200 Monthly
Leasing fee (75% of first month rent) $1,500 Per vacancy
Lease renewal fee $200 Per renewal
Maintenance markup 10% on vendor invoices As incurred
Setup fee $300 One-time

Assuming one tenant turnover per year with $3,000 in maintenance costs, your total annual management expense would be about $4,700, or about 19.6% of gross rent.

That sounds like a lot. But consider what you get: professional tenant screening that reduces eviction risk, faster vacancy fills, lower maintenance costs through vendor relationships, legal compliance, and complete freedom from day-to-day operations.

Who Benefits Most from Full Service Management

Full service is not for everyone. It makes the most sense for these property owner profiles:

Out-of-State Investors

If you live in one state and own property in another, full service management is almost essential. You need a local presence for showings, inspections, emergency response, and vendor coordination.

Distance makes self management impractical and risky.

Time-Constrained Professionals

Doctors, lawyers, business owners, and other high-income professionals earn far more per hour at their day job than they would save by self managing. The property management vs self managing math overwhelmingly favors hiring a manager when your hourly rate exceeds $40.

Portfolio Owners with 5+ Units

Complexity scales faster than you expect. Five properties means five sets of tenants, five sets of maintenance issues, five sets of lease expirations, and five times the legal exposure.

Full service management provides the systems and staff to handle this volume without dropping balls.

Passive Income Seekers

If your goal is passive rental income, you need to outsource the active work. Full service management is the cost of making your investment truly passive.

You review reports and make strategic decisions. The manager handles operations.

New Investors

If you are new to rental property, a full service manager teaches you the business while protecting you from costly beginner mistakes. The Institute of Real Estate Management offers educational resources that can help you understand what good management looks like so you can evaluate your manager's performance.

You learn by reviewing their work, not by learning through trial and error.

How to Compare Full Service Providers

Not all full service companies deliver the same quality. When evaluating providers, compare them on these factors:

Service Scope

Get a detailed list of included services. Some companies call themselves full service but charge extra for services that should be standard, like property inspections or lease renewal management.

Technology Platform

Can you access financial reports, maintenance updates, and property documents online 24/7? Do tenants have an online portal for rent payments and maintenance requests?

Technology is not optional in modern property management.

Staffing and Specialization

How many dedicated staff members work on your account? Is there a single point of contact?

Do they have specialists for leasing, maintenance, and accounting, or does one person do everything?

Track Record

Ask for performance data: average vacancy rate, average days to fill, tenant retention rate, and rent collection rate. A good company tracks these numbers and shares them with prospects.

Contract Terms

Review the property management agreement carefully. Look for reasonable termination clauses, clear fee schedules, and fair allocation of responsibilities.

Avoid long lock-in periods with steep cancellation penalties.

Red Flags to Watch For

Avoid full service companies that exhibit these warning signs:

  • No written fee schedule. If they cannot tell you exactly what they charge, walk away.
  • No owner portal or online reporting. This suggests outdated systems and poor transparency.
  • No documented screening criteria. This increases your legal and financial risk.
  • Resistance to providing references. Confident companies want you to talk to their clients.
  • Very low fees. A manager charging 5% in a market where 10% is standard is cutting corners somewhere. Low fees often mean poor service, hidden charges, or both.
  • No maintenance spending limits. You should always approve large expenses before they happen.
  • High-pressure sales tactics. A professional company answers your questions and lets you decide on your timeline.

Making Full Service Work for You

Once you hire a full service manager, set the relationship up for success:

  1. Establish clear communication expectations. Define how often you want updates and what decisions require your input.
  2. Set a realistic maintenance budget. Plan for 10% to 15% of annual rent going toward maintenance and repairs.
  3. Review reports every month. Do not set it and forget it. Stay informed about your property's performance.
  4. Conduct annual reviews. Sit down with your manager once a year to review performance, discuss market changes, and plan for the coming year.
  5. Think strategically. Use your freed-up time to analyze your rental property cash flow, plan purchases, or improve your portfolio.

Final Thoughts

Full service property management is the most full way to protect your rental investment while keeping your time free. It costs money, but for the right owner, it returns far more in time savings, risk reduction, and portfolio growth potential.

Evaluate your needs honestly. If you are managing more properties than you can handle, live far from your rentals, or simply want true passive income, full service management is the path forward.
