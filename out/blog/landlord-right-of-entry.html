Landlord Right of Entry: Laws and Best Practices

You own the property, but your tenant has a legal right to quiet enjoyment of their home. Entering a rental unit is not as simple as using your key whenever you want. Every state has rules governing when, why, and how landlords can enter occupied units. Violating these rules can result in lease termination claims, harassment lawsuits, and regulatory penalties.

This guide covers the legal framework, state-by-state requirements, and best practices for managing property access.

The Legal Framework

Quiet Enjoyment

Tenants have an implied covenant of quiet enjoyment in virtually every residential lease, whether or not it is written into the agreement. This right is grounded in landlord-tenant law principles. This legal principle means the tenant has the right to use their home without unreasonable interference from the landlord.

Right of entry laws balance this tenant right against the landlord's legitimate need to maintain, inspect, and manage the property.

Federal vs State Law

There is no federal law governing landlord entry. Rules come from:

  • State statutes — Most states have specific landlord entry laws
  • Local ordinances — Some cities add additional protections
  • Lease provisions — Your lease can establish entry procedures, but cannot override state minimum protections (see Nolo's landlord-tenant guide for details)
  • Common law — In states without specific statutes, common law principles apply

Common State Requirements

While rules vary by state, most follow similar patterns. Check your state's landlord-tenant laws for specific requirements.

Notice Requirements

Most states require written notice before entry:

  • 24 hours — The most common requirement (California, Oregon, Washington, and many others)
  • 48 hours — Some states require longer notice (Hawaii, Vermont)
  • 12 hours — A few states allow shorter notice (Arizona)
  • Reasonable notice — Some states use this vague standard, generally interpreted as 24-48 hours
  • No statute — A handful of states have no specific requirement, but best practice is still 24 hours minimum

Permitted Reasons for Entry

State laws typically allow landlord entry for:

  • Repairs and maintenance — Both scheduled and tenant-requested
  • Inspections — Routine property inspections at reasonable intervals
  • Showing the unit — To prospective tenants, buyers, or lenders
  • Emergency — Immediate entry without notice
  • Abandonment — If the landlord reasonably believes the tenant has abandoned the unit
  • Court order — When a court authorizes entry

Permitted Hours

Most states restrict entry to reasonable hours, typically defined as:

  • Business hours (8 AM to 6 PM)
  • Daytime hours as defined by the lease
  • Hours agreed upon by both parties

Entry outside these hours requires tenant consent or an emergency.

Emergency Entry

Emergencies are the primary exception to notice requirements. You may enter without notice when:

  • Fire or flooding threatens the property or occupants
  • Gas leak or carbon monoxide is suspected
  • Burst pipe or major water leak requires immediate shutoff
  • Criminal activity is occurring in the unit
  • Welfare check when a tenant is unresponsive and believed to be in danger

Document the emergency and your actions. Even emergency entry should be minimally intrusive — address the emergency and leave.

Best Practices for Property Entry

Written Notice Protocol

Create a standard notice that includes:

  • Date and time of intended entry
  • Reason for entry
  • Name of person(s) who will enter
  • Contact information for questions or rescheduling
  • Delivery method confirmation

Deliver notice through a verifiable method: email with read receipt, text message, posted on the door with a photo, or hand-delivered with signature.

Respect Tenant Schedules

When possible, coordinate entry times with the tenant:

  • Ask for preferred time windows for non-emergency work
  • Avoid entry during typical sleeping hours
  • Work around tenant work schedules when feasible
  • Give extra notice for lengthy or disruptive work

Entry Procedures

Train anyone entering units on proper behavior:

  • Knock and announce before using a key, even with notice
  • Identify yourself and the purpose of your visit
  • Stay within the scope of the stated reason for entry
  • Do not snoop through tenant belongings
  • Leave the unit secure when you leave
  • Document the condition of the unit if relevant

Contractor and Vendor Entry

When contractors enter for maintenance or repairs:

  • Include the contractor's name in your entry notice
  • Provide contractors with specific instructions about tenant interaction
  • Ensure contractors do not enter areas beyond the scope of work
  • Require contractors to knock and announce before entering
  • Have a process for tenants to report inappropriate contractor behavior

When Tenants Refuse Entry

If a tenant refuses entry after proper notice for a legitimate purpose:

Do Not Force Entry

Unless it is an emergency, do not enter over a tenant's objection. Forced entry can escalate to harassment claims, lease disputes, or even criminal charges in some jurisdictions.

Document and Communicate

  1. Document your notice and the tenant's refusal in writing
  2. Remind the tenant of their lease obligations regarding property access
  3. Explain the legal basis for your entry request
  4. Offer alternative times to accommodate their schedule
  5. If refusal continues, send a formal lease violation notice

Escalate if Necessary

Persistent refusal of legitimate entry may be grounds for action under HUD housing regulations and state law:

  • Lease violation notices with cure periods
  • Mediation or dispute resolution
  • In extreme cases, legal action for lease breach

Courts generally side with landlords who follow proper notice procedures and have legitimate reasons for entry.

Inspections

Regular property inspections are important for maintenance and lease compliance, but they must be conducted properly.

Inspection Frequency

Most landlords inspect properties:

  • At move-in (with the tenant present)
  • Annually or semi-annually during the lease term
  • At lease renewal
  • At move-out (with the tenant present)

Excessive inspections can be considered harassment. Quarterly is generally the maximum reasonable frequency for routine inspections.

What to Inspect

Focus on property condition and lease compliance:

  • Structural integrity and safety hazards
  • Plumbing, electrical, and HVAC function
  • Smoke and carbon monoxide detector operation
  • Signs of water damage, mold, or pest issues
  • Lease compliance (unauthorized occupants, pets, modifications)
  • General cleanliness and maintenance

For more on inspections, see our property inspection requirements guide.

Key Takeaways

Right of entry is a balance between your property rights and your tenant's privacy. Follow your state's notice requirements, limit entry to legitimate purposes, respect tenant schedules, and document everything. Proper entry procedures build tenant trust and protect you from legal claims. When in doubt, provide more notice than required and coordinate with your tenants directly.
